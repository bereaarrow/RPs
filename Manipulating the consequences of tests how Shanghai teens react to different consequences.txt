Manipulating the consequences of tests: how Shanghai teens react to di erent consequences
Anran Zhao a, Gavin T. L. Brown a,b and Kane Meissel a
aFaculty of Education and Social Work, The University of Auckland, Auckland, New Zealand; bDepartment of Applied Educational Sciences, Ume  University, Ume , Sweden

ABSTRACT
Students  test-taking motivation has been found to be a predictor of performance. This study tests whether Shanghai students  conceptions of tests and test-taking motivation di er when the consequence of tests have di erent foci (i.e., none, country, or personal). A between- subjects experiment with vignette instructions systematically assigned 1,003 Shanghai senior secondary school students to one of the three vignettes. Students conceptions of tests and test-taking motivation scales were evaluated using factor analyses. Invariance testing suggested invariant relationships between the two constructs across the three groups. Students  general conception of tests meaningfully predicted their reported e ort (  =.18). Latent mean analyses suggested that students  reported e ort, anxiety, and importance were not signi cantly di erent between country at stakes and personal stakes groups, but higher than when no consequences were attached. This study suggests that Shanghai students  test-taking attitudes may contribute to high e ort and consequently high performance on international large-scale assessments.

Introduction
It is presumed that when students are tested, they do their best (Dorans, 2012; Wise,2015). This assumption arises because tests usually have important conse- quences for individual test-takers (e.g., selection, promotion, opportunities). However, considerable research (Knekta & Ekl f, 2015; Thelk et al.,2009; Wise
& DeMars,2005) has shown that test-taking motivation is not automatically high and is highly variable across jurisdictions (Akyol et al., 2018; Gneezy et al., 2019; Zamarro et al., 2019) when tests are designed to describe jurisdic- tional performance rather than evaluate individuals as such. This is important
in the case of high-pro le international large-scale assessments (ILSA) such as the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) and the Trends in

International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS). Clearly, ranks and scores on ILSAs are invalid if it cannot be assumed all students in all jurisdictions have tried their best (Cronbach, 1960).
As one of the most high-performing societies in PISA1, Shanghai was treated as a role model for its e ective educational system (Organisation
for Economic Co-operation and Development [OECD], 2014). It is thus impor- tant to understand Shanghai students  motivation to perform well when sitting a test that is not personally relevant but is important for a country s international ranking. It may well be that Shanghai s stellar performance
re ects persistent test e ort independent of the consequence attached to a test. Because test-taking e ort may lie in the beliefs students have about
the nature and purposes of tests (Brown, Peterson, & Irving, 2009; Brown & Hirschfeld, 2008; Wise & Cotten, 2009), it may be that Shanghai students think that high e ort is needed on all and any tests. This study attempts
to disentangle this problem by asking Shanghai teens to indicate what they consider the purpose of a test is and how hard they might try on a hypothetical test that varies in consequence (i.e., it matters to their grades,
it matters to the country reputation, and it matters only for research pur- poses). This experimental study helps us understand how test-taking motiv- ation and e ort are construed in a jurisdiction that has outperformed the world more than once.
We rst present a theoretical review focused on test-taking motivation, the impact of consequences on motivation, and the relationship between students attitudestowardstestsandtest-takingmotivation.Then,weintroduceempirical work on di erences in test-taking motivation by jurisdiction and describe the testing culture of Shanghai (China). Finally, we present our theoretical model and hypotheses pertaining to the problem of test-taking motivation by type
of test consequence.
The expectancy-value framework and its relationship with e ort expenditure
Rebranded from its broad usage in achievement motivation (i.e., domain- speci c motivation), the expectancy-value theory (EVT; Eccles & Wig eld, 2002) is the most common framework in conceptualising test-taking motivation (i.e., situation-speci c motivation). EVT assumes that students  achievement behaviour (i.e., performance, e ort, persistence) is in uenced by the combi- nation of the value they perceived on the test (i.e., importance, interest, useful- ness, and anxiety) and their expectancies for success.
Research has consistently found that students  perceived importance of the test is a signi cant and positive predictor of reported e ort expenditure (Finney et al., 2018; Knekta & Ekl f,2015; Zilberberg et al., 2014). In terms of the e ect of test anxiety on e ort expenditure, literature has shown inconsistent results. For example, Knekta and Ekl f (2015) found that students reported Test Anxietydid not statistically signi cantly predict their E ort expenditure in both high- and low-stakes contexts among Swedish secondary school students. Instead, among the ve constructs under EVT, only Expectanciesand Importance were shown to be signi cantly related to E ort. Similarly,Penk and Schipolowski (2015) reported that Anxietyhad negligible impact on E ort in a low-stakes large-scale assessment in Germany (similar result seen in Ekl f & Nyroos, 2013). On the other hand, Putwain (2008) found that students  perceived test Anxietywas negatively related to performance in low-stakes (i.e., mock exam) and mid-stakes (i.e., modular exam) conditions, but a small positive relationship was detected in the high-stakes condition (i.e., terminal exam). Meanwhile, Pekrun et al. (2002) also found that Anxiety had an activating e ect which could induce e ort as a means to avoid failure. It appears that there might be
a moderation e ect of stakes on the relationship between Anxietyand E ort. However, the direction and magnitude of the e ect requires further investi- gation. Lastly, the positive relationship between perceived Anxietyand Impor- tance were consistently reported in literature (Ekl f & Nyroos, 2013; Knekta & Ekl f, 2015).
Test-taking motivation, test consequences, and test performance
The consequences attached to a test have an impact on test-taking motivation,
with higher reported e ort in high-stakes exams (Ekl f et al., 2014; Hawthorne
et al., 2015;Smith&Smith,2004;Wolf& Smith,1995).Highlymotivated students tend to outperform less motivated students with the same skill level (Ekl f, 2010). An open question is whether the consequences attached to ILSA have
the same impact on motivation and e ort as those with personal consequences
(e.g., passing a grade, winning a scholarship, etc.).
Increased motivation as a result of higher test consequences is usually linked to higher performance. For example, making the tests relevant to themselves (i.e., count towards grades) or to their teachers (i.e., impact their teachers  employment) were both e ective on increasing test performance when compared to the low-stakes control group (Rutkowski & Wild, 2015). Meta-analyses have also shown that incentives and consequences raise test- taking motivation and performance (Duckworth et al., 2011; Wise & DeMars, 2005). Many empirical studies into the relationship of consequences to
e ort have shown that the level of consequence seems to be associated
with e ort and motivation (Ekl f et al., 2014; Hawthorne et al., 2015; Knekta
& Ekl f, 2015; Lau et al., 2009; Liu et al.,2012; Silm et al., 2013; Thelk et al., 2009). For example, far fewer students demonstrated rapid guessing when institutional consequences were attached to a reading test than when similar students took the same test with no consequences attached (Liu
et al., 2015). Similarly, Knekta (2017) reported that ve motivational constructs
of EVT all increased among ninth-grade pupils when assessed in the oper- ational national test compared to the earlier national test eld trial.
On the other hand, lack of consequences does not always interfere with test- taking motivation and test score performance. Among American university stu- dents, di erent consequences (i.e., test evaluates institution, test provides feed- back, and test matters to personal academic record) revealed no instructional
e ects on their perceived importance, e ort expenditure, and test performance (Finney et al., 2016, 2018). Likewise, Baumert and Demmrich (2001) found no statistically signi cant di erences on test-taking motivation and performance among ninth-grade German pupils assigned to four di erent consequences (i.e., PISA instruction, feedback, personal, and nancial award). Finney et al. (2018) proposed that the inconsistent inferences revealed in literature on the
e ects of test-taking stakes on test-taking motivation (TTM) and performance could be due to the unrealistic instructions provided, arguing that telling stu- dents that test scores will be used for research purposes is overly nuanced. Instead, the lowest stakes in their study speci ed that the test score will be used in a school accountability programme.
Literature on the moderation e ect of test stakes on test-taking motivation
and test-taking e ort is limited. Contrary to the ndings from Putwain (2008), Knekta and Ekl f (2015) found that stakes did not moderate the relationship between perceived importance and e ort expenditure. Finney et al. (2018) reported similar results in that the fully mediated relationship from perceived importance to test performance via e ort expenditure was not moderated by test instructions.
Students  attitudes on tests
Brown (2011) identi ed that students have four major conceptions of assess- ment (i.e., improves learning and teaching; generates social and a ective bene ts; it is ignored; and it is about external factors outside students control). Within a general model of self-regulation, it has been empirically demonstrated that students who make use of assessments to improve their learning have higher test scores than those who view assessments as some- thing to be ignored (Brown & Hirschfeld, 2008; Brown, Irving, et al., 2009). M. Gao (2010) investigated the relationship between students  conceptions of the high-stakes school-based assessments in Hong Kong and found that higher anxiety was associated with increased awareness that the assessments were unfair, inaccurate, and accountable. Wise and Cotten (2009) found that students who agreed that assessment was for improvement had greater test- taking e ort compared with those who endorsed the concept that assessment
was irrelevant. Zilberberg and co-authors (2014) measured US college stu- dents  attitudes towards K—12 (Kindergarten to Grade 12) accountability testing (low stakes) and examined the impact of the attitudes on their test-
taking motivation and performance in a university accountability test (low stakes). They found that students  attitudes on K—12 accountability testing (i.e., test fairness, purpose, parents  concern, and their own dissatisfaction of the test) had negligible e ects on their test-taking motivation (i.e., perceived importance and e ort expenditure) and performance. Nonetheless, as univer- sity-level tests are distinct contexts from K—12 tests, the relationship among attitudes, motivation, and performance under the same context requires further investigation.
Test-taking motivation and jurisdictional heterogeneity
Wise and DeMars (2010) stated that the results from international achieve- ment programmes, such as TIMSS, PISA and PIRLS [Progress in International Reading Literacy Study], will be distorted to the extent that there are cross- country di erences in the mean e ort exhibited by their students  (p. 40). Pre- sumably, students in societies that use high-stakes tests for grade promotion, university admission, et cetera, may hold di erent test-taking motivation than students where low-stakes tests are predominant. Zamarro and her colleagues (2019) found that e ort could account for 32% to 38% of cross-country vari- ation in the three PISA 2009 test domains (i.e., mathematics, reading, and science). Further, students from East Asian societies invested higher levels
of e ort than elsewhere when reanalysing data from PISA 2009. Similarly, Bor- gonovi and Biecek (2016) investigated students  test-taking behaviour using PISA 2015 data, where they used item-position e ect on students  perform- ance for the item as a proxy for endurance level throughout the test, which is argued to be re ective of test-taking motivation. They found that country-level endurance was positively associated with societal attitudes valuing hard work. Further, evidence from PISA 2015 data suggests that after taking into consideration the non-seriousness shown in rapid responding behaviours, country ranking could change by 15 places (Akyol et al., 2018). It is worth noting that the ranking change was less apparent in societies such as Singapore, B-S-J-G2 (China), but more so in jurisdictions such as Brazil, Sweden, and New Zealand.
However, within a jurisdiction, di erences in e ort may not be highly vari- able. For example, although non-e ortful test-taking behaviour existed in a study of response time among 15-year-old students across 86 schools in the
US (Wise et al.,2019), once non-e ortful responses were removed, the mean scores of each school were stable. This suggests that the impact of e ort motiv- ation may matter only when cross-jurisdictional comparisons are made such as seen in the PISA rankings.
The negative e ect of low stakes in the context of ILSA on e ort expenditure
is not always consistent throughout literature. For example, Ekl f (2007, 2008) found high perceived importance and e ort spent on TIMSS 2003 for eighth- grade students in Sweden. Moreover, Hopfenbeck and Kj rnsli (2016) found that, among the Norwegian PISA 2012 sample, though students did not think
the test was important, they invested maximum e ort. In contrast, low reported motivation for TIMSS Advanced Field trial 2008 was found in Sweden (Ekl f, 2010) and in Sweden, Slovenia, and Norway (Ekl f et al., 2014). Further, a study contrasting the same cohort of Swedish PISA 2012 students with those
in the 2012 national test sample (Ekl f & Knekta, 2017) found a large discre- pancy on strength of endorsement for statements such as I felt motivated to do my best on this test  (Cohen s d =.80).
Recently, Gneezy and his colleagues (2019) conducted an experimental study which investigated US and Shanghai high school students response di erences under the in uence of monetary incentives when completing a short test of 25 public PISA items. Speci cally, in both the US and Shanghai groups, they ran- domly assigned participants to a control group with no incentives for perform- ance on the test and a treatment group with $25 (or the equivalent in renminbi RMB for Shanghai students) for performance on the test. Students in the treat- ment group were informed that one dollar (approximately 7 RMB) would be taken away for each incorrectly answered item. They found that US students
in the treatment group gave more e ort (measured in questions attempted, attempted questions answered correctly in proportion, and questions answered correctly in proportion), and achieved substantially higher scores compared to
the control group (e ect size ranged from d =.24 to .28). In contrast, the incen- tives did not elicit di erential performance behaviours between the control and
the treatment groups in Shanghai, as if Shanghai students were immune to the incentives provided in the study.
Testing in Shanghai (China)
Students in China are no strangers to high-stakes tests. In China, education [is] basically examination preparation  (OECD,2011, p. 84). Students are expected to overcome two high-stakes whole-cohort examinations (i.e., the entrance examinations for senior high schools and for universities —zhongkao and gaokao, respectively) as a means of upward social mobility (T. K. Y. Cheung, 2008; Dorgan, 2000). As a result, students are extensively examined as prep- aration for these examinations (Chen & Brown, 2018).
It is worth noting that the promotion rate from junior secondary school to regular senior high school as opposed to vocational pathway was 55% in 2012 (Shanghai Government, 2013). Thus, in a way, the zhongkao has even higher stakes than the gaokao, which had a higher acceptance rate of 88% in 2012.
This means that most of the Shanghai PISA cohort3 is either going to take, or has just passed or failed, arguably the most high-stakes test in Chinese schooling. This feature of the Shanghai PISA cohort is very similar to samples and populations in eastern Asia (e.g., Korea, Hong Kong, Singapore),
where high-stakes assessments and high average PISA performance is the norm. However, it is a considerable di erence to the Scandinavian samples studied previously (Ekl f et al., 2014).
Given the importance of zhongkao and gaokao in Chinese society, students are expected on the part of their parents and teachers to exert maximum
e ort regardless of the test form. Diligence and good attitudes and behaviours
are valued and expected as much as academic performance (L. Gao & Watkins, 2001,2002; Li,2002). Chinese teachers also perceive assessments as a means for students  holistic development, which contribute to their life-long learning and becoming good citizens (Brown et al., 2011). Chinese students have demon- strated their acceptance of the societal pressure of the high expectations on performance and attitudes (Peterson et al., 2013). Research shows that Chinese students are expected, and accustomed to, exerting persevering
e ort for tests regardless of the test form (e.g., in class or public examinations; Chen & Brown,2018; L. Gao & Watkins,2001; Peterson et al., 2013). Li (2002) has argued that persistence, high levels of e ort, as well as achievement, are highly valued in Chinese societies.
Research into Chinese students  conceptions of assessment has found that secondary school students hold strong beliefs on assessment and that they have internalised the purpose of assessment as student moral and skill develop- ment and associated that with positive emotions towards assessment such as feeling proud and enjoyable (Chen & Brown, 2018). Chen and Brown (2018) indi- cated that the emphasis on political and moral education in China shaped the
idea that loving the people and the nation requires doing well  (p. 105). Simi- larly, university students in Hong Kong recognise the purpose of assessment as being important for determining self-worth, and that it demands self-regulation (Wang & Brown,2014).
Literature has consistently portrayed an image of a highly competitive assessment culture in China. As a result, Chinese students have formed strong perceptions of the purposes of assessments through schooling. However, the e ect of their conceptions of assessments on their e ort expen- diture in the context of test-taking remains unknown. Taken together with the expectancy-value theory of test-taking motivation, the question then becomes: Will students  attitudes about assessments in uence their perceived importance and anxiety of the upcoming test? Subsequently, given the het- erogeneity of test-taking motivation under di erent test-taking stakes, will
the impact of attitudes on test-taking motivation be consistent across
di erent test-taking stakes? Finally, considering the emphasis on moral edu- cation in China, will students perceive the importance and anxiety of tests without personal consequences similarly to the personally relevant ones? Or will they di erentiate the perceived importance and anxiety of the tests between high- and low-stakes contexts, but nonetheless exert optimal test- taking e ort?
The current study
The current study speci ed a partially mediated model linking students  con- ceptions of tests and their perceived test-taking motivation (Figure 1). Speci  - cally, we proposed six hypotheses (H) pertaining to the relationships among students  conceptions of assessments (SCoA), students  perceived Importance and Anxietyof the test, and their test-taking E ort:
(1) H1: Adaptive conceptions of assessment would be positively associated with the perceived importance of the tests (Zilberberg et al., 2014).
(2) H2: Adaptive conceptions of assessment would be positively associated with Anxiety(M. Gao,2010).
(3) H3: Adaptive conceptions of assessment would also be positively associated with e ort expenditure (Wise & Cotten, 2009).
(4) H4: E ort expenditure would be positively related to the perceived impor- tance of the test (Knekta & Ekl f, 2015; Zilberberg et al., 2014).
(5) H5: E ort expenditure would be positively related to the perceived anxiety of the test (Ekl f & Nyroos, 2013; Pekrun et al., 2002).

Method
Participants
Online questionnaires were distributed through the WeChat social network, while paper-based forms were distributed at schools which permitted their stu- dents to be approached. The target population was students over the age of 16 studying in Shanghai secondary schools. It is worth noting that the sample in the current study did not match the PISA cohort for ethical considerations (i.e., parental consents required for participants under 16). Nonetheless, these students would have experienced the high-stakes Zhongkao giving them fam- iliarity with the di erent stakes involved.
In the data-screening stage, questionnaires that appear to be inherently invalid were deleted (7 for identical response to each item, 64 for completing under 150 seconds). Within the remaining 1,010 responses, cases with more than 10% missing values on construct items were removed (n =7 deletions, .07%). Missing value analysis was conducted using expectation maximisation (Dempster et al., 1977) separately for the SCoA (n =14) and TTM (n =2) con- structs. Little s Missing Completely at Random (MCAR) test for SCoA (  2 (318) = 341, p =.18) and TTM ( 2 (24) =28, p =.26) indicated that the missing pattern was random. The nal sample contained 1,003 cases (Table 1), with equivalent sample sizes >300 in each test-consequences condition.
Only institutions with at least 10 respondents were identi ed to avoid identi cation of participants. Of the participants, 90% came from 12 schools, with one third from two vocational high schools and 57% from 10 high schools, three of which were a liated with a university. These numbers roughly correspond to the population statistics of the proportion of students
in high school and vocational secondary education. The sample is thus almost 2:1 senior secondary to vocational students. Most high school students were
in Year 10, while a larger proportion of vocational students were in their nal
year. Ages ranged from 16 to 18, with almost half being in the youngest group. The sample was slightly skewed towards girls (55%) versus boys (44%). Nearly 80% of all returned surveys were administered online as opposed to paper.
Instruments
Students  conceptions of assessment inventory
The Students  Conceptions of Assessmentinventory (SCoA-VI; Brown,2008) was adapted by changing the word assessment  to test  whenever it appeared. The inventory consists of 33 items related to four meta-categories (i.e., improve- ment, irrelevance, a ect, external attribution) and eight speci c categories. Speci cally, aspects of self-improvement and teacher-improvement under the Improvement category re ects self-regulation. Likewise, External Factoralso con- sists of two sub-categories, namely, student future and school quality. It re ects maladaptive external attribution beliefs about assessment. The A ect category also has two rst-order factors that are related to the aspects that assessments
are enjoyable and bene cial to class environment. Lastly, the Irrelevance cat- egory evaluates the degree to which students think they ignore assessments
and that assessments are not fair. The inventory has been successfully implemented in multi-cultural contexts such as Hong Kong, mainland China, Brazil, New Zealand, and Portugal (Brown, 2013; Flores et al., 2019; Matos & Brown, 2015). Students rate their responses to items using a 6-point, positively packed scale (Lam & Klockars,1982). Participants can choose from two negative options (i.e., strongly disagree and mostly disagree) or four positive options (i.e., slightly agree, moderately agree, mostly agree, and strongly agree). This response scale has been shown to be e ective when participants are inclined to endorse
the substance of statements (Deneen et al., 2013). Previous work on the SCoA inventory has identi ed ve di erent model structures as listed below:
. Model 1: Full hierarchical Model #1 of four second-order factors predicting seven rst-order factors plus three items that pertain to the aspect that assessments are irrelevant (Brown, Peterson, & Irving, 2009).
. Model 2: Simpli ed hierarchical Model #2, of four second-order factors pre- dicting eight rst-order factors (Brown, Irving, et al., 2009).
. Model 3: inter-correlational Model #1 of eight inter-correlated factors (Matos
& Brown,2015).
. Model 4: inter-correlational Model #2 of four major inter-correlated factors (Weekers et al., 2009).
. Model 5: bi-factor Model #1 consisting of four orthogonal major factors pre- dicting their respective items (as per Model #4) and one general factor pre- dicting all items (Matos et al., 2019; Weekers et al., 2009).

Test-taking motivation scale
The test-taking motivation scale was adapted from Knekta and Ekl f (2015) and the Student Opinion Scale (Thelk et al., 2009). The original ve factors and 19 items had to be changed because in this study students were not taking an actual test; rather, they were asked to respond to one of three hypothetical test-taking scenarios. Speci cally, Expectancy and Interest are only relevant to situations where students have just nished a real-life test (e.g., I did well on this test  from Expectancy, or It was fun to do this test  from Interest). Therefore, these two constructs were not included in the study. Moreover, several items from the other three constructs were excluded for not tting one or more of the conditions (e.g., I could have tried harder on this test  from E ort, and I was so nervous when I took this test that I forgot things I usually know  from Anxiety). These changesleftthreeitems fortheanxiety scale(i.e., Before takingthis test, I worried about how di cult it would be ; I was scared of failing on this test ; I
felt relaxed while taking this test ). While it might be argued that these items
re ect student self-e cacy or con dence in test-taking, we follow the original
scale meaning of Knekta and Ekl f (2015) that these re ect student anxiety when faced with a test.
Thelk et al. (2009) demonstrated that test importance consisted of both knowing the test results (e.g., I would like to know how well I did on this test ) and that the test itself is important (e.g., It was important to me to get
a good result on this test ). The Knekta and Ekl f (2015) importance scale did not have any items for knowing the test results. Thus, three items from the Student Opinion Scale were included in the study so that the number of retained items per construct would be at least three. An additional item ( I would like to know where I went wrong in the test ) was inserted to the Impor- tance construct, because having feedback regarding tests results has been found to have a positive impact on students  learning (Brown et al., 2016) and the common practice in China of students keeping a notebook consisting
of errors they made in tests (cuo ti ji; Zhou, 2017).
The instrument consists of a 6-point Likert-type scale with three negative and three positive options (i.e., 1= strongly disagree, 2=moderately disagree, 3= slightly disagree, 4=slightly agree, 5=moderately agree, and 6= strongly agree).
Chinese adaptation
The SCoA inventory and the TTM scale were translated into Chinese by the rst author using a functional equivalence approach (Jin & Nida, 2006). Four bi- lingual informants (two in the education eld, one in engineering, one in com- merce) were asked to rate their con dence in the accuracy (on a scale of 1 to 6)
and note their concerns about the translation (Tran et al., 2017). The rst author then revised the translations and repeated the above procedure until all raters gave sixes on all the items.
Procedures
All students completed a self-reported inventory concerning their understand-
ing of the purposes and nature of assessment (i.e., SCoA). Then, within the fra- mework of experimental study using vignettes (Hughes & Huby, 2004; Skilling & Stylianides, 2020), students were randomly assigned to one of three di erent test-taking consequence conditions. Vignettes are e ective at eliciting partici- pants responses when the vignette situation is di cult to create (Skilling & Sty- lianides, 2020), such as the administration of an o cial ILSA. Speci cally,
students were asked to imagine themselves having just nished a test that would in uence their own academic results (i.e., personal stakes), their country s international ranking(i.e., countryat stakes),orhaving no in uenceatall (i.e., no stakes). Then participants were asked to complete a test-taking motivation questionnaire where their perceived importance, anxiety, and e ort expendi- ture were elicited. Given the importance of simplicity and familiarity of vignette instructions (Bradbury-Jones et al., 2014), the exact wording for each vignette was adapted from Liu et al. (2015) as follows:
. Personal stakes. Imagine you have just taken a test that is going to in uence your nal grades, report card, or results for this year s course of study.
. Country at stakes. Imagine you have just taken a test that will in uence your country s ranking in an international assessment. The result will not in uence your personal academic result but will be utilised to compare China with other countries. You yourself will not be given the result from the test, nor will you be identi ed.

Data analysis
Data analyses were conducted in SPSS for missing value analysis and R package lavaan (Rosseel, 2012) for factor analyses. In the current study, both the SCoA and TTM constructs were adopted from previous research; hence, it is appropri- ate to examine if the pre-existing models t well with the new samples using con rmatory factor analysis (CFA). Maximum likelihood estimation method was used as the response options for SCoA and TTM both ranged from 1 to 6 and could be considered as continuous responses (Finney & DiStefano, 2013). The absolute values of univariate skewness and kurtosis ranged from 0.07 to 1.14 and 0.01 to 1.04, respectively. Further, robust standard error (MLR) was appliedbecausethedatawerenotmultivariatenormal(Satorra&Bentler, 2001).
Amulti-criteriaformodelacceptancewasspeci edinlinewithcurrentpractice (G.W.Cheung&Rensvold,2002;Fan&Sivo,2007;Hu&Bentler,1999;Marshetal., 2004). Speci cally, model t indices such as 2, 2/df, comparative t index (CFI), root-mean-square error of approximation (RMSEA), the 90% con dence interval of RMSEA, standardised root-mean-square residual (SRMR), and gamma hat wereexaminedtoevaluatemodelquality.A model isconsideredtohavedemon- stratedagood ttothedatawhen 2/df<3.00,SRMR<.06,gammahatandCFIare
> .95, and RMSEA and the 90% con dence interval of RMSEA < .06. Further, a model is considered acceptable when the p value associated with the ratio of 2
and degree of freedom is not statistically signi cant (i.e., 3.00< 2/df < 3.80), CFI and gamma hat .90, RMSEA .08, with 90% CI .08, and SRMR .08.
As CFI tends to punish complex models with more than three factors (G. W. Cheung & Rensvold,2002) and RMSEA falsely rewards complex models with misspeci cations (Fan & Sivo,2007), emphasis was given to more stable
 t indices such as SRMR and gamma hat. Additionally, Akaike s information cri- terion (AIC) was used to determine the relative advantages of competitive models, with a di erence of AIC larger than 10 indicating that the model with smaller AIC is more favourable (Burnham & Anderson, 2004). When a model did not meet conventional thresholds for acceptance, local t inspection of modi cation indices and correlation residuals (Kline, 2016) was carried out in order to develop an admissible model. Modi cation indices identi ed items
that violated simple structure by being highly attracted to other factors, whereas correlation residual signals whether the model has a good represen- tation of the relationship between pairs of variables (i.e., an absolute value < .01 indicates good representation). The initial model was modi ed according
to local t measurements to improve the correspondence to the sample, but
may not generalise to other samples, and its applicability to the population remains to be determined; further testing with independent samples is still needed (MacCallum et al., 1992).
Measurement models
Initially, the SCoA scale was tested against ve existing models using con rma- tory factor analysis to identify the model that best t the data. The best tting model was selectedby evaluation of the various tindices (e.g., AIC, chi-square).
The test-taking motivation model proposed in Knekta and Ekl f (2015) is a structural model with e ort being the dependent latent variable. In the current study, a three factor inter-correlated model (i.e., Importance, Test Anxiety, and E ort) was examined using con rmatory factor analysis prior to linking them to the SCoA construct.
Final structural model
To address hypotheses H1 to H6, after establishing the equivalence of measure- ment models of SCoA and TTM across the three vignette groups, the constructs (i.e., SCoA and TTM) were linked together according to the hypothesised model (Figure 1). Speci cally, E ort is predicted by SCoA directly and indirectly as mediated by perceived Importance and Test Anxiety.
Invariance testing
To establish whether measurement and structural models were statistically equivalent across the three vignette conditions, invariance was examined using a nested model approach (Brown et al., 2017; Byrne,2013). First, con  - gural, metric, and scalar invariance were examined for the two measurement models. Second, given that both measurement models demonstrated scalar invariance, the two constructs were evaluated for equivalence in a structural model. Third, in two separate tests, (a) the structural relationships among the latent factors and (b) the latent factor covariances (H8) were examined for invar- iance. Di erences in CFI ( CFI) <.01 indicate that the introduction of constraints has not made model t worse than a less constrained model (G. W. Cheung & Rensvold, 2002; Vandenberg & Lance, 2000).
Latent mean analysis
To address H9 and H10, latent mean analyses were performed to determine whether students  endorsement of the instruments di ered across three con- ditions after controlling for level of invariance (G. W. Cheung & Rensvold, 2002; Vandenberg & Lance, 2000). To compare latent mean di erences across three groups, the latent mean of the no stakes group was set as zero (i.e., the reference), allowing the means of the other two conditions to be freely esti- mated.Then,takethe countryatstakes groupasreference,allowingthe personal stakes group latent mean to be freely estimated. The estimated means of the non-reference groups are relative mean di erences to the reference group (Bentler,2005; Hong et al., 2003; Teo & Kam,2014). E ect sizes of the di erences were calculated according to guidelines given by Hancock (2001). A critical ratio (CR) > 1.96 indicates a statistically signi cant latent mean di erence (p <.05). As the prompts for the manipulation of test-taking instructions were not intro- duced until after participants had already nished the SCoA, we expect that any di erences on the endorsement of SCoA should be due to chance —and therefore that any meaningful di erences re ect underlying di erences between the experimental conditions.

Discussion
Addressing the concern that students in high-performing societies might have merely tried harder than students elsewhere, this experimental between-sub- jects study established that the level of consequence had an impact on self- reported test-taking motivation. In general, motivational factors (i.e., E ort, Importance, and Anxiety) were all greater as consequences shifted from none, to country at stakes, and were highest in individual consequences. However,
di erences between country and individual at stake were statistically signi  - cant, albeit trivial, and only for Anxiety. Otherwise, students in these two di erent conditions were indistinguishable.
The latent mean score analyses reveal that in this sample of Shanghai stu- dents, distinctions between country and personal stakes are not made, unlike previous studies carried out in the Nordic region. This result reinforces Li s

The result is consistent with Gneezy et al. (2019) in that Shanghai students seemed to be motivated to make an e ort on a test regardless of experimental conditions. Participants in the study were aware that the country at stakes consequence was not personally relevant to them as they did not report elevated anxiety or importance compared to those in the no stakes group. Yet, they claimed to exert as much e ort as if it mattered personally. Together, these lead us to conclude that there may be no such thing
as a low-consequence test in Shanghai insofar as test-taking motivation is concerned.
Consistent with the theoretical model, Shanghai students conceptions of the purposes of tests had a statistically signi cant but small impact on test-taking motivation. The stronger the general conception of test as improving learning, being related to external attributions, class climate, and personal emotions, the more students thought tests were important, stimulated greater anxiety, and exerted more e ort. It is noteworthy that the SCoA in uence was through a
general factor rather than the subordinate independent factors as seen in pre- vious studies with New Zealand high school students (Brown & Hirschfeld, 2008; Brown, Peterson, & Irving,2009; Brown & Walton,2017). This may arise because of the extensive usage of summative testing in Shanghai rather than the more formative uses of standardised tests in New Zealand. Nonetheless, this result is consistent with Wise and Cotten (2009), who showed that unique factors in stu- dents  conceptions of assessment predicted test e ort on a low-stakes test.
Regarding the relationship between Anxietyand E ort, the path values all suggest, regardless of condition, that greater anxiety resulted in greater
e ort. This is consistent with research that positions the negative emotion anxiety as an activating force for greater e ort and performance (Pekrun
et al., 2002). This suggests that all students in this study, and potentially all Chinese students, see testing as anxiety-prone, but such negative emotions
are not handicapping but rather contribute to more e ort.
The generalisability of this result is somewhat limited because the sample was marginally older than the PISA cohort, but nearly half were only 1 year older than the PISA target age. Nevertheless, the age e ect may be signi cant, as other studieshavefoundthatolderagehighschoolstudentswithexperienceofquali ca-
tion assessments do have di erent conceptions of assessment (Hirschfeld & Brown, 2009). Future studies, provided they can obtain informed parental consent, should replicate this study with age-appropriate students to better match PISA cohorts.

However, it is possible that students might over- or underreport their TTM for various reasons (i.e., self-protection mechanism, items too di cult, fatigue, etc.; Myers & Finney,2019, 2021; Penk & Richter,2017). The results reinforce the importance of reporting test- taking motivation on any international comparative test because of the non-negligible e ect of motivation on e ort and logically performance. This study expands our understanding of Chinese students  test-taking behaviours
in assessment situations such as PISA or TIMSS. Potentially due to high societal expectations on performance, Shanghai students are ready to expend good e ort regardless of the task consequence. It suggests a possible causal factor for the high results seen in Shanghai and suggests caution when evaluating the rank-order results reported by PISA. Even though the results cannot be linked directly to assessments such as PISA itself, the study helps
us to understand how students from di erent contexts behave when test con- sequences are at di erent levels.
