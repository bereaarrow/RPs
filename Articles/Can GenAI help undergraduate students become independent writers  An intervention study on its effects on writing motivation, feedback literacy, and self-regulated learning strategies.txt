Can GenAI help undergraduate students become independent writers? An intervention study on its effects on writing motivation, feedback literacy, and self-regulated learning strategies
Qi Lua  , Yuan Yaob  , Longhai Xiaoc, Xinhua Zhud  and Hongbiao Yina 
aDepartment of Curriculum and Instruction, Faculty of Education, The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong, SAR China; bSchool of Foreign Languages, Central South University, Changsha, China; cCollege of  Education,  Zhejiang University, Hangzhou,  Zhejiang, China; dDepartment  of Language  Science  and Technology, The Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Kowloon, Hong Kong, SAR China

ABSTRACT
This  mixed-methods  study  adopts  a  longitudinal  perspective  to investigate  whether  and  how  GenAI-supported  feedback  practice influences  students   writing  motivation,  feedback  literacy,  and self-regulated  learning  (SRL)  strategy  use,  three  aspects  essential for  developing  as  independent  writers.  Participants  were  61 Chinese undergraduates enrolled in an academic writing program. Quantitative and qualitative findings revealed a differentiated pat - tern of change across the three domains: writing interest increased, while  self-efficacy  showed  only  slight  gains;  feedback  literacy improved  in  appreciating  feedback,  managing  affect,  and  taking action,  but  showed  limited  improvement  in  acknowledging  feed - back  sources  and  making  judgments;  and  SRL  strategy  use improved  in  text-generating  and  revising,  with  modest  gains  in planning  but  a  slight  decline  in  self-monitoring.  Together,  these findings provide nuanced insights into how GenAI-mediated feed - back  settings  can  both  support  and  constrain  students   develop - ment as independent writers, highlighting the need for pedagogical scaffolding and ethical guidance.

Introduction
Feedback  is  increasingly  recognised  as  a  critical  component  in  writing  instruction. With the growing integration of generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) into feedback practices, learners may become more actively engaged in writing activities (e.g. Yan 
& Zhang, 2024; Yeung, 2025).  At  the  same  time,  concerns  have  emerged  regarding potential overreliance on GenAI-generated feedback and its implications for learners  responsibility in managing their writing processes (e.g. Chen et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2026).

In light of these considerations, it is essential to examine whether GenAI-supported feedback  practice  could  genuinely  support  students   broader  development  as  inde - pendent writers. Independent writers are generally understood as those who assume increasing responsibility and exercise autonomous control over their writing processes and  development  (e.g.  Hyland  &  Hyland,  2006;  Ratminingsih  et al., 2018). This  per- spective shifts attention from the immediate pedagogical utility of GenAI to its poten - tial formative role in fostering sustained learner independence in writing.
Writer independence, in this sense, concerns learners  autonomy and responsibility 
in  directing  their  own  writing  development.  These  qualities  are  enacted  through several  complementary,  process-oriented  capacities,  particularly  motivation  to  write (e.g.  Hidi  &  Boscolo, 2006),  the  ability  to  effectively  utilise  feedback  (e.g.  Hyland  & Hyland, 2006), and the capacity to self-regulate the writing process (e.g. Zimmerman 
& Risemberg, 1997).
These  capacities  take  on  renewed  significance  when  GenAI  becomes  embedded 
in writing instruction. In technology-rich environments, writer independence may be understood  as  learners   sustained  capacity  to  exercise  autonomy  and  responsibility while engaging with powerful technological assistance (e.g. Dang,  2025). When certain cognitive and evaluative functions (e.g. identifying problems and assessing text quality) are partially externalised to GenAI, sustaining writer independence can be particularly demanding for second language (L2) learners. Compared with L1 writers, L2 learners face additional linguistic and cognitive demands (e.g. limited linguistic resources for expressing nuanced meanings; Lu, Zhu, et al.,  2024), which may increase their reliance on technological support.
Under these conditions, sustaining writer independence may require more deliberate 
and sustained effort in L2 contexts. More specifically, it may require learners to remain sufficiently  motivated  to  take  ownership  of  their  work  (e.g.  Zhang  et al.,  2025),  to critically engage with feedback rather than accept it unreflectively (e.g. Zhan & Yan, 2025), and to actively regulate writing and revision processes instead of delegating control  to  technology  (e.g.  Mohebbi,  2025).  Writer  independence,  therefore,  is  not reducible to any single attribute. Rather, it reflects the coordinated development of these complementary, process-oriented capacities. It is therefore conceptually distinct from  writing  quality,  which  represents  a  product-oriented  outcome  rather  than  the underlying processes that underpin writer independence.
Derived from these core characteristics, writing motivation, feedback literacy, and self-regulated learning (SRL) strategy use serve as central aspects for understanding how learners, especially L2 learners, may develop as independent writers in the con - text of GenAI-supported feedback practice.
Although  current  research  has  begun  to  explore  the  effects  of  GenAI  on  these constructs,  existing  studies  remain  limited  in  scope  and  fragmented  in  focus.  Most examine individual variables in isolation or treat them as undifferentiated constructs without  attending  to  their  specific  dimensions  (e.g.  Apriani  et al.,  2024;  Liu  et al., 2024; Rad et al., 2024). Few studies have considered these constructs together within 
a shared conceptual framing of independent writer development in GenAI-supported feedback settings.
Guided by the lens of writer independence, the present study investigates whether 
and how students  writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use change 
during a ten-week GenAI-supported feedback intervention. It further explores students  perceptions of this feedback experience through a mixed-methods approach, capturing both  observable  developmental  changes  and  lived  experiences.  By  situating  these changes within an authentic instructional context, the study aims to deepen under - standing of the formative role of GenAI-supported feedback practice and offer ped - agogical insights for supporting independent writer development.
Literature review
GenAI-supported feedback practice and writing motivation
Writing motivation refers to learners  dispositions, beliefs, and goals shaped through their experiences with writing across tasks and contexts (Boscolo & Gelati,  2019). It is a central driver of writing development (e.g. Camacho et al.,  2021; Troia et al., 2012) and is commonly conceptualised as a multidimensional construct that includes, among other components, self-efficacy and writing interest (Graham,  2018).
Self-efficacy reflects writers  judgments of their capability to perform writing tasks and their confidence in executing writing activities (e.g. Sun & Wang,  2020), whereas writing interest refers to the extent to which writers find the writing process engaging and worth investing effort in, e.g. Graham (2018). Together, these dimensions capture both  competence  ( can  I  do  it? )  and  willingness  ( do  I  want  to  do  it? ),  which  are central to learners  development as independent writers.
Previous  studies  have  reported  positive  associations  between  various  forms  of feedback (e.g. teacher, peer, and automated writing evaluation [AWE]) and L2 students  writing self-efficacy and interest (e.g. Fan & Xu, 2020; Ruegg, 2018; Sari & Han, 2024). However, research on GenAI-supported feedback practice remains limited. Preliminary evidence  suggests  that  interacting  with  such  tools  may  enhance  students   writing self-efficacy (e.g. Apriani et al., 2024; Huang & Mizumoto, 2024).
At  the  same  time,  some  scholars  caution  that  the  motivational  effects  of GenAI-supported  feedback  practice  may  be  multifaceted,  potentially  exerting  both facilitating  and  inhibiting  influences  (e.g.  Rodr guez-Ruiz  et al.,  2025). This  suggests that GenAI-supported feedback practice may affect different motivational dimensions 
in distinct ways, highlighting the need for more fine-grained investigation into how 
it shapes motivational processes in writing.
Self-Determination Theory  (SDT;  Ryan  &  Deci, 2000, 2020)  provides  a  useful  lens for  understanding  how  motivational  processes  may  operate  in  the  context  of GenAI-supported  feedback  practices.  SDT  posits  that  autonomy  (a  sense  of  volition and  self-direction),  competence  (a  sense  of  mastery),  and  relatedness  (a  sense  of connectedness  with  others)  are  three  essential  psychological  needs  that  underpin intrinsic motivation. This framework helps illuminate how the satisfaction or frustration of  these  needs  may  shape  learners   motivational  beliefs,  including  self-efficacy  and interest.
From  an  SDT  perspective,  perceived  competence,  a  core  psychological  need,  is theoretically associated with learners  self-efficacy beliefs, as experiences of mastery and  improvement  are  often  linked  to  stronger  beliefs  in  one s  capacity  to  succeed (e.g.  Li  et al., 2022).  GenAI-supported  feedback  practice  may  shape  such competence-related  experiences  in  contrasting  ways.  On  the  one  hand,  by  offering immediate  and  supportive  information  that  helps  students  refine  their  writing  (e.g. Barrot, 2023), it may enhance learners  perceived competence, which may correspond with  stronger  self-efficacy  beliefs.  On  the  other  hand,  excessive  reliance  on GenAI-generated suggestions may limit opportunities for independent mastery expe - riences  (e.g.  Rodr guez-Ruiz  et al., 2025),  potentially  corresponding  with  weaker self-efficacy beliefs.
Writing  interest  may  be  understood  in  relation  to  autonomy  need  satisfaction within SDT. Interest reflects learners  willingness to engage in a task for its own sake and is often associated with experiences of personal choice and self-direction in the learning process (e.g. Graham,  2018; Hidi et al., 2023). In GenAI-supported feedback contexts, students may experience greater autonomy when they are able to decide whether and how to incorporate feedback into their revisions, thereby perceiving a sense  of  control  and  ownership  over  their  writing  (e.g.  Zhang  et al.,  2025).  Such autonomy-supportive experiences may be associated with heightened intrinsic interest 
in writing.
In  addition  to  autonomy,  satisfaction  of  the  need  for  relatedness  may  also  be relevant to understanding learners  writing interest within SDT. Prior studies suggest that GenAI tools can provide socio-emotional feedback, such as praise, encouragement, or  empathetic  responses,  that  helps  students  feel  acknowledged  and  supported throughout the writing process (e.g. Lu et al., 2024). These experiences of perceived relatedness may contribute to more positive emotional engagement with writing tasks and sustained interest over time.
Taken together, from an SDT perspective, GenAI-supported feedback practice may shape learners  motivational beliefs, including self-efficacy and writing interest, through experiences related to the satisfaction or frustration of autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs. However, the ways in which these processes unfold may vary across motivational dimensions, highlighting the need for further inquiry.
GenAI-supported feedback practice and feedback literacy
Feedback literacy is widely regarded as a crucial competence that enables students 
to  benefit  from  feedback  practices,  encompassing  the  knowledge,  skills,  and  dispo - sitions required to interpret and use feedback effectively (e.g. Carless & Boud,  2018). In  writing  contexts,  Yu  et al.  (2022)  conceptualised  feedback  literacy  as  students  capacities to appreciate, evaluate, and utilise writing feedback while managing their emotions during the process. Their framework includes five dimensions: appreciating feedback (valuing its importance), acknowledging different feedback sources (recog - nising  diverse  providers),  making  judgments  (evaluating  feedback  quality  and  rele - vance), managing affect (regulating emotional responses), and taking action (applying feedback for improvement).
The  present  study  adopts Yu  et al. s  (2022)  conceptualisation  and  corresponding L2 scale for three reasons. First, the scale has been empirically validated and aligns with  prior  work  emphasising  the  multidimensional  nature  of  feedback  literacy  (e.g. Carless & Boud, 2018). Second, it was developed and validated in Chinese university academic writing contexts, closely matching the instructional setting and participant population of this study. Third, the five dimensions capture learners  process-oriented capacities to appreciate, evaluate, manage, and act on feedback, capacities that remain necessary even when feedback is generated by GenAI. Recent studies in GenAI-supported writing  contexts  (e.g.  Liu  et al.,  2026)  have  also  adopted  this  scale,  supporting  its applicability in technology-enhanced feedback environments.
Existing studies suggest that different forms of feedback, including teacher, peer, and  self-feedback,  may  influence  the  development  of  students   feedback  literacy  in different  ways  (e.g.  Hoo  et al.,  2022;  Weng  et al., 2024).  For  example,  Weng  et al. (2024)  found  that  peer  feedback  improved  students   ability  to  recognise  and  judge feedback but had limited effects on understanding feedback sources, emotion regu - lation, and feedback utilisation.
However, few studies have examined the potential impact of GenAI-supported feed - back practice on L2 students  feedback literacy. Rad et al. (2024) provide some indirect evidence:  in  an  11-week  intervention  with  46  university  students,  those  using  the  AI tool Wordtune showed greater gains in overall feedback literacy than a control group. Yet this study did not examine specific literacy dimensions, and the tool differs from GenAI systems that provide real-time, adaptive dialogue rather than fixed feedback.
From a socio-constructivist perspective, learning develops through interaction and dialogue, where learners negotiate meaning and construct understanding collabora - tively (e.g. Hickey, 1997; Rannikm e et al., 2020). In writing feedback contexts, oppor - tunities  to  discuss,  interpret,  and  respond  to  feedback  are  therefore  central  to developing feedback literacy (e.g. De Kleijn, 2023). With its real-time, adaptive feedback capabilities (e.g. Barrot, 2023), GenAI may provide such dialogic opportunities, poten - tially shaping how learners understand, evaluate, and use feedback, as well as how they  regulate  their  emotional  responses.  Further  research  is  therefore  needed  to examine  whether  and  how  GenAI-supported  feedback  practice  influences  different dimensions of students  feedback literacy in writing.
GenAI-supported feedback practice and SRL strategy use
Self-regulated learning (SRL) refers to the processes through which learners set goals, monitor progress, and adjust strategies to achieve desired outcomes (e.g. Panadero, 2017;  Schunk  &  Zimmerman, 2011).  In  writing,  SRL  is  reflected  in  strategies  used across the stages of writing — prewriting, during writing, and postwriting — including planning (e.g. setting goals and organising ideas), generating text (drafting content), self-monitoring  (checking  progress  and  detecting  problems),  and  revising  (refining text) (e.g. Bai et al., 2022). These strategies reflect writers  ability to manage cognitive and metacognitive processes throughout writing.
Previous  research  shows  that  feedback  is  an  important  external  factor  shaping how L2 students deploy SRL strategies (e.g. Tian et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024). For example, Tian  et al.  (2022)  found  that  students  invested  greater  effort  in  revising strategies when responding to automated feedback than to peer or teacher feed - back, possibly because automated feedback often focuses on surface-level features and therefore requires additional interpretation and integration. Such findings sug - gest  that  students  adapt  their  SRL  strategies  in  response  to  the  characteristics  of the feedback they receive.
Given that GenAI-generated feedback is real-time, adaptive, and content-rich (e.g. Lu et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2025), students may further adjust their SRL strategies when engaging with this form of feedback. Although direct evidence remains limited, Liu et al. (2024) reported improvements in primary school L2 students  use of planning, text  generation,  self-monitoring,  and  revising  strategies  following  GenAI-mediated writing  instruction  compared  with  conventional  instruction.  While  GenAI  feedback was  only  one  component  of  the  intervention,  these  findings  suggest  that  GenAI s feedback affordances may shape how students regulate their writing processes.
However, whether and how GenAI-supported feedback practice influences SRL strategy use in L2 writing remains underexplored, warranting more fine-grained and theoretically informed  investigation.  Because  SRL  involves  learners   active  management  of  cognitive and metacognitive processes (e.g. Panadero,  2017), GenAI may play a dual role. On the one  hand,  it  can  provide  timely,  adaptive  cues  across  writing  stages  (e.g.  Rong  et al., 2025), potentially supporting key SRL sub-strategies. For instance, during planning it may help clarify task demands and goals; during text generation it may prompt idea elabo - ration or improve coherence; during self-monitoring it may cue learners to check goal—text alignment;  and  during  revision  it  may  facilitate  both  surface  editing  and  higher-level restructuring (e.g. Chen et al.,  2024; Li et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024).
On  the  other  hand,  excessive  reliance  on  GenAI  guidance  may  reduce  learners  initiative  for  independent  regulation  throughout  the  writing  process.  Learners  may defer goal-setting and decision-making to GenAI suggestions, incorporate generated revisions without critically evaluating relevance, or engage less in internal monitoring, patterns consistent with what Fan et al. (2025, p. 492) termed  metacognitive laziness , 
a tendency to depend on external rather than internal regulation. Such reliance may constrain the effective deployment of SRL strategies across planning, text generation, self-monitoring, and revision over time.
The present study
The review of existing research highlights several observations. Although initial inves - tigations have addressed the impact of GenAI-supported feedback practice on writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use to some extent, research on this topic remains limited and fragmented. Existing studies often examine only one vari - able, and when they do, they may overlook its specific dimensions (e.g. Apriani et al., 2024; Rad et al., 2024). Consequently, there is a lack of comprehensive research that simultaneously examines writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use from a unified perspective.  One promising lens is that of fostering independent writers (e.g. Hyland & Hyland, 2006; Ratminingsih et al., 2018).
Writer independence, as noted in the Introduction, concerns learners  capacity to assume autonomy and responsibility in directing their own writing development. Such autonomy  and  responsibility  are  not  merely  abstract  dispositions  but  are  enacted through concrete processes in writing practice (e.g. Hidi & Boscolo,  2006; Hyland & Hyland, 2006; Zimmerman & Risemberg, 1997).
First, to write independently, learners typically need a motivational foundation to engage with writing tasks and sustain effort over time. Writing motivation, reflected 
in constructs such as writing self-efficacy and writing interest, captures learners  will - ingness and confidence to participate in and persist with writing activities.
Second,  independent  writing  involves  critical  processing  of  external  input  rather than unreflective acceptance. In feedback-rich environments, especially those mediated by  GenAI,  learners  need  to  interpret,  evaluate,  and  decide  when  and  how  to  use feedback. Feedback literacy reflects learners  capacity to interpret, evaluate, and use feedback constructively.
Third,  autonomy  and  responsibility  must  be  enacted  through  regulatory  action during writing. Self-regulated learning (SRL) strategy use reflects the planning, mon - itoring,  and  revising  processes  through  which  learners  manage  their  writing  and coordinate feedback use as one source of information for improvement.
In this sense, writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy capture distinct yet necessary functional aspects of independent writer development. They describe how writer independence is enacted through writing processes in practice.
To  address  these  gaps,  the  present  study  examines  whether  and  how  students  writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use develop during a ten-week GenAI-supported  feedback  intervention  in  an  academic  English  writing  program. Guided  by  the  lens  of  writer  independence,  it  aims  to  advance  understanding  of independent writer development in GenAI-supported contexts and inform the design 
of GenAI-integrated pedagogies.
Accordingly, the study addresses the following research questions:
RQ1:  Whether  and  how  students   writing  motivation,  feedback  literacy,  and  SRL 
strategy use change over the course of the ten-week GenAI-supported feedback practice?
RQ2:  If  changes  are  observed,  how  can  these  be  interpreted  through  students  
experiences and perceptions of the GenAI-supported feedback practice?
Methods
Participants and context
A total of 61 Chinese undergraduate students (9 males and 52 females; mean age = 20.33, SD = 0.54) participated in the study. All were enrolled in education-related majors (e.g. curriculum and instruction, comparative education, higher education) at a research university  in  southern  China.  Participants  took  part  in  a  10-week  academic  English writing program designed to develop genre awareness, academic writing conventions, and self-regulated writing strategies. The program consisted of weekly three-hour ses - sions  combining  instruction,  guided  writing  activities,  and  feedback  practice. Writing tasks were designed to consolidate skills practiced in the sessions, with GenAI-supported feedback practice serving as a core instructional component that enabled students to interact with GenAI under instructor guidance. The program was delivered by an instruc - tor with over ten years of experience in academic writing instruction.
Procedures
The  study  spanned  the  entire  10-week  academic  writing  program.  During  the  first two weeks, participants were introduced to the program schedule, task requirements, and evaluation criteria, and completed a pre-test survey assessing baseline levels of 
writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use. They then received pre - paratory instruction on SRL strategies and training on the GenAI tool Kimi (Moonshot AI,  China, October  2023;  https://kimi.moonshot.cn/),  including  guidance  on  formu - lating  effective  prompts  to  obtain  actionable  feedback.  This  preparation  aimed  to support  students   use  of  GenAI  during  subsequent  feedback-and-revision  activities embedded  in  the  writing  tasks  (see  Appendix  A  for  detailed  instructional procedures).
Throughout the ten-week program, participants completed three focal writing tasks (Weeks  3,  6,  and  9).  Each  task  involved  an  in-class  drafting  stage  followed  by  a GenAI-supported  feedback-and-revision  session  using  Kimi.  Detailed  procedures  for the drafting, feedback, and revision stages are provided in  Appendix A.
In  Week  10,  the  program  concluded  with  a  summary  session  reflecting  on  the content  covered  and  participants   progress. The  post-test  questionnaire,  identical  to the pre-test, was administered online during class time. Additionally, semi-structured interviews  were  conducted  with  nine  participants  to  gather  insights  into  their  per - ceptions  of  the  feedback  process  and  writing  experiences.  Ethical  approval  was obtained from the University s Institutional Ethics Review Board prior to the study.
Instruments
Three writing tasks
Throughout the program, participants completed three integrated writing (IW) tasks. This  genre  was  selected  because  it  requires  students  to  extract  information  from source  materials,  evaluate  evidence,  formulate  ideas,  and  construct  coherent  argu - ments. The topics were  Online Learning vs. Traditional Classroom Learning ,  The Impact of GenAI on Undergraduate Learning , and  Choosing a University Major: Interest vs. Employability , all designed to relate to students  experiences and current educational issues. For each task, participants received four reading materials (approximately 500 words in total) presenting diverse perspectives from academic articles, news reports, and  essays.  Based  on  these  materials,  they  wrote  a  200-word  argumentative  essay within one hour.
Task performance was assessed using four key criteria: contextual awareness, cita - tion and synthesis, opinion and argument, and organisation and expression (Cheong 
et al., 2019). The rubric has been employed in prior empirical research (e.g. Lu, Zhu, et al., 2024) and demonstrated acceptable validity. Each criterion was rated on a scale from 1 to 10, yielding a total possible score of 40.
Questionnaire
A questionnaire was administered to assess participants  writing motivation, feedback literacy, and use of SRL strategies via the online platform Wenjuanxing (https://www. wjx.cn/). The questionnaire was presented in Chinese and completed within 10—15 min- utes. The writing motivation and SRL strategy use scales were adapted from Bai et al. (2022),  and  the  feedback  literacy  scale  from Yu  et al.  (2022).  Each  scale  comprised multiple dimensions with several items per dimension. Writing motivation and feed - back  literacy  were  measured  on  a  five-point  Likert  scale  (1 = strongly  disagree, 
5 = strongly  agree),  whereas  SRL  strategy  use  was  measured  on  a  five-point  scale (1 = never,  5 = always).  Detailed  information  on  item  numbers,  example  items,  and reliability coefficients is provided in  Appendix B.
Data collection and analysis
Data were collected at two time points, at the beginning and end of the ten-week intervention, allowing observation of changes across the instructional period. Additional considerations regarding the research design, including the rationale for not including 
a control group, are provided in  Appendix C.
For  RQ1,  paired-sample  t-tests  were  conducted  to  examine  pre—post  changes  in writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen s  d. Skewness (—0.313 to 1.365) and kurtosis (—1.471 to 0.542) fell within –3, indicating acceptable normality.
For RQ2, semi-structured interviews were carried out to explore students  percep - tions and experiences regarding GenAI-supported feedback practice during the three writing tasks. Students were selected based on their initial draft performance in Task 
1, assessed using the four scoring criteria described in the  Three writing tasks  section 
(M = 21.70, SD =  4.05). Task  1  was  chosen  because  it  represented  students   baseline writing  ability  prior  to  sustained  exposure  to  GenAI-supported  feedback  practice, allowing  for  a  more  objective  grouping  that  minimised  the  potential  confounding influence of later learning effects.
Students were rank-ordered according to their total Task 1 scores and divided into three approximately equal-sized proficiency groups (tercile split). This resulted in 22 students in the high group (M = 26.18), 19 in the medium group (M = 21.16), and 20 
in the low group (M = 17.30). Three students from each group were randomly selected 
for interviews, yielding nine interviewees (S1—S9). These proficiency groupings were used solely for purposive sampling in the qualitative interviews and were not included 
in the quantitative analyses.
Details of the interview data collection and analysis are provided in Appendices D and E.
Results
Differences in primary variables between pre- and post-test
To  examine  RQ1,  a  paired  sample  t-test  was  conducted  to  assess  changes  in  the primary variables from the pre- to post-test. For writing motivation (Figure 1), writing interest increased significantly from pre-test to post-test, M_pre = 3.25 (SD = 0.39), M_post  =  3.45  (SD  =  0.36), t(60)  =  4.332, p < 0.001, d = 0.555.  Writing  self-efficacy showed  a  small,  non-significant  increase,  M_pre  =  3.47  (SD  =  0.39),  M_post  =  3.57 (SD = .49), t(60) = 1.630, p = 0.108, d = 0.209.
For feedback literacy (Figure 2), significant gains were found in appreciating feed - back, t(60) = 3.781, p < .001, d = 0.484, and managing affect,  t(60) = 2.125, p = 0.038, d = 0.272,  as  well  as  taking  action,  t(60)  =  2.321, p = 0.024, d = 0.297. No  significant changes were observed for acknowledging sources,  t(60) = 1.189, p = 0.239, d = 0.152, or making judgments,  t(60) = 1.325, p = 0.190, d = 0.170.

Students  perceptions of GenAI-supported feedback practice
To  explore  RQ2,  semi-structured  interviews  were  conducted  to  examine  students  perceptions of GenAI-supported feedback practice, focusing on their understanding, preference, and acceptance.

Regarding understanding, seven out of the nine students reported that they found 
the feedback provided by Kimi to be easily comprehensible, as it avoided technical jargon and was straightforward. Some students (e.g. S1 and S4 from the high- and medium-proficiency groups) also noted that the feedback was organised in a way 
that  facilitated  easy  comprehension.  However,  two  students  (S6  and  S9)  from  the medium-  and  low-proficiency  groups  mentioned  that  understanding  the  feedback required  a  certain  cognitive  effort.  As  S9  explained,   The feedback Kimi gives me is often lengthy. To fully understand it, I need to first spend time comprehending it, and then  identify  which  feedback  points  are  relevant  and  usable,  which  takes  additional time . This suggests that, while the GenAI-generated feedback is understandable, its length  and  the  need  to  filter  relevant  information  can  pose  challenges  for  some students.
Preference
In  terms  of  preference,  students  assessed  the  feedback  from  both  functional  and emotional  perspectives.  Functionally,  all  nine  students  expressed  positive  attitudes regarding Kimi s abilities, highlighting its support for file uploads, rapid feedback, and time-saving features. For instance, S5 (medium proficiency group) commented,  Once 
I upload the writing assessment criteria to Kimi, it immediately provides detailed feedback on my writing, saving me time figuring out the criteria. Also, it feels like having a safety net. No matter how I start my writing, Kimi can always help me  rescue  it .
On  the  emotional  side,  students  from  both  high-  and  low-proficiency  groups expressed different emotional preferences towards Kimi. Specifically, low-proficiency students felt that Kimi provided emotional support. For example, S8 (low-proficiency 
group) shared,   I find it quite amazing. Although I know I m interacting with a machine, 
I feel like it truly understands and listens to what I m saying, which makes me feel respected. So  even  though  it  gives  me  a  lot  of  negative  feedback,  I  can  accept  it  without  feeling uncomfortable . In contrast, high-proficiency students valued Kimi s objectivity in pro - viding  feedback.  For  instance,  S2  remarked,   I think Kimi s feedback is well-organised, first pointing out the strengths and then addressing areas for improvement. This structure creates  a  positive  learning  experience,  and  its  comments  on  weaknesses  are  objective, which makes it feel less like criticism of me as a person .
However, not all students expressed strong preference for Kimi. Some, like S6 from 
the medium-proficiency group, highlighted a more complex feeling. As S6 stated,  On 
one hand, I admit that Kimi is really helpful for revising my writing, but on the other hand, after three writing tasks, I m starting to feel a subtle frustration. I increasingly realise that my  writing  skills  are  lacking.  I  just  wish  I  could  write  an  essay  where  Kimi  doesn t  find many obvious mistakes .
Acceptance
Regarding acceptance, students primarily evaluated GenAI-generated feedback from 
two perspectives: its usefulness and usability. As for the usefulness, students identified 
three main reasons for considering the feedback beneficial. First, it was seen as helpful 
in improving the quality of their texts. Specifically, feedback on language and expres - sion was often regarded as valuable, with Kimi s corrections being both precise and thorough.  However,  feedback  related  to  the  content  of  the  text  (e.g.  organising arguments  and  adding  supporting  evidence)  was  met  with  mixed  reactions.  For example,  S7  from  the  low-proficiency  group  commented,  Sometimes  Kimi  suggests adding more evidence, but where am I supposed to get that data from? 
Second, students found that the feedback was thought-provoking and encouraged reflection. This occurred in two ways: when feedback was general, it prompted stu - dents to think about how to make it more actionable and specific; and when students disagreed with the suggested revisions, it encouraged them to reconsider and explore 
other  potential  improvements.  As  S2  (high-proficiency  group)  stated,  I don t always accept Kimi s revision suggestions because some of them aren t very practical. However, I think about the underlying revision direction and see if there s room for further improve- ment in my writing .
Third, students appreciated the ability to revisit feedback at any time. For instance, 
S1 (high-proficiency group) explained,   When revising for the next writing tasks, I would often look back at the conversation history with Kimi from the previous task to review any  issues  I  might  have  overlooked.  I  also  try  to  consciously  apply  what  I ve  learned from interacting with Kimi, such as argument structures and transitional phrases, in the next task .
However, some students raised concerns about the usefulness of the feedback. S5 (medium-proficiency group) noted,   I ve noticed that Kimi s feedback in all three writing tasks has been quite similar, always suggesting that I add more evidence. In the first two tasks, I reconsidered its suggestions, but for the current topic, which I am more familiar with, I feel I ve done a good job. Yet, Kimi is still suggesting the same thing, making me question whether its feedback is simply a fixed pattern rather than tailored to my writing .
Concerning the usability, students evaluated the ease of converting feedback into revisions. All nine students agreed that the feedback on language, especially spelling 
and grammar, could be directly applied without much effort. However, when it came 
to  content  and  logic,  students  usually  needed  to  further  refine  the  suggestions. Different proficiency levels led to different approaches: high- and medium-proficiency students tended to (1) reflect independently or (2) engage in further discussions with 
Kimi  on  specific  issues.  In  contrast,  low-proficiency  students  tended  to  seek  direct answers from Kimi on particular issues.
Nonetheless, some students pointed out that Kimi excelled in guiding the direction 
of discussions. For instance, S4 (medium-proficiency group) shared,  After a conversation, Kimi sometimes suggests related topics, which makes you want to click on them. I ve done this, and before I knew it, I was no longer discussing my original text. Of course, I acknowl- edge  that  small,  off-topic  conversations  can  help  relax  the  mind,  but  I  still  need  to  be conscious and purposeful in my communication with Kimi. Otherwise, I might just be led down a different path .
Discussion
Building  on  the  lens  of  writer  independence,  the  present  findings  suggest  that GenAI-supported feedback practice may reshape independence, understood as learners  autonomy and responsibility in directing their writing development, in a dual way.  On the one hand, by providing readily accessible, information-rich, and iterative feedback support, GenAI may strengthen learners  capacities across motivational, feedback literacy, and  regulatory  domains  (e.g.  heightened  writing  interest,  enhanced  ability  to  act  on feedback,  and  greater  use  of  text-generating  and  revising  strategies).  On  the  other hand,  when  evaluative  and  monitoring  functions  are  partially  externalised  to  GenAI, learners may rely on the tool to identify problems and judge quality, which may reduce opportunities  to  practice  independent  evaluation  and  metacognitive  regulation  (e.g. limited gains in making judgments and a slight decline in self-monitoring).
Importantly,  these  patterns  imply  that  writer  independence  in  GenAI-mediated contexts is not simply  more independence  or  less independence . Rather, the auton - omy and responsibility central to writer independence become reconfigured: learners may  become  more  willing  and  more  able  to  act  on  feedback  and  regulate  their writing,  while  becoming  less  autonomously  evaluative  and  self-monitoring  if  tool reliance  replaces  internal  judgement. The  following  sections  therefore  discuss  each construct  not  as  separate  outcomes,  but  as  complementary  process-level  indicators that together reveal how writer independence may be strengthened in some dimen - sions while weakened in others.
The potential influence of GenAI-supported feedback practice on writing motivation
The paired-sample  t-test (Figure 1) showed a significant increase in writing interest, while  writing  self-efficacy  improved  slightly  but  not  significantly,  suggesting  that GenAI-supported  feedback  practice  may  boost  writing  enthusiasm  but  has  limited impact on confidence.
Several factors may account for the observed increase in writing interest. Drawing on  SDT,  one  possible  explanation  lies  in  the  enhancement  of  students   perceived autonomy,  which  arises  when  learners  experience  a  sense  of  volition  and  personal choice  in  their  learning  (e.g.  Graham,  2018;  Hidi  et al., 2023).  In  GenAI-supported feedback practices, autonomy is fostered when students independently decide how 
to engage with GenAI tools, such as whether to accept or disregard GenAI-generated suggestions, rather than following them passively.
At  the  same  time,  it  is  possible  that  writing  was  perceived  as  more  manageable when supported by GenAI, which may have reduced initial cognitive strain. If tasks are  experienced  as  less  effortful,  learners  may  report  greater  enjoyment.  However, interview data suggest that students  interest was not solely rooted in task simplifi - cation, but also in their active role in evaluating and integrating feedback. For instance, S2 (high-proficiency group) described assessing the usefulness and feasibility of GenAI feedback  before  deciding  whether  to  apply  it. This  pattern  aligns  with  SDT s  claim that  autonomy-supportive  contexts  foster  intrinsic  motivation,  including  interest  in writing, rather than merely generating enjoyment through reduced task difficulty.
In  addition,  the  increase  in  writing  interest  may  be  associated  with  enhanced feelings of relatedness as proposed by SDT. Some students (e.g. S8, low-proficiency group)  reported  feeling  understood   and  respected   by  the  feedback  provided  by GenAI, which strengthened their emotional connection with the writing task and, in turn, cultivated greater interest in writing.
By  contrast,  the  non-significant  change  in  writing  self-efficacy  may  diverge  from previous findings (e.g. Apriani et al., 2024; Huang & Mizumoto, 2024), which reported notable improvements following interaction with GenAI tools. A possible explanation may lie in differences in research design. For instance, in Huang and Mizumoto (2024) study, students collaboratively wrote group essays, meaning that individual exposure 
to  GenAI  was  relatively  limited.  In  the  present  study,  however,  students  completed three full individual writing tasks with GenAI in the feedback practices, resulting in more sustained and direct interaction with the tool.
From the perspective of SDT, this result may relate to students  perceived compe - tence,  which  can  fluctuate  over  time  with  extended  GenAI  use.  Although  a  slight improvement  was  observed,  some  students  (e.g.  S6,  medium-proficiency  group) expressed  mild  frustration  after  repeatedly  interacting  with  GenAI,  as  the  feedback occasionally  made  them  more  aware  of  their  linguistic  limitations. This  heightened awareness,  while  potentially  beneficial  for  reflection,  may  also  introduce  a  sense  of inadequacy.  Such  feelings  can  be  understood  through  observation  and  modelling processes (Bandura,  2021), where learners unconsciously compare their own perfor - mance  to  that  of  a  model.  When  the  perceived  gap  between  the  learner  and  the model  becomes  too  wide,  such  comparisons  may  dampen  confidence  rather  than enhance it.
Taken together, these findings suggest that prolonged exposure to GenAI-supported feedback  practices  may  not  yield  uniformly  positive  motivational  effects.  As Rodr guez-Ruiz et al. (2025) cautioned, the motivational consequences of GenAI use are likely multifaceted. From the standpoint of writer independence, the motivational pattern suggests that GenAI may strengthen learners  willingness to take up writing, which  is  an  essential  foundation  for  autonomy  in  sustained  writing  development. However,  the  modest  change  in  self-efficacy  and  occasional  frustration  reported  by some  students  indicate  that  autonomy-supportive  experiences  may  coexist  with moments of self-doubt when learners confront perceived gaps between their current writing  and  the  level  of  expression  reflected  in  GenAI  feedback.  This  may  suggest that fostering independence involves not only sustaining interest but also supporting learners in interpreting GenAI feedback in ways that reinforce realistic and adaptive competence beliefs, rather than triggering maladaptive upward comparisons.
The potential influence of GenAI-supported feedback practice on feedback literacy
Figure 2 reveals a nuanced pattern of change in students  feedback literacy, indicating both substantial progress and areas of limited development. This finding partly aligns with  Rad  et al.  (2024),  who  reported  generally  positive  effects  of  AI  integration  on feedback  literacy.  However,  the  present  study  reveals  a  more  differentiated  pattern: students improved significantly in appreciating feedback, managing affect, and taking action, whereas progress in acknowledging feedback sources and making judgments was comparatively limited.
Appreciating feedback pertains to students  recognition of the value of feedback and  their  active  role  in  the  feedback  process.  Interview  data  suggest  that  direct engagement with GenAI fostered greater appreciation for its immediacy and interac - tivity.  Moreover,  as  students  such  as  S1  (high-proficiency  group)  noted,  GenAI  not only illuminated aspects of their writing that they had previously overlooked but also allowed  them  to  revisit  feedback  dialogues  at  any  time.  This  affordance  may  have helped  students  reconceptualize  feedback  as  an  evolving  learning  resource  rather than a one-off corrective measure, indicating a possible shift from a performance-oriented 
to a learning-oriented perspective on feedback, a crucial step towards more sophis - ticated feedback literacy (Molloy et al.,  2020).
Managing affect involves students  regulation of emotions when receiving feed - back. Beyond feeling  respected , as reported by S8 (low-proficiency group), several students  noted  that  GenAI s  neutral  and  consistent  tone  reduced  the  anxiety  and defensiveness often triggered by criticism. From an affective standpoint, such emo - tionally neutral interactions may help create a psychologically safer feedback space, enabling  students  to  process  critical  comments  with  less  ego  threat  and  greater openness to improvement (e.g. McGuire,  2025). This suggests that GenAI, by buffering negative  emotional  responses,  may  promote  more  adaptive  affective  regulation, providing a foundation for sustained engagement in feedback cycles(e.g. Carless & Boud, 2018).
Taking  action  involves  how  students  respond  to  feedback.  Compared  with  the 
 making  judgments ,  taking  action  appears  to  benefit  more  directly  from  repeated, concrete opportunities for revision, as each writing cycle requires tangible responses 
to  feedback.  In  GenAI-supported  feedback  practices,  the  quantity  and  breadth  of information provided by the tool (e.g. Lu et al., 2024; Yao et al.,  2025) likely expand the range of potential revision options available to students. Even if not all sugges - tions  are  directly  implemented,  this  increased  diversity  of  feedback  may  facilitate greater  engagement  in  active  revision,  thereby  supporting  the  development  of  the taking  action  dimension.  Interview  data  further  indicate  that  students  (e.g.  S2,  S7, from high- and low-proficiency groups) tend to engage in deliberate decision-making rather than passive acceptance, suggesting that they become more agentic in trans - lating feedback into concrete revision actions.
Acknowledging different feedback sources concerns students  ability to recognise the learning benefits of various sources of feedback. The lack of significant improve - ment in this area may be attributed to the study design, which exclusively employed 
GenAI as feedback provider. Without exposure to multiple feedback sources for com - parison, students may have limited opportunities to develop their ability to differen - tiate and assess feedback from diverse perspectives.
Making  judgments  refers  to  students   capacity  to  evaluate  their  own  work  and critically assess the quality of feedback received. Unlike the taking action dimension, which showed substantial improvement, gains in judgement-making were more lim - ited, potentially due to two interrelated challenges: cognitive overload from lengthy feedback and students  reliance on GenAI tools.
From  the  perspective  of  cognitive  load  theory,  excessive  information  processing demands can hinder higher-order cognitive tasks such as evaluation and judgement (Sweller, 2011). In GenAI-supported feedback practices, the sheer quantity and length of  feedback  may  have  imposed  substantial  cognitive  demands.  For  instance,  S4 (medium-proficiency group) reported that processing lengthy feedback required con - siderable mental effort, leaving fewer resources available for evaluating its relevance and applicability. While a larger volume of feedback can expand the range of potential revision  options  (supporting  taking  action),  it  does  not  automatically  improve judgement-making; students still need to select, prioritise, and critically appraise the information. In other words, the abundance of feedback may enhance opportunities for action but simultaneously increase the difficulty of making independent evaluative judgments.
Students   reliance  on  GenAI  may  further  constrain  judgement-making.  Some,  like S5  (medium-proficiency  group),  indicated  that  using  GenAI  reduced  the  need  to internalise evaluation criteria, thereby limiting opportunities to practice autonomous judgement.  Moreover,  because  GenAI  often  guides  feedback  in  specific  directions, students may have been inclined to follow suggestions passively rather than critically engage  with  them. This  combination  of  cognitive  load  and  tool  dependence  likely explains why judgement-making improved only modestly, in contrast to taking action, which primarily benefits from the availability of diverse feedback options and tangible opportunities to revise.
Taken  together,  the  differentiated  pattern  across  feedback  literacy  dimensions highlights a key tension in independence under GenAI-supported feedback practice. The gains in appreciating feedback, managing affect, and taking action suggest that GenAI can strengthen learners  capacity to participate in feedback cycles and translate input into revision, thereby supporting responsibility for improvement. At the same time, limited growth in making judgments indicates that abundant and tool-directed feedback  does  not  automatically  cultivate  independent  evaluative  capacity.  In GenAI-mediated environments, independence therefore hinges not only on acting on feedback responsibly, but also on sustaining the autonomy to judge feedback quality and  relevance.  In  this  sense,  being  an  independent  writer   in  the  age  of  AI  entails retaining evaluative authority over one s writing decisions, rather than merely executing externally  generated  suggestions.  Such  evaluative  authority  may  require  targeted scaffolding beyond repeated revision opportunities.
The potential influence of GenAI-supported feedback practice on SRL  strategy use
Figure 3 shows that the GenAI-supported feedback practice had mixed effects on SRL strategy use: text-generating and revising strategies improved significantly, planning showed a modest increase, while self-monitoring declined slightly. According to SRL theory,  learning  involves  forethought  (goal  setting),  performance  (strategy  use  and monitoring), and self-reflection (evaluation) (Schunk & Zimmerman,  2011).
The limited improvement in planning may reflect two factors. First, GenAI was not integrated  into  the  pre-writing  stage.  Second,  some  students  relied  on  GenAI  as  a safety net and became less proactive in organising their ideas. As S5 (medium-proficiency) noted, GenAI support reduced their concern about initial writing quality, suggesting lowered involvement in early planning.
Text-generating strategies, part of the performance phase, improved likely because repeated  exposure  to  GenAI  feedback  across  similar  IW  tasks  enabled  knowledge transfer  (e.g.  Nokes,  2009).  GenAI  served  as  an  additional  source  of  idea  recall;  for example,  S1  (high-proficiency)  reported  reusing  argument  patterns  and  transitions extracted from earlier feedback provided by GenAI. Revising strategies also improved, which is expected since GenAI was directly used during revision activities in this study.
Self-monitoring,  which  encompasses  evaluating  one s  writing  and  adjusting  pro - cesses  accordingly,  spans  both  the  performance  and  self-reflection  phases.  The observed  decline  in  self-monitoring  strategies  is  unexpected,  suggesting  that  pro - longed  exposure  to  GenAI  may  have  had  a  counterproductive  effect  on  students  autonomous regulation. Two potential explanations are proposed. First, the perceived need  for  self-monitoring  may  have  diminished:  as  students  grew  accustomed  to GenAI-generated  corrections,  they  may  have  shifted  from  a  proactive  to  a  reactive revision approach, waiting for GenAI suggestions rather than independently identifying areas for improvement. This reflects a transition from internal self-regulation to external regulation (e.g. Fan et al.,  2025).
Second, metacognitive engagement may have been reduced. The convenience of GenAI,  particularly  its  ability  to  provide  immediate  and  information-rich  feedback, may have limited students  opportunities for deliberate error detection and indepen - dent  evaluation,  both  of  which  are  critical  for  developing  self-monitoring  skills. Importantly, the abundance or complexity of feedback does not necessarily make it easier to act upon; students still need to interpret, prioritise, and decide how to apply 
it. Moreover, when students receive lengthy or information-dense feedback, cognitive resources may be preferentially allocated to implementing revisions rather than crit - ically monitoring their performance or evaluating feedback quality. This redistribution of attention may highlight a potential trade-off between execution-oriented engage - ment (taking action) and reflective, evaluative regulation in GenAI-mediated contexts. Notably,  this  pattern  corresponds  with  the  limited  improvement  observed  in  the making judgments dimension of feedback literacy, suggesting that the cognitive and metacognitive  demands  of  evaluating  feedback  were  not  fully  met  when  students relied heavily on GenAI.
Taken together, the SRL strategy pattern suggests that GenAI-supported feedback practice may differentially shape regulatory processes. While execution-oriented reg - ulation,  such  as  text-generating  and  revising  strategies,  was  strengthened  through repeated  interaction  with  GenAI,  reflective  regulation,  particularly  self-monitoring, showed  slight  decline  when  monitoring  functions  were  increasingly  externalised  to the  tool. This  pattern  does  not  necessarily  indicate  a  loss  of  independence  per  se, but  rather  a  redistribution  of  regulatory  effort:  learners  may  invest  more  cognitive resources in implementing revisions while allocating less to internally detecting prob - lems or evaluating their own performance.
From  the  perspective  of  writer  independence,  this  redistribution  invites  a  recon - ceptualization  of  what  independence  entails  in  GenAI-mediated  writing  contexts. When  GenAI  assumes  part  of  the  monitoring  and  judging  functions  traditionally associated  with  self-regulation,  independence  can  no  longer  be  equated  with  per - forming  all  regulatory  processes  unaided.  Rather,  independence  may  lie  in  learners  capacity  to  retain  autonomous  regulatory  authority  over  their  writing  processes, including deliberate oversight of when and how external inputs are integrated into ongoing planning, drafting, and revision. In this sense, independent writers are not those who avoid technological assistance, but those who sustain metacognitive control over  tool-mediated  regulation  rather  than  allowing  regulatory  processes  to  be  fully externalised.
Conclusions
This study advances understanding of how GenAI-supported feedback practice shapes the development of independent L2 writers in academic writing contexts by clarifying 
a  central  tension. While  GenAI  may  help  learners  feel  and  act  more  independently by  strengthening  their  willingness  to  engage  in  writing  and  supporting  feedback uptake  and  revision-related  regulation,  it  may  simultaneously  reduce  opportunities 
to think and evaluate independently if monitoring and judging functions are increas - ingly  externalised  to  the  tool.  In  this  sense,  independence  in  GenAI-mediated  L2 writing  contexts,  understood  as  learners   autonomy  and  responsibility  in  directing their  writing  development,  is  not  defined  by  the  absence  of  technological  support, but  by  their  capacity  to  retain  ultimate  evaluative  and  regulatory  authority  while engaging  with  such  support.  Independence  is  therefore  reconfigured  rather  than diminished  in  the  age  of  AI:  it  resides  not  in  rejecting  assistance,  but  in  governing its use in ways that sustain autonomy and responsibility.
The  findings  provide  several  implications  for  integrating  GenAI  into  L2  writing instruction. To support writing self-efficacy, instructors should clarify both the strengths and limitations of GenAI tools and guide students in using them appropriately rather than  treating  GenAI  outputs  as  normative  standards.  For  example,  teachers  might explicitly  model  how  GenAI  suggestions  are  generated  (e.g.  by  prompting  the  tool with varying levels of detail) and discuss why certain outputs appear fluent yet may lack contextual appropriateness or critical depth. Such guided analysis can help stu - dents  understand  that  GenAI  outputs  are  generated  responses  shaped  by  prompts and  training  data,  rather  than  ideal  benchmarks  against  which  their  own  writing should be judged.
For feedback literacy, particularly in recognising multiple feedback sources, teachers may combine GenAI-generated feedback with teacher and peer feedback to provide diverse evaluative perspectives and reinforce their complementary roles (e.g. Lu et al., 2024). For example, students may categorise feedback from different sources (GenAI, peers,  teacher)  and  reflect  on  the  distinctive  strengths  of  each  source  to  support more strategic feedback use.
To strengthen judgement-making, instructors may design tasks that require students 
to compare GenAI outputs with evaluation criteria and their own texts. For instance, students could evaluate a GenAI-generated paragraph using a rubric before revising their  drafts  and  then  reflect  on  similarities  and  discrepancies.  Such  activities  may cultivate  critical  evaluation  skills  and  reduce  unreflective  acceptance  of  GenAI suggestions.
Regarding SRL strategy use, explicit instruction in goal-setting and text organisation can  strengthen  planning  during  the  forethought  phase.  For  example,  teachers  may guide  students  to  set  specific  revision  goals  before  consulting  GenAI  (e.g.  focusing on argument clarity or paragraph coherence) rather than requesting broad feedback.
To  enhance  self-monitoring,  both  awareness  and  structured  practice  are  needed. Teachers might incorporate short self-assessment checklists before submitting drafts 
to  GenAI  (e.g. Yan, 2020),  embed  reflective  prompts  during  GenAI  interactions  (e.g.  Why  is  this  suggestion  helpful? ),  and  require  students  to  explain  why  they  accept or  reject  GenAI  suggestions  (e.g.  Teng,  2025).  Such  practices  help  sustain  internal regulation while benefiting from external support.
Finally, differentiated support may be necessary, as lower-proficiency students may experience  cognitive  overload  when  confronted  with  extensive  GenAI  feedback. Teachers can scaffold feedback uptake by helping students prioritise suggestions (e.g. addressing  global  issues  before  local  language  corrections)  or  modelling  how  to identify the most actionable feedback points.
Some  limitations  should  be  noted.  First,  the  study  involved  education-major  stu - dents  from  a  single  Chinese  university,  and  the  sample  was  predominantly  female. These characteristics may limit the generalisability of the findings to other disciplinary, institutional, or demographic contexts. Future research should include more diverse disciplines,  institutions,  and  more  balanced  demographic  compositions  to  examine the robustness of the observed patterns.
Second,  as  all  participants  received  the  same  instructional  program,  no  control group was established. While this design aligns with the study s developmental focus on  examining  changes  in  students   writing  motivation,  feedback  literacy,  and  SRL strategy use under GenAI-supported feedback practice, it limits the ability to attribute these changes solely to GenAI use. Future research could incorporate control groups or comparative designs to better isolate the contribution of GenAI from other instruc - tional factors.
Third, although the two-wave design allowed observation of pre—post changes, it may still overlook nonlinear developmental patterns (e.g. fluctuations in self-monitoring). In  addition,  although  writing  drafts  and  GenAI  chat  logs  were  used  for  qualitative triangulation, the study did not conduct systematic revision-tracking analyses linking specific feedback to corresponding textual changes, nor include finer-grained process indicators  (e.g.  detailed  revision  histories  or  time-stamped  interaction  data). Higher-frequency  longitudinal  designs  and  more  fine-grained  process-tracing approaches (e.g. revision mapping, uptake coding, or keystroke tracking) would better capture long-term developmental trajectories and the dynamics of feedback uptake 
in GenAI-supported writing contexts.
Fourth,  while  the  present  study  focused  on  learners   capacities  to  process  and enact feedback once provided, it did not examine their ability to actively elicit feed - back in GenAI-mediated environments. In such contexts, students  ability to formulate, refine, and strategically adjust prompts may shape both the feedback generated and how it is subsequently interpreted and used. Future research could therefore extend current  conceptualizations  of  feedback  literacy  by  incorporating  prompting-related capacities alongside feedback processing dimensions to better capture the evolving 
demands of technology-enhanced writing practices. Acknowledgements

Appendix A
Preparatory instruction and procedures for GenAI-supported feedback practice
The SRL strategy instruction consisted of three parts. The first part involved explaining SRL strat - egies and their connection to writing evaluation criteria, emphasising the students  awareness of the importance of these strategies in improving their  writing. The  second  part  involved  group discussions, where participants, in groups of four to five, shared their understanding of SRL strat - egies and discussed any previous experiences with using them. In the third part, groups shared their discussions with the larger class.
Regarding  the  Kimi  training,  the  goal  was  to  help  participants  understand  how  to  craft       effective prompts to obtain valuable feedback. Kimi is a large language model that provides       draft-level  feedback  through  a  Chinese-language  interface,  supporting  multimodal  inputs 
such  as  text,  images,  and  documents.  Students  can  submit  full  drafts  and  receive       context-sensitive suggestions to guide revision. Compared with other widely available GenAI      tools (e.g., ChatGPT), Kimi was selected for its stable access within mainland China, compati - bility with local user preferences, and seamless integration into document-based writing tasks.
The  training  was  structured  in  three  phases.  In  the  first  phase,  participants  learned  prompt creation strategies aligned with guidelines proposed by Atlas (2023), including providing contex - tual  information,  assigning  specific  roles,  detailing  tasks,  and  specifying  expected  outcomes. Concrete  examples  were  provided.  For  instance,  an  initial  prompt  (originally  in  Chinese)  could be translated as:
 You are an experienced English writing instructor. Please refer to the evaluation rubric I have uploaded and provide feedback on my writing, indicating areas you think could be improved. 
A follow-up prompt could be translated as:
 Based  on  the  feedback  you  provided  ( XXXX ),  please  further  clarify  how  it  should  be interpreted. 
These examples illustrated how prompts could be structured to elicit detailed and actionable feedback from Kimi. In addition, students were encouraged to use prompts flexibly, particularly by crafting follow-up prompts to refine and expand upon the initial feedback.
The  second  phase  involved  hands-on  practice  with  Kimi.  In  groups  of  four  to  five,  partici - pants were given a writing sample along with its corresponding evaluation criteria and applied the prompt strategies from the first phase to generate feedback. Special emphasis was placed on using the evaluation criteria effectively, including uploading the criteria to Kimi before start - ing the evaluation to ensure clarity and precision in the feedback received.
The  third  phase  involved  sharing  experiences  and  challenges  encountered  during  the  Kimi evaluation  process.  Importantly,  although  guidance  was  provided  during  the  training  sessions, students retained full autonomy in their actual use of Kimi during the feedback practice. They could freely decide how to phrase prompts, which suggestions to adopt, and how to incorpo - rate feedback into their writing. This approach allowed the study to capture authentic interac - tion patterns and examine how writing motivation, feedback literacy, and SRL strategy use may change in a semi-structured yet open learning environment.
Throughout the ten-week program, participants completed three writing tasks (in Weeks 3, 6, and 9). Initial drafts were composed independently in class without the use of GenAI. Students were  instructed  that  GenAI  was  to  be  used  only  during  the  designated  feedback-and-revision stage. After submitting their drafts, participants spent 40 minutes interacting with Kimi, in either Chinese  or  English,  to  obtain  feedback  and  revise  their  writing.  For  these  three  focal  writing tasks, GenAI served as the primary source of feedback, and no additional formal teacher or peer feedback was provided during the revision stage. Both the in-class drafting and the subsequent GenAI-supported  feedback-and-revision  sessions  were  conducted  in  supervised  classroom  set - tings.  During  the  GenAI-supported  feedback-and-revision  sessions,  the  instructor  circulated  to provide technical assistance related to accessing and operating Kimi, as well as procedural clar - ification.  This  in-class  supervision  helped  ensure  adherence  to  the  designated  use  of  GenAI during the intervention.

Appendix C
Considerations regarding the research design
It  is  important  to  note  that  the  ten-week  academic  English  writing  program  was  designed  to provide  all  participating  students  with  access  to  GenAI-supported  feedback  practices  within  a naturalistic instructional setting. As a result, no formal control group was established. This design aligns with the developmental focus of the present study, which aims to explore whether and how  students   writing  motivation,  feedback  literacy,  and  self-regulated  learning  (SRL)  strategy use change during participation in GenAI-supported feedback practices, rather than to rigorous - ly establish causal effects of GenAI on these variables.
To enhance the interpretability of the observed changes, several measures were implement - ed. First, both quantitative and qualitative data were collected from multiple sources, including pre- and post-questionnaires administered in Weeks 1 and 10, students  writing products, GenAI chat logs, and semi-structured interviews with a subset of nine students. Second, all participants engaged in the same instructional program with consistent writing tasks and structured oppor - tunities to interact with GenAI during feedback activities, thereby minimising variation in instruc - tional exposure. Third, the alignment between writing tasks and feedback sessions allowed for a closer  connection  between  the  feedback  received  and  subsequent  changes  in  students  writing-related variables.
Together, these design features provide a more informed basis for interpreting whether and how  GenAI-supported  feedback  practices  relate  to  observed  changes,  while  also  offering  rich insights into students  experiences and perceptions of these practices.
Appendix D
Interview data collection and analysis
The interviews, conducted in Chinese, followed a structured protocol that prompted participants 
to (1) reflect on their experiences with the three writing tasks and the strategies they employed, (2) discuss their interactions with Kimi during the tasks, (3) share their perceptions of Kimi s role in supporting their writing (including understanding, preferences, and acceptance), and (4) de - scribe their revision processes. The interview questions (e.g.,  How well did you understand the feedback  provided  by  Kimi? )  were  adapted  from  existing  research  (e.g.,  Lu  et al.,  2024;  Yao et al., 2025)  to  encourage  detailed  responses.  Each  interview  lasted  approximately  40 minutes, was audio-recorded with participant consent, and transcribed verbatim for analysis by the first author and a research assistant with a master s degree in education. 
The  data  were  analysed  using  NVivo  12.0. Two  independent  coders  conducted  the  analysis, resolving  any  discrepancies  through  discussion.  Coding  primarily  focused  on  students   percep - tions  of  GenAI-supported  feedback  practice,  specifically  their  understanding,  preferences,  and       acceptance,  in  line  with  Lu  et al.  (2024).  For  example,  statements  such  as  I  liked  the GenAI-supported feedback activities; they felt novel  were coded under preference. Coders also re - mained attentive to any unexpected insights emerging from the interviews. In addition to inter - view data, students  writing drafts and Kimi chat logs were incorporated for data triangulation.
Triangulation was conducted by cross-referencing interview accounts with the corresponding Kimi chat interactions and students  pre- and post-revision drafts. When students described how they used Kimi during feedback uptake and revision, coders examined relevant segments of the chat logs to identify the type of suggestions provided and compared the paired drafts to deter - mine whether and how those suggestions were taken up.
For instance, in the subsection  Students  perceptions of GenAI-supported feedback practice,  
S7  (low-proficiency  group)  remarked,   Sometimes Kimi suggests adding more evidence, but where am I supposed to get that data from?
