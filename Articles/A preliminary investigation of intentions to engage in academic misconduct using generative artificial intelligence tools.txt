The  growing  popularity  of  Arti cial  Intelligence  (AI)  has  led  to  gross  technological advancements  across  numerous  domains,  including  education,  healthcare,  transpor - tation, communication, and  nance (Khalil and Er 2023). However, amidst the numer- ous bene ts, there are robust concerns about the impact of AI on academic integrity, particularly  within  tertiary  education  (Parnther  2022).  Despite  there  being  extensive research aimed at better understanding and explaining academic misconduct, violations of academic integrity remain highly prevalent across student bodies within higher edu- cation settings (M. Birks et al. 2020). Unfortunately, the introduction of Generative AI tools (GAITs), while offering many opportunities to  assist and support the learning process, has simultaneously afforded students a new avenue for engaging in academic misconduct.

GAITs are arti cial intelligence systems that can generate novel content, such as text, images, and other forms of media (Salvagno, Taccone, and Gerli 2023). The emergence of this  technology  has  placed  signi cant  pressure  on  tertiary  institutions  to  develop methods to manage their use in the context of academic misconduct. However, the exist- ing literature predominantly focuses on traditional forms of academic misconduct and overlooks speci c predictors of academic misconduct using GAITs. To develop solutions for management, it is  rst necessary to explore and understand who is most likely to use GAITs to engage in academic misconduct and why. This study aims to address this gap by investigating such behaviors among a sample of undergraduate psychology students through the lens of the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB; Ajzen 1991), a framework that has been rigorously applied to academic misconduct, but not yet in the context of breaches of academic integrity using GAITs. The TPB posits that three factors — attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control — are predictive of the intention to engage in a certain behavior, which in turn forecasts engagement in the behavior itself (Ajzen 1991).  Extending  beyond  the  TPB,  the  role  of  two  other  factors  known  to influence decisions about AI use were also of interest to this study; namely, attitudes towards AI and perceived likelihood of detection of GAIT use in university settings.
Generative AI tools (GAITs)
GAITs are arti cial intelligence systems that can generate new content, such as images, text, audio, music, and videos (Salvagno, Taccone, and Gerli 2023). Popular examples of such tools include ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, DALL-E, and Google Gemini. GAITs offer many bene ts, including but not limited to the ability to facilitate social interactions and enhance student learning (Bozkurt et al. 2023). However, their capacity to generate academic content, such as in the context of traditional assessment paradigms (e.g. written assignments, essays, reports, etc.), has raised concerns about their potential use for aca- demic misconduct, including plagiarism and cheating (Bozkurt et al. 2023; Salvagno, Taccone, and Gerli 2023). While earlier research focused on misuse of GAITs and the limitations  of  AI  detection  technologies  (Dalalah  and  Dalalah  2023;  K bis  and Mossink 2021), recent attention has shifted towards promoting responsible and transpar - ent use, recognizing that detection alone is insufficient for safeguarding academic integ- rity (An, Yu, and James 2025; Ardito 2025; Dalalah and Dalalah 2023; Perkins et al. 2023; Peterson 2025; Weber-Wulff et al. 2023). Within this evolving landscape and apparent increase in the ease of engaging in academic misconduct, it is important that educational institutions shift their focus from a parochial emphasis on AI detection to fostering an understanding of the motivations driving these behaviors. Though empirical work on motivations  for  academic  misconduct  in  the  context  of  GAITs  is  still  emerging, broader research pertaining to the reasons why students engage in traditional academic misconduct is suggestive.
Academic misconduct
Academic misconduct refers to behaviors that violate academic integrity, resulting in unfair academic advantages (Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). Traditional forms of academic misconduct include plagiarism, cheating, fabrication of data, unauthorized collaboration, and contract cheating (Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). There is substantial evidence that academic misconduct remains a prevalent issue among students in tertiary institutions (M. Birks et al. 2020). For example, in Australia, Curtis and Popal (2011) reported plagiar- ism rates as high as 74%, and found that 7.9% of Australian university students utilized con- tract cheating services, while 11.4% resorted to obtaining pre-written work.
Students may engage in academic misconduct for a number of reasons including peer pressure, perceptions of cheating as the norm, confusion about what constitutes academic misconduct, ignorance as to the boundaries of acceptable academic practices, high aca- demic expectations, intense competition, and a fear of failure (Bayaa Martin Saana et al. 2016; Miles, Campbell, and Ruxton 2022). The growing popularity of online flexible modes of study has further enabled academic misconduct due to the increased relative ease with which this can occur in remote learning environments, relative to more tra- ditional study contexts (Chen and Chou  2017; Miles, Campbell, and Ruxton 2022). The rapid emergence of GAITs presents a new avenue for such behavior. In the context of AI, academic misconduct refers to the use of GAITs that substitutes a students  writing or intellectual contribution, misrepresents authorship, or violates assessment rules regard - ing originality and permitted assistance (Shaw 2025). This includes, for example, submit- ting  AI-generated  work  as  one s  own,  using  AI  where  it  is  prohibited,  or  failing  to acknowledge AI assistance where required (D. Birks and Clare 2023; Shaw 2025).
However, the boundaries of acceptable versus improper AI use are often unclear to students as institutional policies are still evolving and course expectations vary widely. Consequently, students may struggle to distinguish permitted and supportive use from behaviors that constitute academic misconduct (Chan  2025). Importantly, not all AI use is considered misconduct, with many institutions now permitting and encouraging responsible engagement (An, Yu, and James 2025). Thus, improper use of AI is best understood as use that violates stated assessment rules or institutional integrity policies (An, Yu, and James 2025; Shaw 2025). Given the increasing pro ciency with which stu- dents can produce human-like text using GAITs, understanding the factors that motivate students to use AI in ways that cross these boundaries is essential.
Academic misconduct has signi cant consequences for students, including assurance of skill development and graduate capabilities, as well as the imposition of severe penal- ties, such as formal notes on students  academic transcripts, failure or expulsion (Abdol- mohammadi and Baker 2007; Parnther 2022). The consequences also extend to higher education providers, undermining their credibility, devaluing assessments and quali ca - tions, reducing trust in the institution, lowering enrollments, diminishing funding, com - promising  accreditation,  and  damaging  institutional  reputation  (Denisova-Schmidt, Prytula, and Rumyantseva 2019; Bertram Gallant, Binkin, and Donohue 2015). Research on how academic misconduct occurs, and how it might be mitigated, must therefore keep pace with advancements in the  eld and emerging risks to academic integrity, including those posed by GAITs.
The theory of planned behavior: an explanatory model for academic misconduct
The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB; Ajzen 1991) is a robust framework that has been extensively applied to explain and predict human behavior, including but not limited to episodes of academic misconduct (e.g. Chudzicka-Czupała et al. 2016; Hendy and Mon- targot 2019; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2009). It posits three key reciprocally linked predictors of behavioral intention — attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control — which indirectly influence behavior via intention. Attitudes refer to an individ- uals  positive or negative evaluations of a behavior, person, or event (Ajzen 1991). Numer- ous  studies  examining  traditional  forms  of  academic  misconduct  consistently demonstrate that favorable attitudes signi cantly increase the likelihood of engaging in such behaviors (Alleyne and Phillips 2011; Harding et al. 2007; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisa- more 2009; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). Furthermore, Hendy and Montargot (2019) identi ed attitudes as the strongest predictor of academic dishonesty. Next, subjec- tive norms refer to an individuals  perception of the beliefs and expectations of the people important to them (Ajzen 1991). Positive subjective norms signi cantly predict intentions to engage in academic misconduct, a  nding that has been replicated across numerous studies (Alleyne and Phillips 2011; Cronan, Mullins, and Douglas 2018; Harding et al. 2007; Hendy and Montargot  2019; Juan et al. 2022; Maloshonok and Shmeleva 2019; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2009). Finally, perceived behavioral control refers to an indi- viduals  belief about how easy or difficult it is to perform a certain behavior (Ajzen 1991). Several studies indicate greater perceived behavioral control predicts stronger intention to engage in academic misconduct (Alleyne and Phillips 2011; Cronan, Mullins, and Douglas 2018; Juan et al. 2022; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2009; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). It appears the more capable or enabled a person feels to breach academic integrity, the greater their intent to engage in such behaviors — at least in the context of more tra- ditional forms of academic misconduct. Studies have demonstrated that these three ident -
i ed predictors account for 21% to 50% of variance in cheating intention, and over 30% for cheating behavior itself (Armitage and Conner 2001; Hendy and Montargot 2019; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010; Yang et al. 2021). Nonetheless, there exists a gap in literature regarding the application of this model to academic misconduct using GAITs.
Expanding the explanatory model: factors relating to AI use
While the TPB offers potential insight into how it is the student in higher education may come to violate academic integrity policy via the unauthorized use of GAITs, research in the area of more traditional forms of academic misconduct has also begun exploring extensions of the model that may yield more explanatory power (e.g. Imran, Hussien, and Alaraimi 2025; Rajah-Kanagasabai and Roberts 2015; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). Accordingly, the relationship between two known determinants of AI use and intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs, over and above contributions from the TPB, were of interest in this study: attitudes towards AI and perceived likeli- hood of detection of GAIT use.
Attitudes towards AI
Within the TPB, attitudes refer to evaluations about performing a speci c behavior, such as engaging in academic misconduct in the context of GAITs. However, a student s actions may be influenced not only by their evaluation of misconduct itself, but also their  perspectives  on  the  technology  enabling  the  behavior.  Research  consistently demonstrates that general attitudes toward a technology can predict acceptance and 
use, independent from attitudes towards any given behavior (Martin  2012). This is con- sistent with the Technology Acceptance Model, which suggests attitudes towards tech- nology  are  a  distinct  antecedent  of  technology  use,  separate  from  perspectives  on speci c behavioral engagement. This distinction supports the consideration of attitudes toward AI as a potential predictor of intention to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs, beyond the TPB model (Martin 2012).
Multiple studies have demonstrated that individuals who possess positive attitudes towards AI tend to report greater acceptance and use of these technologies (Darda, Carre, and Cross  2023; Katsantonis and Katsantonis 2024; Kim 2019; Lambert et al. 2023; Schepman and Rodway 2020). Interestingly, a study conducted by Darda, Carre, and Cross (2023) has further highlighted that the valence of these attitudes may also be important,  nding that positive, but not negative, attitudes towards AI appear to cor - relate with appreciation for AI-generated texts. It is thus plausible that students posses- sing more positive attitudes towards AI may also be more accepting of GAITs, and therefore  increasingly  prone  to  their  use,  including  for  the  purposes  of  committing acts of academic misconduct.
Perceived likelihood of detection
Beliefs about perceived likelihood of detection may play a further role in students  inten- tions  to  engage  in  academic  misconduct  using  GAITs.  Unlike  perceived  behavioral control, which reflects students  beliefs about their own ability to perform a behavior, perceived likelihood of detection refers to an individuals  perception of how likely they are to be caught engaging in a speci c behavior (Megehee and Spake 2008), highlighting a complementary but distinct pathway to misconduct intention. According to economic theory, individuals weigh the bene ts and costs of a behavior before deciding whether to engage in it (Megehee and Spake 2008). There are many potential perceived bene ts of engaging in academic misconduct, including the prospect of better grades, reduced stress, and more time for managing competing commitments (Megehee and Spake 2008; Miles, Campbell, and Ruxton 2022). However, there are also signi cant negative consequences associated with getting caught, including the application of severe academic penalties (Dalalah and Dalalah 2023; Miles, Campbell, and Ruxton 2022). Deterrence theory simi- larly posits that individuals are less likely to engage in misconduct when they believe authorities have both the capacity and willingness to detect and sanction such behavior (Ogilvie and Stewart 2010).
Perceptions of detection may be important in the context of GAITs as students often possess limited understanding of how detectable AI-generated text is and may underesti- mate or overestimate institutional capacity to identify unauthorized use (Ortiz-Bonnin and Blahopoulou 2025). This uncertainty can work to enhance or weaken deterrence and may impact the likelihood of students engaging in improper GAIT use (Choo and Tan 2023). Consistent with this, studies have demonstrated students are likely to avoid acts of academic misconduct when they perceive the likelihood of detection to be high (de Lambert, Ellen, and Taylor 2006; McCabe, Trevino, and Butter eld  2002; Megehee and Spake 2008; Power 2009), presumably due to the high costs seemingly outweighing the bene ts of such behaviors. This supports the inclusion of perceived likelihood of detec- tion in models of AI-enabled academic misconduct, as risk perception could influence GAIT use for the purpose of academic misconduct (Ortiz-Bonnin and Blahopoulou 2025).
Present study
The present study aimed to investigate the relationship between university students  inten- tion to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs and three constructs purported by the TPB to influence behavioral intention; namely attitudes, subjective norms, and per- ceived behavioral control. While the existing evidence has demonstrated that the TPB model provides a useful explanatory framework for understanding student intentions to engage in traditional forms of academic misconduct, no studies to date appear to have explored these associations in the context of breaches of academic integrity facilitated by GAITs. Furthermore, the role of other factors, such as attitudes towards AI and per- ceived likelihood of detection if engaging in academic misconduct, both of which have been linked to more traditional violations of academic integrity, remain unexplored in the context of GAITs. Therefore, a secondary aim of this study was to extend application of Ajzen s (1991) model of the TPB to investigate potential relations between attitudes towards AI, perceived likelihood of detection, and intentions to engage in academic mis- conduct using GAITs (see Figure 1).

As such, participants in the current study were also asked to complete a short measure of social  desirability  to  control  for  potential  bias  (Hart  et  al. 2015;  Staats  and  Hupp 2012). It was hypothesized that attitudes towards academic misconduct, subjective norms about academic misconduct and perceived behavioral control over engaging in academic misconduct would be positively associated with student intentions to engage in academic misconduct facilitated by GAITs (H1). Furthermore, positive, but not negative, attitudes towards AI were expected to be associated with greater intention among students to engage in academic misconduct facilitated by GAITs (H2). Finally, it was anticipated that higher perceived likelihood of detection would be negatively associated with the intention to commit such acts (H3).
Method
Participants
The  nal sample consisted of 186 undergraduate university students (126 females; 57 males; 2 non-binary; 1 preferring not to say; age range 17—48 years, M = 19.98, SD =  4.37). Participants were enrolled in a  rst-year psychology unit and participated in the study in exchange for course credit.
Missing data and excluded participants
A total of 204 students completed the survey. A conservative rejection criterion was used, leading to the exclusion of two student participants due to completion of less than 80% of the online survey (Buchanan and Sco eld  2018). A further 14 students were excluded for failing  an  attention  check  item,  while  two  more  participants  were  excluded  for  not responding  to  this  item.  Of  the  remaining  186  participants,  there  was  very  little missing  data  at  the  individual  item  level  (0.13%).  Little s  Missing  Completely  at Random  (MCAR)  test  was  conducted  using  IBM  SPSS  Statistics  and  revealed  these data were missing completely at random (p = .549). Single imputation through expec- tation-maximisation  was  performed  prior  to  analyses  to  account  for  these  missing values (Schafer and Graham 2002).
Measures
Intention to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs
An 8-item measure adapted from Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore (2010) was administered to assess intention to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs. The original scale assesses  intention  to  engage  in  behaviors  such  as  cheating  and  plagiarism.  For  the present study, item wording was modi ed to explicitly reference the use of GAITs, as no other existing measure was  t for purpose at the time of the current research. No 
changes were made to item structure, response format, or underlying construct. Partici - pants rated their level of likelihood of engaging in a given behavior on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (very unlikely) to 5 (very likely), e.g.  How likely are you to consider turning in work generated by GAITs as one s own? . An overall mean was calculated, with higher scores representing greater intent to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs. The resultant scale demonstrated excellent internal consistency (α = .93).
Attitudes towards academic misconduct
A 13-item self-report measure of attitudes towards academic misconduct was adapted from Maring et al. (2018), a measure originally designed for health professional students. As no other available measure was identi ed and deemed suitable for the present study, items were reworded to reflect academic misconduct within broader university contexts, e.g.  A culture of cheating is prevalent in university . Participants rated their level of agree- ment with each statement on a 5-point Likert scale of 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). An overall mean was calculated, with higher scores representing more accepting attitudes towards academic misconduct. Moderate internal consistency for this scale was found in the present study (α = .75).
Subjective norms
An 8-item subscale from the Perceptions and Attitudes towards Cheating among Engin- eering Students Survey (PACES-2) was administered to assess subjective norms about academic misconduct  (Harding et  al.  2007).  Participants rated  their agreement with each  statement  on  a  5-point  Likert  scale  from  1  (strongly  disagree)  to  5  (strongly agree), e.g.  People whose opinions I value (e.g. my family, friends, colleagues, teachers, etc.) expect me to cheat on an in-class test or exam . An overall mean was calculated, with higher scores representing greater positive subjective norms about academic mis- conduct. High internal consistency was demonstrated in this study (α = .84).
Perceived behavioral control
A 4-item measure was administered to assess perceived behavioral control over engaging in academic misconduct (Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). Participants rated their level of agreement on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree), e.g.  If I wanted to cheat on exams it would be easy . An overall mean was calculated, with higher scores representing greater perceived ease of engaging in aca- demic misconduct. Adequate internal consistency was demonstrated in the present study (α = .68).
Attitudes towards AI
The 20-item General Attitudes towards Arti cial Intelligence Scale (GAAIS) (Schepman and Rodway 2020) was administered to assess attitudes towards AI. The scale has two subscales: positive, reflecting AI bene ts (e.g.  Arti cial Intelligence is exciting ), and nega- tive, reflecting AI disadvantages (e.g.  I  nd Arti cial Intelligence sinister ), as well as one attention check item (e.g.  Please choose Strongly Agree to show that you have read this statement ).  The  attention  check  item  was  modi ed  for  clarity  in  accordance  with author recommendation (A. Schepman, personal communication, March 29, 2023). Par - ticipants rated their level of agreement with each statement on a 5-point Likert scale from 
1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). Mean subscale scores were calculated from items  reflecting  positive  attitudes  and  items  reflecting  negative  attitudes.  An  overall scale  mean  was  not  calculated  due  to  construct  dimensionality.  In  this  study,  the GAAIS demonstrated high internal consistency for the positive (α = .83) and moderate internal consistency for the negative (α = .75) subscales.
Perceived likelihood of detection
At the time of study design, no brief psychometrically validated instruments measur- ing  perceived  likelihood  of  detection  for  GAIT-related  academic  misconduct  were available.  Therefore,  two  items  were  purpose-created,  informed  by  prior  academic misconduct  research.  More  speci cally,  the  authors  drew  on  Megehee  and  Spake (2008),  who  assessed  probable  detection  using  multiple  behavior-speci c  single items  that  were  combined  into  a  composite  score,  and  McCabe,  Trevino,  and  But- ter eld  ( 2002),  which  examined  perceived  certainty  of  being  caught  using  single- item  indicators.  Consistent  with  these  approaches,  two  items  to  capture  perceived likelihood  of  detection  for  self  and  peers  when  using  GAITs  were  developed  for the  current  study  ( How  likely  do  you  think  it  is  you  would  be  detected  if  you  use GAITs to complete an assignment or exam?  and  How likely do you think it is your peers  would  be  detected  if  they  use  GAITs  to  complete  an  assignment  or  exam? ). Item clarity and face validity were assessed through internal review prior to data col- lection.  Participants  rated  the  likelihood  of  the  two  situations  on  a  5-point  Likert scale  ranging  from  1  (very  unlikely) to  5  (very  likely).  The  two  items  were  strongly correlated,  suggesting  that  they  reflected  a  similar  underlying  construct,  leading  to the calculation of an average composite score. Higher scores represented greater per- ceived likelihood of GAIT detection. This scale demonstrated adequate internal con- sistency (r = .74).
Social desirability
The 16-item Behavior in Desirable Responding Scale (BIDR-16) was used to measure social  desirability  (Hart  et  al. 2015).  The  scale  measures  two  factors:  Self-deceptive enhancement (e.g.  I never regret my decisions ) and impression management (e.g.  I never cover up my mistakes ). Participants rated their level of agreement with each state- ment on an 8-point Likert scale of 1 (totally disagree) to 8 (totally agree). An overall score was calculated by scoring one point for every response of 7 or 8 on a statement, with higher scores representing greater tendencies for socially desirable scoring. Internal con- sistency was moderate for the BIDR-16 (α = .77).
Procedure
The study was granted ethics approval by the institutional Human Research Ethics Com- mittee  (Reference  Number:  520231313949982).  Participants  were  given  information about  the  study  and  provided  informed  consent  for  their  participation  prior  to  the administration of a 30-minute online questionnaire via the Qualtrics survey platform. Basic demographic information, such as participant age and gender were collected, fol- lowing which participants completed study measures.
Results
Data analytic strategy
All  statistical  analyses  were  conducted  using  IBM  SPSS  Statistics  (Version  29).  The nominal  alpha  was  set  at  .05  for  all  signi cance  testing.  In  terms  of  assumption testing, independence was met through study design. The normality of residuals was con rmed through observation of a normal probability plot and a Shapiro—Wilk test for normality (p = .39). Variance inflation factor values were also inspected and evidence of multicollinearity not found. However, an examination of a scatterplot of residuals against predicted values revealed both fanning and non-linear patterns, suggesting the assumptions of linearity and homoscedasticity of residuals were violated. Consequently, bootstrapping with 5000 replications was utilized for relevant analyses (Banjanovic and Osborne 2019; Field and Wilcox 2017). Results are presented in two parts. First, descrip- tive statistics and Pearson correlations between all study variables to examine bivariate relationships are shown. Secondly, a hierarchical linear regression examining factors associated  with  the  intention  to  engage  in  academic  misconduct  using  GAITS  is described.

Discussion
The primary aim of the current study was to investigate associations between inten- tions  to  engage  in  academic  misconduct  using  GAITs  and  predictors  of  behavioral intention as per Ajzen s (1991) TPB model (attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral  control).  A  secondary  aim  of  the  study  was  to  expand  this  model  to include the exploration of relations between student intentions to engage in academic misconduct  using  GAITs  and  two  additional  factors;  namely,  attitudes  towards  AI and perceived likelihood of detection. Considering the implications of academic mis- conduct for students and institutions alike (Abdolmohammadi and Baker  2007; Mar- tinson, Anderson, and de Vries  2005; Parnther  2022), it was envisaged the results of this research would inform education policies and interventions, and ultimately con - tribute to the emerging  eld of research in authentic assessment in higher education in the AI era.
The TPB model and intentions to engage in academic misconduct using gaits
Partial support for H1 was obtained, such that greater positive attitudes towards aca- demic  misconduct,  as  well  as  greater  subjective  norms  about  academic  misconduct, were  associated  with  greater  intentions  to  engage  in  academic  misconduct  using GAITs. These  ndings accord with literature demonstrating a relationship between atti - tudes towards and subjective norms about traditional forms of academic misconduct and intention to engage in such academic misconduct (Alleyne and Phillips 2011; Cronan, Mullins, and Douglas 2018; Harding et al. 2007; Juan et al. 2022; Maloshonok and Shme- leva 2019; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2009). Interestingly, consistent with the  ndings of Hendy and Montargot (2019), albeit in the context of more traditional forms of aca- demic misconduct, attitudes towards academic misconduct using GAITs yielded the strongest association with intentions to engage in academic misconduct, compared to the other constructs comprising the TPB model.
It  thus  follows  when  it  comes to  institutional  policies  and  intervention  strategies designed to encourage behaviors aligning with academic integrity, institutions may be best to understand the subjective norms relating to academic misconduct using GAITs at their respective institution, and then seeking to address these accordingly. Further- more, in what is perhaps an encouraging insight for the  eld, while violations of aca- demic conduct remain highly ubiquitous (M. J. Birks et al. 2018; M. Birks et al. 2020; Curtis and Popal 2011), and perhaps even more so with the advent of generative-AI tech- nologies, the current  ndings suggest the higher education student s decision to engage in academic misconduct is likely impacted  by perceived subjective norms about the engagement of such acts, beyond frequency of engagement across peers alone, which is arguably more amenable to intervention.
In opposition to H1, perceived behavioral control and intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs were not related in the  nal model inclusive of attitudes towards AI and perceived likelihood of detection of AI use. Notably, in the previous model omitting these auxiliary factors, the relationship between perceived behavioral control and intentions was signi cant. Multicollinearity was discon rmed via an inspection of variance inflation factor  values,  and  accords  with  the  magnitude  of  correlations  observed  between  these factors. Instead, these  ndings implicate these auxiliary factors as perhaps the mechanism of influence between behavioral control and intention found in previous studies. An exam- ination of the literature suggests likelihood of detection may be particularly relevant. Ajzen, in his 2002paper seeking to clarify conceptual and methodological ambiguities surrounding the concept of perceived behavioral control, emphasized that control beliefs — de ned as beliefs that may further or hinder performance of the behavior — give rise to perceived behavioral control. It is highly plausible that perceptions of the likelihood of GAIT detection would hinder academic misconduct using GAITs. This proposed interplay between percep- tions of behavioral control, perceived likelihood of detection, and intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs warrants further exploration.
Beyond the TPB model: attitudes towards AI and perceived likelihood of detection
The current  ndings suggest reasonable applicability of the TPB to intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs. The exploration of auxiliary factors that may enhance understanding of student intention to engage in academic misconduct is con - sidered  commonplace  (e.g.  Imran,  Hussien,  and  Alaraimi  2025;  Rajah-Kanagasabai and Roberts 2015; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2010). In this study, the addition of atti- tudes towards AI and perceived likelihood of GAIT detection contributed to variance in intention to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs, over and above the inclusion of constructs derived from the TPB (Ajzen 1991).
Consistent with H2, an association between the positive subscale of the GAAIS and greater intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs was found, while no relationship was present for negative attitudes towards AI. These  ndings align with prior research suggesting those with positive attitudes towards AI are more likely to adopt AI (Darda, Carre, and Cross 2023; Lambert et al. 2023; Schepman and Rodway 2020). That student attitudes regarding the drawbacks of AI do not seem related to intentions to use GAITs for the purposes of committing academic misconduct is especially noteworthy for higher education providers. Possibly, students acknowledge the drawbacks of AI, but do not prioritize these over the perceived bene ts. This suggests institutional policies and inter - vention strategies focused on highlighting the potential negative implications of AI for stu- dents in terms of the attainment of graduate capabilities may be misguided (Bobula 2024); rather, fostering among students a mindset where GAITs are viewed as supplemental, as opposed to, replacements for their knowledge and skills (Ardito 2025; L. Bennett and Abu- salem 2024), may be more advisable. It is however worth noting that while most contempor- ary  universities  have  sought  to  proactively  embrace  GAITs  in  this  way,  establishing guidelines regarding acceptable and unacceptable use for students, considerable concerns exist regarding mere advocacy for the implementation of AI in education, given understand- ing about the appropriate use and risks of AI in academic settings remains in its infancy (Chan  2023). This inevitably raises questions about the efficacy of current methods for addressing the challenges posed by GAITs in educational settings.
In support of H3, higher perceived likelihood of detection was related to lower intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs. This suggests that the perceived risk of being detected acts as a deterrent for students who intend to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs (de Lambert, Ellen, and Taylor 2006; Makarova 2019; McCabe, Trevino, and Butter eld  2002). Aligning with economic theory, students may weigh the consequences of detection to be greater than the bene ts gained from using GAITs for academic misconduct (Megehee and Spake 2008). Unfortunately for higher education providers, current research casts signi cant doubt over the accuracy and reliability of even state-of-the art AI-detection tools (Ardito 2025; Weber-Wulff et al. 2023), with  ndings that no doubt serve to reduce perceptions of the likelihood of detection for the savvy university student. Perceived chal- lenges detecting the use of GAITs may thus bolster its improper use among higher edu- cation students who, recognizing the limitations in AI-generated text detection in tertiary institutions,  may  possess  diminished  fear  of  detection  (Dalalah  and  Dalalah  2023; McCabe, Trevino, and Butter eld  2001). This can create a contagion effect that encourages non-engaging students who feel disadvantaged to do the same (Megehee and Spake 2008; Sattler, Wiegel, and Veen 2017). In response, universities could consider alternative detec- tion methods to human identi cation, including the integration of AI-based detection tech - nologies (Chaka 2023; Weber-Wulff et al. 2023). Though these authors would contend the likelihood of developing robust AI-detection tools that effectively leverages AI itself in the battle against AI seems unlikely.
Indeed, it appears there is no  easy solution  for improving AI-detection (Weber-Wulff et al. 2023). Such circumstances may serve to reduce both the self-efficacy and inclination of educators to identify and report suspected cases of academic misconduct using GAITs, seeing these efforts as perhaps futile in the current climate or unsupported by university administrators. Paradoxically, it follows logically that where a strong commitment to reporting instances of academic misconduct using GAITs exists, perceptions of the like- lihood that one might be detected if engaging in such behaviors should also increase, which, according to the results of this study, may serve to curtail intention to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs. Drawing on research regarding peer-to-peer whis- tleblowing when it comes to breaches of academic integrity (e.g. Amin et al. 2024), future investigations would bene t from better understanding factors that might support and empower potentially disenfranchized members of academic faculty to adopt a culture of identi cation when it comes to unauthorized GAIT use by students.
Strengths and limitations
A strength of the present study was the inclusion of social desirability as a control variable. While  previous  literature  has  recognized  biased  responding  in  self-report  data  (Imran, Hussien, and Alaraimi 2025; Juan et al. 2022; Stone, Jawahar, and Kisamore 2009), few studies have accounted for it. That social desirability was strongly associated with intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs highlights the importance that studies in this domain similarly seek to address this issue in their research, preferably using creative and more robust methods than afforded by the use of self-report measures alone.
The present study possessed several limitations. Firstly, due to the cross-sectional study design, causal relationships could not be established. Furthermore, the sample con- sisted primarily of females. While not a factor of interest in this study, research has reported gender differences in engagement in misconduct (K. K. Bennett, Behrendt, and Boothby 2011; Zhang, Yin, and Zheng 2018). Given 58% of the variance in intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs remained unaccounted for in this study, future research would bene t from considering whether gender, alongside a range of other  variables,  can  boost  the  explanatory  power  of  such  models.  It  is  also  worth noting potential restrictions to the generalisability of the current results given the partici- pant sample, which consisted solely of students enrolled in a  rst-year psychology unit of study and at a single university. It is further possible the conduct of a study investigating intentions to engage in academic misconduct within a tertiary setting may have biased the participant pool, despite assurances of anonymity and con dentiality having been pro - vided to prospective participants. Finally, though the predictive relationship between behav- ioral intention and the actual conduct of that behavior remains the bedrock of the TPB (Ajzen 1991), whether intentions to engage in academic misconduct using GAITs precipi- tated actual academic integrity infringements was untested in this study.
Implications and conclusions
The current  ndings convey important implications for the development of policy and intervention  in  relation  to  breaches  of  academic integrity  through  the  unauthorized use of GAITs as tertiary institutions continue moving towards meaningful and ethical integration of such technologies in learning and assessment practice. Indeed, a number of commonsense solutions have already been offered in the literature with respect to com- bating improper GAIT use in higher education. These include but are not limited to the implementation of robust assessment strategies (e.g. Ardito 2025), improving AI-literacy (Russell Group 2023), and adopting a programmatic approach to assessment (Luo (Jess) 2024; The Tertiary Education Quality and Standards Agency 2023). Reducing tendencies towards over-assessment, which is known to impact student success (Yu et al. 2023), and thus perhaps increases pressure to use by fueling positive attitudes towards AI, may also prove effective.
However, what these  ndings principally highlight is that in developing sound aca- demic integrity policies and assessment strategies, higher education providers would bene t from seeking to  rst understand their student bodies with respect to positive atti - tudes regarding academic misconduct and AI, subjective norms surrounding the use of GAITs in academic misconduct, and perceived likelihood of detection if using GAITs for these purposes. Shown in this study to be associated with the intention to engage in aca- demic misconduct using GAITs, educational institutions should focus on tailoring inter - ventions  that  proactively  target  these  factors.  This  includes  education  programs emphasizing AI ethics to encourage responsible and conscientious use of GAITs. Addres- sing positive attitudes towards academic misconduct using GAITs through strategies that motivate and encourage the learner to authentically engage in the learning process, in spite  of  the  ubiquitous  availability  of  GAITs,  in  support  of  competency  attainment, and  in  turn,  graduate  employability  outcomes,  also  seems  important.  Efforts  should further be made to challenge perceptions of the acceptability of academic misconduct using GAITs, as well as warn students about the possibility of detection and related con- sequences. Adopting an integrated, but targeted, intervention strategy addressing such factors  may  serve  to  effectively  reduce  engagement  in  academic  misconduct  using GAITs within higher education settings.
