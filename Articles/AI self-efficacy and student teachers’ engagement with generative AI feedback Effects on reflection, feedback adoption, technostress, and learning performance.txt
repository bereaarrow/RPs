Introduction
Within teacher education, self-reflection plays an important role in student teachers  professional  development,  including  their  evolving  understanding  of  professional identity,  beliefs,  and  values  (Engelbertink  et al.,  2021;  K rkk   et al.,  2016;  McKay, 2021).  Digital  storytelling  (DST),  implemented  as  a  short  narrative  video,  has  been widely  used  to  engage  student  teachers  in  reflecting  on  personal  experiences  and expressing  developing  perspectives  on  teaching  and  learning  (Isaacs  et al.,  2024;

Khoo et al., 2024; Mar n et al., 2018). However, student teachers often lack high-quality and real-time feedback during the DST process, which may hinder the depth of their reflections (Kim et al., 2021; Rohloff, 2021).
Generative  artificial  intelligence  (GenAI)  has  rapidly  transformed  education  by offering timely, diverse, context-aware feedback that supports content creation, lan -guage refinement, and creative expansion (Gillani et al.,  2023; Hwang & Chen, 2023). GenAI  has  the  potential  to  optimise  DST  by  providing  personalised  feedback  to facilitate  student  teachers   reflective  learning  (Cai  et al.,  2025;  Chan  &  Tsi, 2024). However, the role of GenAI feedback in education remains debated (Lu & Ba,  2026). Some studies have indicated that learners adopt GenAI feedback because it provides targeted guidance and improves task quality, whereas others avoid adopting GenAI feedback  due  to  concerns  about  reliability,  accuracy  or  over-dependence  (Yang  & Appleget,  2024;  Ziqi  et al., 2026).  Despite  the  growing  integration  of  GenAI  into education, limited research has examined how student teachers engage with GenAI feedback  in  reflective  learning  and  professional  development  contexts,  and  how individual differences, such as AI self-efficacy, influence this process.
Self-efficacy  is  a  psychological  factor  that  influences  individuals   perceptions, behaviours,  and  performance  when  adopting  new  technologies  (Bandura,  1977). Typically,  student  teachers  with  high  self-efficacy  tend  to  adopt  technology  more flexibly, while those with low self-efficacy may face stress and low willingness to use technology (Bai et al., 2023; Joo et al., 2018). In the context of AI, AI self-efficacy refers to  individuals   perceived  confidence  in  their  ability  to  use  AI  technologies  (Wang  & Chuang,  2024).  However,  little  is  known  about  how  AI  self-efficacy  shapes  student teachers  engagement with GenAI feedback during reflective learning processes.
Moreover,  AI  self-efficacy  may  shape  contextual  and  psychological  outcomes  in GenAI-facilitated learning contexts. GenAI literacy reflects the competence to understand, apply, and critically evaluate GenAI (Chen, 2025); technostress represents the psycho - logical strain of experiencing GenAI (Qi, 2019); and continuous usage intention indicates the likelihood that training transfers to sustained use of GenAI in teaching (Liu et al., 2025). Several studies have found a direct relationship between self-efficacy and GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention (Chen,  2025; Joo et al., 2018; Qi, 2019).  However,  empirical  evidence  regarding  how  AI  self-efficacy  influences  these outcomes and how these factors are related to one another remains limited.
Building on these insights, this study integrated GenAI feedback and DST for stu - dent teachers  professional identity reflection. Within this context, it investigated the reflection level and feedback engagement (GenAI feedback adoption, GenAI feedback satisfaction,  and  self-evaluation  of  DST)  of  student  teachers  with  different  levels  of AI self-efficacy. It further explored group differences in learning performance, GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention, and examined the relationships among these factors. The research questions are as follows:
RQ1: What are the differences in the reflection level of student teachers with dif -
ferent levels of AI self-efficacy?
RQ2:  What  are  the  differences  in  the  GenAI  feedback  adoption,  GenAI  feedback 
satisfaction, and self-evaluation of DST work of student teachers with different levels of AI self-efficacy?
RQ3: What are the differences in the GenAI literacy, technostress, continuous usage 
intention, and learning performance of student teachers with different levels of AI self-efficacy? What are the relationships among these factors?
Literature review
Reflection and professional identity through digital storytelling (DST)
Professional identity is a self-concept that reflects teachers  understanding of professional roles and beliefs (Walkington,  2005). It is developed through an ongoing process of pro - fessional development (Kim et al.,  2021; K rkk  et al.,  2016). Within this context, reflection plays an important role in shaping student teachers  professional identity, as it deepens their understanding of their professional roles and values through self-examination and meaning-making (McKay, 2021; Sutherland et al., 2010). Kelchtermans (2009) highlighted task perception as one component of professional identity that can be inferred from how teachers interpret what their profession demands. Engelbertink et al. (2021) further indi- cated that task perception can be traced through reflections on how teachers understand 
their  duties,  values,  and  moral  considerations.  Grounded  in  Kelchtermans  (2009)  and Engelbertink et al. (2021), this study operationalised identity-relevant reflection using three observable indicators: (1) role interpretation, reflected in how student teachers describe and make sense of their professional roles and responsibilities; (2) pedagogical judgement, reflected in how they assess teaching practices, decisions, or experiences; and (3) peda - gogical beliefs, reflected in how they connect their reflections to broader teaching philos - ophies, values, or ethical considerations.
DST has been introduced to support student teachers  reflection on their profes - sional identity (Isaacs et al., 2024; Mar n et al., 2018). Previous studies have demon - strated that student teachers can create digital stories to vividly present their personal 
and professional experiences and to reflect on their identity (Kim et al.,  2021; Mar n et al., 2018). For example, Kim et al. (2021) introduced DST to support student teach - ers   reflection.  By  analysing  DST  works,  the  researchers  found  that  DST  facilitated student  teachers   deep  reflection  and  transformed  their  perspectives  from  purely relying  on  personal  experiences  to  understanding  educational  goals.  Additionally, Mar n  et al.  (2018)  found  that  DST  could  enable  student  teachers   reflection  and achieve the transition from an emotionally driven identity to a more value-oriented and  reality-aware  identity.  However,  reflection  often  depends  on  effective  feedback during the DST process (Kim et al., 2021; Rohloff, 2021). Without sufficient support, student teachers may struggle to deepen their reflections. Therefore, providing timely and specialised feedback in DST is essential for professional identity reflection.
GenAI feedback-facilitated learning
GenAI refers to AI technologies based on large language models that can generate various types of content (Hwang & Chen,  2023). By allowing users to input requests, GenAI can offer timely and corresponding feedback, which can help content creation, language  refinement,  creative  expansion,  and  self-reflection  (Gillani  et al.,  2023). In recent years, GenAI feedback has been introduced at different educational levels and  disciplines  (Chien  et al.,  2025;  Koong  Lin  et al., 2025; Yang  &  Appleget, 2024). Several  studies  have  demonstrated  that  GenAI  feedback  could  enhance  students  learning outcomes, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills (e.g., Chen et al., 2025; Koong Lin et al., 2025).
GenAI feedback has also demonstrated potential in teacher education. For example, Yang and Appleget (2024) found that GenAI feedback could improve pre-service teach - ers  efficiency in designing questions and lesson plans, help diversify their perspectives, and enhance their creative thinking. Additionally, Moorhouse and Kohnke (2024) found that GenAI could offer personalised corrective feedback to enable pre-service English teachers to refine their lesson plans and teach more effectively.
Moreover, recent studies have integrated GenAI feedback into DST. For example, Cheung and Shi (2024) and Chu et al. (2025) applied GenAI feedback to support university students  DST. They demonstrated that GenAI could enhance the structure, accuracy, and interactivity of  digital  stories.  Despite  these  positive  findings,  evidence  of  GenAI  feedback  and  DST supporting identity-relevant reflection remains limited. Accordingly, this study incorporated GenAI feedback into DST to support identity-relevant reflection.
Despite the benefits of GenAI feedback, existing studies have demonstrated that students have different degrees of engagement in the adoption and perceptions of GenAI feedback. For instance, Ziqi et al. (2026) found that in second-language essay writing, college students tended to reject or disregard content-related GenAI feedback while demonstrating higher acceptance and satisfaction with form-related feedback. Those  who  rejected  GenAI  feedback  tended  to  adhere  to  their  own  ideas.  These findings suggest that learners  engagement with GenAI feedback is not uniform and may be shaped by individual differences.
AI self-efficacy in GenAI-facilitated learning
Self-efficacy involves individuals  confidence in their ability to perform specific tasks (Bandura,  1977).  Self-efficacy  should  be  studied  in  context  (Tsai  et al.,  2011).  This study focused on self-efficacy in AI contexts and described AI self-efficacy as individ- uals  perceived confidence in their ability to use AI technologies (Wang & Chuang, 2024).
Self-efficacy is described as being related to learners  engagement and behavioural decisions (Bandura, 1977). Previous work has shown that learners with higher self-efficacy tend to monitor their learning and critically evaluate and integrate feedback into their understanding, which may support productive reflection (Bassleer et al.,  2025; Tu et al., 2026). This reflective process has been linked to learners  acceptance of GenAI feedback (Tu et al., 2026). Evidence also suggests that higher AI self-efficacy is associated with greater engagement with GenAI feedback, as learners report feeling more confident in interacting with and utilising GenAI outputs (Bergdahl & Sj berg,  2025; Liu et al., 2025). Accordingly, these studies suggest a conceptual pathway in which AI self-efficacy may be associated with GenAI feedback engagement through reflective processes.
AI  self-efficacy  has  also  been  identified  as  a  key  factor  associated  with  a  range  of learning outcomes and perceptions in GenAI-facilitated learning (e.g., Bergdahl & Sj berg, 2025; Wang & Chuang, 2024). Studies have suggested that teachers  self-efficacy is related to their attitudes towards and intentions to use technology (Bai et al.,  2023; Joo et al., 2018). Collie et al. (2024) and Joo et al. (2018) reported positive relationships between self-efficacy and student teachers  learning performance, GenAI literacy, technostress, and willingness to adopt new technologies. Dong et al. (2020) further found a negative rela - tionship between teachers  self-efficacy and technostress; teachers with high self-efficacy tended  to  report  feeling  more  at  ease  using  technology,  whereas  those  with  low self-efficacy reported higher levels of stress. However, empirical evidence regarding how AI self-efficacy is associated with student teachers  reflection, GenAI feedback engagement, and  related  contextual  constructs  remains  limited.  Hence,  this  study  investigated  the reflection  level  and  feedback  engagement  (GenAI  feedback  adoption  and  satisfaction, self-evaluation of DST) of student teachers with different levels of AI self-efficacy. It further explored  group  differences  in  learning  performance,  GenAI  literacy,  technostress,  and continuous usage intention, and examined the relationships among these factors.
Method
Context and participants
This  research  was  conducted  based  on  a  professional  development  program  at  a Chinese  normal  university.  The  sample  included  76  student  teachers  (14  males,  52 females; Meanage = 20.8 years). This program was taught by an experienced instructor. All participants had previous experience of using text-GenAI and DST, but none had received formal training in using GenAI with DST. This study received approval from the  ethics  board  of  the  researchers   university  (WZU-2024-091).  All  participants  vol- untarily engaged in this research.
Research implementation
This study was conducted based on a compulsory professional development program 
 The Applications of GenAI in Education.  This program aimed to help student teachers understand the potential and challenges of GenAI, comprehend various GenAI tech - nologies (text-GenAI, image-GenAI, music-GenAI, and video-GenAI), master the basic techniques of DST, and reflect on their professional identity in relation to integrating GenAI into instruction. It was expected to enhance their ability and belief in applying GenAI in instruction. The final task involved identity-relevant reflection, which required student teachers to create a DST work on  an ideal teacher for integrating GenAI into instruction.  Student teachers were encouraged to interact with GenAI and receive its feedback during the DST process.
Figure  1  presents  the  research  procedure.  The  course  lasted  6 weeks  (80-minute session/week). The details are presented as follows, and refer to the TEP-AIED model recommended by Hwang et al. (2026):
Week  1  involved  an  orientation  on  the  course  content  and  objectives,  and  then 
the  student  teachers  were  invited  to  complete  the  pre-questionnaire  on  AI self-efficacy. The teacher instructed learners on the fundamental knowledge of GenAI,  and  invited  them  to  explore  its  advantages  and  challenges  through two case studies (one that met an educational need and another that contained biased or misleading information) to foster their critical thinking.

Figure 1.  Experimental procedure.
Week  2  involved  DST  training.  The  teacher  explained  key  elements  of  DST  and 
demonstrated typical GenAI tools. Student teachers created digital stories and generated images using simple prompts to gain hands-on experience.
Week 3 involved training on the application of GenAI in instruction, and encouraged 
discussions  on  pedagogical  ideas  regarding  GenAI  in  instruction.  The  teacher showcased  pedagogical  applications  of  GenAI  with  two  examples,  and  used GenAI  to  generate  historical  event  stories  to  boost  engagement  and  compre - hension. For example, students used GenAI to generate short stories with images to enhance narrative and visual expression skills for language learning.
Week 4 involved GenAI-based lesson design and group practices. Student teachers 
collaborated in small groups to design GenAI-based teaching activities. During the  process,  they  received  peer  feedback  and  improved  their  instructional designs.
Week 5 served as the final course work. Student teachers created a DST work on 
 an ideal teacher who can integrate GenAI into instruction.  During the process, they were encouraged to interact with GenAI and receive its feedback. Then, they completed a reflection report to evaluate their experiences.
Week 6 involved sharing DST work and completing post-questionnaires on GenAI 
literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention.
Measuring tools
The  measuring  tools  included  a  pre-questionnaire  of  AI  self-efficacy  and post-questionnaires of GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention. All questionnaires were assessed using a 5-point Likert scale. The reliability of these tools (see  Table 1) met the criteria of Nunnally and Bernstein (1994).
The  AI  self-efficacy  questionnaire  was  adapted  from  Dong  et al.  (2020)  to  assess student  teachers   confidence  in  their  ability  to  use  AI  technology.  It  included  five items. A sample item is:  If I put in my best effort, I believe I can overcome all diffi- culties  in  applying  AI  technology.   Previous  research  demonstrated  that  using  the upper and lower half of mean scores is appropriate to divide high- and low-performance groups  (Chen  et al.,  2025;  Hwang  et al., 2023).  Based  on  this  practice,  the  sample was divided into two groups according to their mean scores of AI self-efficacy (N = 76, Mean = 3.79): the high AI self-efficacy (HAISE) group (n = 38, Mean = 4.14) and the low  AI  self-efficacy  (LAISE)  group  (n = 38,  Mean  =  3.45).  Significant  differences  were detected in the two groups  AI self-efficacy ( t = 9.85, p < 0.001, d = 2.26).
The GenAI literacy questionnaire was adapted from Wang et al. (2023) to evaluate student  teachers   ability  to  understand,  apply,  and  evaluate  GenAI.  It  included  12 items. Sample items are:  I can proficiently use GenAI applications or tools to assist in my daily tasks,  and  I always adhere to ethical principles when using GenAI pro - grams or products. 
The  technostress  questionnaire  was  adapted  from  Dong  et al.  (2020)  to  assess learners  stress when using GenAI tools. It included 10 items. A sample item is  Using GenAI tools does not make me feel nervous or anxious.  This scale is reverse-scored, where higher scores indicate lower perceived technostress.
The continuous usage intention questionnaire was adapted from Falode (2018) to assess learners  intention to continue using GenAI. It had five items. A sample item includes:  The integration of GenAI technology in teaching and learning is a positive initiative. 
The reflection report was designed by two experts in educational technology, and comprised three parts:
Adoption of GenAI feedback
Student teachers reported whether they adopted GenAI feedback or adhered to their own ideas, and explained their reasons.
Satisfaction with GenAI feedback
Student teachers rated their satisfaction with the GenAI feedback on a 5-point scale (1 = very dissatisfied, 5 = very satisfied).

Data analysis
For RQ1, two well-trained researchers coded reflection reports based on the coding scheme (see  Figure 2 and Appendix). Discrepancies were resolved through discussion. The Cohen s Kappa at this stage was 0.84, indicating good reliability (Landis & Koch, 1977). Descriptive statistics of the coding results are presented.
For RQ2, descriptive statistical analyses were conducted to examine the following aspects  across  the  two  groups:  (1)  GenAI  feedback  adoption,  (2)  GenAI  feedback satisfaction, and (3) self-evaluation of DST works. Additionally, a content analysis of student teachers  reflection reports was conducted to explore the reasons for adopting 
or  rejecting  GenAI  feedback.  Responses  were  categorised  into  themes.  The  theme frequencies are presented descriptively overall and by groups. The Cohen s Kappa was 0.88, indicating good reliability (Landis & Koch, 1977).
For  RQ3,  according  to  data  normality,  either  an  independent  samples  t  test  or  a Mann—Whitney U  test  was  conducted  to  compare  group  differences  in  the  GenAI literacy,  technostress,  continuous  usage  intention,  and  learning  performance.

Additionally, Pearson correlation analysis was performed to examine the relationships between  variables.  Moreover,  tests  for  linearity  and  homoscedasticity  assumptions were conducted before regression analysis. Hierarchical regression analysis was then employed to explore the effects of GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention on learning performance.
Common method bias
As  this  study  involved  self-reported  measures,  several  efforts  were  made  to  minimise potential common method bias (Podsakoff et al.,  2003). First, participants were assured of anonymity and confidentiality to reduce social desirability and evaluation apprehen - sion. Second, to reduce same-source and same-time measurement concerns, AI self-efficacy was  measured  at  the  pre-test  stage,  whereas  the  other  variables  were  collected  after the  program.  Moreover,  Harman s  single-factor  test  was  conducted.  The  first  factor accounted for 32.05% of the variance, which was below the commonly used 40% thresh - old, suggesting that common method bias was not a serious concern in this study.
Results
The reflection level

Analysis of learning performance, GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention of the HAISE and LAISE groups
Learning performance
The Shapiro-Wilk tests showed the normal distribution of the learning performance data  (p  > .05).  Hence,  the  independent  sample  t  test  was  performed  (see  Table  2), showing that the two groups differed significantly in learning performance (t = 5.18, 
p < .001, d = 1.19). Specifically, the HAISE group (Mean = 21.11, SD = 2.06) had signifi- cantly better learning performance than the LAISE group (Mean = 18.58,  SD = 2.19).
GenAI literacy
As GenAI literacy data were not normally distributed ( p < .001), the Mann—Whitney U test  was  performed. The  results  (see  Table  3)  showed  that  the  two  groups  differed significantly  in  GenAI  literacy  (U = 157.00,  p  < .001, r = 0.78).  Specifically,  the  HAISE group (Mean = 4.19,  SD = 0.15) had significantly higher GenAI literacy than the LAISE group (Mean = 3.92,  SD = 0.23).

Discussion and conclusions
This  study  examined  the  reflection  level,  GenAI  feedback  adoption,  GenAI  feedback satisfaction,  and  self-evaluation  of  DST  work  among  student  teachers  with  different levels  of  AI  self-efficacy.  It  also  explored  group  differences  in  learning  performance, GenAI literacy, technostress, and continuous usage intention, and learning outcomes, and investigated the relationships among these factors. The results suggest that most student teachers achieved the reflection level in professional identity reflection. Most student  teachers  reported  that  they  adopted  GenAI  feedback  more  frequently  than rejected it. The HAISE group reported greater acceptance of GenAI feedback than the LAISE  group.  Additionally,  the  HAISE  group  demonstrated  higher  levels  of  learning performance,  GenAI  literacy,  and  technostress  based  on  self-reported  measures. However, no significant difference was detected in the two groups  continuous usage intention.  Hierarchical  regression  analysis  further  revealed  that  GenAI  literacy  and technostress were significantly associated with learning performance, whereas contin - uous usage intention had no substantial predictive effect on learning performance.
In terms of RQ1, the final work of the program required student teachers to create digital  stories  to  reflect  on  ideal  teachers  integrating  GenAI  into  teaching.  Student teachers  were  encouraged  to  interact  with  GenAI  and  consider  its  feedback  during 
the DST process. By analysing their DST work, most student teachers were identified as reaching a reflection level in professional identity reflection, where their DST work mainly focused on how teachers understand and implement teaching activities with GenAI  (e.g.,  the  applications  of  GenAI  technologies,  the  solutions  for  ethical  issues, and the design of teaching strategies). The finding echoes previous evidence on the effectiveness of DST in self-reflection (Kim et al., 2021; Mar n et al., 2018). For example, Kim  et al.  (2021)  indicated  that  DST  can  support  student  teachers  to  present  their professional  experiences  vividly  and  reflect  on  their  roles.  Additionally,  the  current finding  aligns  with  the  role  of  GenAI  in  DST  and  self-reflection.  Chu  et al.  (2025) found  that  GenAI  feedback  could  enhance  the  structure,  accuracy,  and  interactivity 
of digital stories. Koong Lin et al. (2025) also found that GenAI feedback could offer targeted  guidance  and  promote  critical  thinking.  In  this  study,  student  teachers reported  that  integrating  GenAI  feedback  into  DST  helped  narrative  coherence  and content.  They  also  perceived  GenAI  feedback  as  supporting  critical  examination  of their experiences and guiding them to review their teaching philosophy (Chien et al., 2025; Chu et al., 2025). These interpretations are based on participants  self-reported experiences  and  may  reflect  student  teachers   perceived  role  of  GenAI  feedback  in DST in supporting identity reflection.
As  for  RQ2,  based  on  the  reflection  reports,  the  HAISE  group  reported  that  they had higher adoption and satisfaction with GenAI feedback and better self-evaluation 
of their DST work than the LAISE group. Consistent with the conceptual pathway (Bai et al., 2023, Joo et al., 2018), this finding suggests that AI self-efficacy is associated with GenAI feedback engagement in the reflective context. The findings are interpreted 
as  perceived  associations  and  group  differences  rather  than  the  causal  effects  of cross-sectional evidence. First, social cognitive theory echoes this finding, as individ - uals  behaviours are related to confidence and attitudes (Bandura,  1977). Specifically, student teachers with higher AI self-efficacy perceived GenAI feedback as a valuable tool  and  reported  higher  acceptance  of  GenAI  feedback. They  reported  a  tendency 
to  utilise  GenAI  feedback  to  improve  their  DST  work,  and  self-evaluated  their  DST work more positively (Joo et al.,  2018; Liang et al., 2011). Owing to their low confi- dence in using GenAI, the LAISE group might have struggled to understand the GenAI feedback, reported lower acceptance of GenAI feedback, and self-evaluated their DST work more conservatively.
This study further analysed the reasons for adopting or rejecting GenAI feedback reported in student teachers  reflection reports. Most student teachers reported that they  adopted  GenAI  feedback  for  reasons  of  content  creation,  optimising  language expression, and supplementary perspectives, consistent with prior reported benefits 
of GenAI feedback (Chien et al., 2025; Moorhouse & Kohnke, 2024). However, student teachers  also  noted  their  attention  to  emotional  authenticity,  efficiency,  creativity, and independence when adopting GenAI feedback. Notably, the HAISE group reported their focus on content richness, emotional authenticity, and the realism and humanity 
of the GenAI feedback, indicating an inclination to engage in co-creation with GenAI feedback to improve content quality and produce emotional resonance and authentic narratives. However, the LAISE group reported challenges in adapting to GenAI feed - back and prioritising its use to improve work efficiency. In other words, they described GenAI feedback as a tool to streamline or speed up the DST creation process. They also reported concerns about their ability to understand and manage GenAI feedback. 
As indicated by Collie et al. (2024), this pattern may be related to their lower confi - dence  in  handling  GenAI  feedback.  Meanwhile,  it  is  important  to  recognise  that student teachers  selective adoption or resistance to GenAI feedback may not indicate 
a deficiency (Lu & Ba, 2026). Rather, it may reflect perceived agency and professional judgement,  which  are  integral  to  professional  identity  reflection  (Engelbertink 
et al., 2021).
Regarding RQ3, according to the self-reported data after the GenAI feedback-supported reflective  activities,  the  HAISE  group  reported  higher  levels  of  GenAI  literacy,  lower levels  of  perceived  technostress,  and  higher  learning  performance  than  the  LAISE group. This finding is similar to previous studies that reported that stronger self-efficacy 
is associated with greater technology literacy, higher learning performance, and lower technology stress (Dong et al.,  2020; Liu et al., 2025; Tu et al., 2026). However, contrary to prior findings (Bai et al., 2023; Joo et al., 2018), no significant difference was found in  continuous  usage  intention  between  student  teachers  with  high  or  low  AI self-efficacy. One  possible  interpretation  is  that  student  teachers  in  both  groups reported  recognising  the  usefulness  of  GenAI  feedback  for  meeting  course  require - ments or improving DST work. This shared perception may be associated with their similar intention to continue using GenAI feedback (Liu et al.,  2025).
The  hierarchical  regression  analysis  showed  that  GenAI  literacy  and  technostress were significantly associated with learning performance. These results align with Salimi et al.  (2025),  who  reported  a  positive  role  of  technological  literacy  in  learning  per - formance. Chen (2025) noted that GenAI literacy is a core competency for using GenAI tools.  In  this  study,  higher  levels  of  GenAI  literacy  may  facilitate  student  teachers  ability  to  utilise  GenAI  feedback  to  improve  their  DST  work  for  identity-relevant reflection (Sutherland et al.,  2010). Additionally, consistent with Qi (2019), technostress was  identified  as  a  significant  correlate  of  learning  performance.  Lower  levels  of technostress  may  increase  student  teachers   focus  and  more  effective  use  of  GenAI feedback, thereby leading to higher learning outcomes (Qi,  2019). On the other hand, high technostress is associated with increased cognitive load, reduced effectiveness 
of technology use, and lower learning outcomes (Dong et al.,  2020). However, unlike Ye et al. (2022) findings, our study found no significant relationship between contin - uous  usage  intention  and  learning  performance.  One  possible  interpretation  is  that 
continuous  usage  intention  reflects  individuals   future-oriented  expectations  rather than  their  current  perceived  competence  or  performance  in  learning  (Liang  et al., 2011; Wang  et al., 2023). Thus,  even  when  student  teachers  reported  intentions  to use  GenAI  feedback,  this  intention  may  not  link  to  higher  learning  performance, particularly with lower GenAI literacy or higher perceived technostress.
Implications and contributions
This study offers several contributions and implications related to feedback engage - ment,  identity  reflection,  and  AI  self-efficacy  in  GenAI-facilitated  learning  contexts. First, this study advances the understanding of the positive role of GenAI feedback and DST in student teachers  identity-relevant reflection. Therefore, training programs could consider incorporating GenAI feedback to scaffold students  reflective practices.
Second,  this  study  unpacked  differences  among  student  teachers  with  different levels of self-efficacy in GenAI feedback engagement in identity reflection activities. Hence,  educators  could  facilitate  student  teachers   critical  thinking  and  make  more informed  decisions  to  effectively  utilise  GenAI  feedback.  Meanwhile,  personalised GenAI feedback strategies may be needed for student teachers with different levels of  AI  self-efficacy.  For  example,  the  HAISE  group  may  need  support  to  incorporate emotional  depth  and  realism  into  GenAI-generated  content,  while  training  should focus on strengthening the LAISE group s ability to understand and use GenAI feed - back effectively.
Furthermore, the findings validated the role of AI self-efficacy in student teachers  GenAI literacy, technostress, and learning performance, and highlighted GenAI literacy and  technostress  as  important  factors  relating  to  learning  performance  in GenAI-facilitated learning. Therefore, professional training should consider cultivating student teachers  AI self-efficacy, prioritising their GenAI literacy, and reducing tech - nostress through optimising technology design and providing targeted support.
Limitations and future directions
This  study  has  some  limitations.  First,  the  sample  was  from  one  Chinese  university. Future  research  could  include  more  diverse  samples.  Second,  professional  identity development  is  a  process-oriented  construct  (Friesen  &  Besley,  2013).  However,  this study did not capture professional identity as a longitudinal developmental process; rather,  it  focused  on  identity-relevant  reflection  within  a  specific  learning  context. Future  research  could  adopt  a  longitudinal  design  to  explore  how  identity  evolves with the support of GenAI feedback. Third, the cross-sectional design may limit the ability  to  draw  causal  conclusions.  Future  research  could  adopt  rigorous  designs  to evaluate the causal influences of the designed activity. Fourth, this study measured AI self-efficacy at the pre-test, while other key outcome variables were assessed after the intervention. This limits the ability to consider potential pre-existing differences between  participants.  Future  research  could  adopt  a  pre-test-posttest  design  for  all variables to provide a comprehensive understanding of the changes and causal rela - tionships.  Finally,  self-reported  questionnaires  were  utilised  to  assess  key  variables, which may introduce potential response bias. Future research could collect qualitative data  (e.g.,  observations  and  interviews)  to  obtain  comprehensive  evidence.  Some prospective directions are proposed for future research:
1. As  this  research  explored  student  teachers   GenAI  feedback  engagement  in reflection learning, researchers could explore the role of GenAI feedback across different contexts. Additionally, researchers could investigate perceptions and acceptance regarding GenAI feedback of other samples. It would also be worth delving into their reasons for adopting or rejecting GenAI feedback.
2. The  findings  indicate  that  technostress  significantly  affected  student  teachers  learning performance. Future studies could examine how different levels of tech - nostress influence the adoption and adaptation of GenAI feedback. Additionally, research could explore adaptive instructional design and technological support strategies to mitigate stress and enhance learners  performance.
3. Future researchers could design targeted training programs incorporating GenAI feedback.  For  instance,  personalised  GenAI  feedback  and  activities  could  be developed based on different levels of AI self-efficacy. Moreover, future studies could  examine  whether  and  how  other  characteristics  (e.g.,  GenAI  literacy, TPACK)  influence  learning  performance  and  perceptions  in  GenAI  feedback- facilitated learning.
