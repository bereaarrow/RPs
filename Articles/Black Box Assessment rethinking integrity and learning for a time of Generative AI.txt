Black Box Assessment: rethinking integrity and learning for a time of Generative AI

ABSTRACT
The design of effective assessment has long presented a complex challenge  for  educators,  one  that  has  intensified  with  the  emer - gence of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI). The maintenance of assessment validity in the context of GenAI has been character - ised in the literature as a  wicked problem: a multifaceted issue lack- ing  a  singular  solution  and  necessitating  structural  rather  than purely  discursive  responses.  This  paper  advances  a  blueprint  for one  such  structural  response  premised  upon  evaluating  the  pro - cesses through which artefacts are produced. We engage the con - cept  of  Black  Box  Thinking  to  propose  a  processual  assessment design  model  termed  Black Box Assessment. This  model  is  under- pinned  by  four  core  principles:  (1)  assessing  process  to  capture evidence of learning and cognitive change; (2) valuing errors and revisions  as  indicators  of  progress;  (3)  fostering  transparency  and learner agency; and (4) rewarding the learning trajectory not just polished  products. We  outline  the  key  steps  in  designing  a  Black Box  Assessment  and  tackle  potential  challenges  to  this  approach. We conclude by arguing that  Black Box Thinking provides not only a conceptual basis for process-focused assessment design but also 
a  valuable  evaluative  framework  for  addressing  the  wicked  prob - lem of assessment in the age of GenAI.

Education is fundamentally about development and change; learning occurs through dynamic  processes.  Yet  when  we  assess  students,  we  typically  focus  on  a  static snapshot: the product rather than the process. Concerns about verifying the validity of the assessment product as evidence of attainment against learning outcomes are not new; plagiarism, essay mills or contract cheating, and collusion have long created challenges for educational institutions seeking to demonstrate the validity and integ- rity of their assessment and certification processes (Dawson 2021).
However,  the  rise  of  Generative  Artificial  Intelligence  (GenAI)  has  led  scholars to describe the sector as experiencing an era of postplagiarism (Eaton 2023), where all  the  rules  around  academic  integrity  that  we  have  learned  to  abide  by  need  to be rewritten. Assessment and GenAI: defining the challenge
The challenge of assessment design in an era of GenAI is not something that can be definitively  solved   (Corbin  et al. 2025b),  and  the  complexity  of  the  challenge  is  a significant  source  of  stress  and  disillusionment  for  students,  educators,  institutional leaders, researchers, and policy makers. For example, in an interview study with stu- dents and teaching staff at an Australian university, Corbin et al. (2025a) demonstrated that both students and educators need to engage in  boundary work  to try and navigate the minefield of what  appropriate  AI usage in assessment might be. For some edu- cators,  uncertainty  about  those  boundaries  led  to  inertia,  even  paralysis,  preventing any change to their assessments despite clear recognition that validity was at risk.
It  is  perhaps  not  surprising  that  the  assessment  design  challenge  in  response  to GenAI is a source of concern for those involved in education. Corbin et al. (2025b) have framed the GenAI-assessment challenge in terms of Rittel and Webber s (1973) characterisation of a  wicked problem : one with no  correct  solution. Wicked problems, according to Rittel and Webber (1973), are not clearly defined, require context-specific approaches, and can potentially be addressed through an infinite number of responses. This is true of GenAI-assessment; there is no clear  problem space  (Simon and Newell 1971) which defines the confines of the challenge, and we already see myriad ways in which different educational systems and sectors are seeking to address the challenge (e.g.  Moorhouse  et al. 2023).  Wicked  problems  also  have  no  correct  answer,  and potential responses always involve trade-offs (Rittel and Webber 1973). In redesigning assessment in response to GenAI, compromises are a prominent part of the discourse (Corbin  et al.  2025b).  Part  of  the  challenge  for  GenAI-assessment  is  the  constant mutation of the problem space as GenAI technologies increase in sophistication and systemic constraints continue to evolve. This is important considering another feature of wicked problems: that their problem space interacts with that of other challenges. In GenAI-assessment, this is most likely to be seen through the way in which design decisions  are  often  influenced  by  systemic  challenges  (often  themselves  wicked   in nature) such as academic workload, space and timetabling, and policy and governance. The  framing  of  a  wicked  problem  shapes  our  mindset  in  thinking  about  potential responses. In GenAI-assessment, defining the problem space as one of securing aca- demic  integrity  would  lead  to  identification  of  different  approaches  compared  to framing the issue as one of assessment design (Corbin et al. 2025b).
It could be interpreted that the unassailable nature of a  wicked  problem leaves us with a sense of paralysis and demoralisation in responding to the GenAI-assessment challenge. Corbin et al. (2025b) counter this potential narrative by discussing what a wicked problem framing opens up in terms of three  permissions . First, the per- mission to compromise allows us to confidently say that there is no single best-practice solution  to  GenAI-assessment.  There  will  be  trade-offs,  sacrifices,  and  imperfect outcomes.  Such  a  framing  also  gives  permission  to  diverge  (Corbin  et al. 2025b): we cannot expect a single type of assessment task or design principle to work with the GenAI challenge in all circumstances. Contextual nuance is essential.
Finally,  framing  GenAI-assessment  as  a  wicked  problem  provides  permission  to iterate (Corbin et al. 2025b). There is no fixed, permanent solution, because wicked problems themselves evolve. We also believe that an approach to GenAI-assessment that involves principles rather than predetermined  solutions  is most likely to be of benefit to both educators and students. Principles are necessarily loose rather than fixed (permission to iterate), are designed for adaptation to different contexts (per- mission to diverge) and can be the compass for guiding decision-making about how and where to make trade-offs (permission to compromise).
The framing of GenAI-assessment as a wicked problem appears to caution against simplistic policies or universal approaches. This is echoed by Corbin et al. (2025c) who draw a critical distinction between discursive and structural responses to the GenAI-assessment challenge. Discursive responses are those which seek to commu- nicate to students what is and is not permitted in terms of the usage of GenAI in assessment,  such  as  traffic  light  systems  or  student  declarations.  The  challenge  of these  approaches  is  that  they  are  difficult  to  enforce,  that  they  place  the  onus  on students taking responsibility, and we do not know whether students have understood or followed the guidance (Corbin et al. 2025c).
In  contrast,  structural  changes  are  those  that  modify  the  nature  of  the  task  or assessment itself (Corbin et al. 2025c). The use of GenAI is specified and constrained not  purely  by  verbal  instructions,  but  by  the  very  nature  of  the  task  design  itself. This  approach  is  also  echoed  in  Corbin  et al.  (2025a),  where  it  is  asserted  that discursive approaches are challenging for students too, as they are required to engage in boundary work. In contrast, a structural approach can remove this ambiguity as appropriate  boundaries  of  GenAI  usage  are  determined  by  the  assessment  design itself. One structural change identified by Corbin et al. (2025c) is to focus assess- ment not on products but the process of learning.
Assessment design: from product to process
Many approaches to assessment redesign in response to GenAI focus primarily on the type of task (e.g. Moorhouse et al. 2023). An alternative perspective is to recon- ceptualise  design  not  in  terms  of  the  task  alone,  but  in  terms  of  how  learning  is 
assessed.  One  practical  implementation  of  this  approach  is  to  allocate  assessment weighting not only to the final product but also to evidence of the process through which  it  was  produced.  We  define process  in  the  context  of  assessment  design  as the dynamic, traceable sequence of cognitive, creative, and practical actions, decisions, errors, revisions, and reflections through which students construct understanding and develop  their  final  artefact  or  performance.  This  process  represents  the  visible  and intentional  journey  of  learning,  making  the  evolution  of  thinking  and  practice  a core source of assessment evidence. A processual focus emphasises the relationships among  formative,  in-process  interactions  and  the  material  arrangements  within specific  learning  environments,  as  well  as  the  forms  of  knowing  these  generate, including associated knowledge artefacts such as assignments (Elkington and Irons 2025). For example, Lodge et al. (2023) describe an essay assignment in Law, where the development of students  argument is influenced by the incorporation of peer, teacher,  and  AI  feedback.  The  final  submission  places  emphasis  on  how  students have  responded  to  feedback  encounters  and  demonstrating  how  their  judgement during the construction of legal argument has developed.
Adopting a processual approach to assessment is not a specific response to GenAI; for example, the value of shifting the assessment lens to processes instead of prod- ucts  has  been  discussed  in  the  context  of  experiential  or  problem-based  learning (e.g.  Simm 2005). A processual approach to assessment seeks to counter some of the  challenges  inherent  in  the  traditional  assessment  of  products  of  learning  (e.g. submitted essays, performed presentations, completed dissertations). Even before the challenge of GenAI-Assessment, there was an acknowledgement that a product focus in  assessment  was  based  on  implicit  assumptions  that  if  a  product  had  been  suc- cessfully completed, then the anticipated skills and understanding had been developed (Simm 2005). In the GenAI era, there is also a validity challenge to product-focused assessment,  as  students  can  potentially  submit  a  polished  product  created  by  AI, without using their own learning and skill development to get there (Dawson et al. 2024;  Lodge  et al. 2023).  This  recognition  echoes  Bearman  and  Ajjawi  (2023,  p. 1162,  emphasis  in  original),  who  argue  in  relation  to  AI  that  with  such  a  rapid changing environment, how will be more important than what .
Processual assessment also carries important implications for inclusivity, particularly regarding flexibility and choice. Research indicates that flexible assessment can enhance student engagement, positively influencing motivation, attitudes towards learning, and the exercise of choice and self-direction, thereby developing greater autonomy and a sense  of  control  (O Neill  and  Padden 2022).  It  also  represents  a  shift  in  the  distri- bution of knowledge, positioning learners as co-creators or co-designers and empha- sising  formative  assessment  processes  as  central  to  supporting  student  agency (Leenknecht et al. 2021). In some contexts, the selection of  process evidence  itself can serve as a site for student agency and intentionality. However, embedding flex- ibility  in  assessment  requires  careful  attention  to  how  students  perceive  themselves as learners and whether they are adequately prepared to take responsibility for their learning (Elkington and Chesterton  2023). In this regard, scaffolding is likely to be essential in enabling meaningful engagement with process-oriented assessment.

Black Box Assessment: the proof is in the process
The metaphor of opening a  black box  in education, as a way to bring visibility to commonly invisible processes, is not new. The concept is perhaps most well-known in  the  context  of  Black  and  Wiliam s  (1998)  work  on  formative  assessment  and classroom  practice,  where  they  argued  that  traditional  classroom  practices  hide critical learning processes. Typically, the inputs (teaching, tasks) and outputs (student performance) are visible, but what happens in between is often hidden from view. Black and Wiliam argued that formative assessment is a way of  opening the black box   to  make  learning  itself  visible,  and  to  enable  students  to  progress.  For  Black and  Wiliam,  the  formative  processes  of  assessment  are  fundamental  to  learning. Practically speaking, formative assessment is how we shift the focus from designed teaching to the process of student learning (Elkington and Irons  2025). The choices made  by  educators  and  students  with  regard  to  the  configuration  of  tasks,  tools, and  social  arrangements  for  the  purposes  of  formative  assessment  shape  the  ways learners can exercise their agency in (re)shaping what has been proposed.
Bringing  visibility  to  invisible  processes  has  also  been  used  to  understand  how students process and respond to feedback internally through cognitive and emotional processes that shape future actions (Lui and Andrade  2022). The metaphor has also been deployed in evaluation theory (e.g. Salter and Kothari 2014). Typically, evaluation of interventions is outcome-focused, seeking to determine whether or not an inter - vention was effective. Theory-driven and realist approaches to intervention contrast black box models with  white box  models, where transparency of mechanisms and pathways, rather than purely outcomes, is prioritised (Salter and Kothari  2014).
More recently, the notion of the black box has been widely employed to under- stand  the  opacity  of  AI  technologies  and  how  they  operate  (Bearman  and  Ajjawi 2023).  Drawing  from  sociomaterial  scholars  such  as  Bruno  Latour,  the  notion  of the  black  box  refers  to  the  ways  in  which  scientific  and  technical  work  is  made invisible by its own success. When a machine runs efficiently   one need focus only 
on its inputs and outputs and not on its internal complexity. Thus, paradoxically, the more  science  and  technology  succeed,  the  more  opaque  and  obscure  they  become  (Latour  1999,  p.  305).  But,  with  respect  to  GenAI,  scholars  increasingly  note  the problems associated with this opacity, for example with respect to issues of diversity, equity,  and  inclusion,  and  how  black-box  algorithms  are  entangled  with  broader societal  structures  (Rapanta  et al. 2025)  and  the  need  to  work  critically  with  the opacity that the black box represents.
Assessment  practices,  including  their  outcomes,  are  emergent  and  contingent, shaped by complex networks of relations rather than fully determinable in advance (Boud  et al. 2018).  They  unfold  through  processes  of  co-configuration,  in  which students actively appropriate, adapt, and transform the designs and structures estab - lished  by  educators.  Student  assessment  activities  are  thus  entangled  with  broader teaching  and  learning  practices,  mediated  through  dynamic  assemblages  of  digital and  material  tools  and  resources,  and  co-shaped  by  the  experiences,  actions,  and interactions  of  all  participants  involved.  So  how  can  the  commitment  to  working with opacity inform assessment design in the GenAI era?
The process paradox in assessment: why the Black Box is both the problem and the solution
A central challenge for assessment design posed by GenAI concerns the increasing difficulty  of  ascertaining  the  authenticity  of  student  work.  As  educators,  we  lack direct access to the cognitive and creative processes that culminate in a submitted artefact,  rendering  the  production  of  knowledge  and  its  representation  effectively opaque.  Paradoxically,  this  opacity,  often  framed  as  a  threat  to  academic  integrity, also illuminates a potential design principle: a reconceptualisation of assessment that privileges  process  over  product.  Rather  than continuing to evaluate only the final, ostensibly polished output, and thereby reproducing the invisibility of the learning journey,  assessment  might  instead  engage  with  the  generative  pathways  through which  artefacts  come  into  being.  Within  this  framing,  Black  Box  Assessment  is advanced as a structural response, predicated on the principle that the assessment of learning should seek to capture  windows  into the formative, iterative, and devel- opmental processes that have traditionally remained obscured within the metaphorical  black box  of student production. The idea of  windows  into the black box acknowl- edges that processes are rarely fully explainable (Bearman and Ajjawi 2023), but we can capture glimpses. Equally important is the need to focus on the holistic assess- ment  journey  as  a  relational  process,  where  how  things  work  together  is  more important than complete visibility (Bearman and Ajjawi  2023).
Traditional assessment designs not only obscure the processes of learning within a metaphorical  black box  but also systematically conceal the errors, diversions, and changes of direction that are intrinsic to authentic learning. Assessment criteria have conventionally  privileged  the  production  of  highly  polished  artefacts,  rewarding outcomes  that  appear  seamless  and  error-free.  Yet,  as  the  concept  of Black  Box Thinking  (Syed 2015)  illustrates,  the  deliberate  recovery  and  analysis  of  error  or failure  can  serve  as  a  powerful  mechanism  for  learning.  In  aviation,  for  instance, the  retrieval  of  the  flight  recorder  following  an  accident  enables  a  detailed 
reconstruction of the events and decisions that led to the incident, offering critical insights into how similar errors might be prevented. Analogously, in education, the assessment  of  the  processes  underlying  student  work  can  illuminate  the  cognitive and  developmental  pathways  that  give  rise  to  learning.  Errors,  revisions,  and  false starts  thus  constitute  valuable  evidence  of  growth  rather  than  deficiencies  to  be concealed. If learning is understood as a dynamic and iterative process, it is peda- gogically counterproductive to design assessment systems that mask the very traces of that process. In discussing different cultural attitudes to performance in mathe- matics, Syed (2015, p. 298) describes how in China and Japan, in contrast to many Western  cultures,  mistakes  are  held  up  not  as  evidence  of  a  fixed  inferiority,  or  as showing that you have  the wrong kind of brain , but as evidence of learning  (emphasis added). This, we argue, is the significant culture change that is needed in educational assessment. Errors, false starts, and changes of direction should neither be concealed nor simply disclosed; they should be recognised, evaluated, and credited alongside polished  final  products.  Valuing  process  in  assessment  does  not  necessarily  imply an exclusive focus on mistakes, yet we contend that normalising error and assessing process  are  fundamentally  intertwined.  Without  such  integration,  process-oriented assessment risks becoming performative, encouraging students to present a polished product  accompanied  by  a  retrospectively  curated  account  of  its  development.  By contrast,  genuinely  process-oriented  assessment  opens  the  possibility  of  awarding credit for cognitive and conceptual transformation, thereby positioning error not as 
a deficit to be penalised but as an essential indicator of learning and growth. Calling our assessment design model Black Box Assessment reflects two closely related ideas. First, learning processes are often concealed within a  black box , and bringing them into view by valuing evidence of the learning process offers a way to address this opacity. Second, drawing on Syed s (2015) concept of Black Box Thinking, we argue that process-based assessment should surface and value errors and changes in direc- tion, rather than focusing solely on polished representations of learning.
The  Black Box : mining the metaphor
Assessing process alongside product is not a radical proposition; it has already been identified  as  a  promising  response  to  the  challenges  posed  by  GenAI  (e.g.  Corbin et al. 2025c; Moorhouse et al. 2023). However, bringing a new lens to errors, failed approaches,  and  cognitive  change  in  assessment  is  a  heretofore  under-explored application of the Black Box analogy to educational assessment. Within this framing, Black  Box  Assessment  inverts  the  logic  of  traditional  assessment  by  privileging transparency  over  opacity.  In  aviation,  the  black  box  is  not  a  mechanism  of  sur- veillance or punishment but a repository for learning: an artefact that enables sys- temic reflection following error. Analogously, Black Box Assessment foregrounds the evidentiary and developmental dimensions of learning rather than its performative surface. Artefacts such as drafts, research logs, reflective commentaries, and records of  GenAI  interaction  constitute  educational  flight  data :  multimodal  traces  that authenticate  learning  without  invoking  punitive  oversight.  Integrity,  in  this  view, emerges through the visibility of cognitive and creative processes, not merely through the policing of outcomes. This approach also foregrounds learner agency: the capacity 
to  set  goals,  make  judgements,  and  adapt  through  reflection.  Such  an  emphasis repositions GenAI from an existential threat to a catalyst for epistemic participation. When  assessment  foregrounds  agency,  iteration,  and  reflexivity,  students  are  posi- tioned not as subjects of evaluation but as co-constructors of knowledge.
Traditional assessment practices, structured around high-stakes  one-shot  perfor - mances,  capture  learning  as  a  static  snapshot  severed  from  its  generative  context. Black  Box  Assessment  reconceptualises  learning  as  a  temporally  and  materially distributed trajectory, incorporating multiple interwoven streams of activity: exper- imentation, collaboration, revision, and error. In postdigital higher education envi - ronments  (Fawns  2023),  where  physical,  virtual,  and  algorithmic  modalities  are entangled, such an approach acknowledges the complex assemblages through which learning unfolds. Just as aviation safety advances through the systematic analysis of failure, meaningful learning depends upon surfacing and interrogating the productive role  of  error.  In  this  sense,  Black  Box  Assessment  nurtures  a  form  of  academic safety,  legitimising  and  valuing  intellectual  risk-taking  and  positioning  error  as  a constitutive element of learning rather than its negation.
How Black Box Assessment can shift the culture of assessment in education
Engaging with ideas of emergence, entanglement, and multimodality foregrounds an understanding  of  learning  as  postdigital  practice  that  troubles  the  logic  of  prede- termined and fixed assessment designs. This draws attention to the multiple acts of construction  that  lead  to  what  we  typically  identify  as  assessment  practice  (Boud et al. 2018).  In  essence,  Black  Box  Assessment  shifts  the  question  from Did  they write this? to What can we see about how they learned? and  How does this connect with  the  rest  of  their  learning  journey?.  The  discourse  of  assessment  frequently encourages students to put forward their most polished work for evaluation, and to hide errors or struggles. Because GenAI can produce sophisticated assessment prod- ucts, we believe that an important shift is needed: to value process over polish. In so doing, processual approaches to assessment have the potential to shift the culture of assessment in education.
Reframing mistakes as evidence of learning
Assessment has always been entangled with power, commonly understood as something  done to  students (Gravett, et al. 2024). Fear of failure often leads to the concealment of  mistakes  (Syed 2015),  and  contemporary  concerns  about  student  resilience  and mental  health  highlight  the  importance  of  creating  opportunities  for  the  experience of failure early in students  educational journeys (Rawle et al. 2026). However, such initiatives can be tokenistic if they are confined to low-stakes activities while high-stakes, credit-bearing  assessments  still  reward  only  polished  final  outcomes,  reinforcing one-shot, risk-averse learning (Feigenbaum 2021). Black Box Assessment reframes this tension by moving beyond failure as merely preparatory and instead valuing mistakes and  error  as  integral  components  of  learning  itself.  By  asking  students  to  submit drafts,  false  starts,  and  abandoned  ideas,  the  model  makes  error  a  visible  and legitimate part of the assessed journey rather than something to conceal. A student who demonstrates intellectual movement (e.g. I began with Argument A, but I realised it was too simplistic and shifted to Argument B) is rewarded for critical reflection and growth, not penalised for initial missteps. In this way, working with and responding to  error  becomes  an  outcome  in  its  own  right,  encouraging  greater  authenticity  in learning.  Assessment  is  also  constructed  as  a  more  open-ended,  relational,  practice that values the situated particularities of students  learning journeys with the potential to unsettle assessment as a practice of teacherly power (Gravett et al. 2024).
Engaging  with  error  can  yield  significant  learning  benefits.  For  instance,  the concept of failure-driven memory suggests that reflecting on the causes of mistakes develops the ability to avoid similar errors in the future (Tawfik and Jonassen 2013). Similarly, the notion of negative knowledge highlights that understanding what not to do can be as instructive as knowing the correct course of action (Gartmeier et al. 2011). Framing learning as a struggle also encourages students to embrace challenging tasks,  fostering  greater  willingness  to  engage with complex problems in the future (Brown et al. 2014).
However,  incorporating  evidence  of  error  or  re-routing  in  assessment  requires wider systemic change. The notion of  failure  is imbued with issues of power and privilege; not all students may have the same opportunity to experience and benefit from things not going to plan (Ross et al. 2022), and the impact of  failure  might  stick   to  some  students  in  stronger  ways  than  others  (Ahmed  2004).  A  broader cultural change is also needed because students are likely to notice if educators are promoting  generative failure , but the education system espouses failure as  stigma- tized  (Feigenbaum 2021, p. 14).
Valuing cognitive change not just cognitive competence
One  of  the  most  significant  potentialities  of  Black  Box  Assessment  is  its  capacity to make cognitive change more visible. Traditional assessments typically foreground polished  products,  erasing  the  missteps,  pivots,  and  evolving  understanding  that occur along the way. By embedding structured opportunities for reflection such as prompts asking  What did you believe at the start that you later changed your mind about, and why? or Which piece of feedback or new evidence most altered your think- ing?,  this  approach  turns  cognitive  change  into  a  valued  and  assessable  dimension of  learning.  It  recognises  flexibility,  openness,  and  critical  growth  as  markers  of academic strength rather than instability. This emphasis can also operate at a pro-gramme level, as argued by Lodge et al. (2023, 2025), enabling educators to examine not only how students change their thinking within a single task but how cognitive processes evolve as students progress through a course of study, providing a richer and more authentic picture of learning over time.
Normalising challenge and struggle as part of a generative learning process
When  rubrics  reward  critical  self-awareness  and  adaptive  thinking,  mistakes,  dead ends, and uncertainty become evidence of intellectual maturity rather than failure, and assessment values authentic learning rather than perfect outputs (Simm  2005). Process-oriented  assessment  encourages  reflection  on  reasoning,  iterative problem-solving,  and  metacognitive  development,  normalising  the  struggle   as  a driver  of  learning.  Practically,  this  can  be  achieved  through  Smith  and  Kennett s (2017) concept of  generative constraints : those technologies, resources, and activities that an educator orchestrates to guide a student s experience and that capture creative demonstrations of associated learning (Elkington and Irons  2025). Such designs shift focus  from  polished,  potentially  AI-generated  artefacts  to  the  learning  journey, supporting  reflective  documentation  of  GenAI  use,  including  prompts,  rationale, critiques, and ethical considerations, and embedding technology within structured, intentionally reflective learning contexts.
Building capacity for employability and lifelong learning
Processual  assessment  is  central  to  developing  skills  essential  for  lifelong  learning. Employers  increasingly  seek  graduates  who  can  adapt,  reflect,  and  learn  from  set- backs,  not  merely  produce  polished  outputs  (Scheuring  and  Thompson  2025). Embedding  these  dispositions  into  assessment  signals  that  reflection,  adaptability, and developmental growth are valued outcomes in their own right. By opening the  black box  of learning, assessment strengthens academic integrity, deepens learning, and  fosters  the  judgement  and  transparency  expected  in  professional  contexts. Critically,  engaging  in  systematic  analysis  of  one s  own  learning  processes  enables students  to  transfer  strategies  across  domains,  moving  beyond  fixed  procedures towards flexible, adaptive problem-solving.
Finally,  Black  Box  Assessment  also  supports  educator  learning.  By  attending  to students   formative  processes,  teachers  can  guide  and  assess  learning  in  real  time while  simultaneously  evaluating  and  refining  their  own  instructional  designs  in response to diverse learning contexts. This reflective stance is vital for sustaining a process-focused approach, one that prioritises the evolution of pedagogy alongside student  outcomes.  Having  established  the  rationale  for  emphasising  process  and cognitive change in assessment, we now turn to a discussion of how this approach can be implemented in practice.
Implementing Black Box Assessment
At its simplest, Black Box Assessment design requires four components: a task for students to work towards; one or more learning outcomes focused on process evi- dence;  the  submission  to  include  one  or  more  windows   into  the  Black  Box  (i.e. process  evidence);  and  one  or  more  elements  of  the  grading  criteria/rubric  that represent different levels of performance in demonstrating the relevant evidence of process (see Table 1).
There  are  potentially  infinite  ways  in  which  these  principles  can  be  applied, depending on the contextual nuances of the learning outcomes and the discipline. In this way, then, Black Box Assessment is intended to be a  conceptual  toolkit to guide thinking about assessment design in the GenAI era, not a  top down prescrip- tive  assessment  model   (Corbin  et al.  2025c,  p.  9). Many assessment tasks that lose their validity in the era of GenAI still hold value (see, for example, Corbin et al. 2025d). Black Box Assessment enables educators to keep  existing  tasks  if  they  wish,  instead  changing  the  way  the  task  is  assessed. Products remain important, but their evaluation is complemented by assessment of the  processes  used  to  create  them.  As  Simm  (2005)  notes,  this  requires  careful calibration of the weighting between process and product to ensure both are mean- ingfully  recognised.  It  could  also  be  possible  for  process  evidence  ( Black  Box Windows ) to serve the purpose of validating the authenticity of submitted products without being graded. Beyond existing tasks, the approach can also inspire creativity in  task  design.  Incorporating  processual  elements  may  necessitate  reframing  or adding learning outcomes (Corbin et al.  2025c). Evidence of process can be captured in multiple ways (see Online Supplementary Materials 1 for an indicative taxonomy of  Black  Box  windows ),  and  while  more  than  one  window  may  enhance  validity, two to three well-justified windows per task are generally sufficient to avoid over- whelming  students.  Selection  of  windows  should  align  with  authentic  disciplinary practices and ideally combine natural process evidence (e.g. draft histories, process screencasts) with reflective evidence (e.g. reflection on challenges and problem-solving strategies). Black Box Windows can be negotiated with students through co-creation, or  can  be  a  site  of  agency  and  choice  where  students  can  choose  from  a  set  of potential examples of process evidence which they wish to submit.
Existing grade descriptors or rubrics may also require revision to explicitly reward process transparency, iteration, and learning from mistakes (see Online Supplementary Materials 2 for examples across disciplines). All elements of Black Box Assessment should align with learning outcomes (see Figure 1), and the rationale for the process focus should be clearly communicated to students. This intentional design mitigates the  risk  that  producing  process  evidence  becomes  unproductive  busywork   (Motz et al. 2021) while supporting meaningful learning for both students and educators (Irons and Elkington  2021).
Black Box Assessment: facing the challenges
Large-scale assessment change is challenging, requiring educators to enter unfamiliar territory  and  question  entrenched  practices  (Elkington  et al.  2024).  However,  the

scale of the GenAI challenge, arguably the most significant disruption to educational assessment  since  the  COVID-19  pandemic,  makes  inaction  untenable.  In  the  fol- lowing  section,  we  address  potential  concerns  regarding  Black  Box  Assessment, offering counterpoints while recognising that no single approach will fit every context or discipline. Nevertheless, the core principle of surfacing process and valuing mis- takes  and  cognitive  development  can  be  adapted  and  nuanced  to  suit  diverse  dis- ciplinary contexts and levels of study.
 But this is going to increase marking workload
A  common  and  legitimate  concern  regarding  Black  Box  Assessment  is  the  potential increase in marking and feedback workload. Asking students to submit both a product and evidence of process may initially be perceived as requiring additional assessment without extra time or resources, a particularly pressing concern in already stretched higher education systems where assessment and feedback are major sources of workload pressure (e.g. Chan and Colloton 2024). However, Black Box Assessment is not intended to  increase  the  amount  of  work  being  evaluated,  but  to  make  learning  more  visible and,  in  some  cases,  simplify  evaluative  judgements  by  providing  richer  insight  into how students reach their outcomes. Clear, targeted process-focused learning outcomes, concise and structured process  windows , and streamlined rubrics can make the addi- tional evidence manageable. Staged submissions and peer feedback offer engagement points  that  support  learning  without  requiring  extensive  formal  assessment  at  every stage.  Some  windows,  such  as  brief  reflective  statements  linked  to  annotated  drafts, are quick to review yet provide powerful evidence of learning. Moreover, integrating process evidence can reduce the time spent decoding polished but opaque final prod- ucts, as teachers gain direct insight into students  reasoning and decision-making. In practice, reducing the length of final outputs to make room for process evidence (for example,  replacing  a  2,500-word  essay  with  a  1,500-word  essay  plus  1,000  words  of process evidence) can help minimise any increase in marking workload.
A process focus potentially allows for fewer, but more impactful, feedback com- ments,  replacing  the  extensive,  often  underutilised  feedback  typical  of  traditional product-focused  assessment  (Winstone  et al.  2017;  Winstone  and  Boud,  2022). Feedback targeting the process is generally more useful (Hattie and Clarke2019) and memorable  (Winstone  and  Nash  2024)  than  comments  focused  on  the  task  itself. However, it can be challenging to provide detailed process-focused comments when those processes are commonly hidden from view. Valuing error and re-routing within Black  Box  Assessment  can  further  enhance  feedback  utility,  as  guidance  following a  misstep  may  be  more  instructive  than  comments  on  the  final  product  alone (Feigenbaum  2021).  Ultimately,  Black  Box  Assessment  does  not  aim  to  increase workload,  but  to  change  how  assessment  time  is  used,  prioritising  evaluation  of learning processes alongside outcomes. However, as a  wicked problem , the effects of  assessment  changes  in  response  to  GenAI  are  difficult  to  predict.  We  therefore argue  that  Higher  Education  Institutions  should  closely  monitor  the  impact  on workload  across  teachers,  students,  and  professional  services  colleagues  wherever assessment designs are adapted.
 But this won t work in my discipline
A common concern is that Black Box Assessment may not suit all disciplines, par- ticularly those seen as highly technical or objective, which emphasise correct answers or final outputs over exploratory processes. However, this view underestimates the adaptability of the approach. Every discipline, whether STEM, humanities, arts, social sciences  or  professional  practice,  involves  a  set  of  underlying  processes,  decisions, and iterative steps that can be made visible and assessed.
In STEM fields, process evidence may include lab notebooks, code commits, error analyses,  or  debugging  logs,  which  naturally  document  reasoning  pathways,  false starts, and adjustments. In the arts and humanities, drafts, annotated bibliographies, and  reflective  journals  provide  insight  into  the  evolution  of  ideas,  interpretations, and arguments. Professional disciplines such as medicine, law, or education similarly involve iterative practice, with case notes, clinical reasoning logs, policy drafts, and design iterations mirroring real-world workflows. In essence, every human activity has a black box: the often invisible chain of cognitive, creative, and practical actions that  lead  to  an  outcome.  The  key  is  not  whether  a  discipline  can  accommodate process assessment, but which kinds of process evidence are most meaningful within that  discipline.  This  flexibility  means  that  Black  Box  Assessment  is  not  a  rigid template but a framework that can be tailored to align with disciplinary epistemol- ogies, practices, and professional expectations.
Far  from  diluting  disciplinary  authenticity,  embedding  process  assessment  can enhance  it.  By  foregrounding  the  thinking  and  iterative  practices  of  practitioners, Black  Box  Assessment  helps  students  develop  discipline-specific  capabilities  that reflect professional expectations. Valuing error analysis in engineering, for example, aligns  with  accountability  and  problem-solving  in  the  field,  while  attending  to evolving interpretations in literary studies mirrors scholarly work. In this way, Black Box  Assessment  bridges  academic  learning  and  professional  or  scholarly  practice, offering students transferable skills and a deeper understanding of how knowledge is generated in their discipline.
 But students can just use GenAI to write their process evidence
The  validity  of  Black  Box  Assessment  does  not  depend  on  perfectly  detecting  AI use,  but  on  thoughtful  assessment  design  (see Online  Supplementary  Materials  3). By creating tasks, outcomes, and evidence requirements that favour authentic, evolv- ing,  and  situated  learning  processes,  educators  can  reward  critical  thinking  and reflection  rather  than  scripted  narratives.  Structural  and  rubric  design  can  make outsourcing more difficult and less effective, while positioning potential engagement with GenAI as a transparent and assessable element of the learning process rather than  a  hidden  shortcut.  Although  this  approach  cannot  make  assessments  entirely AI-proof,  it  raises  the  threshold  such  that  genuine  engagement  becomes  the  most straightforward and rewarding option for students. In other words, we can ask: Does this  design  make  the  outsourcing  of  process  evidence  harder,  riskier,  and  more effortful than genuine engagement?
 But students will find this approach to assessment too different to what they are used to
A  further  concern  is  that  students  may  find  Black  Box  Assessment  unfamiliar  or disruptive compared to conventional task designs. Many students have been socialised into  an  assessment  culture  that  rewards  polished  outputs,  making  documentation and reflection on processes a potential source of uncertainty. With careful scaffolding (e.g. assessment dialogue, engagement with exemplars, formative feedback) aligned to flexible assessment task design (i.e. introducing negotiated learner choice) students can  develop  the  evaluative  judgement  (Tai  et al. 2018)  needed  to  succeed  in  this new  mode  of  assessment.  Embedding  flexibility  via  structured  formative  processes also  allows  for  personalised  learning  experiences,  supporting  accessibility  and  fos- tering  independent,  self-directed  learning  (e.g.  Elkington  and  Chesterton  2023; Hanesworth et al. 2019).
Although initially unfamiliar, Black Box Assessment mirrors the iterative, trans- parent, and adaptive practices that students will encounter in professional environ - ments.  By  developing  skills  in  reflection,  problem-solving,  and  process  adaptation, the approach helps students understand how they can reinterpret formal assessment into  situated  practice.  Introducing  these  expectations  within  degree  programmes equips  students  with  capabilities  that  enhance  both  academic  success  and  lifelong learning.
Conclusion
The emergence of GenAI as an agent within the educational ecosystem represents both a disruptive and transformative force. Recent scholarship frames the GenAI— assessment challenge as a  wicked problem : one without a single solution, entangled in broader systemic complexities, and requiring ongoing negotiation and compromise (Corbin et al. 2025b). As Bearman and Ajjawi (2023) note, this perspective situates AI  as  one  element  within  a  broader  sociotechnical  ensemble,  where  meaningful assessment  design  involves  students  generating  and  interpreting  knowledge  about themselves and their evolving contexts.
We have proposed Black Box Assessment as a structural, flexible, and processual response to the validity challenges posed by GenAI. Rather than offering a definitive solution to a wicked problem, it provides a blueprint for iterative design, one that values  transparency,  experimentation,  and  adaptation.  In  this  model,  assessment moves  beyond  documenting  how  products  are  created  to  genuinely  valuing  error, redirection,  and  cognitive  change,  reflecting  the  ethos  of  Black  Box  Thinking (Syed 2015).
Framing GenAI-assessment as a wicked problem legitimises iteration, compromise, and divergence (Corbin et al. 2025b), not only in student learning but in assessment design  itself.  The  same  willingness  to  analyse  errors,  surface  changes  of  direction, and  refine  practices  that  underpins  Black  Box  Assessment  is  equally  crucial  for educators confronting the GenAI challenge. In this sense, Black Box Thinking (Syed 2015) offers both a philosophy for assessing learning and a methodological stance for  evolving  assessment  systems  through  reflective,  data-informed,  and  adaptive design cycles.
