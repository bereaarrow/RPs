ABSTRACT
Generative AI is rapidly entering communication education and reshaping how students create video-based messages, yet evi - dence remains limited on how AI adoption relates to learning motivation, career confidence and creativity in communication programs. To fill this void, this mixed-methods study draws on the UTAUT and Self-Determination Theory to frame regression models. It analyzes survey data from 768 undergraduates at three Chinese universities and further supplements the quantitative findings with thematic analysis of 20 semi-structured interviews. Findings show that higher generative AI adoption is positively associated with learning motivation (β = 0.723, p < 0.001) and career confidence (β = 0.849, p < 0.001); interview data further indi - cate a creativity tension, whereby AI supports efficiency and experimentation while also prompting concerns about standardisa - tion, diminished originality and reliance on AI outputs. This study advances research on technology adoption in communication education by highlighting students' dual experiences of empowerment and constraint, and it suggests that AI integration in video creation should combine tool training with structured evaluation and reflective practices to sustain creativity while supporting career development.

1   |   Introduction
The  rapid  advancement  of  generative  artificial  intelligence (AI) has transformed communication education and media production.  AI-powered  tools  are  increasingly  utilised  in video  creation,  automated  content  generation  and  mul - timedia  storytelling,  enhancing  both  efficiency  and  cre - ative  possibilities  for  communication  students  (Cools  and Diakopoulos 2024; Franganillo 2023; Wang 2026). As AI be- comes integral to digital newsrooms, communication educa - tion must adapt to prepare students for AI-assisted content production (Gomez-Diago 2022). Prior research has focused primarily  on  AI's  technical  functions,  while  less  attention has been paid to its influence on students' learning motiva - tion, career confidence and creativity (Dahri et al. 2024). A 
key driver of communication students' AI adoption is career necessity. UTAUT posits that adoption is shaped by perfor - mance expectancy, social influence and enabling conditions (Venkatesh et al. 2003). Students may adopt AI tools as they perceive them to be indispensable for career success (Dai and Wang 2023; Wang and Gao 2026; Wang and Guo 2026). Recent reports  from  media  organisations  and  journalism  schools indicate that AI literacy is now a key requirement for entry- level positions in digital newsrooms, with over 70% of major media  outlets  incorporating  generative  AI  tools  into  their production workflows (Cools and Diakopoulos 2024; Feitosa et al. 2024).  This  growing  industry  demand  reinforces  the perception  among  students  that  AI  proficiency  is  essential for employability. Self-Determination Theory (SDT) suggests that learners'motivation is driven by their need for autonomy,

If AI tools enhance students' sense of com - petence and control over their creative work, they are more likely to be motivated to integrate AI into their professional skillset (Neufeld et al. 2023; Park and Derakhshan 2026; Wu et al. 2025; Wu and Wang 2025; Yang et al. 2025). 2   |   Literature Review
2.1 |   Theoretical Framework: UTAUT and Self-Determination Theory
The  Unified  Theory  of  Acceptance  and  Use  of  Technology (UTAUT)  provides  a  well-established  framework  for  under - standing  how  individuals  adopt  and  use  new  technologies. According to UTAUT, technology adoption is influenced by per - formance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence and facilitating conditions (Venkatesh etal.2003). Communication students may be particularly motivated to use AI tools if they believe that these tools enhance their professional competencies and are widely accepted in the media industry. Studies suggest that career-driven necessity plays a crucial role in technology adoption in communication education, as students are increas - ingly aware of AI's relevance to future employment (Dai and Wang 2023; Liu and Wang2024; Feitosa etal.2024; Wang etal. 2024; Wu etal.2024).
However, AI adoption is not purely a rational decision-making process—it is also shaped by students' intrinsic motivation and psychological needs. Given that journalism and related communication  fields  is  not  merely  a  technical  discipline but also a creative and investigative field, understanding the 
motivational dimension of AI adoption is equally important. Therefore, this study incorporates Self-Determination Theory (SDT) as a complementary framework. SDT posits that moti - vation is driven by the fulfilment of autonomy, competence and relatedness (Deci and Ryan 1985; Ryan and Deci 2000). If AI tools foster a sense of competence (i.e., improved tech - nical skills) and autonomy, students may be more likely to en - gage with them. Conversely, if AI diminishes students' sense of authorship, it may reduce their intrinsic motivation and hinder  their  willingness  to  experiment  creatively  (Neufeld 
et al. 2023). Although UTAUT and SDT originate from differ - ent paradigms—technology acceptance versus psychological motivation—their  combination  enables  a  dual  explanatory mechanism:  UTAUT  captures  how  external  structures  and career-oriented incentives prompt AI use, while SDT uncov - ers how such use affects internal states such as autonomy and competence. By considering both external adoption and inter - nal motivation, this framework provides a more comprehen - sive understanding of communication students' engagement with generative AI.
2.2 |   AI Adoption in Communication Education
The  integration  of  AI  in  communication  education  has gained  significant  attention  in  recent  years.  Studies  have explored AI's role in content creation, automated reporting and  fact-checking,  revealing  both  benefits  and  challenges (Derakhshan  and  Park 2026a,  2026b;  Gomez-Diago 2022; Kaminskaia and Erokhina 2023; Li and Wang 2026; Wang et al. 2026). AI-driven newswriting tools such as automated transcription,  text  summarisation  and  machine-generated news  reports  have  demonstrated  their  potential  to  reduce workload and enhance efficiency for communication students (Cools and Diakopoulos 2024). However, concerns have been raised  regarding  AI's  potential  impact  on  editorial  control and  journalistic  integrity.  Lopezosa  et al. (2023)  examined how  AI-generated  news  content  is  being  incorporated  into communication-related  curricula  and  found  that  students struggled to balance AI assistance with their own editorial judgement. Similarly, Quinonez and Meij (2024) investigated AI  adoption  at  Bloomberg  and  highlighted  concerns  about news automation reducing human oversight and increasing reliance  on  algorithmic  content  selection.  These  findings suggest that while AI can improve productivity, it also raises ethical questions about journalistic independence and the po - tential deskilling of future journalists. In terms of students' attitudes toward AI adoption, research has revealed mixed perspectives. Duckett and Westrick (2025) found that many communication students appreciate AI for its ability to stream - line production tasks, yet some express concerns regarding job security and professional credibility. Similarly, Cools and Diakopoulos (2024) noted that while AI adoption is increas - ingly common in newsrooms, scepticism remains regarding its potential to replace human journalists (Bakonyi et al. 2024). This  divergence  in  perceptions  highlights  the  importance 
of studying how AI affects students' career confidence and long-term  professional  aspirations.  AI's  role  in  enhancing journalistic creativity remains a topic of debate. Some schol - ars argue that AI democratises creative production, allowing students to experiment with AI-assisted visuals, automated

2.3 |   The Psychological and Social Dimensions of AI Use
Beyond technical skills, students' attitudes toward AI tools are  also  shaped  by  psychological  and  social  factors.  The classroom social climate, including peer influence, educator attitudes and institutional policies, plays a critical role in AI adoption (Derakhshan et al. 2022; Lin and Wang 2025; Wang and Kruk 2024). Peer collaboration—such as sharing AI tools, co-editing  scripts,  or  exchanging  prompts—can  normalise experimentation  and  lower  the  perceived  barriers  to  using AI  in  creative  work.  Educators  also  significantly  influence students' perceptions: if instructors actively promote AI as a valuable career asset, students are more likely to embrace it 
as a professional tool (Feitosa et al. 2024). Conversely, a lack of structured AI training may lead to student resistance and uncertainty, particularly among those unfamiliar with AI ap - plications (Micaletto-Belda and Serrano 2024). Beyond class- room  interactions,  institutional  support  shapes  the  broader ecosystem of AI integration. Universities that embed AI tools into assessments, offer licensed platforms, or include AI eth - ics  in  coursework  may  encourage  more  proactive  engage - ment. However, students' career confidence is also affected by  broader  concerns  about  automation.  On  the  one  hand, 
AI can empower students by expanding their technical ca - pabilities; on the other, it may raise concerns about job dis - placement in the journalism industry (Kang and Lou 2022). Wen et al. (2025) found that students who frequently use AI tools report higher perceived employability, yet also express greater anxiety about AI's long-term impact on careers. These findings suggest the need for a socially supportive, pedagog - ically structured and critically reflective environment for AI adoption.  Despite  the  growing  literature  on  AI  in  commu - nication education, existing studies have largely focused on technical adoption and skill development, often overlooking the nuanced roles of peer networks, instructor messaging and institutional  frameworks  in  shaping  students'  attitudes.  To address these gaps, this study examines how career necessity influences communication students' adoption of generative AI tools and how AI use affects their learning motivation, career confidence and creativity. Specifically, the research aims to answer the following questions:
RQ1.  How  does  career  necessity  influence  communication students' adoption of generative AI tools?
RQ2.  To what extent does AI adoption associate with commu - nication students' learning motivation, career confidence and creativity?
RQ3.  How does AI-assisted video and content production in - fluence communication students' professional skill development and career perceptions?
Given the mixed-methods design, the research questions provide the broader inquiry framework, while the quantitative strand further  tests  the  following  hypotheses  derived  from  UTAUT and SDT:
H1.  Career necessity is positively associated with communica- tion students' adoption of generative AI tools.
H2.  Greater adoption of generative AI tools is positively associ- ated with communication students' learning motivation.
H3.  Greater adoption of generative AI tools is positively associ- ated with communication students' career confidence.
H4.  Greater  adoption  of  generative  AI  tools  is  positively  as- sociated  with  communication  students'  perceived  creativity enhancement.
Together, these hypotheses specify the directional relationships examined  in  the  survey  analysis,  whereas  RQ3  is  addressed primarily through the qualitative interview data to capture stu - dents' nuanced experiences and concerns regarding AI-assisted content production.
3   |   Method
3.1 |   Participants
This study involved 768 undergraduate communication stu - dents  from  three  randomly  selected  universities  in  Henan Province,  China.  Participants  were  recruited  via  voluntary self-selection. Eligible participants were those who had prior experience using generative AI tools in video creation, con - tent  production,  or  multimedia  storytelling  tasks.  The  sur - vey invitation clearly stated the eligibility criteria, including prior experience using generative AI tools. These categories were based on students' self-reported levels of usage and fa - miliarity  with  generative  AI  tools,  with  frequent  users  re - ferring  to  those  who  used  such  tools  regularly  in  learning or  content-production  tasks,  occasional  users  referring  to those who used them intermittently, and beginner users re - ferring to those with limited prior experience. While volun - tary,  recruitment  targeted  communication-related  networks 
to ensure relevance. The total response rate from the initial distribution (1180 invitations sent) was approximately 71.2%, and the final valid sample comprised 768 students after data cleaning. The sample ensured diversity across academic years, AI adoption levels and career orientations, providing a com - prehensive overview of communication students' engagement with AI in their educational and creative processes. 3.2 |   Research Design
This  study  employed  a  mixed-methods  design  (Creswell  and Clark 2007), integrating quantitative survey data with qualita - tive thematic analysis. The survey component allowed for the measurement of trends and correlations between AI adoption, career necessity and learning motivation, while the interview component provided rich, in-depth insights into students' sub - jective  experiences.  This  approach  ensured  a  comprehensive understanding of how AI tools are reshaping communication education  by  combining  broad-scale  statistical  analysis  with personal narratives. The study was theoretically grounded in the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (Venkatesh et al. 2003) and Self-Determination Theory (SDT) (Deci and Ryan  2000). UTAUT was used to assess how perfor - mance expectancy, social influence and facilitating conditions drive AI adoption, while SDT provided a framework for ana - lyzing how AI impacts students' intrinsic motivation, sense of competence and career aspirations. By integrating these frame - works, the study examined not only why students adopt AI but also how it affects their professional development and creative autonomy.
3.3 |   Instruments
To  investigate  communication  students'  AI  adoption  be - haviours, career motivation and perceptions of AI's impact on creativity and career confidence, this study employed a structured self-reported survey alongside semi-structured in - terviews. The survey was designed based on validated con - structs derived from the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) and Self-Determination Theory (SDT). Specifically, the UTAUT-informed component drew on the original framework developed by Venkatesh et al. (2003), particularly the dimensions of performance expectancy, social 
influence and facilitating conditions, which are widely used to  explain  technology  adoption.  The  SDT-informed  com - ponent was grounded in the foundational work of Deci and Ryan (1985) and Ryan and Deci (2000), with particular atten - tion to competence and autonomy as key drivers of students' learning motivation and engagement. These theoretical con - structs were contextually adapted to examine communication students' adoption of generative AI tools and their perceptions of AI's influence on creativity, motivation and career confi - dence.  The  interview  component  provided  qualitative  in - sights to complement the survey findings, allowing a deeper understanding  of  students'  subjective  experiences  with  AI- driven tools.
3.3.1 |   Self-Reported Survey
The questionnaire was developed to measure four key dimen - sions:  career-driven  necessity,  AI  adoption  behaviours,  per - ceived impact on creativity and learning motivation and career confidence and job security concerns. The survey items were adapted from validated constructs associated with UTAUT and SDT, rather than copied verbatim from a single pre-existing in- strument. Specifically, the UTAUT-related items were informed by Venkatesh etal.'s(2003) original model of technology accep - tance, especially the dimensions of performance expectancy, so - cial influence and facilitating conditions. In the present study, these constructs were operationalised in relation to students' perceptions that generative AI is useful, professionally necessary and increasingly normalised within communication education and future media work. The SDT-related items were informed by the foundational work of Deci and Ryan(1985) and Ryan and Deci (2000), particularly the motivational dimensions of com - petence and autonomy. In this study, these dimensions were adapted to assess whether using generative AI tools enhanced students' sense of capability, creative control and engagement in communication-related learning tasks. Because the study fo - cused on generative AI in communication education, all items were contextually reworded to fit students' experiences in AI- assisted content production, storytelling and career preparation. Each item was rated on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly dis - agree, 5 = strongly agree), allowing for a nuanced measurement of students' attitudes toward AI adoption. To ensure linguistic clarity and accessibility for participants, the original survey was developed in English and then translated into Chinese follow - ing a back-translation procedure (Brislin 1986). Two bilingual 
experts independently translated the survey into Chinese, and a third expert conducted a reverse translation to English to verify semantic equivalence. Discrepancies were resolved through dis - cussion until full agreement was reached. A pilot test involving 30 communication students was conducted prior to full distribu - tion. Feedback from the pilot study led to minor modifications in item phrasing to enhance clarity and comprehension. Feedback from the pilot study led to several targeted modifications in item phrasing.
The  internal  reliability  of  the  survey  was  assessed  using Cronbach's  Alpha,  which  demonstrated  strong  internal  con - sistency across all dimensions. The Cronbach's Alpha values for  each  construct  were  as  follows:  career-driven  necessity (α = 0.82), AI adoption behaviours (α = 0.88), creativity and mo - tivation (α = 0.85) and career confidence and job security con - cerns (α = 0.79). Given that all values exceeded the commonly accepted threshold of 0.70, the scales were deemed reliable for measuring students' attitudes toward AI adoption.
3.3.2 |   Semi-Structured Interviews
In addition to the survey, semi-structured interviews were con - ducted to provide a deeper qualitative understanding of commu - nication students' perceptions of AI adoption and its impact on their learning motivation, creativity and career confidence. The interview component was designed to complement the quan - titative findings, allowing for a more nuanced exploration of the underlying motivations and concerns that shape students' engagement with AI tools. By adopting a semi-structured ap - proach,  the  study  ensured  consistency  in  thematic  coverage while allowing participants the flexibility to elaborate on their personal experiences. The interview questions were structured around three core themes: students' motivations for AI adoption, AI's impact on their creativity and learning engagement, and concerns  regarding  AI's  influence  on  communication-related careers  and  professional  ethics.  These  themes  were  selected to align with the research questions and provide insights into both the benefits and potential drawbacks of AI integration in communication education. Representative interview questions included: ‘How has the use of generative AI influenced your mo - tivation to learn communication-related skills?’, ‘In what ways, if any, has AI affected your creativity in content production or storytelling?’ and ‘How has your experience with AI shaped your confidence about future career development in the com - munication field?’ To ensure clarity and appropriateness, the interview protocol underwent a pilot test with two communi - cation students, who provided feedback on question clarity and relevance. Based on this feedback, minor modifications were made to improve question phrasing and enhance comprehensi - bility. The final interview guide was structured to facilitate in- depth discussions while maintaining coherence with the study's research objectives.
3.4 |   Data Collection Procedure
To ensure a comprehensive understanding of communication students' engagement with AI tools, a structured data collec - tion process was implemented, incorporating both quantitative 
surveys  and  qualitative  interviews.  This  mixed-methods  ap - proach allowed for the triangulation of data, ensuring method - ological rigour and the credibility of findings. The self-reported survey was administered online through university networks, media-related  forums  and  academic  platforms.  Participation was entirely voluntary, and all respondents provided informed consent before completing the survey. The survey distribution targeted communication students from multiple universities in China, ensuring a diverse representation of AI adoption expe - riences and career aspirations. To minimise response bias, no incentives were provided, and participants were informed that their responses would remain anonymous and be used solely for research purposes. A total of 768 valid responses were obtained after a rigorous data cleaning process. The initial dataset con - sisted of a larger number of responses, but to enhance data qual - ity and analytical rigour, several exclusion criteria were applied. Specifically, surveys were removed if they met the following conditions: Surveys with more than 10% missing responses were removed, and straight-lining responses, where participants se - lected the same option for all Likert-scale items, were excluded. Additionally, responses containing inconsistent answers (e.g., contradictory  responses  to  logically  related  questions)  were flagged and discarded. After applying these criteria, 768 valid responses remained in the final dataset.
For the qualitative phase, semi-structured interviews were con - ducted to gain deeper insights into students' perceptions of AI's impact on their learning, creativity and career confidence. A total of 20 students were selected from the survey pool using a maximum variation sampling strategy. In addition to AI adop - tion level (frequent, occasional, beginner), we considered factors such as gender, academic year and contrasting survey responses regarding creativity and career confidence. Participants were categorised  into  three  groups:  frequent  AI  users,  occasional users and beginners. This purposeful sampling strategy ensured 
a balanced representation of students with varying levels of AI engagement Curtis et al. (2000). The interviews were conducted online via WeChat, providing flexibility in scheduling and ac - cessibility for participants from different universities. Each in - terview lasted approximately 40 min and followed a structured protocol covering three key themes: motivations for AI adoption, the impact of AI on creativity and learning, and concerns re - garding professional and ethical implications. To accommodate participant  preferences,  interviews  were  conducted  in  either English or Chinese. For responses in Chinese, transcripts were translated and cross-checked by two independent researchers 
to  ensure  semantic  accuracy  and  consistency.  All  interviews were audio-recorded, transcribed verbatim, and analysed using MAXQDA, a qualitative data analysis software that facilitated systematic coding and theme identification. By ensuring a struc - tured and rigorous approach to qualitative analysis, MAXQDA contributed to enhancing the credibility of the study's findings. (Corbin and Strauss2014).
Ethical considerations were rigorously upheld throughout the data  collection  process.  Ethical  approval  was  obtained  from the institutional review board (IRB) at the affiliated university. Participants were fully informed of their rights, including the right to withdraw from the study at any time. To ensure con - fidentiality, all responses were anonymised, and no personally identifiable  information  was  stored.  Digital  data,  including

3.5 |   Data Analysis and Ensuring Trustworthiness
This  study  employed  a  mixed-methods  design.  Quantitative and qualitative data were collected and analysed in two distinct phases. First, the quantitative results from the survey identified core patterns in AI adoption, learning motivation and career con - fidence. These results then informed the development of the inter - view protocol and the purposeful selection of participants. During the qualitative phase, interview themes were analysed to elabo - rate on and interpret the statistical findings. For instance, while the survey showed strong positive correlations between AI use and perceived creativity, the interview narratives revealed a more nuanced ‘creativity paradox,’ with students expressing simultane - ous excitement and concern. This sequential integration strategy allowed the qualitative data to explain and enrich the quantitative results, achieving interpretive depth. The quantitative survey data were analysed using SPSS 27.0, while qualitative interview data were processed through MAXQDA to identify emerging themes.
3.5.1 |   Quantitative Analysis
The  quantitative  analysis  involved  several  statistical  tech - niques to examine the relationships between key study vari - ables. Descriptive statistics were first computed to summarise AI adoption levels, career motivations and perceptions of AI's impact on creativity. Measures of central tendency (mean, me - dian)  and  dispersion  (standard  deviation)  were  calculated  to provide a general overview of participants' responses. To test H1–H4, a series of regression analyses were conducted to ex - amine the relationships between career necessity, AI adoption, learning motivation, career confidence and creativity enhance - ment. Additionally, Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) was per - formed to assess the construct validity of the survey instrument. Principal component analysis with varimax rotation was used 
to extract latent factors underlying AI adoption, creativity and career concerns. Multicollinearity was assessed using Variance Inflation Factors (VIF), and all key predictors yielded VIF values below 3.5, indicating acceptable independence among variables. Detailed statistical results are presented in the Results section.
3.5.2 |   Qualitative Analysis
The qualitative data from 20 semi-structured interviews were analysed using thematic analysis (Braun and Clarke 2006) to identify key patterns related to students' experiences with AI in communication education. To ensure transparency and analyt - ical rigour, the thematic analysis followed a structured three- stage approach: open coding, axial coding and selective coding.
In the open coding stage, interview transcripts were carefully reviewed to identify recurring patterns and key concepts within students' narratives. This phase involved line-by-line coding, where each segment of the transcript was labelled based on its meaning and relevance to the research questions. Initial codes 
emerged from the data, reflecting students' experiences, con - cerns and perspectives on AI adoption. During the axial coding stage, the initial codes were categorised into broader themes by identifying relationships between different codes. At this stage, patterns began to emerge, revealing contrasts in students' expe - riences with AI. Codes were systematically grouped under over - arching categories, resulting in the formation of preliminary themes. In the selective coding stage, the final themes were further refined and linked to the study's theoretical framework (UTAUT and SDT) to provide deeper interpretative insights. For example, student statements such as ‘AI helps me finish ed - iting faster’ and ‘It saves me hours on subtitles’ were initially coded under the open code ‘time-saving functions.’ These were then grouped into the axial theme ‘efficiency gains,’ which ulti - mately contributed to the selective theme of ‘AI as productivity enhancer.’ This process reflects how lower-level codes were con - ceptually consolidated through constant comparison and itera - tion. To ensure coding reliability, two independent researchers coded 25% of the interview transcripts, and Cohen's Kappa was computed to assess intercoder agreement. The resulting κ = 0.87 indicated strong agreement, suggesting high consistency in the coding framework. Any discrepancies were discussed and re - solved through consensus, ensuring a robust analytical process.
To enhance the trustworthiness and validity of both the quantita - tive and qualitative analyses, multiple strategies were employed. Triangulation was conducted by cross-validating findings from the survey and interviews, ensuring that interpretations were supported by multiple data sources. Member checking was also performed, where interview participants reviewed their tran - scripts and initial thematic interpretations to verify accuracy and provide clarifications where necessary. Additionally, data saturation was monitored during the qualitative analysis, and no new themes emerged after analysing the final interviews. This ensured that the thematic framework comprehensively captured students' perspectives on AI adoption, motivation and creativity.

The KMO measure of 0.89 confirms that the dataset was highly suitable for factor analysis, exceeding the recommended min - imum value of 0.60. Similarly, Bartlett's Test of Sphericity was significant (χ2 = 1357.8,  p < 0.001), indicating that the correla - tion matrix was adequate for factor extraction. These results provide preliminary support for the construct validity of the instrument,  suggesting  that  the  questionnaire  captures  AI adoption behaviours, career confidence and creativity-related perceptions  as  distinguishable  but  related  dimensions.  Given the exploratory nature of the present study and the contextual adaptation of items, future research may further examine the measurement structure through confirmatory factor analysis.

5   |   Discussion
The findings of this study provide critical insights into the role of generative AI in communication education, particu - larly its effects on learning motivation, career confidence and creativity.  By  integrating  Self-Determination  Theory  (SDT) (Deci and Ryan  2000) and the Unified Theory of Acceptance and  Use  of  Technology  (UTAUT)  (Venkatesh  et al. 2003), this study advances our understanding of how communica - tion  students  perceive  and  engage  with  AI-driven  content creation. While prior research has primarily focused on the technical affordances of AI in journalism and its role in auto - mating routine reporting tasks (Cools and Diakopoulos 2024; Franganillo 2023; Lopezosa et al. 2023), this study shifts the focus toward students' psycho-affective responses—an area that remains underexplored (Dahri et al. 2024). The results suggest that AI integration enhances students' career confi - dence and creative engagement, but at the same time, it raises concerns about originality, editorial agency and professional preparedness. These findings align with broader discussions 
in media studies regarding AI's dual role as both an enabler and  disruptor  of  journalistic  practice  (Guerrero-Sole  and Ballester 2023; Duckett and Westrick 2025).
5.1 |   AI Adoption as a Career Necessity
One of the most compelling findings of this study is that com - munication students perceive AI adoption not as an option but as a career necessity. This is consistent with UTAUT's 
framework,  which  posits  that  performance  expectancy  and social  influence  are  key  predictors  of  technology  adoption (Venkatesh  et al. 2003).  As  journalism  increasingly  relies on  AI-powered  video  editing,  automated  transcription  and generative content tools (Feitosa et al. 2024; Quinonez and Meij 2024), students recognise that AI literacy is essential for employability. This is particularly evident in the quantitative results, where AI adoption frequency strongly predicted ca - reer confidence ( β = 0.849,  p < 0.001). Similar patterns have been observed in education and public relations fields, where AI skills are increasingly regarded as fundamental compe - tencies rather than supplementary assets (Dahri et al. 2024; Micaletto-Belda and Serrano 2024).
However, while AI integration enhances technical proficiency and workplace readiness, students also voiced concerns about automation-induced  job  insecurity.  This  reflects  ongoing  de - bates in journalism and AI ethics regarding whether AI will aug - ment or replace human journalists (Duckett and Westrick2025; Moravec et al. 2024). While some students felt that AI could elevate journalistic practice by reducing mundane tasks, oth - ers worried that news automation could erode professional au - tonomy and weaken traditional storytelling skills—a concern echoed in prior studies on AI's disruptive role in creative indus - tries (Mao etal.2024; Stanton and Johnson2024). Thus, while 
AI adoption is seen as professionally indispensable, it also intro - duces anxieties about over-reliance and the shifting nature of journalistic labor.
5.2 |   AI and Learning Motivation
From  a  motivational  standpoint,  the  results  provide  strong empirical support for Self-Determination Theory (SDT) (Deci and Ryan 1985, 2000). Students who actively engaged with AI tools reported higher levels of perceived competence, aligning with SDT's assertion that mastery experiences drive intrinsic motivation (Ryan and Deci 2022). This finding is consistent with research suggesting that AI tools, when designed as as - sistive rather than prescriptive technologies, can foster greater autonomy in creative production (Glick et al. 2024). The pos- itive impact of AI on creativity ( β = 0.927, p < 0.001) indicates that AI functions as a catalyst for ideation and workflow op - timisation, enabling students to experiment with multimodal storytelling, AI-assisted video editing and personalised con - tent curation. However, not all students experienced AI as a motivation-enhancing tool. The qualitative findings suggest that some students felt disempowered by AI, expressing con - cerns about its tendency to generate standardised content and reduce critical engagement. This aligns with the concept of cognitive offloading (Moravec et al. 2024), wherein excessive reliance on AI can lead to diminished active learning and critical thinking skills. Prior studies have highlighted similar concerns in other domains of education, where AI-enhanced learning environments risk reducing students' sense of own - ership over their creative output (Wen et al. 2025; Levy-Nadav et al. 2024). Thus, while AI fosters a sense of competence for some, it may also diminish intrinsic motivation for those who perceive  it  as  undermining  their  editorial  decision-making and originality.

5.3 |   The Creativity Paradox: Expanding vs. Constraining Creative Autonomy
The role of AI in journalistic creativity remains paradoxical—ex - panding possibilities for some while constraining originality for others. While the quantitative results indicate that AI positively correlates with creativity (M = 4.31, SD = 0.67), qualitative nar - ratives suggest that students hold divergent perspectives on AI's creative affordances. Some viewed AI as a tool that enables rapid prototyping, multimedia enhancement and data-driven story - telling (Guerrero-Sole and Ballester 2023), whereas others feared that AI-generated content risks becoming formulaic. This aligns with prior studies emphasising that AI democratises access to so - phisticated media tools but also introduces homogeneity in cre - ative production (Mao et al. 2024; Gallagher et al. 2024). Notably, 10 out of 20 interview participants raised concerns about AI- generated outputs becoming formulaic or overly standardised. These students described content as ‘too similar,’ ‘lacking per - sonal touch,’ or ‘flattened in style,’ especially when relying on templates. This pattern reflects a minority but meaningful con - cern and aligns with the broader creativity paradox identified in the quantitative and qualitative data. This echoes findings from AI studies in media and public relations, where researchers have warned that AI-driven automation may reduce human oversight, leading to ethically ambiguous or creatively uninspired outputs (Lopezosa et al. 2023; Kaminskaia and Erokhina 2023). These concerns suggest that while AI can augment creative workflows, 
it should be carefully integrated to ensure it remains an enabler rather than a substitute for originality. This divergence in student experiences may be partly explained by differences in AI usage frequency. Frequent users often reported using generative AI to test creative boundaries—such as experimenting with narrative tone or multimodal formats—indicating a sense of agency and exploration. In contrast, beginners tended to express concerns about over-reliance on AI or the loss of originality, suggesting lower confidence and reduced perceived control. This pattern is consistent with Self-Determination Theory, which emphasises the importance of competence and autonomy in fostering intrin - sic creativity. It also aligns with UTAUT's concept of facilitating conditions, as frequent users may have greater access to support 
or prior exposure.
5.4 |   Implications for Communication Education and Future Research
While UTAUT and SDT were used to frame distinct aspects of this  study—technology  acceptance  and  learning  motivation, respectively—the findings suggest potential interrelations be - tween the two. For instance, students who adopted AI tools often reported feeling more efficient and capable, which may re - flect enhanced perceived competence—a key SDT component. This  suggests  a  possible  mediating  pathway,  where  external facilitating conditions (UTAUT) contribute to internal psycho - logical need satisfaction (SDT), thereby enhancing motivation. Conversely, intrinsically motivated students may be more in - clined to engage with AI tools when institutional support and perceived usefulness are present, indicating a potential moder - ating effect. Although our study did not formally test these path - ways, they offer a promising direction for future research using SEM or conditional process analysis.
The findings of this study hold significant implications for com - munication education, particularly regarding the integration of AI literacy, ethical considerations and the development of hybrid AI-human workflows. As generative AI becomes increasingly embedded in journalism practices, it is imperative that commu - nication programs evolve to provide students with structured AI literacy training. This training is expected to extend beyond technical proficiency to encompass critical skills in AI ethics, bias  mitigation,  editorial  accountability  and  media  integrity (Wen et al. 2025). Without a well-rounded AI education, stu - dents may risk becoming overly reliant on automation, poten - tially compromising investigative rigor, editorial independence and journalistic authenticity. A crucial implication of this study 
is the need for a balanced approach that fosters both AI techni - cal fluency and critical thinking in communication education. While AI-powered tools significantly enhance efficiency, cre - ativity and multimedia storytelling, they also pose risks related to over-standardisation of content, potential job displacement and diminished critical engagement. Prior studies have empha - sised that while AI assists with routine tasks such as transcrip - tion, summarisation and automated reporting, its role in more nuanced  editorial  decision-making  remains  contested  (Mao etal.2024; Kaminskaia and Erokhina 2023). Educators should therefore design curricula that integrate AI literacy while re - inforcing  human  editorial  judgment,  ensuring  that  students learn to critically assess and collaborate with AI tools. A hybrid AI-human workflow model—where AI facilitates but does not dictate  content  production—can  ensure  that  students  retain agency over storytelling while benefiting from AI's efficiency. Moreover, this study highlights the growing need for AI ethics and critical AI literacy programs in communication education. The findings indicate that while students are becoming increas - ingly proficient in AI-driven content creation, they lack formal training in AI accountability and bias detection. This echoes concerns raised by Wen et al. (2025), who argue that students who receive structured education on AI's ethical and legal ram - ifications are better equipped to navigate AI's role in journalism responsibly. Communication programs should, therefore, incor - porate AI ethics modules that address algorithmic bias, misin - formation risks and ethical dilemmas related to AI-generated content. Future curricula should also provide hands-on train - ing in AI-assisted investigative reporting and multimedia pro - duction, equipping students with both technical competencies and critical evaluation skills. In addition to curriculum devel - opment,  this  study  underscores  the  necessity  of  longitudinal research on AI adoption in communication education. Given the rapid evolution of AI technologies, future research should explore how communication students' attitudes toward AI shift over time and how their AI competencies impact their career trajectories. Moreover, cross-cultural comparative studies could provide valuable insights into how communication education institutions  worldwide  are  adapting  to  AI-driven  transfor - mations (Levy-Nadav et al.2024). While this study provides a snapshot of AI's impact on communication students in China, further  research  could  examine  AI  adoption  patterns  across different media ecosystems, identifying regional variations in AI integration, ethical concerns and pedagogical approaches. Furthermore, this study contributes to the broader discussion on 
AI literacy in higher education by emphasising the gap between AI usage and AI understanding. While students are actively using AI in journalism-related tasks, many lack a conceptual

grasp of how AI-generated content is shaped by underlying algo - rithms and biases. This finding supports existing research sug - gesting that media students often adopt AI tools without fully comprehending their implications for media ethics and public trust (Dahri et al.2024). As such, future communication edu - cation initiatives should integrate interdisciplinary approaches, drawing from computer science, ethics and media studies to fos - ter a well-rounded AI-literate generation of journalists.
Ultimately, the challenge for educators is not whether to incor - porate AI into training programs, but how to do so in a way that safeguards critical thinking, originality and professional agency. While AI-driven communication programmes are inev - itable, educators and policymakers should ensure that AI serves as an empowering tool rather than a constraining force. AI can undoubtedly enhance content production, but it should comple - ment rather than replace the core journalistic skills of investiga - tion, analysis and ethical storytelling. Future research should continue exploring how AI reshapes media production practice flows, ethics and creativity, ensuring that the next generation of journalists is both AI-savvy and critically engaged with technol - ogy's evolving role in media.
6   |   Limitations and Conclusion
Despite its contributions, this study has several limitations that should be acknowledged. First, the study relied on self-reported data, which may introduce social desirability bias (Podsakoff et al. 2012). Students who chose to participate may have been more enthusiastic or experienced with AI tools, which could lead to overrepresentation of positive attitudes and underrep - resentation of skepticism or resistance. Future research should incorporate objective measures of AI usage to triangulate self- reported perceptions with behavioral data. Second, this study provides only a cross-sectional perspective on AI adoption in communication education. Given that AI tools evolve rapidly, students' attitudes and career confidence may change over time. 
A longitudinal study could track how AI literacy and attitudes develop across multiple academic years or post-graduation as students enter the workforce. Third, creativity was treated as a unidimensional construct in this study. Future research should consider disaggregating creativity into its constituent forms— such as visual innovation, narrative experimentation and struc - tural originality—to capture more nuanced impacts of AI tools on student expression. Third, sample representativeness is an - other limitation. While the study includes 768 communication students from multiple universities, findings may not generalise 
to students in other disciplines or media industries. Future re - search should explore cross-disciplinary perspectives, compar - ing communication students with those in communication, film production and digital media arts. Finally, this study focuses on the Chinese context, where communication education operates within unique institutional and regulatory frameworks. While some findings may be universally applicable, comparative stud - ies across different media ecosystems could provide a more ho - listic understanding of AI's global impact on media training.
This study provides empirical insights into how generative AI influences communication students' learning motivation, career confidence and creativity, bridging the gap between technological 
adoption theories (UTAUT) and motivational frameworks (SDT). The findings indicate that while AI tools enhance technical profi - ciency and career readiness, they also introduce ethical dilemmas, creative constraints and anxieties about automation. One of the key takeaways is that communication students increasingly view AI as a career necessity rather than an optional tool, reinforcing the need for structured AI literacy training in communication ed - ucation. However, the study also highlights a creativity paradox— AI facilitates faster content creation but may erode originality if used uncritically. The implications for communication education are clear: AI integration should not replace fundamental journal - istic skills such as investigative rigor, ethical decision-making and editorial judgment. Instead, AI should be leveraged as an assistive tool that enhances, rather than dictates, storytelling. To achieve this balance, communication curricula need to incorporate AI ethics, bias mitigation and hybrid AI-human workflows to prepare students for an AI-driven media landscape. As AI continues to evolve, communication education faces a pivotal challenge: ensur - ing that future journalists remain both technologically adept and critically independent. This study contributes to ongoing discus - sions on how AI can be harnessed not just as a tool for efficiency, but potentially—as this exploratory study suggests—as a catalyst for journalistic innovation—provided it is implemented with care - ful consideration of its ethical and creative implications.
