Abstract
Given the increased autonomy and limited direct instructor presence in online learning settings necessitating advanced self-regulated learning skills (SRLs), many online students face significant learning difficulties, such as low course completion rates. External support, particularly in the form of feedback, is crucial in addressing these difficulties. However, providing timely and personalised feedback at scale remains a key challenge. This study examines the effects of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) feedback on students' SRLs in an online higher education context. The study compares GenAI feedback, informed by student trace data 
and learning analytics, with tutor-generated feedback, assessing both student perceptions of feedback and their SRLs development. Employing a mixed-methods research design, we collected survey data, trace-based SRL indicators, and open- ended responses from 46 university students. Quantitative findings revealed that students rated GenAI feedback more positively than tutor-generated feedback, with 
a statistically significant difference in Genuineness. Moreover, the treatment group showed significant improvement in the Task Strategies dimension of SRLs, suggesting that personalised GenAI feedback may enhance specific regulatory behaviours. Qualitative insights revealed varying student awareness of the feedback source, with some prioritising content over the source of feedback. In contrast, others indicated their attitudes might have differed had they known the provider, highlighting the socio-emotional aspects of feedback adoption and impact. This study concludes that GenAI has the potential to scale SRLs feedback in online learning environments, but the student perceptions of feedback should be carefully managed to observe the expected impact.

Introduction
Feedback is widely recognised as a critical component of effective learning and student achievement (Hattie,  2003; Hattie & Timperley,  2007). However, providing timely and personalised feedback remains a significant challenge, especially within large and diverse student populations typically found in online learning environments (Julia et al., 2021). Due to limited tutor resources and increasing student–teacher ratios, feedback often becomes generic and delayed, failing to adequately meet students' individualised learn - ing needs (Dai et al., 2024; Meyer et al., 2024; Nazaretsky et al., 2024). Consequently, students often experience "feedback gaps," in which the feedback provided is misaligned with their individual learning needs. This discrepancy can diminish motivation and hin - der their capacity to engage with feedback in a meaningful and constructive manner (Nicolini & Cole, 2019). These gaps frequently lead to student dissatisfaction, as learners tend to value feedback that is personalised, timely, and contextually relevant (Blair et al., 2013; Holmes & Papageorgiou, 2009). This underscores the pressing need to investi - gate innovative feedback mechanisms capable of providing personalised and actionable insights at scale.
Self-regulated learning skills (SRLs) play a critical role in enabling learners to effec - tively manage their learning processes (Pintrich, 2000; Winne & Hadwin, 1998; Zimmer- man, 2000). While the positive impact of feedback on SRLs is well-documented (Butler 
& Winne,  1995; Hattie & Timperley,  2007), students in online learning contexts often struggle  with  engagement  and  self-regulation  due  to  inadequate  or  ineffective  feed - back (Kizilcec et al., 2017; Viberg et al., 2020). Learning Analytics (LA) has emerged as a promising avenue for addressing these challenges by providing data-driven insights for personalised learning support (Zimmerman,  2008). Automated LA-based systems, such as OnTask, have demonstrated potential in enhancing self-regulation of learning through personalised feedback interventions (Lim et al.,  2021; Tsai et al., 2021). How- ever, these systems typically utilise hard-to-scale human feedback distribution or fixed, rule-based approaches to feedback generation, limiting their adaptability to students' evolving needs and face issues to be scaled (Garrison, 1995; Troussas et al., 2023).
Recent advances in Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI), particularly through Large  Language  Models  (LLMs),  offer  promising  new  opportunities  for  mitigating these limitations by providing dynamic, context-sensitive, and human-like feedback at scale (Bozkurt, 2023; Milana et al., 2024). Recent studies suggest GenAI-based exam - ples can deliver feedback of comparable quality to tutors across various tasks, including data science projects (Dai et al.,  2023, 2024), argumentative writing (Meyer et al.,  2024; Weber et al.,  2024), and in statistics and psychology courses in higher education con - texts (Zhang et al.,  2025). Despite these encouraging findings, more in-depth research is required to examine the impact of GenAI feedback on students’ perceptions, levels of engagement, and overall learning outcomes within authentic educational contexts. Most studies investigate the impact on content acquisition and learning gains, yet there 
is limited research looking at the impact of GenAI feedback on higher-order thinking skills, such as students’ SRLs. In addition, understanding students’ perceptions is of par - ticular significance, as these perceptions, as conceptualised by Nazaretsky et al. (2024), encompassing objectivity, usefulness, genuineness, and credibility of the provider, shape not only students’ engagement with feedback but also the extent to which they inter - pret and act upon it, ultimately influencing their overall satisfaction with the learning process (Mulliner & Tucker, 2017). By foregrounding both students' perceptions of feed - back and the role of SRLs as a determinant of academic success, this study examines how these two dimensions are shaped in response to varying feedback modalities, including those generated by GenAI. In doing so, it offers novel insights into the learning experi - ences associated with GenAI feedback interventions in higher education contexts, while also shedding light on how students' awareness of feedback source shapes their attitudes towards feedback.
Specifically, this study aims to address the following research questions:
RQ1: How do students perceive GenAI versus tutor-generated feedback regarding its objectivity, usefulness, genuineness, and the credibility of the feedback provider?
RQ2: How do feedback interventions generated by tutors and GenAI influence stu - dents' SRLs?
RQ3:  How  does  students'  awareness  of  feedback  source  influence  their  attitudes towards feedback?
Addressing these questions offers insights into the opportunities and challenges of using GenAI feedback in higher education settings. First, examining student perceptions uncovers critical determinants influencing their acceptance of and engagement with GenAI interventions. Second, evaluating the effects of different feedback regimes on the development of SRLs provides evidence that can contribute to the development of feedback strategies to better support students’ SRLs in online higher education environ - ments. Third, analysing students’ qualitative reflections offers nuanced insights into how the perceived source of feedback shapes their interpretations and attitudes.
Related work
Shifting paradigms for educational feedback
Feedback interventions are well-established as critical for promoting student achieve - ment and success in educational settings (Butler & Winne,  1995; Hattie, 2003; Hattie 
& Timperley, 2007; Shute, 2008; Winne & Hadwin,  1998). Hattie and Timperley (2007) emphasise  that  effective  feedback  must  explicitly  address  learners’  performance  by answering three fundamental questions: "What are the goals?", "What progress is being made toward these goals?", and "What actions are necessary to enhance progress?" (p. 
86). In line with this conceptualisation, feedback serves primarily to reconcile the dis - parity between actual performance and achievement targets, thereby distinguishing it from alternative instructional strategies. Thus, effective feedback necessitates the inclu - sion of personalised attributes (Brinko,  1993; Shute, 2008), customised to address the specific  proficiencies  (Lipnevich  et  al.,  2016)  and  weaknesses  of  individual  students (Brinko, 1993).
However,  the  provision  of  personalised,  learner-specific,  and  effective  feedback remains labour-intensive (Dai et al.,  2024; Meyer et al., 2024; Nazaretsky et al., 2024; Wiley, 2006), necessitating a thorough consideration of each student's unique learning trajectory (Brinko,  1993). As a result, despite strong theoretical justifications, the prac - tical application of effective feedback often faces significant limitations (Henderson et al., 2025; Lipnevich et al., 2016). This frequently leads learners to perceive feedback as inconsistent, impersonal, or insufficient (Carless et al., 2011; Perera et al., 2008), thereby contributing to widespread dissatisfaction and adversely affecting learning outcomes (Blair et al., 2013; Carless & Boud, 2018; Mulliner & Tucker, 2017).
Although traditional feedback models seek to accommodate the individual attributes, goals, and trajectories of learners, their reliance on manual, labour-intensive processes poses significant challenges to scalability. This often results in generic and delayed feed - back, failing to sufficiently meet students' individual learning needs (Dai et al.,  2024; Meyer et al., 2024; Nazaretsky et al., 2024). These limitations have encouraged a shift toward more adaptive approaches that are capable of delivering personalised feedback at scale (Carless et al., 2011; Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick, 2006). This shift represents a depar - ture from labour-intensive practices, advocating for more sustainable methods that are responsive to learners’ evolving needs (Nicol & Macfarlane-Dick,  2006; Sadler, 2010). Building  on  these  evolving  perspectives,  emerging  approaches  increasingly  integrate automated and data-driven solutions to enhance the efficiency and scalability of feed - back practices.
Recent scholarship has sought to exemplify the practical implications of this evolv - ing feedback paradigm by examining adaptive, scalable feedback examples in authentic learning  environments  and  investigating  students’  perceptions  of  feedback  provided by data-informed GenAI and tutor-generated traditional methods. For instance, Naza - retsky et al. (2024) explored student perceptions of feedback generated by both tutors and GenAI based on task-centred responses, using a framework that includes four key dimensions: objectivity, usefulness, genuineness, and the credibility of the feedback pro - vider. Objectivity relates to a fair, factual alignment between the content provided and the recipient's actual performance; usefulness measures how meaningful, informative, and pertinent the feedback is for improvement; genuineness indicates authenticity and personal significance; and credibility concerns whether the feedback provider is seen as trustworthy and well-intentioned. In their study, GenAI feedback was produced using GPT-4, informed by student responses and task descriptions. Conducted within authen - tic university course settings, their findings revealed that under blind review condi - tions, students rated GenAI feedback more favourably. However, once the identity of the feedback provider was disclosed, perceptions of GenAI feedback declined, while tutor feedback was rated more positively, highlighting both the potential and limitations of GenAI in educational feedback contexts. This pattern suggests that although learners may find AI-generated feedback applicable, they view its credibility as fragile. Moreover, it prompts a shift from prioritising perceived quality to critically examining the extent 
to which such practices lead to observable improvements. Specifically, the key question is whether students' perceptions of feedback meaningfully contribute to progress, espe - cially in challenging areas like higher-order thinking skills, such as SRLs.
Taken together, these considerations point out the importance of further empirical research into how learners perceive, interpret, and respond to GenAI feedback.
Self-regulated learning in higher education
Self-regulated learning (SRL) refers to the proactive management of cognitive, emo - tional, and behavioural processes by students in pursuit of their academic objectives (Zimmerman, 2000). Proficient self-regulated learners typically achieve greater academic success  and  develop  essential  skills  for  lifelong  learning  (Zimmerman,  2002).  While students typically receive guidance from teachers in early education, higher education increasingly expects them to manage their learning processes independently. Despite this expectation, many students in higher education settings still struggle significantly with self-regulation of learning (Bjork et al.,  2013; Kizilcec et al., 2017). A significant contributing factor is the reluctance to seek help due to the fear of appearing incom - petent (Zimmerman, 2002). This reluctance highlights the importance of external, non- intrusive support mechanisms that encourage learners without triggering such anxieties.
Self-regulation  challenges  are  particularly  pronounced  in  online  learning  environ - ments, where increased autonomy and reduced direct instructor presence necessitate advanced SRLs (Afacan Adanir & Gulbahar Guven,  2023). To better understand and support SRLs in these environments, Barnard et al. (2009) proposed a comprehensive six-dimensional framework. This framework includes goal setting (GS), task strategies (TS), environment structuring (ES), time management (TM), help-seeking (HS), and self-evaluation (SE). Each of these dimensions plays a crucial role in enabling students to manage their learning processes independently. Facilitating the development of these skills requires intentionally designed and adaptive support mechanisms, among which personalised feedback stands out as particularly effective (Hattie, 2003). Nevertheless, as highlighted in meta-reviews of the field (e.g. Viberg et al., 2020), the provision of timely, personalised feedback to effectively foster SRLs remains limited across many online edu - cation environments.
To confront these challenges, LA-based solutions show significant promise by leverag - ing learners’ digital traces to generate personalised feedback. These digital performance traces can represent students' SRLs status through observable indicators, such as GS (for example, engagement with the syllabus or rubric), TS (for example, course logins or the number of item visits), and TM (for example, the average time spent on modules), among others (Ye & Pennisi,  2022). Empirical studies indicate that feedback informed by LA can enhance self-regulation by offering timely and actionable insights (Chen, 2024; Lim et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2021). However, many current LA systems depend on raw interaction data (e.g., number of clicks or total logins), which often neglects these nuanced observable indicators of SRLs. While such raw interaction trace data can be used to assess SRLs, this approach has limitations that hinder a comprehensive under - standing of students' broader needs and skill-based SRL performance (Ye & Pennisi, 2022). For example, it may not capture the full range of skill-based indicators (e.g., GS, TS),  restricting  a  comprehensive  view  of  students’  learning  needs,  underscoring  the need for trace-based frameworks that are explicitly aligned with SRLs constructs. Fur - thermore, many existing studies rely on rule-based systems for automating personalised feedback delivery (Araka et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2021; Pardo et al., 2019; Tsai et al., 2021). These pre-set tools often consist of similar messages and lack the flexibility to adapt to learners' evolving needs in a timely manner (Garrison, 1995; Troussas et al., 2023). As a result, the feedback they provide may fail to address students’ changing learning chal - lenges, thereby limiting its relevance (Jansen et al.,  2024). Thus, there is an imperative need to explore more responsive feedback systems that alleviate the rigidity of rule- based systems and better align with learners’ evolving needs, dynamically adjusting to their SRLs profiles as they engage with tasks. Addressing this need requires not only more adaptive feedback mechanisms but also a trace-based framework capable of cap - turing comprehensive SRLs indicators from trace data, a gap this study aims to address through the framework proposed by Ye and Pennisi (2022).
Exploring more responsive feedback practices
Considering the findings above, exploring more responsive feedback approaches con - stitutes a valuable contribution to the evolving paradigm of feedback provision. Com - pared to current systems, which rely on predefined rules and static templates and often lack the flexibility to adapt to learners' evolving needs in a timely manner (Garrison, 1995; Troussas et al.,  2023), GenAI models, particularly LLMs, offer a more dynamic and adaptable alternative. Over the past decades, researchers have developed an inter - est in human-like computational models that go beyond deterministic systems (e.g., Arf, 1959). GenAI models exemplify this evolution, as they can produce flexible, context-sen - sitive language outputs that approximate human communication and reasoning (Kos - myna et al., 2025). In educational environments, natural language prompts allow users to guide these models in generating human-like, tailored outputs that support individual learning goals (Bozkurt, 2023; Milana et al., 2024).
While the technical flexibility of GenAI offers clear advantages over rule-based sys - tems, empirical research examining its actual effectiveness in educational contexts is still in its early stages. Thus, there is a need to better understand the impact of GenAI-based interventions (Henderson et al.,  2025; Meyer et al., 2024). Some recent studies have begun addressing this gap (e.g., Kumar et al.,  2025; Nazaretsky et al., 2024; Steinbach et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Kumar et al. (2025), for example, showed that explana - tory LLM-generated feedback produced greater learning gains than answer-only feed - back  in  mathematics.  Interestingly,  learners  were  sometimes  able  to  extract  helpful strategies and benefit from explanations even when they contained errors, a pattern also observed by Steinbach et al. (2025) in their work on hallucinated feedback. These find - ings suggest that learners’ characteristics may play an important role in how they inter - pret and respond to feedback, even when its quality is imperfect. However, this research also raises equity concerns, as not all learners may possess the proficiency to detect and adapt to misleading outputs without support. Since AI-generated outputs can be erroneous, it is crucial that AI-based feedback interventions are designed to strengthen learners’ critical capacities, such as self-regulation, reflection, and evaluation, to prevent learning from being undermined. Learners’ engagement with feedback depends not only on its accuracy but also on their capacity to self-regulate. Accordingly, responsive feed - back practices should address both the correctness of the feedback and the ways it can foster learners’ regulatory skills, enabling them to benefit even from imperfect feedback.
Beyond the challenges of feedback accuracy and learners' characteristics, another limi - tation of current evidence is that most studies examine GenAI feedback in short-term, single-session contexts, overlooking longitudinal processes through which critical skills like SRL develop. Additionally, GenAI feedback practices are often evaluated against static feedback rather than authentic tutor-based outputs, which do not fully reflect real educational contexts. For example, Meyer et al. (2024) conducted an experimental study to explore the potential of GenAI in supporting essay writing through revision feedback. Students  who  received  GenAI  feedback  demonstrated  significantly  greater  improve - ments in essay scores, motivation, perceived usefulness of feedback, and positive emo - tions compared to those in the control group who received only static (i.e., standardised feedback messages) feedback. While these findings underscore the promise of GenAI for short-term revision outcomes, it remains unclear whether such feedback also fosters long-term benefits, longitudinal processes such as SRLs development. Moreover, rather than comparing GenAI feedback with a static feedback context, assessing its real-world classroom applicability requires comparisons with tutor-generated feedback.
Taken together, these studies offer insights for improving responsive feedback prac - tices, which not only provide timely and adaptive guidance but also foster learners’ self- regulation. In response, the present study introduces and evaluates GenAI-generated feedback intervention, examining students’ perceptions of this feedback alongside mea - surable improvements in their longitudinal SRLs performance. Specifically, the study compares  the  effectiveness  of  tutor-generated  and  GenAI  feedback  in  an  authentic higher-education context, providing insights into the practical value and real-world fea - sibility of GenAI-supported feedback systems.
Methodology
Research context
Ethical approval for this study was granted by the University College London (UCL) Institute of Education Ethics Committee. The study was embedded within a nine-week distance-learning core module entitled Basic Statistics, designed for students in higher education. The module aimed to enhance students’ comprehension of the role and appli - cation of statistics in both academic inquiry and professional practice. Throughout the nine-week period, students engaged in a multifaceted learning experience that incorpo - rated weekly slide presentations, moderated discussion forums, core and supplementary readings, as well as interactive workshops.
Participants
The research participants include 46 higher education students enrolled in Basic Sta- tistics core module at a university during the second term of 2024–2025 academic year. Participants were randomly assigned to two equal groups: the GenAI feedback group (n = 23) and the tutor feedback group (n = 23). All students received detailed informa - tion about the research via an information sheet and were asked to provide informed consent at the commencement of the course. Participation was entirely voluntary, and participants could withdraw at any time. Invitations to participate were disseminated via email and a MOODLE course announcement. Students were blinded to their group allo - cation and the source of feedback to avoid negative attitudes towards GenAI feedback. The information sheet informed participants that they would receive personalised writ - ten feedback during the course, but did not disclose whether this would be generated by 
a tutor or by GenAI during the experimental phase.
Data collection
Data collection to measure feedback perception
To investigate students’ perceptions of the feedback they received, a post-intervention questionnaire adapted from Nazaretsky et al. (2024) was administered. The question - naire assessed various dimensions of feedback perception, including perceived objec - tivity, usefulness, genuineness, and credibility of the feedback provider. Data collection took place during the final week of the course. All participants were invited to com - plete  the  questionnaire  via  an  online  form  distributed  through  MOODLE.  Notably, all participants were still blind to the feedback source at the time they completed the questionnaire. The perception instrument demonstrated high internal consistency in the present sample (Cronbach's α = .923), with a mean score of 3.15 (SD = 0.70) on a 0–4 scale.
Data collection for SRLs
Feedback studies grounded in LA have faced growing scrutiny regarding their validity. In response to these concerns, researchers have increasingly adopted evidence-centred frameworks (e.g., Bauer et al., 2025; Divjak et al., 2024) to facilitate more robust and reli - able interpretations of learner data. This study, therefore, adopts the SRLs framework proposed by Ye and Pennisi (2022), as the course structure and resources from which the data were collected closely align with the indicators outlined in this framework. A detailed summary of the SRLs adopted in this study and their corresponding indicators 
is provided in Table A1 in Appendix 1. Their framework provides a structured basis for modelling trace data by linking observable indicators, derived from raw online interac - tion data, with significant SRLs (e.g.
• GS: engagement with the syllabus, rubric, and other planning resources;
• TS: course logins, number of item visits, and other learning navigation behaviours;
• TM: average time spent on modules, use of supplementary materials, among others;
• HS: frequency of questions and asking for an extension;
• SE: revisiting completed modules, optional self-assessments, and related activities.)
This framework integrates Pintrich's (2000) model of SRL, comprising forethought, plan - ning,  activation,  monitoring,  control,  and  reflection,  with  the  Online  Self-Regulated Learning Questionnaire (OSLQ; Barnard et al.,  2009), as a foundational lens for data collection. Student activity within MOODLE was segmented into three distinct phases (please see Figure. 1), corresponding to key periods of the intervention. Data mining procedure was also employed to map raw log data into analytically usable SRLs. This multi-step process comprised:
I. Importing raw trace data from the MOODLE system.
II. Filtering out records generated by non-student users.

Open-ended questions
To gain qualitative insights, we included two open-ended questions related to the feed - back, which were administered only to the treatment group. The first question explored their awareness of the feedback source. The second question aimed to examine whether recognising the source, particularly if it was AI, led to any changes in their attitudes and whether those who could not identify the source would have reacted differently had they known it. Table  1 presents the open-ended questions. Notably, participants in the treatment group were informed regarding the source of feedback before the time they answered the questions.
To investigate RQ1 (students’ perceptions of feedback) and RQ2 (the impact of feed - back  on  SRLs),  a  quasi-experimental  design  was  employed.  For  RQ1,  the  feedback perception questionnaire was administered in the final week of the study. For RQ2, to examine the impact of the feedback intervention on students’ SRLs engagement, we compared participants’ pre-  (before the first intervention) and post-  (after the first intervention)  standardised  scores  across  SRLs  dimension  proxies  described  in  Sec - tion “Data collection for SRLs”. In addition, over the first two questions, rank-biserial correlation effect sizes were calculated (Tomczak & Tomczak, 2014). Although the origi - nal SRLs framework includes indicators such as Optional Self-Assessments (SAssCom) and Bonus Quiz Completion (BQCom), these were not applicable in our course context. To address RQ3, we collected qualitative data in the final week through the questions were asked only of students in the treatment group, who had been informed just prior to this stage that their feedback was generated by a GenAI system. The analysis was carried out collaboratively by the first two authors, following qualitative data analysis proce - dures outlined by Yildirim and Simsek (2013), which involved coding, theme develop - ment, and interpretation of findings.

Intervention design
The intervention design was facilitated as summarised below in Figure. 1. In a quasi- experimental design, participants in the control group received tutor-generated feed - back, while those in the experimental group received GenAI feedback.
The first intervention was based on SRLs scores calculated from trace data collected up to the fifth week. The second intervention, conducted in the seventh week, updated these scores to reflect timely changes in SRLs proxies observed since the first interven - tion. For example, if a learner’s HS score decreased from 1.7 to 1.3, the resulting change of –0.4 was incorporated into the second intervention. The interval between interven - tions aligns with recommended feedback practices targeted at SRLs, as allowing time for reflection helps students better comprehend the feedback (Butler & Winne,  1995). During the intervention, each participant received two written feedback messages via email, delivered simultaneously to both groups and following a consistent structure (e.g., personalised salutation, feedback message, and closing remarks).
Tutor-feedback intervention
Studies on tutor-led feedback often highlight challenges related to the level of expertise required to draft effective and meaningful feedback (Bennett,  2011). This study devel- oped a tool, called RefleXED, to support tutors in interpreting and utilising behavioural data. This tool simplifies data interpretation and reduces the expertise barrier commonly faced by instructors. It presents each student’s standardised scores derived from observ - able indicators of SRLs behaviours (hereafter referred to as proxy scores), along with detailed observations on the five SRLs pillars adopted in the study. Proxy scores were calculated by aggregating raw interaction data into SRLs-relevant indicators for each pillar (GS, TS, TM, HS, SE) and then standardising them (z-scores) to enable compari - son across students. For example, the number of logins and time spent on modules were combined to calculate a composite proxy score for TM, following the same procedure applied to the other SRLs.
For each pillar, the interface displays the z-standardised score of SRLs proxies, along with  the  associated  observable  indicators  and  their  respective  calculation  methods, enabling tutors to formulate tailored feedback messages aligned with individual student needs. Scores are centred around zero, with negative values meaning performance below the cohort average, corresponding to a need for negative feedback, as explained in the following  section.  Conversely,  positive  values,  meaning  above-average  performance, correspond to a need for positive/reinforcement feedback. While the tool is designed to lower the expertise barrier, it still benefits from being used by the tutor with familiar - ity with data interpretation. In this study, the tutor possessed a background in statistics, which supported the effective use of the system. GenAI-feedback intervention
To generate GenAI feedback messages, we employed the ChatGPT-4 language model via the OpenAI API, guided by a custom-designed set of prompts implemented through a purpose-built tool developed by the authors. The model parameters were a temperature of 0.9 and a top-p value of 1.0 to foster generative flexibility, tailored to students’ varying performance profiles, and we kept these settings constant for all students. The GenAI feedback focused on the polarity of students' standardised scores, which was sensitive to the current SRLs proficiency of participants. All GenAI feedback was monitored by the first author and reviewed for ethical consid - erations prior to its delivery to students, given the potential ethical concerns associated with AI-generated content. No change to the GenAI-generated feedback was deemed necessary during the intervention period, thanks to the quality of the GenAI feedback being high without any observed hallucinations. Notably, students in the GenAI feed - back group were not informed that their feedback was generated by an AI system during the intervention, which helped prevent any adverse attribution effects. Only after the experiment concluded, and before their open-ended answers were collected, students in the GenAI feedback condition were fully debriefed and informed about the source of feedback. The rest of the students were briefed after the project was completed due to ethical requirements.

Thematic analysis of open-ended responses
To address RQ3, a thematic analysis of the open-ended responses was conducted. While the treatment group consisted of 23 participants, only 17 students completed the open- ended questions.
A categorical coding scheme was used to determine whether students were aware of the feedback source ("Yes" or "No"). Students who responded "Yes" were directed to a follow-up  question  that  explored  how  their  attitude  changed  upon  recognising  the source, while those who responded "No" were directed to a subsequent question asking whether their attitude would have changed had they known the source. A more detailed line-by-line coding was conducted for both the follow-up and subsequent questions to document students' attitudinal responses. During this process, student responses were labelled with four descriptive codes:  No Change, Would Not Change if Known, Condi- tional/Unsure, and Would Change if Known. These codes were then synthesised into four overarching themes: Awareness- Stable Perception, Unaware- but Content-Focused, Unaware- but Open to Change, and Unaware- Would Reconsider if Aware. To ensure analytical rigour and consistency, the first two authors independently applied the coding scheme to the entire set of qualitative responses, resulting in a Cohen's kappa of κ = .830, indicating almost perfect agreement (Viera & Garrett, 2005).

Discussion
This study examined students’ perceptions of feedback and its impact on their SRLs in an online higher education setting, while also investigating how awareness of the feedback source influences students' attitudes towards feedback. Our hypotheses were grounded 
in the well-documented challenges that tutors face in delivering timely, personalised, and meaningful feedback (Henderson et al.,  2025; Lipnevich et al., 2016; Wiley, 2006). Recent developments in LLMs offer promising opportunities to address these limita - tions (Bozkurt, 2023; Milana et al., 2024). Of particular importance, this study does not claim that GenAI feedback is superior to that provided by experienced and well-sup - ported human tutors. Rather, we aimed to explore the feasibility of using LA-informed GenAI as a potential solution to alleviate persistent challenges in traditional feedback practices. In educational contexts where the provision of high-quality, individualised feedback is constrained by tutor workload and limited resources, our approach aligns with Chan and Tsi’s (2024, p. 2) argument of “liberating teachers from time-consuming tasks and allowing them to concentrate on higher-level responsibilities.” In such settings, the proposed model may serve as a viable complementary support mechanism.
RQ1-perceptions of feedback
First, perception scores (RQ1) indicated that the treatment group evaluated GenAI feed - back they received more highly than tutor-generated feedback. Although the small sam - ple size prevents firm conclusions, the findings suggest that students respond positively to GenAI-generated feedback, which may help alleviate the longstanding dissatisfaction with traditional feedback practices (Blair et al., 2013; Mulliner & Tucker,  2017). Several factors may account for this outcome. Most notably, the GenAI-generated feedback in this study was constructed using personalised prompts that were sensitive to students’ performance, incorporating learners’ personalised LA-based data. This likely contrib - uted to the contextual, timely relevance of the feedback, thereby enhancing its perceived quality.
Interestingly,  the  Genuineness  dimension  showed  a  statistically  significant  differ - ence in favour of the treatment group with a moderate effect. At first glance, this may appear contradictory. However, this finding aligns with previous work by Nazaretsky et al. (2024), who also observed that students often attribute greater genuineness to GenAI-generated feedback, especially under blind conditions. Our results also resonate with Henderson et al. (2025), who reported that GenAI feedback was perceived as less negative in tone and emotional impact, slightly more utility (usable), and more objec - tive than tutor feedback, reflecting similar patterns in student perceptions in our study. The only divergence was in perceived credibility (referred to as 'reliability' in Henderson et al (2025)), which may be attributed to methodological differences: To the best of our knowledge, Nazaretsky et al. (2024) involved a diverse group of instructors across mul - tiple courses in the collaborative design of tutor-generated feedback. In contrast, in our study, the tutor-generated feedback was produced by a single course instructor, without the benefit of team-based refinement. Additionally, unlike Henderson et al. (2025), who relied on students' prior experiences with using AI for educational feedback, we pro - vided timely, tailored, and performance-specific feedback, which may have shaped cred - ibility perception differently.
Returning to the Genuineness dimension, one plausible explanation lies in the design of  the  feedback  prompts.  Instructions  such  as  “write  like  a  tutor”  combined  with learner-performance sensitivity may have led the AI to adopt a tone that is consistently supportive, promotive, and empathetic, emphasising encouragement, clarity, and action - able suggestions. This linguistic consistency may bring a sense of care and attention that students associate with genuineness. As a result, GenAI stability may have been inter - preted by students as a sign of sincerity and authenticity. While these findings challenge common assumptions about the emotional superiority of human feedback, they point to important opportunities for designing feedback systems that simulate human-like com - munication in beneficial ways.
Regarding the remaining three perception dimensions (Objectivity, Usefulness, and Credibility), GenAI-generated feedback received higher mean scores; however, no sta - tistically significant differences were observed between groups. While we acknowledge the statistical power limitations associated with our relatively small sample, this study was undertaken in an ecologically valid context with real students. The relatively small cohort size in this study is indeed a limitation (n = 23 in the intervention group) but comparable to ecologically valid studies in the field (e.g., 17 students in the tutor-led group in Er et al., 2025).
RQ2-tangible improvements in SRLs engagement
With respect to improvements in SRLs (RQ2), students’ SRLs engagement showed a sta - tistically significant improvement in the Task Strategies skill for the treatment group. Although  this  finding  should  be  interpreted  with  caution,  given  the  relatively  small sample of the current study, it is consistent with previous research suggesting that LA- informed, automated feedback with different tools can positively influence self-regu - lation of learning development (Chen,  2024; Lim et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2021). It also resonates  with  recent  studies  that  employed  GenAI  feedback  and  reported  promis - ing improvements (Kumar et al.,  2025; Meyer et al., 2024; Steinbach et al., 2025). This improvement may reflect students’ relatively higher perceptions of the practical value of GenAI-generated feedback, as prior studies have estimated a clear link between feed - back perception and engagement (Carless, 2006; Er et al., 2025). The observed moderate effect size may reflect both the statistical power limitations of the relatively small sample and the granular nature of the trace-based indicators used in this study. Unlike stud - ies relying on raw interaction data, these indicators derived from raw interactions and explicitly mapped to specific SRLs proxies. For instance, Task Strategies were evaluated using four specific observable indicators (please see Table A1 in Appendix 1). While this enhances the interpretability of SRLs by providing separate measures for each skill, it may have limited the range of detectable changes, potentially accounting for the moder - ate effect sizes observed in our study.
The remaining four SRLs (GS, TM, HS, and SE) did not show statistically significant between-group  differences.  However,  TM  approached  significance  with  a  moderate effect size, indicating a potentially meaningful trend that warrants examination in larger samples. Although the GenAI-supported design produced promising effects on the TS proxy, these findings suggest that the value of feedback lies not in technical sophistica - tion alone, but in the extent to which a design enables students to act on the feedback they receive. In this regard, while GenAI feedback may inherently tend toward greater elaboration at scale, the length of feedback alone does not guarantee its effectiveness. We align with Shute (2008), who argues that overly detailed feedback can sometimes obscure rather than clarify the intended message. The effectiveness of feedback is deter - mined not by the quantity of information provided, but by how targeted, focused, and responsive it is, as emphasised by Black and Wiliam (2009) and Shute (2008). Accord- ingly, GenAI should not be viewed as indisputably superior, but rather as a feasible and scalable complement to human feedback (Henderson et al., 2025; Nazaretsky et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
In addition, one notable pattern appeared in the control group: specific SRLs proxies, such as TS and TM, showed a lowered trend over the intervention. This decline may be connected to students’ existing SRLs levels, aligning with Kizilcec et al. (2017), who present that students, particularly those with lower SRLs, are more prone to disengage - ment or even withdrawal when support is inconsistent or insufficient over time. Fur - thermore, as Steinbach et al. (2025) note, learners with initially low performance benefit most from targeted feedback and may struggle to stay engaged when feedback fails to meet their evolving needs. Taken together, these patterns suggest that students’ engage - ment with feedback may be influenced by learner-related characteristics (Daumiller & Meyer, 2026), which shape how feedback is interpreted and enacted.
RQ3-feedback source awareness
Finally,  feedback  source  awareness  (RQ3)  among  students  in  the  treatment  group revealed variations. While some students were aware that the feedback was generated by GenAI, others did not recognise the source. This pattern aligns with findings from stud - ies employing Turing test-like designs, which similarly observed variation in students' ability to identify whether feedback was AI- or tutor-generated (Nazaretsky et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
Interestingly, students who were aware of the source generally reported that their atti - tude toward the feedback remained unchanged. This contrasts with prior studies, which found that awareness of the source, as a form of disclosure, usually led to less favour - able ratings. This difference may be explained by our longitudinal, process-integrated design. Instead of merely assessing perceptions before and after disclosure, we gathered students’ views at the end of an extended learning process. During this time, students received feedback tailored to their changing needs. This ongoing approach is highly likely to have provided meaningful learning benefits over time and may have reinforced students’ trust in the feedback, regardless of its origin, as Meyer et al. (2024) argued. In this context, the perceived value of the feedback appears to have outweighed concerns about its source, resulting in a more stable and resilient acceptance than what is typically observed in short-term or one-time feedback scenarios. This aligns with the statement of Ackerman-Barger et al. (2016), who emphasise that students value feedback mainly for its perceived benefit and relevance to their actual performance. As Carless (2006) notes, when this relevance is clear, students are more likely to focus on how the feedback can support their learning.
In addition, many students who were unaware of the feedback source expressed sim - ilar views, suggesting that the perceived value of the feedback content outweighed its source and reflected a pragmatic, content-first orientation. Motivations varied; however, some prioritised alignment with performance goals, while others expressed a gener - ally positive disposition toward AI, potentially reflecting their familiarity with AI-based applications and the benefits they derive from everyday use. However, a subset of stu - dents noted that their approach might have differed had they known the feedback was generated by GenAI from the beginning, as similar studies (e.g., Nazaretsky et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025) reported hesitation, more critical scrutiny and a subsequent decrease in perception. These contrasting views may reflect individual learner characteristics, such as academic proficiency, existing self-regulation level, and prior AI experience. As Carless et al. (2011) argue, capable learners are more likely to focus on the functional value of feedback, while those with lower achievement may be more sensitive to its delivery mode or source (Zimmerman, 2002). Notably, only one student explicitly stated that knowing the source from the outset would have altered their attitude towards the feedback, suggesting that attitudinal sensitivity to source disclosure, while present, was not the prevailing response.
Limitations and future research
This section discusses the limitations of the present study, directions for future research, and broader implications for integrating GenAI into educational practice.
Limitations
The findings of this study should be interpreted in light of several limitations. First, the relatively small sample size (n = 46) from a single online course limits both statistical power and generalisability. Accordingly, the results should be interpreted as context-spe - cific, and replication with larger and more diverse samples is needed. Second, the study primarily focused on perception and behavioural outcomes; accordingly, broader rela - tional aspects of feedback quality, such as the extent to which feedback accurately and comprehensively addressed the targeted needs, were beyond its current scope. Third, the first author reviewed the GenAI-generated feedback prior to delivery to ensure compli - ance with ethical requirements and contextual appropriateness. Although the cognitive demands of reviewing text are not necessarily equivalent to those of composing feedback from scratch (Xavier et al.,  2026), this reviewing process was not formally measured or accounted for as a workload factor in the present study. Fourth, the tutor's awareness of the GenAI feedback condition may have influenced their approach to feedback drafting in unquantifiable ways, leading them to engage in greater deliberation or inadvertently alter their usual practice.
Future research
Given the promising patterns observed here and the relatively small, single-course cohort on which they are based, future research should investigate whether these findings per - sist across diverse educational settings and larger, more diverse student populations. Future studies should also compare GenAI and tutor feedback with a no-feedback con - dition to provide more robust insights into the longitudinal impact of different feedback 
regimes on learning progress. Given that the tutor in this study was aware of the GenAI feedback condition, future studies should consider withholding this information until all interventions are completed, to ensure that tutor-generated feedback remains consis - tent with usual practice and is not influenced by awareness of the experimental condi - tions. Future studies should also consider tracking the same students' SRLs performance in contexts without feedback, enabling a more robust assessment of whether observed changes persist and translate into sustainable skill development beyond the intervention period. In addition, future studies should examine both feedback perception and uptake, encompassing processes such as understanding, accepting, elaborating on, and critically engaging with feedback (Meyer et al.,  2025), to develop a more nuanced understanding of how feedback interventions shape learning. This distinction matters because higher perception scores alone should not be interpreted as evidence that feedback functions effectively in all respects. To enable more rigorous comparisons of feedback quality across conditions and their association with observed outcomes, future studies should consider content-based evaluation techniques, such as precision, recall, and F1-based analyses of how accurately feedback addresses targeted learning needs, as well as den - sity-based metrics, such as the proportion of targeted needs addressed per unit of the text, and their association with learning outcomes. Finally, future studies should inves - tigate whether GenAI-generated feedback is equally effective for students from diverse backgrounds and learning profiles, attending to questions of educational equity (Stein - bach et al., 2025). How individual characteristics such as prior AI experience and cur - rent self-regulation level interact with source awareness to shape feedback engagement remains an important question.
Broader implications and critical considerations
While our promising findings suggest potential, they do not mean that GenAI should be integrated into educational settings merely because of its practicality. As Cukurova (2025) argued, such decisions require careful planning and ongoing evaluation to ensure effective implementation. The mere availability of automation does not justify its ped - agogical adoption. Furthermore, automation systems may stress a reliance on external factors that could inadvertently undermine learners’ ability to regulate their learning. Learning is fundamentally an intrinsic human capability; historically, we have flour - ished  by  adapting  and  learning  in  complex  and  uncertain  environments  (Cukurova, 2025; Zimmerman,  2000). Without careful planning, GenAI integration risks encour - aging superficial learning or metacognitive laziness (Fan et al., 2025), whereby students bypass the process of synthesising information from memory, potentially hindering their understanding and long-term benefits (Kumar et al., 2025). Similarly, providing targeted, accurate information to all students, regardless of their level, should be approached with caution. Research in AI in education has consistently underscored the concept of “pro- ductive failure” (Kumar et al., 2025; Steinbach et al., 2025), allowing students to identify and correct misleading information, thereby self-regulating and reinforcing their histori - cally intrinsic learning capability (Cukurova, 2025; Zimmerman, 2002). Accordingly, we 
argue that it is crucial to explore the potential benefits of GenAI-supported interven - tions while ensuring that such technologies complement, rather than replace, learners’ intrinsic learning abilities.
Conclusion
This study examined the impact of GenAI-generated feedback on students’ perceptions and SRLs in online higher education. Findings indicate that, overall, students evaluated GenAI feedback more positively than tutor feedback, with the treatment group show - ing significant improvement in TS skill and the Genuineness perception dimension. This suggests that our feedback provision model may enhance specific feedback perception and SRLs. Qualitative insights further revealed variation in students’ awareness of the feedback source and its perceived relevance. While many students prioritised the quality and alignment of feedback content regardless of its origin, others indicated that know - ing the source would have influenced their perception, underscoring the role of feedback and AI literacy and individual learner characteristics.
Rather than positioning GenAI and tutor feedback as opposing options, the results suggest that both can serve complementary functions, with neither of these feedback regimes  being  overwhelmingly  superior.  Alongside  tutors’  guidance  and  pedagogical sense, GenAI feedback offers scalability and adaptability. Leveraging the strengths of each may help institutions and instructors design feedback ecosystems that better sup - port learning. Despite the limitations inherent in any initial study, such as a smaller, non- representative sample, our findings provide authentic and valuable insights from actual educational environments. The patterns that emerged highlight promising directionsfor further exploration.
