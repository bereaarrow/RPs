ABSTRACT
Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) has emerged as a powerful tool in the Informal Digital Learning of English (IDLE) environment, offering personalized, interactive, and innovative language learning experiences that may enhance learning engagement. However, the influencing path of perceptions and acceptance of GenAI on the learning engagement of English- as-a-foreign-language (EFL) students has not been sufficiently explored. Specifically, little is known about how cognitive and motivational factors jointly mediate this relationship. Self-efficacy in language learning has been found to play a critical role in regulating motivation through resilience, perseverance, and goal determination, thereby influencing the quality and quantity of effort that students invest in engaging with GenAI-assisted IDLE. This study investigated the effects of GenAI perceptions and acceptance on IDLE engagement among 369 university EFL learners. Using structural equation modeling (SEM), the study analyzed both the direct effects of perceptions and acceptance of GenAI on learning engagement and the serial mediating roles of self-efficacy and motivation. The results showed that (1) GenAI perceptions and acceptance exerted a significant and positive direct effect on learners’ engagement; (2) motivation significantly mediated the relationship between GenAI perceptions and acceptance and engagement; and (3) self-efficacy indirectly enhanced engagement by increasing motivation, forming a chain mediation pathway. These findings provide insights into how self-efficacyand motivation jointly mediate the relationship between GenAI perceptions and acceptance and learning engagement and offer guidance for the implementation ofGenAI-enhanced tools and personalized learning strategies in fostering student autonomy and optimizing engagement in IDLE environments.

1  Introduction
Informal Digital Learning ofEnglish (IDLE) refers to self-directed and autonomous language learning that occurs beyond the for- mal classroom, often through engagement with digital platforms such as YouTube, online games, and social media (Liu et al. 2024). This form of learning has become increasingly significant for English-as-a-foreign-language (EFL) learners who seek flexible and personalized opportunities to enhance their language skills. Research suggests that IDLE may support learner autonomy and self-regulation (Zheng and Xiao 2024) and is positively associated with motivational factors that sustain engagement in language learning. From the self-determination theory (SDT) perspective, GenAI could strengthen students’ engagement in the IDLE environment by satisfying students’ autonomy, com- petence, and relatedness needs through providing personalized feedback, social-like interactions, and multiple solutions.
Recent studies have started exploring the educational potential of perceptions and acceptance of GenAI within IDLE contexts, focusing on listening (Wong et al. 2021), speaking (Yang et al. 2022),reading (Yapp et al. 2023),and writing (Baiand Wang 2023). Despite these promising outcomes, the underlying mechanisms through which GenAI perceptions and acceptance influence learners’ engagement remain underexplored, particularly regarding how learners’ psychological characteristics mediate this process.
Among these psychological factors, self-efficacy and motivation have been identified as critical predictors oflearners’ engagement in GenAI-mediated IDLE from SDTtheory. Self-efficacyrefers to individuals’ beliefs in their ability to perform specific tasks (Dong et al. 2022). Motivation encompasses intrinsic and extrinsic needs that shape learners’ willingness to participate and engagement in learning activities. GenAI can support key motivational needs, including autonomy, competence, relatedness, and identified regulation. From an SDT perspective, learners are more likely to sustain engagement in IDLE when these basic psychological needs (BPNs) are satisfied (Yin and Fathi 2025). Accordingly, perceived GenAI support and BPN satisfaction are central to understanding learner engagement in GenAI-assisted learning contexts (Reeve 2013; Wu et al. 2025). Therefore, the study aims to investigate the impact of GenAI perceptions and acceptance on EFL learners’ engagement in IDLE environments, with a focus on the mediating roles of self-efficacy and motivation. Specifically, this study offers two novel contributions. Theoreti- cally, it advances current understandings ofGenAI-assisted IDLE through SDT. Practically, this research identifies cognitive and motivational factors that can be leveraged to enhance EFL learn-
ers’ GenAI-assisted IDLE learning. These insights can inform the design of instructional activities and GenAI-based tools to cultivate more positive learner perceptions and to promote sustained engagement in IDLE environments.
2  Literature Review
2.1  Theoretical Framework: The Self-Determination Theory
Self-Determination theory (SDT) (Deci and Ryan 2002) explains human motivation and personality development through three basic psychological needs (BPNs): autonomy, competence, and relatedness. As a motivation-based framework, SDT provides a valuable theoretical lens for examining student engagement in GenAI-assisted IDLE. Autonomy refers to individuals’ perceived control over their actions and goals, relatedness stresses meaning- ful interaction with others or learning activities, and competence denotes learners’ perceived capability to successfully perform tasks (Akbari et al. 2015).
SDTexplains how psychological needs, motivation, and learners’ perceptions interact within social and cultural contexts (Ryan and Deci 2017). While SDT has been widely applied in face-to- face, blended, and online learning settings (Chiu 2024), Ryan and Deci have explicitly called for research examining how emerging educational technologies support BPNs and foster engagement. However, digital support in GenAI-assisted learning contexts remains underexplored.
2.2  Perceptions and Acceptance of Generative Artificial Intelligence and Learning Engagement in the Informal Digital Learning of English Environment
From the SDTperspective, GenAI-enabled digital support shapes student engagement by satisfying learners’ motivational needs. GenAI refers to technologies trained on large datasets and powered by large language models that enable interactive com- munication with human users. Self-directed GenAI perceptions and acceptance, mainly through GenAI chatbots, afford greater learner autonomy and interactivity, leading to enhanced IDLE engagement (Guan et al. 2024). GenAI tools, such as GPT-5, can simulate authentic communication scenarios and provide learners with personalized feedback, enriching both receptive and productive language practices (Escalante et Al. 2023; Zou et al. 2025b). These affordances make GenAI not merely a
technological extension of IDLE but also a means of providing learners with dynamic, generative, and vivid language learning experiences.
In self-directed IDLE contexts with limited teacher guidance, engagement plays a critical role in learners’ learning outcomes. A growing body of research has explored how learners engage with language through digital means in IDLE settings (Liu et al. 2024a, b;Liu et al. 2025a,b). For instance, behavioral engagement in IDLE is reflected in learners’ sustained exposure to authentic English input through videos, games, and social media (Liu et al. 2024a). Emotional engagement is promoted by the informal and self-paced nature of IDLE, which helps reduce language anxiety and foster positive learning attitudes (Zou et al. 2025b). Cognitive engagement manifests in learners’ capacity to plan, select, and evaluate learning pathways independently, often supported by digital tools for metacognitive reflection (Goyal 2025). Together, these findings suggest that IDLE provides fertile ground for multidimensional engagement, although its depth varies with learners’ digital literacy and motivation (Zheng et al. 2024).
As an emerging form of IDLE, GenAI provides key affordances that support EFL learners’ engagement, including dynamic dialogue, personalized generation, co-construction, and reciprocal scaffolding. (Huang and Zhang 2025; Jiang and Zhao 2025). First, GenAI’s dynamic dialogue engages learners in real-time, context-sensitive exchanges that meet students’ autonomy needs; For example, GenAI-based 3D simulations promote active meaning negotiation through immersive interaction and immediate feedback. Instead of viewing GenAI platforms as machines, students tend to perceive them as friends, peers, or tutors. In this anthropomorphized GenAI-assisted IDLE context, learners exercise greater agency and invest more effort in engagement through immersive human-GenAI interactions (Xie et al. 2024). Second, GenAI supports personalized content generation and meaning co-construction. Large language models such as iFlytek and GPT-5 can produce tailored texts or multimodal materials to help learners grasp abstract concepts and collaboratively build logical arguments (Karri et al. 2025). This comprehension support enhances learners’ critical thinking, thereby increasing their willingness to engage in GenAI- scaffolded IDLE interactions. Finally, GenAI provides learners with reciprocal support, such as contextualized examples, quick lexical clarifications, and immediate collocation feedback, fulfilling learners’ need for relatedness by fostering a sense of social support and partnership. Consequently, learners would devote themselves more to interacting with GenAI in IDLE.
Recent studies have emphasized the potential of GenAI tools to enhance each dimension ofstudent engagement in IDLEenviron- ments (Baiand Wang 2023;Pan et al. 2025a,b).However, the exact psychological mechanisms through which GenAI influences engagement in the IDLE environments remain unexplored. Self- efficacy and motivation are two essential psychological factors that affect students’ perceptions and acceptance of GenAI to shape their IDLE engagement. Self-efficacy is a construct that can affect students’ choices, persistence, effort, and language learning achievement in the GenAI-assisted IDLE context, while motivation is a variable that can affect how learners invest in GenAI-assisted IDLE language learning behaviors and efforts (Darvin and Norton 2023; Liu et al. 2024). Therefore, this study
bridges this gap by examining how self-efficacy and motiva- tion mediate the relationship between GenAI perceptions and acceptance and Chinese EFL learners’ IDLE engagement.
2.3  Self-Efficacy as a Mediator
From the SDT perspective, self-efficacy reflects learners’ per- ceived competence, referring to their confidence in successfully performing language learning tasks through interaction with GenAI tools (Ryan and Deci 2017). Learners who feel competent are more motivated to engage in learning tasks, leading to higher achievement levels. Thus, self-efficacy not only directly influences learners’ engagement but also mediates the relation- ship between GenAI perceptions and acceptance and learning engagement.
In EFL contexts, self-efficacy is a robust predictor of learners’ engagement in both online and IDLE environments (Wu 2023; Zheng and Xiao 2024). Learners with higher self-efficacy tend to exhibit greater persistence, deeper cognitive engagement, and stronger autonomy in self-directed IDLE activities (Zheng and Xiao2024).Given the task-specific nature ofself-efficacy,research in L2 contexts further suggests that learners’ confidence varies across language skills. GenAI-assisted IDLE has been commonly conceptualized as productive (e.g., writing and speaking) and receptive (e.g., reading and listening) AI-mediated Informal Digi- tal Learning of English (AIIDLE) based on task types. Productive AIIDLE involves active engagement in language output tasks supported by GenAI, whereas receptive AIIDLE entails exposure to English input with GenAI assistance (Lee and Cho 2025; Feng and Yang 2025). Prior research has shown that skill-specific moti- vational beliefs and anxiety in receptive domains are differentially related to language achievement (Mills et al. 2007).Extending this line of inquiry, a distinction can be drawn between productive self-efficacy and receptive self-efficacy, indicating that learners’ confidence in language production and comprehension may develop through partially distinct pathways and exert differential effects on engagement and performance. Both dimensions are essential for sustaining engagement in IDLE activities.
Technologically, GenAI tools can enhance learners’ perceived competence by providing immediate feedback, adaptive scaffold- ing, and personalized guidance throughout language learning processes. In addition, engaging in prompt formulation and refinement enables learners to clarify their learning goals and reg- ulate interactions with GenAI, which strengthens their sense of control and task-specific competence (Woo et al. 2025). For recep- tive self-efficacy, AI-generated explanations, paraphrases, and comprehension checks may strengthen learners’ input processing and enhance learners’ confidence in understanding authentic language materials. For productive self-efficacy, prompt-guided output generation, automated feedback, reformulation sugges- tions, and iterative revision support help reduce cognitive load and reinforce learners’ belief in their ability to produce accurate and appropriate language output.
2.4  Motivation as a Mediator
Based on SDT, motivation includes both intrinsic and extrinsic motivations in shaping how individuals engage in learning
(Kashefian-Naeeini et al. 2024). From the intrinsic motivation perspective, motivation arises from the fulfillment of BPNs, which drive intrinsic motivation and sustained engagement (Deci and Ryan 2002). When learners feel in control of their learning, experience mastery, and establish meaningful connections, they are more likely to engage in tasks for inherent satisfaction rather than external rewards (Hassan et al. 2020). In EFL learning, GenAI technologies can serve as a catalyst for promoting learners’ BPNs.
Extrinsic motivation involves engagement in an activity for the purpose of attaining externally regulated outcomes, such as rewards or incentives provided by GenAI in the IDLE context (Kashefian-Naeeini et al. 2024). Studies have shown that the use of digital learning tools, including GenAI, can positively influ- ence motivation both intrinsically and externally by satisfying these BPNs (Chan and Lee 2023; Zadorozhnyy and Lee 2024). GenAI-based tools offer students opportunities for individualized interaction, enhancing both intrinsic motivation by making learning enjoyable and extrinsic motivation by providing rewards such as instant feedback and progress tracking (Szabó and Szoke 2024). AsGenAI technologies become more integrated into IDLE, they have the potential to enhance learners’ intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, resulting in increased engagement in EFL learning activities.
In technology-enhanced language learning, motivation is a key predictor of engagement, particularly in IDLE contexts. He et al. (2023, 1) suggest that students’ “endogenous motivation of learning” can be enhanced due to positive perceptions of GenAI-related learning experiences. In addition, students with clearer self-concepts, such as an ideal L2 self or ought-to L2 self, are more inclined to engage in digital language learning (Lee et al. 2022). This suggests that motivational constructs rooted in learners’ self-identity are critical to sustaining engagement in GenAI-supported environments. Similarly, students with a well-defined ideal L2 self exhibited a higher willingness to engage in out-of-class communication, indicating that a vivid self-concept can drive long-term motivation in IDLE (Lee and Lu 2023). Therefore, motivation is likely to play a mediating role between GenAI perceptions and acceptance and learning engagement.
2.5  Chain Mediation Effect of Self-Efficacy and Motivation Between Perceptions and Acceptance of Generative Artificial Intelligence and Chinese EFL Learners’ Engagement in Informal Digital Learning of English
According to SDT, satisfaction of BPNs motivates learners to engage in IDLE activities. Their intrinsic motivation may not necessarily translate into actual engagement behaviors if they lack confidence in their capacity for engaging in IDLE activities (Alharfi and Alamer 2025). To date, research has largely focused on the chain mediation effect of self-efficacy and motivation on students’ engagement with GenAI-assisted EFL learning in formal classroom settings (Waluyo and Kusumastuti 2024). Scant research has examined their mediating roles in GenAI-supported IDLE contexts, which place greater demands on learners’ self- regulated learning. Research has demonstrated that students with
high self-efficacy are more likely to engage in self-regulated learning, such as goal setting, task planning, self-monitoring, and self-evaluation. These behaviors are essential for effective engage- ment in GenAI-supported IDLE tasks, where learners must actively manage real-time feedback and personalized learning pathways. Moreover, intrinsic motivation has been demonstrated to drive sustained self-directed learning and engagement in IDLE activities. Learners with higher self-efficacy are more likely to develop stronger self-determined motivation and demonstrate greater persistence in overcoming technical challenges, such as hallucinations, misinformation, and connectivity issues, thus maintaining self-directed engagement in GenAI-assisted IDLE (Komarraju and Dial 2014).
Therefore, our study attempts to examine the chain mediation effect of self-efficacy and motivation between students’ per- ceptions and acceptance of GenAI and their engagement in IDLE through SDT. Enhanced self-efficacy subsequently fuels autonomous motivation, as SDTposits that a heightened sense of competence supports intrinsic motivation and identified regula- tion. Motivation thus operates as the self-regulatory engine that transforms efficacy beliefs into sustained learning behaviors— driving perseverance, resilience, and goal commitment when learners refine prompts, iterate outputs, and overcome technical or linguistic challenges. Therefore, these interlinked mechanisms provide a coherent rationale for the serial mediation chain (GenAI → Self-efficacy→ Motivation → Engagement).
2.6  Research Hypotheses
According to SDT, this increased self-efficacy satisfies the need for competence, which, in turn, generates intrinsic and identified motivation. When learners perceive GenAI-supported activities as pathways toward their envisioned English-using identities, motivation istranslated into sustained behavioral, emotional, and cognitive engagement. The directional order, self-efficacypreced- ing motivation rather than the reverse, is both theoretically and empirically supported. In SDT, competence satisfaction enables autonomous motivation; by contrast, reversing the path would imply that desire alone produces confidence before any mastery experience occurs, which contradicts theoretical assumptions and observed learning dynamics. Based on these theoretical foundations and the assumed directionality among the variables, the following hypotheses are proposed:
Hypothesis 1. Perceptions and acceptance of GenAI positively predict EFL learners’ IDLE engagement.
Hypothesis 2. Self-efficacy mediates the relationship between EFL learners’ perceptions and acceptance of GenAI and IDLE engagement. Specifically, perceptions and acceptance of GenAI positively predict self-efficacy, which in turn positively predicts EFL learners’ IDLE engagement.
Hypothesis 3. Motivation mediates the relationship between EFL learners’ perceptions and acceptance of GenAI and IDLE engagement. Specifically, perceptions and acceptance of GenAI positively predict motivation, which in turn positively predicts EFL learners’ IDLE engagement.

3  Research Methods
3.1  Participants
The data for the current study were collected from 466students at Chinese universities using a convenience sampling method. The online questionnaire was distributed via Wenjuanxing platforms between June 16and July 20, 2024, prior to the final examination period. Tofacilitate distribution, the first author collaborated with 15 English language instructors, who shared the questionnaire link with their students across different institutions. To ensure data quality, responses that were incomplete or submitted in an unusually short time were excluded. This cleaning process yielded 369valid responses for analysis.
Ethical approval was obtained from the university ethics com- mittee prior to data collection. Participants were informed of the study purpose, procedures, voluntary participation, confidential- ity, and research-only data use, and provided electronic informed consent via a mandatory confirmation item. Table 1summarizes participants’ demographic characteristics.
3.2  Instruments
The study employed four sections to measure key variables in the questionnaire. GenAI perceptions and acceptance in IDLE were assessed using Liu et al.’s (2024a) 21-item, 5-point Likert scale covering five dimensions: perceived ease of use, perceived usefulness, attitude, behavioral intention, and actual use. EFL learning engagement was measured with Eerdemutu
et al.’s (2024) 9-item scale addressing behavioral, cognitive, and emotional engagement. Self-efficacy was evaluated using Wang et al.’s (2014) 31-item, 7-point scale measuring confidence in listening, speaking, reading, and writing. Given that Wang et al.’s questionnaire assesses self-efficacy among Chinese EFL learners, we adapted it to align with established classifications of GenAI-assisted IDLE tasks by distinguishing receptive self- efficacy (listening and reading) from productive self-efficacy (speaking and writing) (see Section 2.3). Motivation was assessed through Lamb and Arisandy’s (2020) 15-item,6-point Likert scale examining intended effort, ideal L2 self, ought-to L2 self, and self-assessed proficiency.
Prior to formal analysis, all scales were theoretically and con- textually screened to enhance readability and ensure alignment with GenAI-assisted IDLE. Conceptually redundant, double- barreled, or contextually misaligned items were removed to reduce respondent burden and improve validity. Cross-cultural adaptation procedures, including translation, back-translation, and expert review, were conducted to ensure clarity and cultural appropriateness.
3.3  Data Analysis
SPSS 26.0 was used for preliminary analyses, including the Harman single-factor test to assess common method bias, descriptive statistics to profile the sample, and reliability checks via Cronbach’s alpha coefficients. Subsequently, AMOS 26.0 was used to conduct confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) and SEM. Specifically, CFA was conducted to examine the factor structure of the four latent constructs: perceptions and acceptance of GenAI (PAGAI), self-efficacy (SEFF), motivation (MOTI), and EFL learning engagement (ELEN).
The second-order measurement model was assessed for inter- nal consistency, convergent validity, and discriminant validity. Specifically, composite reliability (CR) and average variance extracted (AVE) were calculated at the first-order factor level to

evaluate convergent validity, while the Fornell-Larcker criterion was used to assess discriminant validity among the second-order constructs. Following validation of the hierarchical factor struc- ture, SEM was employed to test the hypothesized relationships among the four latent constructs: PAGAI, SEFF, MOTI, and ELEN. All SEM analyses were performed using the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) method. Model fit was evaluated using several indices: the chi-square statistic (χ2/df; acceptable
< 5), parsimony goodness-of-fit index (PGFI; acceptable >.50), comparative fit index (CFI; acceptable >.80), Tucker-Lewis index (TLI; acceptable >.80), root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA; acceptable <.08), and standardized root mean square residual (SRMR; acceptable <.08) (Friedrich et al. 2018). Finally, the mediating effects of self-efficacy and motivation were tested using bootstrapping procedures with 5,000 resamples and 95% bias-corrected confidence intervals.
4  Results
4.1  Preliminary Analysis
Preliminary analyses include descriptive statistics, skewness and kurtosis tests, and internal consistency reliability. The skewness values for the four variables were between −0.364 and .185,and the kurtosis values ranged from .392 to 1.218,falling for normal distribution. The Cronbach’s alpha coefficients for all constructs exceeded the threshold of .600, indicating satisfactory internal consistency of the scales.
In addition, the Harman single-factor test was conducted by SPSS 26. A total of six factors with eigenvalues greater than 1with the first factor accounting for 45.243%of the variance, which is below the recommended threshold of 50% (Zhuang et al. 2023). This indicates that there is no serious common method bias in the data.
4.2  Confirmatory Factor Analysis
A pooled CFA with the full dataset (N = 369) validated the four-factor measurement model. This CFA-based refinement was conducted after an initial theoretical and contextual screening of items and therefore reflects only the final psychometric trimming stage of the measurement development process. Following an iterative refinement process (Teo et al. 2013) that examined mod- ification indices against established benchmarks, two items from the PAGAI scales with high modification indices were removed to improve model fit, conceptual coherence, and construct validity. In a similar vein, during the refinement process, four from the ELEN scale and two items each from the SEFF and MOTI scales were deleted from the model to ameliorate the unidimensionality and clarity of their respective constructs. The revised model fit for each respective construct is shown in the tables. Subsequent to these modifications, another round of CFA analysis was conducted on the revised measurement model, illustrating a great improvement in fit indices. The fit indices for the revised model are also displayed in Table 2.
As shown in Appendix 1, all factor loadings were statistically significant and demonstrated strong item reliability. Additionally,
Table 2 presents the model fit indices for each construct within an acceptable model fit. Specifically, the χ2/df ratio (2.572) and other indices—CFI = .899, PGFI = .688, TLI = .892, RMSEA
= .065,SRMR= .064—are within the acceptable range (Wheaton 1987; Ou 2025). Furthermore, the model fit for each construct was satisfactory: PAGAI = 3.095, ELEN = 3.206, SEFF = 3.292, and MOTI = 3.563. Therefore, the measurement model pro- vides a solid foundation for subsequent structural equation modeling.

Convergent validity was assessed by examining the Composite Reliability (CR) and Average Variance Extracted (AVE) for each construct, as shown in Appendix 1. The CR values ranged from .809 to .939, all exceeding the recommended threshold of .700, indicating good internal consistency and reliability (Hair et al. 2010). Additionally, the AVEvalues ranged from .566to .800, with all values above the .500 threshold, suggesting that more than 50% of the variance (Zhuang et al. 2023). As shown in Table 3, as all bold values meet this criterion, the results confirm that discriminant validity has been satisfactorily achieved for all constructs.

5  Discussion
This study examined the mechanism of the effects of GenAI perceptions and acceptance on Chinese university students’ EFL learning engagement in the IDLE context and the serial mediating roles of self-efficacy and motivation.
The results demonstrated that GenAI perceptions and acceptance had a direct influence on EFL learners’ IDLE engagement (t
= 4.441, p < .001). The strong and significant correlation (r

Nevertheless, it is worth noting that direct effects account for only 45%of the total effects. 5.2  Motivation as a Mediator
In our study, motivation emerged as a significant mediator between positive GenAI perceptions and acceptance and EFL learners’ engagement in IDLE (t = 4.066, p < .001). The strong explanatory power of motivation (70.4%)lends more credence to this hypothesis.
Our findings aligned with Zou et al. (2025a)’s research, which suggested that GenAI might enhance students’ autonomy and, consequently, their motivation and engagement in IDLE activi- ties. Positive GenAI perceptions and acceptance enhance learn- ers’ intrinsic motivation by making learning more meaningful, enjoyable, and autonomous (Renfeng et al. 2025). Nonetheless, it deviates from Liu et al.’s research (2024b), which found that
ought-to-L2-self (a motivational sub-construct) had no significant predictive effects on AIIDLE. The reason may lie in the com- position of participants across academic year levels. Compared with Liu et al. (2024b)’sstudy, whose participants involved 34.20% of freshmen, our sample is constituted of a larger proportion of freshmen (69.4%). For the freshman, College English Test (CET) 4 or CET6 seems to be a more stressful burden for them mainly because of their unfamiliarity with the exam standards and transition pressure faced with a sudden academic transition period between high school and college English. Bycomparison, sophomores and juniors have had prior experience of attending CET4 or 6 or have even passed these exams, which significantly reduces their extrinsic motivation needs throughoutght-to-L2- self.

5.4 Self-efficacy and Motivation as Chain Mediation Effects
In our study, the path from PAGAI to SEFF was also significant (β = .446, t = 7.282,p < .001), indicating that PAGAI positively
influences SEFF. Consistent with Li et al.’s finding (2025), the interplay between learners’ personal attributes, such as self- efficacy beliefs, motivations, and cognitive strategies, is pivotal in determining their engagement and control over their learning experience, particularly when interacting with GenAI-supported tools. The effectiveness of motivation regulation is not only dependent on the specific motivational challenges but also deeply rooted in the individual’s efficacy beliefs (Ji et al. 2025). Our data analysis result also resonated with Trautner and Schwinger’s (2020) study, which further elucidated the connection between self-efficacy and motivation regulation, demonstrating that self- efficacy beliefs directly influence the success of self-regulation strategies. The effectiveness of motivation regulation is not only dependent on the specific motivational challenges but also deeply rooted in the individual’s efficacy beliefs. Thus, these beliefs empower learners to deploy strategies that bolster their motiva- tion, enhancing their capacity to engage with learning tasks and achieve desired outcomes. In GenAI-supported environments,
where adaptive feedback is readily available, learners with strong self-efficacy are more likely to utilize these tools effectively, reinforcing their motivation and deepening their engagement.
Additionally, the significant mediation effects of self-efficacy and motivation are different from Li and Chiu’s (2025) study, which revealed that needs satisfaction (motivation) may fully mediate students’ perceived support and their emotional engagement in the AI chatbot-supported learning context. One possible explana- tion is that emotional engagement in the teacher-led formal class- room setting differs from that in the informal classroom context in our research. The GenAI-IDLE contexts necessitate students’ stronger self-efficacy beliefs to engage in IDLE activities. Aside from the motivation factor, self-efficacy beliefs in utilizing GenAI also had salient impacts. The joyful emotional engagement hinges largely on the students’ capability to solve technological issues and interact with the GenAI anthropomorphic interface (Wang et al. 2025).
6  Limitations and Future Research
This study has several limitations that must be acknowledged. First, as the findings were based on the self-reported data, future studies should consider a mixed-methods design, integrating qualitative interviews or classroom observations. Second, this cross-sectional study may not fully capture how psychologi- cal constructs evolve. Future research can adopt longitudinal designs, such as latent growth modeling, to investigate the developmental trajectories of GenAI-supported learning across different stages of EFL acquisition. Third, convenience sampling may introduce bias and limit the generalizability; stratified or ran- dom sampling would help enhance the sample representativeness and external validity. Fourthly, although this research adopted the TAM framework, the construct of PAGAI reflects learners’ subjective perceptions, acceptance, and behavioral intentions toward GenAI rather than their actual usage behaviors in IDLE. Future studies should combine self-reports with objective data to reveal students’ real use of GenAI in IDLE activities. Finally, although the model illustrated an acceptable fit, it did not fully meet the satisfying model fit benchmarks. Further refinement and additional indicators, such as emotional and cognitive load factors, should be considered, as they may strengthen its explanatory power to meet the standards for a satisfying model.
7  Conclusion and Implications
This study examined the relationships among GenAI perceptions and acceptance, self-efficacy, motivation, and EFL learners’ engagement in IDLE. SEM results revealed three key findings:
(1) GenAI perceptions and acceptance and motivation exerted significant direct effects on engagement; (2) self-efficacy and motivation mediated the relationship between GenAI percep- tions and acceptance and engagement; and (3) self-efficacy influ- enced engagement only indirectly through motivation, forming a significant chain mediation pathway.
From a theoretical perspective, this study extends SDT to GenAI-assisted IDLE by showing that learners’ perceptions and acceptance of GenAI satisfy BPNs. While self-efficacy reflects
perceived capability and shapes initial engagement willingness, SDT conceptualizes it as a distal antecedent whose effects on engagement are primarily mediated by motivation. By contrast, autonomous motivation derived from BPNs’ satisfaction func- tions as a proximal determinant of engagement. The stronger mediating role ofmotivation thus supports SDT’score proposition that need satisfaction influences behavior mainly through moti- vational quality rather than self-efficacy appraisals alone (Sweet et al. 2012).
Pedagogically, the findings highlight the importance of strate- gically integrating GenAI into instructional design to create autonomy-supportive, feedback-rich, and personalized learning environments. Increasing GenAI-supported self-directed tasks can maintain IDLE participation. Moreover, automated prompt- engineering platforms can enhance learners’ confidence and meaningful engagement, while scaffolded autonomous learning designs foster intrinsic motivation, such as enjoyment and curios- ity (Alfares 2025). Finally, GenAI-based adaptive systems that tailor task difficulty to learners’ behavioral patterns and learning trajectories can address individualized motivational needs and further sustain engagement.
