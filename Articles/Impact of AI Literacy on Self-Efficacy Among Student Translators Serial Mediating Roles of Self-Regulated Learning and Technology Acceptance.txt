 ABSTRACT The recent advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) technology has revolutionized the languagetranslationindustryandunavoidablyexertsanimpactonstudenttranslatorsinmultipleways.Inthis context, this study investigated the antecedents of student translators’ self-efficacy in AI-enhanced contexts, an underexplored area in current academic research. Data were collected via validated quantitative scales from 446 undergraduate student translators across eight public universities in China, who self-reported their AI literacy, technology acceptance, self-regulation, and self-efficacy in leveraging AI tools for translation tasks. Results indicate that AI literacy, technology acceptance, and self-regulation positively predicted translationself-efficacy.Furthermore,AIliteracyservedasapositivepredictoroftechnologyacceptanceand self-regulation in AI-assisted translation, while technology acceptance positively predicted self-regulation. Additionally,technologyacceptanceandself-regulatedlearning(SRL)actedaspartialmediatorsbetweenAI literacyandtranslationself-efficacy,andtheserialmediationpathway‘‘AIliteracy→ technologyacceptance → SRL → translation self-efficacy’’ was confirmed. This research offers theoretical and practical insights for translation education research and pedagogy.

II. THEORETICAL FOUNDATION: SOCIAL COGNITIVE THEORY
This research is grounded in Social Cognitive Theory (SCT), developed by seminal psychologist Albert Bandura. Central to SCT is the ‘‘triadic reciprocal determinism,’’ positing that personal cognitive processes (e.g., self-efficacy beliefs), environmental factors (e.g., physical resources and social feedback),andbehavioralmanifestations(e.g.,actionchoices and execution outcomes) function not through unidirectional causality but as a dynamically interacting system [13], [14]. A critical mechanism bridging cognition and behavior within this framework is self-regulation: individuals actively man- age their learning through iterative cycles of goal setting, processmonitoring,strategyadjustment,andoutcomereflec- tion [15], [16]. By synthesizing cognitive processes, social interaction, and behavioral regulation, SCT has emerged as a canonical framework for explaining human learning, moti- vation, and behavioral change—with particular relevance to exploring the integration of educational technology into lan- guage instruction [17], [18].
Within SCT, self-efficacy emerges as the most central con- struct,definedas‘‘beliefsinone’scapabilitiestoorganizeand execute the courses of action required to manage prospective situations’’[19].Fourexperientialsourcesunderliethedevel- opment of self-efficacy: direct mastery experiences (past successes),vicariousexperiences(observingmodeledbehav- iors’ outcomes), verbal persuasion (social support/feedback), and physiological-affective states (reduced anxiety and posi- tive emotional arousal) [20], [21]. In the era of AI, students’ learning ecology has undergone profound transformations, withagrowingnumberoflearnersintegratingAItoolsacross diverse learning scenarios [22]. As social and environmen- tal resources, AI tools can deliver real-time feedback and

III. LITERATURE REVIEW AND HYPOTHESES DEVELOPMENT
A. AI LITERACY AND LEARNING SELF-EFFICACY Against the backdrop of exponentially expanding AI appli- cations in educational contexts, scholars have turned their attention to learners’ AI literacy. Conceptually, it refers to an individual’s comprehensive competencies in critically evaluatingandcollaborativelydeployingAItools[27].AIlit- eracyencompassesfourcoredimensions:(1)awareness—the ability to identify and understand AI; (2) usage—the capac- ity to proficiently apply AI tools to complete tasks; (3) evaluation—the competence to analyze, select, and critically evaluate AI tools and their outcomes; and (4) ethics—the capability to recognize responsibilities and risks associated with AI technology use [28].
Learners’ AI literacy is closely associated with their aca- demic self-efficacy, which has been empirically evidenced. Illustrative of this, Zhang et al. found that AI literacy signif- icantly predicted self-efficacy in AI-enhanced EFL learning, with AI learning self-efficacy serving as a full mediator between AI literacy and willingness to communicate among Chinese college EFL learners [24]. In a parallel investiga- tion, Pan and Zhang explored factors influencing second language (L2) writing motivation among Chinese university students in AI-mediated writing environments. Their results indicated that AI literacy positively predicted writing self- efficacy, while writing self-efficacy partially mediated the association between AI literacy and writing motivation [29]. Collectively, these findings confirm that AI literacy func- tions as a robust positive predictor of language learners’ self-efficacy; however, none of these investigations were con- ducted specifically within translation education. Zhang and Doherty investigated Chinese student translators’ AI liter- acy and revealed that while students exhibited proficiency in AI translation knowledge and tool utilization—coupled

B. SRL AND LEARNING SELF-EFFICACY
SRL represents a core construct underpinning diverse facets of the learning process [31]. It denotes learners’ capacity to actively regulate cognition, motivation, and behavior toward learning goals through various activities [32]. Numerous empirical investigations have verified the influence of SRL on academic self-efficacy. For instance, Cho and Kim con- firmed a positive association between self-efficacy beliefs and SRL strategy utilization among Korean high school EFL learners [25]. Xu et al. surveyed Chinese college EFL learners in smart classrooms, showing that SRL exerted a positive effect on self-efficacy. They further identified self-efficacy as a full mediator between SRL strategies and learning engagement [33]. Parallel evidence emerges from Almayez et al.’s study of Saudi college EFL learners using online language applications: their analysis not only revealed SRL as a positive predictor of EFL learning self-efficacy but alsoindicatedthatSRLmediatedthelinkbetweenmotivation and self-efficacy [34]. With the integration of AI technology into education, SRL has expanded to encompass higher- order self-regulation, requiring learners to critically assess AI outputs and balance human-machine collaboration [35]. Such advanced regulatory capacity is particularly critical in high-skill domains such as translation. Synthesizing these empirical findings derived from different language learning contexts, the following hypothesis is advanced:
H2:SRLpositivelypredictstranslationself-efficacywithin AI-enhanced learning environments.
C. TECHNOLOGY ACCEPTANCE AND LEARNING SELF-EFFICACY
Technology acceptance is another potential antecedent of learning self-efficacy. Davis et al.’s Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) [36] is acknowledged as a robust theory for interpreting users’ acceptance of technological systems [37]. TAM comprises four key constructs: perceived usefulness (the extent to which users cognitively assess the benefits derived from using a technology), perceived ease of use (users’ evaluation of the effort needed to operate the technol- ogy), attitude toward use (users’ overall evaluative judgment ofthetechnology),and behavioralintention (users’readiness foractualusage)[38].WithinTAM,perceivedusefulnessand easeofusecollectivelyshapeusers’attitudetowardaspecific technology and their behavioral intention to adopt it; attitude toward use, in turn, exerts a direct influence on behavioral intention, which ultimately translates into actual technology usage [39].
Empirical research has examined the interplay between learners’ technology acceptance and learning self-efficacy, yet the literature presents inconsistent findings. In general learning contexts, Le analyzed Vietnamese undergraduates’ acceptance of ChatGPT and its impact on academic self- efficacy. The results indicated that students’ attitudes toward ChatGPT directly predicted academic self-efficacy, whereas perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness exerted only indirecteffectsonacademicself-efficacyviaattitudestoward the technology [40]. Within EFL contexts, Huang et al. investigated the effects of Chinese university EFL learners’ generative AI acceptance, perceived teacher enthusiasm, and self-efficacyontheirEFLwell-being.Theirfindingsrevealed that generative AI acceptance positively predicted learners’ language learning self-efficacy across both productive and receptive skills [41]. In contrast, Yulian et al. found no significant effect of augmented reality technology accep- tance on English learning self-efficacy among Indonesian university students enrolled in English for specific pur- poses courses [26]. Given these unresolved inconsistencies in the relationship between technology acceptance and self- efficacy, coupled with the lack of attention to the association betweenstudenttranslators’acceptanceofAItechnologyand their translation self-efficacy, further empirical investigation is warranted. Building on this body of research, the present study proposes the following hypothesis:
H3: Student translators’ acceptance of AI technology pos- itively predicts their translation self-efficacy.

Across diverse settings, studies converge on AI literacy’s predictive power: Pan et al. demonstrated its impact on perceived ease of use, perceived usefulness, and behavioral intention among Chinese scholars using AI-assisted liter- ature reading [43]; Gedik et al. identified AI literacy as explaining 17.5% of the variance in positive AI attitudes among individuals in Turkish universities [44]; and Salhab confirmed a direct effect of AI literacy on generative AI acceptance in a sample of Chinese undergraduates [45]. A. PARTICIPANTS AND PROCEDURE
This study employed a quantitative approach to examine the antecedents of student translators’ self-efficacy in AI- enhanced contexts. Data were gleaned through administering an online questionnaire survey, with participants recruited through a hybrid sampling strategy combining convenience sampling and teacher referrals. The final sample comprised 446studentsfromeightuniversitiesacrossChina,withdemo- graphic characteristics distributed as follows: (1) Regional and institutional diversity: two universities in North China, three in Central China, two in Southwest China, and one in South China; (2) Gender distribution: 87 males (19.51%) and 359 females (80.49%); (3) Age range: 17–25 years (Mean =20.47, SD=1.33); (4) Academic standing: 55 freshmen (12.33%), 155 sophomores (34.75%), 185 juniors (41.48%), and 51 seniors (11.44%).

The researchers contacted translation fac- ulty members at each university via WeChat or email and requested their assistance in distributing the questionnaire. Collaborating teachers shared the questionnaire link via course-specific WeChat groups, and a two-week data col- lection period was implemented, with one reminder sent to improve response rates.
B. INSTRUMENTS
A composite online questionnaire was used for data collec- tion,consistingofsixsections:(1)aninformedconsentform;
(2) demographic information, including gender, age, univer- sityaffiliation,andtypesofAItoolsusedfortranslationtasks;
(3) AI literacy scale; (4) technology acceptance scale; (5) SRL scale; and (6) translation self-efficacy scale. All items measuring the four constructs were rated on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = ‘‘totally disagree,’’ 5 = ‘‘totally agree’’).
1) AI LITERACY MEASURES TheArtificialIntelligenceLiteracyScaleforChineseCollege Students (AILS-CCS), developed by Ma and Chen [27], was used to assess participants’ AI literacy. This scale comprises four dimensions: awareness (4 items; e.g., ‘‘I understand the definition of artificial intelligence’’); usage (3 items; e.g., ‘‘I amproficientinusingAItools’’);evaluation(4items;e.g.,‘‘I can evaluate the limitations of different AI tools’’); ethics (4 items; e.g., ‘‘I always adhere to ethical principles when using AI tools’’).
2) TECHNOLOGY ACCEPTANCE MEASURES
Participants’ acceptance of AI technology in translation contexts was measured based on Davis et al.’s [36] and Teo’s [56] scales regarding perceived usefulness and ease of use. To adapt to the context of this study, the original items were modified accordingly: ‘‘computers/system’’ and
‘‘work/job’’ were replaced with ‘‘AI tools’’ and ‘‘translation work,’’ respectively, and contextual clarifiers (‘‘when I am doing translation work’’) were added where necessary. The adapted AI Technology Acceptance Scale—Translation con- tains 8 items (4 items per dimension). Sample items for each dimension are as follows: perceived usefulness (‘‘Using [AI tools] will improve my [translation] work’’); perceived ease of use (‘‘I find it easy to get [AI tools] to do what I want them to do [when I am doing translation work]’’).
3) MEASURES FOR SRL IN AI-ENHANCED TRANSLATION Student translators’ SRL was evaluated using an adapted ver- sion of Xia et al.’s four-item scale [57], originally developed for chatbot-assisted learning contexts. The original scale was modified by replacing ‘‘learning English’’ and ‘‘chatbot’’ with ‘‘doing translation work’’ and ‘‘AI tools,’’ respectively. A sample item is: ‘‘When [doing translation work] with [AI tools], I will normally try to identify the knowledge that I do not understand well.’’
4) AI-ENHANCED TRANSLATION SELF-EFFICACY MEASURES An adapted version of the Translating Self-Efficacy Scale (TSE), originally developed by Yang et al. [58], was used
to measure participants’ self-efficacy in AI-assisted transla- tion. This 15-item scale comprises three factors: Efficacy in Internal Competence (EIC, 9 items), Psycho-physiological Components(PPC,3items),andEfficacyinExternalCompe- tence (EEC, 3 items). The contextual modifier ‘‘When doing translationworkwithAItools’’wasaddedtothebeginningof each section of the original scale. Sample items for each fac-
tor are as follows: EIC (‘‘I can identify subtle word meaning differences’’); PPC (‘‘I can work hard to become a compe- tent translator’’); and EEC (‘‘I can overcome difficulties in translation technology use’’).
Tomitigaterespondents’cognitiveload,oneexpertlinguist was invited to translate the English scales into Mandarin Chinese—the mother tongue of all participating students. Another linguist then conducted back translation to ver-
ify accuracy. Subsequently, an educational psychologist was requested to judge the face validity of the translated scales. Following expert review, a pilot study was administered to
37 student translators (8 males and 29 females), who were presented with the Chinese versions of the four scales and asked to evaluate item clarity and understandability. Based
on synthesized feedback from experts and pilot participants, corresponding revisions were made to the scales.

VII. DISCUSSION
The present study investigated the antecedents of trans- lation self-efficacy among Chinese undergraduate student translators in AI-enhanced learning environments, with a specificfocusonthreekeyconstructs:AIliteracy,technology acceptance, and SRL. Additionally, it examined the dynamic interplay among these variables and systematically analyzed their direct and mediating effects on translation efficacy. In this section, the major findings are interpreted and dis- cussed in light of the theoretical framework of SCT and relevant empirical literature.
The results show that participants’ AI literacy directly and positively predicted their self-efficacy in leveraging AI tools for translation tasks. As a bridge connecting individuals’ cognitive capacities with technological environments, this direct effect aligns with the proposition of SCT that envi- ronmental resources are converted into self-efficacy through individual capabilities [66]. Specifically, students with high AI literacy demonstrate deeper comprehension of and profi- ciency in utilizing AI tools to accomplish translation tasks. Through ‘‘mastery experiences’’ [20], they accumulate suc- cessful translation outcomes, thereby bolstering the efficacy belief: ‘‘I can efficiently complete translations using AI tools.’’ AI literacy serves as an essential element in mod- ern education, equipping students to ‘‘navigate and succeed in a technology-driven world’’ [67]. Technological profi- ciency serves as a critical enabler in fostering students’ learning confidence during AI-integrated educational activ- ities. The predictive effect of technology acceptance on SRL reflects an ‘‘environment-behavior’’ interaction in SCT [13]: upon recognizing AI tools’ value and ease of use, students may proactively develop AI-assisted learning plans (e.g., lever- aging AI for glossary expansion during translation) and monitortheeffectivenessoftoolusage,consequentlyenhanc- ing SRL [52]. This SRL process may ultimately enhance translation self- efficacy [33].
Another key finding of the present study is the chained mediation effect among the four variables: ‘‘AI literacy → technology acceptance → SRL → translation self-efficacy.’’ This pathway unveils a cascading mechanism of self-efficacy formation in AI-empowered environments: AI literacy first enhances technology acceptance, creating favorable environ- mental conditions for SRL; driven by positive technological attitudes, students actively utilize AI tools for self-regulation (e.g., AI-based translation quality assessment), ultimately elevating translation self-efficacy through SRL practices. This process aligns closely with Bandura’s triadic recip- rocal model of SCT, wherein personal factors or beliefs (i.e., AI literacy and translation self-efficacy), environmen- tal evaluation (i.e., technology acceptance), and behavioral regulation (i.e., self-regulation in translation) dynamically interact [13]. When student translators possess high AI lit- eracy, they are likely to perceive AI tools as useful and easy to use, subsequently leveraging them for self-regulation by setting revision goals—thereby potentially enhancing their self-efficacy in translation through vicarious and mastery experiences.

IX. CONCLUSION
The advent of AI technology turns out to be both a boon and bane for language teaching and learning, and it exerts profound impacts on the translation industry. For student translators, the optimal approach lies not in total avoid- ance of AI tools but in resourceful utilization to streamline translation tasks and enhance translation efficacy. Against this backdrop, this study conducted a preliminary inquiry into the antecedents of student translators’ self-efficacy in AI-augmented contexts and tested a hypothesized model involving the interplay of AI literacy, technology acceptance, SRL, and translation self-efficacy. To enhance student trans- lators’ self-efficacy, higher education translation programs are recommended to cultivate their AI literacy, foster their technology acceptance, and strengthen their self-regulation capabilities in translation tasks.
