Background
The ongoing advancement of generative arti cial intelligence (GenAI) technologies has presented higher education  institutions  (HEIs)  with  unprecedented  opportunities  and  challenges.  In  particular,  following the emergence of OpenAI s ChatGPT in November 2022 and Google AI s Gemini in December 2023, there have been widespread discourses in academia regarding the potentialities and risks of integrating GenAI technologies in pedagogical practices, such as teaching, learning, and research, including assessment, feed - back, and student engagement (Tam, 2024). Furthermore, the wrongful use of GenAI technologies by stu - dents  and  their  adverse  impacts  on  learning  and  assessment  practices  have  garnered  considerable attention from higher education scholars (Zhang et al.,  2024). Studies suggest that students  increasing dependence on and unethical use of GenAI technologies for completing assignments may aggravate assess - ment challenges, such as assessment security and originality of students  work (Ardito, 2025; Ghimire et al., 2024b; Luo, 2024). It is concerning that, when students use GenAI technologies to produce their work that does not genuinely reflect the standard level of knowledge and competencies that HE envisions, more chal - lenges may stand ahead for HEIs to uphold assessment validity and academic integrity in their systems (Dawson  et  al.,  2024).  Nonetheless,  scienti c  evidence  on  how  HE  students  perceive  and  experience GenAI  in  connection  with  academic  integrity,  originality  of  students   work,  and  assessment  validity remains underexplored. Few conceptual studies have discussed that the wrongful adoption of GenAI may exacerbate challenges associated with the validity of assessment practices. However, most of them were focused on HE contexts belonging to the United States and Australia, where many universities have formu - lated publicly available guidelines on GenAI use in assessment practices (Luo,  2024; Morjaria et al., 2023; Perkins et al., 2024). Further, the available literature from the Global South, where policy and literacy gaps persist, has primarily focused on ethical practices of GenAI in learning and research, with limited attention

Literature review
Assessment and GenAI use in higher education
Assessment as a fundamental part of the HE course cycle is discussed as the evaluation process designed to operate a complex ecosystem of academic standards, support students  learning, and measure their progress 
in knowledge, skills, and capabilities (Fischer et al., 2024). It is broadly agreed among scholars that assess - ment demands learners  active engagement in cognitive processing to meet both the learning objectives 
and assessment targets, ranging from basic information recall to complex analytical, evaluative, and creative capabilities (Toker & Akgun, 2025). Within the HE courses, assessment tasks require students to engage in a range of formative and summative writing activities to support the judgment of their performance, both lower-order and higher-order thinking skills.
Research has shown that the current advancement of AI technologies has been instrumental in simplifying assessment practice by overcoming assessment challenges, most notably linguistic barriers, across disciplines (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2025). Similarly, it is argued that the integration of GenAI technologies in writing activities can signi cantly alleviate writer s block and enhance creativity as well as the quality of assessment (Yan, 2023).  However,  there  is  a  growing  concern  that  HE  students  may  misuse  GenAI  technologies  in writing activities, such as homework, assignments, and research, due to the capability of GenAI to autono - mously generate human-like content in various ways, including both textual and visual content (Gonsalves, 2025). This, as a result, might downplay the assessment requirements in HE (Nikolic et al.,  2025). Scholars have expressed concerns that the unacceptable usage of AI technologies in assessment may call assessment validity into question in HE (Dawson et al., 2024). Although assessment validity itself remains a troublesome construct in HE, and its critical examination falls outside the scope of this article, we argue that assessment 
is valid only when students are fairly engaged in assessment practices to meet the standards of HE course cycles through an intended level of performance and achievement (Corbin et al., 2025; Dawson et al., 2024).
Understanding what constitutes fair assessment practices in the context of GenAI is a pressing concern across HEIs. Yet, there is limited evidence regarding how HE students  increasing dependence on GenAI tech - nologies and the normalisation of their misuse as assistance could gradually result in tension with the purpose of assessment systems within HEIs.
Concerns of academic integrity and originality of student work
Academic integrity is conceptualised as fundamental ethical standards or moral values that students, tea - chers, and members of the academic community should comply with in academic activities. These values require  all  members  of  academic  communities,  including  administrators,  educators,  and  learners,  to uphold honesty, trust, fairness, respect, responsibility, and courage to sustain integrity in academic activities (International Center for Academic Integrity, 2021). In other words, the violations of these fundamental moral values in any form of dishonesty, including cheating in assessments, are discussed as threats to academic integrity  (Fajt  &  Schiller, 2025;  Ghimire  et  al., 2024a).  Several  studies  have  shown  that  students   rapid uptake and misuse of GenAI technologies, such as ChatGPT and Gemini, in assessment have imposed signi  - cant challenges to academic integrity within HEIs that have intensi ed the challenges of AI-assisted cheating and plagiarism (Ghimire et al., 2024b; Hutson, 2024). In this respect, research has consistently emphasised the urgent need for HEIs to update their academic integrity policies (Perkins & Roe,  2024). Furthermore, it is feared  that  students   misuse  of  GenAI  technologies  in  content  generation  and  the  submission  of  such content as their own original work without proper attribution of sources and inadequate modi cation under - mines established norms around originality, authorship, and academic integrity (Salvagno et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2025).
These studies highlight the concerns about GenAI misuse in assessment practices and its potential nega - tive implications for academic integrity values and the originality of student work. In particular, the above literature suggests that GenAI malpractices by students not only compromise the ethical value of student work but also misreflect the original value of their efforts, thoughts, creativity, and intellectual contributions that HE aims to cultivate through assessment requirements.
Attitudes towards GenAI use in assessment
Many studies have reported students  mixed attitudes towards GenAI use in assessment, suggesting that the 
 ndings could broadly compromise the existing understanding of the originality of student work and aca - demic integrity principles. For example, in a survey conducted among students from various disciplines of 
a university in Hong Kong (Chan, 2024), students were found to have been unsure about subtle misuses 
of AI tools in academic writing (in terms of generating ideas and modifying AI-generated content, and sub - mitting with acknowledgement of AI use), indicating that they had much confusion about whether their use 
of AI in assessment potentially enabled academic misconduct. Similarly, a recent study by Corbin et al. (2025) at an Australian university reported teachers and students  confusion regarding what constitutes legitimate 
and illegitimate uses of GenAI technologies in assessment practices. In their study, both teachers and stu - dents considered only copying and pasting GenAI-mediated content in assessments as unethical practices. 
In another study, Kim et al. (2025) investigated students  perspectives about GenAI use in academic writing after they were involved in ChatGPT4-assisted writing tasks designed by the authors. In the study, students appreciated GenAI as a multitasking writing assistant that helped them generate the initial draft of their writing, which they could further work on, modifying, editing, and adding their thoughts. These students further observed that although the use of GenAI, as an active virtual tutor and peer, enhanced their pro - ductivity, performance, and affective domain across various stages of the writing process, they expressed concerns that time constraints and over-reliance on GenAI had negative impacts on higher-order thinking skills and deep learning abilities.
Together, these studies illustrate the diversity of educators  and students  perspectives and experiences of using GenAI in assessment, along with unfolding challenges that may stand ahead for HEIs to ensure the principles of academic integrity, originality of student work, and assessment validity. Nonetheless, a conso - lidated body of knowledge is lacking in the literature regarding how these tripartite dynamics of assessment ecosystems interact with one another in the context of GenAI. Therefore, this study addresses the aforemen - tioned gap through a critical examination of Nepali HE students  evolving perspectives, experiences, and per - ceived impacts of GenAI in assessment practices.
Methodology
Research context
Nepal is home to 17 universities, both central and provincial, and seven autonomous medical academies, all 
of which are offering degree programmes from the undergraduate to doctoral levels. A total of 1,432 HEIs fall under these universities and academies, and they have enrolled 633,053 students in the academic year 2023—2024 (UGC, 2024). Notably, Tribhuvan University (TU), the oldest of all, alone accounts for 491,299 stu- dents, representing 77.61% of total enrolment, and plays a signi cant role in shaping the academic and assessment landscape of the country (UGC,  2024, p. 1). These HEIs operate under annual, semester, and tri - mester systems, with considerable variation in assessment policies across universities and disciplines.
Assessment practices in Nepali HEIs are largely reliant on conventional formats: paper-based sit-in exam - inations conducted under centralised monitoring. Most universities run  nal examinations through desig - nated exam centres, while internal evaluations (project work, presentations, quizzes, term papers, concept notes,  reviews,  research  papers)  are  left  to  the  discretion  of  departments  or  instructors.  During  the COVID-19 pandemic, however, these practices faced unprecedented disruption (Baral et al.,  2024). While some universities suspended examinations, others adopted alternative methods such as online exams or home-centre assessments. These temporary arrangements exposed systemic gaps that include inconsistent technological  infrastructure,  underdeveloped  digital  assessment  strategies,  and  a  lack  of  preparedness among faculty (Baral & Ghimire, 2025; Thapaliya et al., 2023). These structural shortcomings are further com - pounded by institutional weaknesses in upholding academic integrity. In a recent policy analysis, Dahal and Eaton (2025) revealed that although the UGC and 10 universities have established some integrity-related provisions, only a few have formal integrity policies in place. Even where such policies exist, the emphasis tends to fall on punitive responses rather than on cultivating a culture of ethical academic practice.
The need to revisit assessment practices is further highlighted by persistent quality concerns. Baral (2021) identi ed centralised governance, outdated curricula, and limited pedagogical training as structural barriers 
that hinder academic credibility. A pass rate of only 30.1% at Tribhuvan University and 30% at Nepal Sanskrit University (UGC, 2024, p. 2) indicates not only academic underperformance but also possible misalignments 
in curriculum, instruction, and evaluation.
Although, prior to our 2023—2024 interviews, universities did not have comprehensive or clearly articu - lated policies on the responsible use of AI tools, one limited provision existed at Tribhuvan University. The Secretariat of the TU Senate and Executive Council (2023), in its regulation, stated that any AI-generated content detected by plagiarism-checking software shall not be accepted in dissertations, theses, articles, or reports (Section 10.3). Most recently, the Government of Nepal (2025) introduced an AI policy that imagines the establishment of an AI Excellence Center to enable universities, academic and research bodies, and edu - cational institutions to conduct studies, research, and national-level initiatives related to AI, yet it remains largely silent on responsible AI use.
Against this backdrop, the emergence of GenAI tools, as suggested above, has introduced new opportu - nities and challenges for assessment integrity, originality, and validity. As Nepali HEIs begin grappling with the implications of GenAI, a better understanding of existing assessment cultures becomes imperative for designing future-ready, ethically grounded evaluation frameworks.
Materials and procedures
In this study, we generated overall study data by conducting longitudinal interviews (in three phases) with 
10 undergraduate and 10 graduate students from three Nepali universities. This approach was chosen to broadly capture the evolving nature of HE students  attitudes toward and interactions with GenAI, as well as their concerns associated with academic integrity, originality, and validity of assessment practices. We recruited participants through a purposive sampling approach to gather diverse perspectives on the use 
of GenAI in assessment. To involve participants in this study, we obtained informed consent from all partici - pants to conduct semi-structured interviews with them over three semesters of their academic courses. Before commencing the interviews, we briefly explained the purpose and the nature of our study, along with research questions, to all the participants individually. To answer our research questions, our longitudi - nal interview guidelines involved several open-ended questions to draw the required dataset for this study. For this purpose, Galletta s (2013) ideas provided the guidelines to design primary questions, sub-questions, and probes to generate in-depth discussions on this study s key concerns — academic integrity, originality of student work, and assessment validity.
In the  rst phase, we asked the students,  How have you utilised GenAI in writing activities?  and followed up  with  questions  regarding  potential  opportunities  and  risks  of  GenAI  technologies  in  HEIs  to  gauge immediate user responses. In the second phase, the students were asked,  How have the ways compared 
to the  rst phase how you utilised GenAI in writing changed over the time  and follow-up questions if they had any concerns in terms of acceptable and unacceptable use of GenAI technologies. Finally, in the third phase, we asked them,  How do you think the use of GenAI throughout these three semester courses has shaped the knowledge, skills, creativity, and capacity that you have been supposed to master/acquire?  and other follow-up questions regarding the impacts of GenAI use on assessment practices. Using phone calls or video conferencing tools, such as Zoom, Google Meet, and Facebook Messenger, all the interviews were conducted individually in Nepali between July 2023 to December 2024. Each interview lasted between half an hour to one hour. These interviews were audio-recorded on laptops or mobile phones, translated, and transcribed into English by capturing a verbatim account based on word-for-word as well as free translation methods to ensure cultural appropriateness. We repeatedly listened to the audio recordings and collabora - tively resolved our disagreements on word choices to ensure participants  intended meaning as well as the accuracy and reliability of translation. Then, strictly following the guidelines recommended by Braun and Clarke  (2006)  for  inductive  thematic  analysis,  the  data  were  systematically  coded,  collated,  categorised under potential themes, analysed, and interpreted along with compelling extracts in the report. Throughout 
the  coding  procedures,  we  reread  the  transcribed  verbatim  multiple  times,  had  regular  meetings,  and resolved  our  disagreements  on  codes  by  collaboratively  reinterpreting  and  rede ning  codes  until  we reached  consensus.  This  allowed  us  to  both  enhance  coding  reliability  and  identify  the  centrality  of themes in our report. To ensure the con dentiality and anonymity of participants, as suggested by Cohen 
et al. (2017), the real names of all the participants and their educational institutions have been anonymized 
as shown in Table 1.
Results
Students  experiences of and attitudes toward GenAI technologies in assessment
In this study, we found that students have varying experiences and attitudes towards the integration of GenAI technologies in assessment. In the  rst phase, almost all students attributed their use of GenAI to 
an  emerging  societal  trend  driven  by  curiosity  and  excitement.  In  this,  many  students  admitted  using these tools as a virtual assistant to experiment with, generate basic information and ideas (S3, S4), para - phrase them to resolve writer s block (S1), write content (S3), and frame assignments in a manageable way to accomplish their project work, presentation, term papers, concept notes, and research writing (S7, S10). During the second phase, most of these students reported that GenAI technologies gradually func - tioned as saviours (S6, S7, S18), which they variously identi ed as feedback generators (S8, S16), task expe - diters (S2, S12), efficient summarisers (S1), and intimate writing partners (S5) that could help them break down complex ideas, re ne their understanding, and expedite research activities. For example: 
As part of our assignments, we are required to write literature reviews and reflection notes on research papers every week. You know that tools like ChatGPT allow us to upload our  les, books, and articles, and instantly sum - marize whatever we want. What s more, they even help us organize, re ne, and proofread our writings. They are the real saviours in times of need, for students like us who are hugely occupied with jobs and need to accomplish multiple tasks. (S11, Interview II)
In the above excerpt, S11, who represented the voice of the majority of students, highlighted the time and effort-saving capabilities of GenAI tools to reduce cognitive loads required for reading and writing tasks, most notably through summarisation, feedback, and content writing facilities. This, many students indicated, eventually expedited assessment tasks.
Further, in the third phase, while the majority of students  GenAI use in assessment activities mostly remained the same as in the second phase, many students, particularly those at the Master s and MPhil levels, identi ed GenAI tools as task enhancers (S1, S11) not only to synthesise complex ideas but also organ - ise and enhance random thoughts and research data input to accomplish their assessment tasks in an advanced manner. Ambiguous attitudes towards GenAI (mis)use and its impacts on academic integrity and originality
Across all phases of the interviews, we found students  ambiguous attitudes towards what counts as accep - table GenAI use and whether their GenAI use compromised the values of academic integrity and the orig - inality of their work. In other words, the students expressed uncertainty about the misuse of GenAI in assessment  activities.  In  particular,  although  all  the  students  throughout  the  study  agreed  that  the blatant copying and pasting of AI-generated content and submitting such writing tasks without modi cation could be unethical, they were largely unaware of the subtle misuse of GenAI tools in assessments.
In the  rst phase, many students described that it was acceptable to use GenAI for brainstorming, para - phrasing, summarising, and writing initial content to clear writer s block, and start their project. In this phase, students, particularly belonging to Master s and MPhil levels, thought that GenAI use was acceptable to the extent that GenAI-mediated content could pass through plagiarism detection tools. In the second and third phase, they explained that the use of GenAI in assessment gradually shifted from personal habit to normal - ised practice among their peers for completing the entire assessment, highlighting its use as fair so long as they could rewrite or modify the content written by GenAI technologies, either based on their prompts or GenAI feedback. Surprisingly, both in the second and the third phase, the majority of students belonging to Bachelor s levels established their line of reasoning that rewriting the content  rst and then using other GenAI humaniser tools for multiple modi cations should not be regarded as threats to academic miscon - duct. When asked about why these students did not prefer to acknowledge the use of GenAI in assessment, they, interestingly, attributed the confusion either to the dearth of GenAI policies at their universities (S1, S9, S17,  S20),  a  lack  of  proper  guidance  from  their  professors  (S4,  S6,  S10,  S12),  or  reasoning  that  GenAI- mediated content were based on their original prompts (S1, S2, S8, S11, S12, S13, S15, S16, S18, S19, S20). For example: 
I always use my ideas to modify the content [ ] either by rewriting it in my new sentence structure or summar - izing it in my own words, and then asking GenAI tools to proofread and polish the content before submission. Why should I acknowledge GenAI when I use my data, apply my critical insights, become heavily engaged in my work, and do signi cant work on my own? (S2, Interview II).
It is much clearer that such voices further raised an important tension about the lack of AI literacy regarding how they utilised GenAI technologies to compromise honesty and fairness in assessment processes seemed 
to be heavily rationalised upon what they assumed as acceptable usage of GenAI.
Additionally, in the  rst phase, when we asked these students about how GenAI could impact the orig - inality of their work, they expressed their concerns that over-reliance on GenAI could deteriorate creativity, personal thoughts, and intellectual contribution in assessment tasks. However, in the second phase, students 
of Master s and MPhil levels associated the originality of their assessment tasks with technical compliance, whether their writing could pass the plagiarism detection tools. Secondly, they highlighted the original values  of  their  assessment  with  how  critically  and  creatively  they  could  modify  the  GenAI-mediated content. But for the majority of Bachelor s level students, originality was mostly a matter of modi cation through the use of other GenAI humaniser tools and alteration of words, syntax, and sentence structures. Nonetheless,  in  both  cases,  all  the  students  associated  the  originality  of  their  work  with  the  series  of prompts (from sketches to  nal production), from identifying research gaps, framing research questions, and generating literature themes, to revising their work. It indicates that students initially assumed their creativity  and  intellectual  contribution  remained  intact  as  long  as  they  engineered  the  prompts  and made  edits  to  the  AI-produced  text.  However,  this  assumption  was  questioned  in  the  third  phase  by some of the Master s and MPhil students, who recognised a tension between their personal voices and GenAI s resulting output. Some of them reported a sense of voice loss and reduced agency. For instance, S12 shared: 
[ ] Nonetheless, over time, I have become more confused about whether the logics I go for are really my own! The machine has really affected the way I take my positions, give prompts, and modify the content. I  nd myself lost in what these machines generate. (Interview III)
These reflections suggest a growing awareness that originality is not limited to producing texts but extends to sustaining one s creative presence and intellectual identity throughout the writing process.
Blurred lines between relieving affective burdens or eroding assessment validity?
In all phases of interviews, the majority of students repeatedly highlighted that the use of GenAI technol - ogies in assessment practices helped considerably relieve mental burdens required to handle their work, from deeply engaging in reading tasks (S5, S13, S19), drafting their writings (S9, S14, S15), and seeking feed - back from professors (S1, S11 S13), to revising and  nalising their work (S6, S8, S17). This, these students further suggested, helped them cope with reading and writing anxieties and improve learning outcomes 
in terms of grades. S17 shared: 
You might know well how hard it is to carry out multiple reading and writing projects every week. Lots of worries, right? Particularly for students like me who can t easily cope with coding and debugging issues that are too complex to understand and handle in writing. Showing one s report to professors and gaining criticism on that would frustrate anyone further. These tools are real guiding lights. (Interview II)
The  above  interview  excerpt  suggested  that  GenAI  technologies  could  be  instrumental  in  overcoming reading and writing stress that students experience owing to either linguistic, technical, conceptual, or cog - nitive limitations to ful l course requirements. Nonetheless, when asked about how con dent they were about meeting intended learning outcomes, in terms of mastering the skills and abilities envisioned by their courses, these students expressed their uncertainty and confusion. In particular, their voices indicated that the over-reliance on GenAI for summarisation of reading materials, content writing, and modi cation purposes gradually harmed their critical and analytical abilities. For instance: 
Initially, using ChatGPT to summarize reading materials that are mostly beyond my level felt like getting rid of my burden [ ] Now, I am in my  nal semester and working on my thesis, and I feel like I have lost my patience and criticality. I sometimes really worry about how I can handle piles of books, research papers, and study data on my own. (S15, Interview III)
It is much clearer that the given expression pointed out how over-reliance on GenAI could disengage stu - dents with authentic materials, leading to a reduced sense of self-efficacy and con dence in handling assess - ments that require critical reading and reasoning abilities. S16 added: 
It is true that if students like us keep skipping the critical thinking and manual effort involved in reading, writing, or research, we are bound to miss out on valuable learning and writing experiences. (Interview III)
In many respects, these examples illustrate the fact that the use of GenAI, on the one hand, can provide a false sense of reduced cognitive loads that students often experience for the time being. On the other hand, the ten - dency to avoid reading culture gradually can erode one s ability to comprehend authentic materials that are foun - dational to a deep understanding of disciplinary knowledge and criticality in assessment practices.
Discussion and conclusions
This study aimed to investigate university students  perceptions and experiences of GenAI use in assessment practices, with a major focus on tripartite concerns of academic integrity, originality of student work, and assessment validity. While these concerns are not new and have gained increasing traction in global scholar - ship, albeit in isolation (He et al., 2025) or conceptual (Dawson et al.,  2024; Luo, 2024), this study s contri- bution lies in the single body of empirical insights on how they interact with each other and impact the complex ecosystem of assessment practices in HE systems.
Our  ndings showed that HE students in Nepal had evolving experiences and perceptions of GenAI use 
in assessment. While these students initially linked GenAI tools with their curiosity and excitement, they 
gradually identi ed these technologies as brainstorming and paraphrasing tools to head start their project 
work, concept notes, term papers, and research writing. Students  reported information in the second and third phases indicated GenAI tools functioned as efficient saviours that helped them break down complex ideas, re ne their understanding, and expedite research activities. In particular, these students recognised GenAI technologies as an integral part of assessment processes, from brainstorming assessment ideas, identifying research gaps, generating research questions, writing literature reviews and  ndings, to proof - reading and  nalising the assessment. These  ndings, to a great extent, are consistent with prior studies 
that highlight the time and effort-saving capabilities of GenAI in education, in terms of overcoming the assessment challenges, including writer s block and linguistic barriers (Ghimire et al.,  2024b; Kim et al., 2025). However, these students  gradual awareness of GenAI hallucinations and the importance they high - lighted for cross-checking AI-generated content indicated these students  concerns regarding evolving threats that GenAI potentially could impose on education. Nonetheless, the temptation to over-rely on GenAI for cross-validation, summarisation of reading materials, feedback generation, and modi cation 
of  assessment  content  pointed  to  these  students   perceived  bene ts  of  GenAI  or  a  sense  of  lack  of viable alternatives.
One concerning  nding is that although the majority of students in this study accepted that they heavily relied on GenAI tools to write and modify content, many of them expressed confusion about whether the processes involved in the modi cation of the content compromised academic integrity. These students viewed only copying and pasting AI-generated content as a violation of academic integrity. While these 
 ndings are to some extent consistent with prior literature (Corbin et al.,  2025; Fajt & Schiller, 2025; Kim et al., 2025), the confusion regarding what is acceptable and unacceptable use of GenAI in assessment remains a pressing concern, as Hutson (2024) highlighted. It is worth noting that these students justi ed 
that they used their ideas and prompts to retain the originality of their work and denied the proper acknowl - edgment of GenAI use in assessments. Interestingly, these students attributed their failures to maintain transparency to the dearth of policy and the lack of proper guidance from their professors, which probably imposed barriers to the transparent usage of GenAI in assessment processes. This suggests that when indi - viduals devalue one s intellectual contribution and independent efforts (Luo,  2024), they naturally tend to normalise unethical behaviours, fall victim to academic malpractices, and downplay the values of academic integrity, including responsibility, transparency, and accountability in research writing (Tlili et al.,  2025). Further,  what  is  more  concerning  is  that  our  participants  had  drawn  their  self-assumed  framework  to justify the unacknowledged use of GenAI as acceptable. In this sense, our  ndings have serious implications regarding how students  self-assumed framework for acceptable GenAI use violates the traditional notion of academic integrity and the originality of student work, thereby pointing to adverse risks to the effectiveness 
and purpose of assessment practices in HE. Therefore, given the centrality of fairness, accountability, and the value of independent intellectual effort in assessment practices within education, it is paramount that HE institutions  in  Nepal  carefully  devise  regulatory  frameworks  that  motivate  students  to  engage  in  the thoughtful practice of GenAI technologies.
Further,  ndings from this study suggested that GenAI provided personalised interactive support for learning engagement for HE students, tailoring to the speci c needs of these students to effectively handle the perceived complexity of authentic materials and research papers, as part of the assessment requirement. Surprisingly, these students later described how they avoided a deeper approach to reading that resulted in a false concept of increasing con dence in their ability to handle multiple assessment tasks for the time being. But, in the long 
run,  they  gradually  realised  how  over-reliance  on  GenAI  for  a  swift  understanding  of  course  materials  and research papers resulted in super cial cognitive processing, deteriorating their critical reading and thinking abil - ities. Contrary to  ndings of prior studies (Pan et al., 2025), these students  over-reliance on GenAI for the sum - marisation  of  authentic  materials  to  expedite  their  learning  and  research  tasks  appears  to  have  adversely impacted  the  expected  learning  outcomes,  skills,  and  capabilities  envisioned  by  their  university  courses (Corbin & Walton, 2025). It should be acknowledged that assessment practices, both reading and writing, in higher education have a unique purpose to foster deep learning engagement, creativity, criticality, and reasoning among students. In light of these  ndings, we contend that if students are unaware of and devalue the function - ality of authentic readings (Baron & Mangen,  2021) and independent intellectual contributions in assessment practices to garner unfair advantage, HE will soon be presented with unforeseen challenges to retain the effec - tiveness and purposes of assessments.
In many ways, Nepali HE students  evolving views and experiences of GenAI in assessment practices, nonetheless,  indicated  a  clear  tension  between  ethical  reasoning  and  growing  awareness  of  GenAI misuse. These students rationalised ethical usage of GenAI and originality of their work in relation to sub - jective  judgements  and  text  prompts.  Interestingly,  they  accepted  over-dependence  on  GenAI  for assessment completion led  to a reduced sense  of personal voices and  agency among Master s  and MPhil students, which pointed to their implicit awareness of GenAI misuse and its undesired conse - quences  on  intellectual  capabilities,  including  creative  abilities,  critical  reasoning,  and  higher-order thinking skills.
In sum, our  ndings have greater signi cance in terms of informing HEIs about the unfolding prospects and challenges that GenAI poses to university course cycles, most notably across assessment systems. Further, our analysis suggests that, insofar as the unclear boundaries between what is ethical and unethi - cal practices of GenAI are based on subjective judgment, it is almost impossible to determine the extent to which students  prompts, GenAI-mediated outputs, and modi cations reflect originality of student work to meet the core purpose of the entire HE assessment systems. In light of our  ndings, we argue that stake - holders across HE systems have a greater responsibility to recognise the urgency of policy interventions and educators to support GenAI literacy programmes. In this, we encourage HEIs to set a minimum stan - dard in assessment policies to motivate students  responsible, accountable, and transparent usage and dis - closure of GenAI assistance in their work, and teachers to critically evaluate student work that prioritises authentic learning and writing efforts and sustains the effectiveness of assessment practices within HE systems.
