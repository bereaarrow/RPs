ABSTRACT
Artificial intelligence (AI) has transformed higher education. The research shows that university students use AI and acquire academic ideas to develop innovative solutions to their problems. However, there is limited research on how AI feedback helps students improve their idea implementation. This study was rooted in the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and the Social Cognitive Theory (SCT). The objective was to examine how the AI assessment feedback (AIAF), trust in AI (TAI) and AI literacy (AIL) influence university students' idea implementation skills (IIS). A descriptive survey design was used to collect data from 486 university students. The analysis revealed that AIAF significantly contributes to students' IIS at the university level. TAI was found to partially mediate the relationship between university students' AIAF and IIS. AIL was found to contribute to students' IIS individually. However, the interaction effect (AIAF*AIL) was not found to be a significant contributor to students' IIS. We found that the interplay among the study's variables (AIAF, TAI and AIL) positively enhances university students' IIS in solving their academic problems. It was recommended that students should use self-regulated learning in AI feedback to improve their achievements. In addition, the current study has several practical, research and policy implications.

1   |   Introduction
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a new field of study that helps stu - dents' self-assessment at the university level. University students frequently use AI for the sake of feedback in their academic tasks (Lee2023). They make this feedback personalised, action - able and precise (Hooda etal.2022). AI tools are based on ma - chine learning and natural language processing algorithms that analyse written material and provide feedback to improve its various aspects, such as grammar, clarity and coherence (Chang etal.2023).
AI  assessment  feedback  (AIAF)  has  the  potential  to  guide students  in  the  revision  process.  This  helps  them  to  make continuous improvements and refine their academic work, im - proving learning outcomes and academic performance (Kuleto 
et al. 2021). This literature supports that this type of feedback can help students think more creatively, outside the box and solve problems (Sidekerskienė and Damaševičius2023). As re- searchers, we found that various studies provide arguments in favour of developing ideas for managing student-related prob - lems (Habib etal.2024; Lin and Chen2024).
The role of AI in education is the product of the COVID pan - demic. Then, OpenAI worked to launch Generative Pre-trained Language Models. In this way, students started using its free ver - sions to get various study-related ideas (Kim etal.2022). AIAF may enable students to identify cognitive gaps, reorganise argu - ments and reiterate their initial thoughts. The research indicates that such feedback is essential for developing and implementing ideas (Banihashem etal.2024). But, the implementation of ideas depends on students' personal abilities as well (Jang etal.2022).

Students have a better chance of effectively incorporating AI tool feedback into their work when they trust the AI systems for feedback (Hooda etal.2022; Kaplan etal.2023). Trust in AI (TAI) helped students to use these tools freely and openly with - out any ethical responsibilities.
The success of this type of feedback and students' dependence on such tools shows their trust and confidence. In such a situ - ation, TAI got the central position to influence students' opin - ions  and  beliefs.  It  is  also  important  that  such  tools  should provide reliable, fair, accurate and valuable feedback (Yang and Wibowo 2022). In the absence of such trust, students ignored these tools and their feedback. This can reduce the value of the AI tool in the learning activities (Kaplan etal.2023). However, 
if students show trust in AI tools and their feedback, then these students can achieve their career targets by completing their ed - ucational degrees with distinction.
The studies on AI show that students' trust in AI determines how obedient and willing they are to follow its recommen - dations, which, in turn, enhances problem-solving, creativ- ity and learning outcomes (Lin and Chen 2024). Also, trust decreases both cognitive distance to the machine-generated feedback  and  leads  to  emotional  and  motivational  involve - ment in the learning process (Zhai and Nehm 2023). However, despite its importance, the mediating effect of trust between AIAF and the IIS remains understudied, and it is necessary to investigate how trust influences students' capacity to promote AI in education.
AI literacy is meant to have conceptual and hands-on practice 
of using AI tools effectively (Chiu et al. 2024; Ng et al. 2021). This kind of literacy can make students learn the effective and efficient use of such tools to get feedback for their class-related work, such as assignments and exams (Khurma et al. 2024). Students use AI tools to explore the solutions to their various problems. Previous studies have had a limited understanding of the interplay among AIL, TAI and AIAF in shaping creative ac - ademic behaviours. To illustrate, Kuleto etal.(2021) suggest that AIL is a key to utilising feedback; however, they do not discuss the practical applications of this concept in terms of ideas and implementation. This finding highlights the need for further re - search on the moderating effect of AI literacy.
Although there is an increased interest in the use of AI in edu - cation, a serious gap remains in the literature regarding the in - terplay of AIAF, TAI and AIL on students' IIS at the university level. Most of the available literature focuses on these variables' individual relation to academic success, but not on how they in - fluence the implementation of ideas. Few studies employ a holis - tic model that includes both mediators and moderators linking these concepts. Filling such a gap is essential, particularly in an increasingly digitalised educational age driven by innovation. Thus, this paper is valuable to fill the research gap because it postulates and tests an integrative model to examine the com - bined effects of AIAF, TAI and AIL on university students' IIS. The objective of the current study was to investigate the con - tribution of AIA to university students' IIS, with TAI and AIL mediating and moderating the relationship between university students' AIAF and IIS.
2   |   Literature Review
2.1 |   AI Assessment Feedback and Idea Implementation Skills
The  research  mentioned  that  for  higher  education  students, AIAF is a significant help to apply various ideas in their stud - ies (Topping et al. 2025). The immediate and constant nature of such individualised feedback helps students to clarify their weaknesses and develop their problem-solving skills. A study by Bećirović etal.(2025) emphasises the importance of AI feedback that provides timely, personalised evaluation and helps students develop  and  implement  their  ideas.  According  to  Demartini 
et al. (2024), feedback from these AI tools enhances the learning process by providing students feedback. This shows that such feedback provides sufficient help in finding various and alterna - tive solutions to these students.
The  research  supports  that  effective  and  ethical  use  of  AI and its feedback motivates students to think differently (Chiu 
et al. 2024). This feedback encourages university students to reconsider  and  rearrange  their  thoughts.  AI  tools  facilitate 
a  deeper  understanding  of  the  concepts  being  implemented. Increasing awareness and feedback enable students to become more  proficient  in  utilising  AIAF  for  idea  implementation (Almatrafi et al. 2024). The research supports the notion that AI assessment feedback is an essential factor in students' imple - mentation of ideas. Based on these arguments, the researchers developed the first directional hypothesis.
H1.  AI assessment feedback significantly contributes to univer - sity students' idea implementation skills.
2.2 |   Trust in AI, AI Assessment Feedback and Idea Implementation Skills
AI-based feedback is getting overwhelming important in higher education. Thus, the researchers reviewed an extensive litera - ture using various research databases and did not find any study that explored the mediating effect of TAI on university students' AIAF and IIS. However, a few studies investigated the nature of  relationships  on  these  constructs  in  separate  studies.  For example, Hooda et al. (2022) conducted a study on the use of AIAF to enhance students' academic performance and problem- solving skills. They found that feedback effectiveness depends on the level of trust in the AI system. Trusting AI, students can take actions and use this feedback to enhance their ability to implement ideas. This situation motivates students to develop creativity, which is crucial in AI-driven formative assessment of students (Zhai and Nehm2023).
The literature indicates that the impact of TAI on IIS following AIAF raises questions about how trust operates in the relation - ship between AIAF and IIS (Owan et al.2023). Trust-building towards AI tools also increases emotional involvement and cre - ativity among students, which further enhances their potential 
to integrate AIAF into academic work (Lin and Chen 2024). While TAI enables students to work with AI tools, trust will be crucial for utilising AIAF. Research also indicates that trust 


may serve as a supporting factor in the implementation of feed - back and ideas (Bećirović et al.2025). Similarly, learners who trust AI more tend to act based on its suggestions, leading to improved grades and the successful execution of ideas (Kaplan etal.2023). Based on the above arguments regarding the inter - actions among these variables, the following directional hypoth - esis was formulated.
H2.  Trust in AI positively mediates the relationship between university students' AI assessment feedback and their ability to implement ideas.
2.3 |   AI Literacy, AI Assessment Feedback and Idea Implementation Skills
The researcher found a few of the studies examining the indi - vidual relationships among AIL, AIAF and IIS at the university level. However, gaps persist in understanding the moderating role of AI literacy in the relationship between AIAF and IIS. For instance, Ng et al. (2021) theorised that AIL includes the knowledge of the concept and applications of AI. After analys - ing the data, they found an increase in the ethical use of AI. However, they did not establish a connection between the di - mensions, such as AIAF and IIS, leaving a gap that could be filled in future research. Guo(2024) has demonstrated that peer review aided by AI enhances feedback literacy. They did not ex - plore the correlation between feedback and the implementation 
of creative ideas.
Kuleto etal.(2021) found that AI tools can enhance the practice 
of providing feedback to students in higher education; however, they also state that AIL is essential to control students' imple - mentation of AIAF. However, the significance of AIL in im - proving skills in idea implementation has not been extensively studied. Chang et al. (2023) emphasised that feedback from AI advances self-regulation and goal-setting in learning. However, they paid no attention to the effect of AI literacy on the practice of generating creative ideas. It is also evident from the literature that AI tools may help increase creativity levels; however, the aspects of how AIL and IIS interact are not discussed in depth (Lin and Chen2024). This shows the need for future research, which could be carried out to address these gaps on AI literacy and related variables included in this study. To address this gap, the researchers developed the following directional hypothesis.
H3.  AI  literacy  significantly  moderates  the  relationship  be - tween university students' AI assessment feedback and their idea implementation skills.
3   |   Theoretical Background
Two theories guided this study. Both of the theories focus on the effective and ethical use of AI for helping students in their co- curricular activities. Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) the - ory fits into the current study and talks about the usefulness and ease of use of AI tools in education (Davis 1989). This operation - alises the study's first variable, AIAF, by providing students with confidence to use it in education. Perceived ease of use, which operationalises AIL, gives students confidence to manage AI systems in education. TAM hypothesises that a combination of trust and literacy is very important for students and this supports H1, which focus that AIAF can improve students' IIS.
The Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) discusses the self-efficacy and confidence students have to overcome academic hurdles (Bandura 1986). This aspect is also very important even for the case of implementing various educational ideas. In the present study, AI literacy serves as a form of self-efficacy, with students who have better AIL being more confident in using AIAF. This increased confidence contributes to the better implementation 
of their ideas. The SCT hypothesises that self-efficacy affects students' task and feedback orientation. When it comes to AI, the more AI-literate students there are, the more likely they are 
to trust and use feedback to improve their ability to implement ideas. Such an idea corresponds to the second hypothesis (H2), which posits that TAI mediates the relationship between AIAF and IIS. The third hypothesis (H3) examines AIL's role as a moderator of the relationship between AIAF and IIS, high - lighting the importance of self-efficacy in implementing ideas.

It shows how constructs of these theories (per - ceived ease of use, perceived usefulness and self-efficacy) link to AI assessment feedback, trust in AI, AI literacy and idea imple - mentation skills, and they are associated with H1, H2 and H3.

4   |   Methods
4.1 |   Research Design
The present study focused on investigating the contribution of AIAF, TAI and AIL to university students' IIS. This study was conducted on university students of a developing country where students have limited access to the internet and digital tools. Studies like this one are typically carried out under a positiv - ist research worldview (Ally 2024; Calzolari et al. 2025; Wasan et al. 2025). Following the positivist school of thought, a descrip - tive survey design was adopted in the current study, utilising cross-sectional survey data collection. This design is employed to study the specific situation or trends of a phenomenon at a given time and place, helping us understand how AIAF, TAI and AIL contribute to university students' IIS in the present tech-driven era. Thus, descriptive design with cross-sectional data collection was used in this study.
4.2 |   Participants
The participants in the current study were university students enrolled in master's programmes at public and private sector universities in Pakistan during the academic year 2024–2026. Inclusion and exclusion criteria were developed. Only students who were using AI in their academic tasks were considered. Students from master's programmes were chosen because they were relatively familiar with AI tools and recent developments in AI, having encountered them during their bachelor's studies. Bachelor's and PhD students were excluded due to unfamiliarity with tools and lower enrollment rates, respectively. Multi-stage, multi-method sampling techniques were used to select a sample of 486 university students. The researchers got support from the literature that this sample size is enough for descriptive studies (Kunselman 2024; Villamin et al. 2024). An overall summary of participants' characteristics is presented in Table 2.

It had two parts: the first covered their demographic information, including variables such as gender, discipline and locality. The second part consisted of four sub-scales. The items that measured these constructs were developed on a five-point Likert scale with options ranging from 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree). Initially, a pool of 36 items was developed, which was later sent to experts and piloted with 65 master-level students. Experts and piloted respondents provided feedback on the relevance of items to the study's objective and hypotheses, as well as on sentence structure and language. Based on their feedback, we modified certain items, and we removed overlapping and double-barreled items. The final questionnaire had 30 items. The reliability anal - ysis revealed that Cronbach's alpha for each sub-scale exceeded the 0.70 threshold, meeting the acceptance and suitability crite - ria for the scales (Bayır and Akel2024). The data were collected online using Google Forms. The final questionnaire was posted in Google Forms, and the link to the published form was shared with participants via WhatsApp and email. After sharing the links, participants were reminded twice, with a week between reminders. The data were collected during April and May 2025. The current study was approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of the School Education Department, Government of  Punjab,  Pakistan. The researchers suggest that universities must support and train their students in the use of AI-based tools that provide person - alised responses to enhance the implementation of ideas.
The mediation analysis for H2 shows that TAI partially medi - ates the relationship between AIAF and IIS. This finding aligns with Zhai and Nehm (2023), who found that trust in AI is cru - cial  for  students  to  act  on  AI-delivered  feedback  effectively. Conceptual work and meta-analyses on trust in AI indicate that students who are familiar with AI depend heavily on AI recom - mendations. They believe the AI systems are fair, transparent and accurate (Glikson and Woolley 2020; Kaplan et al. 2023). The current study's findings add to the literature by showing that students' trust contributes to the implementation of ideas in academic tasks. This implies that building trust in AI is essen - tial, but future studies should address factors of motivation and emotional engagement in the feedback-to-implementation pro - cess. The mediating effect of trust was not examined in previous studies, which mainly focused on either the immediate conse - quences of AIAF or isolated factors, such as AIL. This gap is addressed in our research, which demonstrates that partial trust mediates the relationship between AIAF and the implementa - tion of ideas. This extends the employability of SCT, which fo - cuses on the role of trust in students' self-efficacy in AIAF for successful IIS.
The third hypothesis aimed to examine the moderating role of AIL between university students' AIAF and IIS. The analysis revealed that AIAF and AIL contribute to university students' IIS at individual levels. However, their combined effect was not that effective. This may happen: focusing on both variables at a single point in time may lead to disengagement or a lack of focus when generating or implementing problem-solving ideas. One possible explanation is that our participants may already have a similar, relatively high level of AI literacy, reducing the variation needed to detect a moderating effect. The previous studies reported that AI literacy is unevenly conceptualised and measured.  This  makes  behavioural  effects  harder  to  observe 
(Almatrafi et al.2024; Chiu et al. 2024). These results indicate that although AIAF and TAI trust are significant factors in en - hancing students' implementation skills, the role of AI literacy may require further exploration.
7   |   Conclusion
The researchers developed this study in a higher education in - stitution in a developing country. The researchers' focus was to explore how AIAF, TAI and AIL may influence university students in successfully implementing ideas. The results af - firm that AIAF is essential for improving students' IIS skills, both  directly  and  indirectly  through  TAI.  Those  students who were confident in the AIAF were more ready to act on it, having improved their academic performance and creative thinking. This highlights the relevance of TAI as a mediator between feedback and implementation. Although AIL had a positive relationship with IIS, it did not moderate the AIAF- IIS  relationship  significantly,  an  unexpected  finding.  This indicates that even though AIL has worked in terms of inter - action with AI tools, it is not as significant in regulating the correlation between AIAF and the implementation of ideas. Furthermore, the current study extended the implications of SCT in the higher education context with the emerging trends 
in AI. A few of the results, like AIAF significantly predicting and contributing to university students' IIS, are significant contributions to SCT. However, there is still a need to exam - ine how SCT can contribute more to exploring the mediation and moderation roles of TAI and AIL in the higher education context.
8   |   Limitations and Future Directions
Despite the several significant findings, our study has a few lim - itations. Firstly, although the mediating role of trust in AI was par - tially supported, the mediation effect was weaker than expected.

This suggests that other factors might influence the process of AIAF to IIS. This limitation can be addressed by increasing the sample  size  or  by  including  participants  from  the  elementary or secondary levels. Secondly, AIL was found to be an insignif - icant moderator, which may limit the study's generalisability to 
a broader population. However, this limitation can be addressed by designing the study to examine the possible causes of the in - significant moderation effect. Lastly, the research was conducted quantitatively through a questionnaire. To complement the lim - ited knowledge regarding the extent to which AIL and the level of trust may influence the implementation of ideas, future research could employ a mixed-methods approach, gathering additional qualitative information to define students' perceptions of and ex - periences with AIAF and how they utilise it in IIS.
9   |   Implications
The present study had several significant implications for the - ory, practice, policy and research. We extended the implications of SCT in the context of higher education systems in developing regions, where technology is not readily available and students lack AI education skills. We found that students' trust in their ability to use AI to generate and implement ideas for problem- solving and academic success is prominent. Furthermore, the findings revealed that AIAF greatly increases university stu - dents' IIS. The individual contributions of TAI and AIL in uni - versity students' IIS are also significant predictors. With these findings,  educational  institutions  can  provide  students  with support and infrastructure to learn more deeply how to incorpo - rate AI tools into the learning process, enabling them to respond more actively to AIAF and further develop their problem-solving and creativity skills. Based on these findings, we strongly sug - gest that higher education institutions develop and implement institutional policies providing support to students in the eth - ical and efficient use of AI in education. Universities can also launch a series of sessions to train students to receive relevant feedback by improving their AIL skills. Future research is also recommended to examine the causes and factors underlying the partial mediation of TAI and the insignificant moderating role of AIL in the relationship between university students' AIAF and ISS.
