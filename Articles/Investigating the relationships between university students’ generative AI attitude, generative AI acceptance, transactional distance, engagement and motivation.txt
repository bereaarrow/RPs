Investigating the relationships between  university students  generative  AI attitude, generative  AI acceptance, transactional distance, engagement  and motivation
Fatma Gizem Karaoglan Yilmaz a, Ahmet Berk Ustun a, Ramazan Yilmaz a and Hatice Uzun b
aComputer Technologies and Information Systems, Bartın University, Bartın, Turkiye; bSafranbolu Şe k Yılmaz Dizdar Vocational School, Karabuk University, Karabuk, Turkiye

ABSTRACT
Students  need  to adopt  artificial intelligence  (AI) technologies  and cultivate positive attitudes to realize their potential fully. This study examines  the  relationships  between  university students   attitudes toward  generative  AI, AI acceptance,  their perceptions  of transac- tional  distance,  student  engagement,  and  course  motivation  in generative  AI-supported  learning  environments.  Using structural equation  modelling,  this study  analyzes  data  from university stu- dents  in AI-supported courses to examine how engagement,  moti- vation,  and  transactional  distance  influence  generative  AI acceptance  and  attitudes.  The findings  of the  study  show  that student  engagement  and  course  motivation  positively  influence students   perceptions  of transactional  distance,  and  these  factors significantly  shape  students   acceptance  and  attitudes  towards generative  AI. This study provides  valuable  information  for educa - tors and  policymakers developing  strategies  to increase  the  effec- tive  use  of  AI technologies.  The  acceptance  of  AI and  the development  of positive  attitudes  towards  AI can  contribute  to higher  academic  achievements  by  improving  students   learning experiences.

For instance,  autonomous  assessment  systems  can quickly and  accurately evaluate  exams  and  assignments.  This allows teachers  to  deal  with  more  students  on a one-to-one basis and improves the quality of teaching. Teachers can give feedback very late  or not  at  all, mainly due  to  the  high  number  of students. Using data  analytics and machine  learning algorithms,  teachers  can identify students   learning  tendencies  and  challenges  so that  teachers  can plan lessons more effectively (Ustun, Zhang, et al., 2023). Theoretical background  and hypotheses Students engagement  and course motivation
The relationship between student engagement and motivation can be better understood within the framework of Self-Determination Theory (SDT) (Deci & Ryan, 2000). SDT argues that  students   needs  for autonomy  and  competence  are  nurtured  when  they  actively participate  in learning  activities, especially in environments  enhanced  with technology powered  by generative  AI. This active  participation  can  act  as a catalyst,  transforming external educational  stimuli into intrinsic motivation.  In addition, if student  motivation  is defined as the degree of interest, energy and curiosity that students show in the learning process  (Martin, 2008), it becomes  clear  that  there  could  be  a significant  relationship between  student  engagement and motivation.  Although motivation  is often conceptua - lised as an antecedent of engagement, studies also suggest that the relationship between engagement  and  motivation  may be  reciprocal  and  dynamic. Ensuring students   active participation  in learning  processes  at  both  behavioural  and  cognitive  levels serves  as a mechanism that reinforces their perceptions of competence, autonomy, and task value, 
thus  creating  a foundation  for the  development  of intrinsic motivation  (Fredricks et al.,  2004; Reeve, 2012). Consequently, higher levels of participation lead to more internalised and  sustainable  motivation  for the  course,  providing  a theoretical  basis  for our  initial hypothesis.
H1: Students  engagement  will significantly predict their motivation
Students engagement  and transactional  distance
Moore  (1973) examine  transactional  distance  in three  dimensions:  learner  autonomy, dialog  and  structure.  Learner  autonomy  refers  to  the  learner s  own  capacity  to  learn, dialog refers to the two-way interaction of the learner with other learners and the teacher, and  structure  refers to the  flexibility of the  teaching  process.  The relationship  between these  dimensions  can  be  expressed  simply as follows. The perceived  transactional  dis- tance  for a student  with low learner autonomy  increases with greater  curriculum rigidity and/or  decreased  interaction  (Moore, 2019). From this point  of view, students  need  to have a high capacity for self-learning to overcome the difficulties they face in the teaching process  in a rigidly structured  teaching  environment  with limited dialog  opportunities. 
The level of the student s own learning capacity can be related to the level of engagement 
in the course. If the student s commitment to the course is simply considered as the effort 
to learn the subject, the more a student studies and practices a subject, the more knowl- edge  he/she  has  about  that  subject  and  the  higher  the  level of managing  complex information about that subject (Kuh, 2009). In addition, engaged students tend to interact 
and  share  information  with  each  other,  which  improves  the  quality  of their  learning experience (Bolliger & Martin, 2018). Similarly, teachers can encourage students to actively participate  in course  activities and  stay connected  to the  learning  community  through their interactions with students (Martin et al., 2018), and by being role models themselves, they  can  increase  students   engagement  in the  course  (Ustun & Tracey, 2020). In this context, students  commitment to the course is of critical importance to students  percep - tion of transactional  distance  (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Zhang, et al., 2024).
H2: Students  engagement  will significantly predict their transactional  distance perception Course motivation  and transactional  distance
The importance  of motivation  can be  seen  from the  students   intention  to enrol in the courses  to their experiences  in the  learning  process,  which may lead them  to drop  out without  completing  the  course.  A student s  motivation  to  learn  is based  on  intrinsic factors such as curiosity, interest and personal development (Tsai et al., 2018) or extrinsic factors such as a diploma, certificate, finding a job (Bayeck, 2016) may lead them to enrol in various courses. However, if student motivation does not continue at the desired level, 
the student may end up dropping the course. In particular, student motivation is linked to their  acceptance  of learning  tasks, the  effort and  time  they  put  in, their  persistence  in finishing tasks, and  their belief in their capability to navigate  learning  obstacles  (Bakar,  2014). If a student  loses motivation  in this process, he/she  loses the  desire to study and tends  to drop  the  course. In addition,  studies  show that  the  interaction  factor comes  to the fore in the successful maintenance of learning environments (Bolliger & Martin, 2018; Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Zhang, et al., 2024). It can be stated that students with high motivation will interact more with the teacher, course content and other students to perform in-class tasks and  activities and  will exhibit a more  participatory  behaviour.  Also, a highly moti- vated student will be eager to make the best use of and develop his/her capacity for self- learning  during  the  learning  process  to  accomplish  in-class tasks and  activities. In this context,  students   motivation  levels  are  related  to  their  perception  of transactional distance.
H3: Students motivation will significantly predict their transactional  distance perception Course motivation  and generative  artificial intelligence  acceptance
The use  of AI in the  educational  environment  can  contribute  to  increased  academic achievement by providing personalised learning, reducing teachers  workload, and allow-
ing  them  to  interact  with  more  students  (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Yilmaz, et  al., 2024). By 
integrating  AI into learning management systems, automatic  scoring and evaluation  can be done, and the materials used by students  and the activities they participate  in can be analysed based on learning analytics to reveal students  performance and behaviours and provide personalised recommendations (Chen et al., 2020). Therefore, the use of AI in the field of education has many advantages and facilitates the learning process in many ways. However, in order to achieve maximum efficiency from the use of AI tools in the teaching environment,  it can  be  ensured  that  students  accept  the  technology  (Prasad Agrawal,  2023). Students  level of acceptance of AI tools in the educational environment is essential 
in ensuring the optimum benefit that can be obtained from these technologies. Students  perception  of how  useful a technology  is and  how  easy it is to use  shows  the  level of acceptability  of that  technology  by students  (Ustun et al., 2024). Since students   percep - tions can be influenced  by various factors, their acceptance  of technology  can also vary accordingly.  For instance,  a  motivated  student  tends  to  exert  more  effort  to  achieve learning goals and is more eager to achieve those goals (Deci et al., 1991). In this respect, motivation  can  positively  affect  their  perception  of the  technologies  that  are  used  to achieve  these  goals.  So, adopting  and  effectively using  AI technology  may  be  closely related  to students   motivation  levels.
H4: Student  motivation  will significantly predict their  generative  artificial intelligence acceptance
Transactional distance and generative  artificial intelligence  attitude
Attitude refers to an individual s feelings, thoughts,  and behavioural  tendencies  towards a particular  object,  person,  or situation  (Aiken, 2002). This disposition  has  often  been found to be a strong predictor of intention to perform a behaviour (Banas et al., 2023). In this respect, AI attitude is critical in terms of determining the intention to benefit from AI. 
If one  considers  attitude  in terms  of education,  the  student s  attitude  in the  teaching environment  has  the  potential  to  directly  affect  the  student s  academic  success  and successful progress in the learning process (Mensah et al., 2013). Especially in the teaching process,  while the  student s  negative  attitude  towards  the  teaching  material  can be  an obstacle  to  effective  learning,  the  positive  attitude  towards  that  material  can  facilitate learning  (Mensah et al., 2013). Thus, the  importance  of the  student s  feelings, thoughts, and  behavioural  tendencies,  i.e. attitude  towards  using  AI in the  learning  environment, becomes  prominent.  Considering  the  benefits  of using  AI in education,  students  are expected  to develop  positive attitudes  towards  the use of AI in order for the benefits  to be optimal. This expectation generally turns into an obligation rather than an expectation, especially in online education. Because AI has started to be used as an individual assistant 
to enrich students   learning  experiences  in such environments  (Chen et al., 2023). In AI- supported  online courses  based  on data  mining and  learning  analytics, students  can be given individualised feedback on the educational videos and other learning materials they interact  with (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Yilmaz, et al., 2024). Therefore, using AI technologies  in different  learning  environments,  especially  online  learning  environments,  can  improve student interaction and learning experience. As a result, increasing student interaction in AI-supported  learning environments  may positively affect the student s  attitude  towards using AI.
H5: Students  transactional  distance  perception  will significantly predict their generative artificial intelligence attitude
Generative  artificial intelligence  acceptance  and generative  artificial intelligence attitude
The main prerequisite  for utilising a technology s  benefits  is students   acceptance  (Ustun et al., 2024). Factors such as ease  of use and  usefulness  of AI technologies  stand  out  as elements  affecting  the  acceptance  of educational  AI (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Yilmaz, et  al.,  2024). On the  other  hand,  students   knowledge,  skills and  attitudes  need  to  be  at  an adequate level in order to use technology safely, decisively and responsibly (Ferrari, 2012). Crompton et al. (2024) state in their study that one of the main obstacles to the use of AI in education  is that  negative  attitudes  towards  AI should  be  overcome.  This is because a positive  attitude  towards  using  AI brings  about  a behavioural  change  that  results  in increased motivation to interact with AI technology (Galindo-Dom nguez et al., 2024). This highlights the critical role that AI acceptance and attitudes towards AI play in obtaining its benefits. In this respect, it is essential to test and examine the relationship between these 
two factors based  on data  because  factors such as the  ease  of use and  usefulness  of AI technologies  have the  potential  to affect students   attitudes  towards  AI usage.  Students may  develop  positive  attitudes  towards  AI technology  and  facilitate  the  use  of the technology  when  they  adopt  AI technologies.  Conversely, they  may develop  negative attitudes and resistance or rejection towards this technology when they do not adopt it. If students   attitude  towards  AI is considered  as their perceptions,  beliefs and  behavioural tendencies  towards  AI technologies,  factors  such  as acceptance  of AI technologies,  i.e. ease  of use  and  usefulness,  can  positively  affect  students   perceptions,  beliefs  and behavioural  tendencies,  i.e. attitudes  towards  AI.

Method
Using a correlational  research  design,  this study explored  how changes  in the  indepen - dent  variables might  predict  changes  in the dependent variables.
Participants
The study  included  136 undergraduate  students  enrolled  in Visual Programming  II and Object-Oriented  Programming  II courses  at  a  state  university  during  the  Spring 2023—2024 academic  year. Among  these  participants,  84% (n = 114) were  male,  and 16% (n = 22) were female. Seventy-two students were in their second year of study, while sixty-four were in their third year. Their ages ranged between 18 and 24 years. They were all part  of  the  Department  of  Computer  Technology  and  Information  Systems.  All responses  were  complete,  and  no  missing  data  were  detected.  The adequacy  of the sample  size was evaluated  based  on  the  complexity  of the  structural  model.  A power analysis conducted via G*Power 3.1 indicated that with the current sample size of 136, the study achieved a statistical power of over 0.80 for a medium effect size at an alpha level of 0.05. Furthermore,  the  sample  size was  deemed  sufficient  for robust  the  Structural Equation  Modeling (SEM) estimations,  meeting  the  recommended  minimum  thresholds for stable  parameter  estimates  (Kline, 2005).
Tasks
This study  was carried out  in the  context  of Visual Programming  II and  Object-Oriented Programming  II courses  using  the  flipped  classroom  (FC) instructional  approach.  The research was carried out over a 14-week teaching semester, starting with an introductory session in the first week where students  were introduced  to the course material and the FC methodology.  The learning management system (LMS) was the required  platform for students to register for the course. The instructor uploaded lecture videos to the LMS each week, which students were expected to watch before attending the in-person classes. The face-to-face sessions, conducted  in computer  labs, focused  on practical activities related to  the  weekly topics,  encouraging  active  student  participation.  Using the  LMS forum feature,  a virtual learning  community  was created  to facilitate collaboration  and  knowl- edge exchange over the 14 weeks of the course. This platform allowed students to discuss course  videos and  activities, encouraging  peer  interaction.  The instructor  also used  the forum to post  higher-order  thinking questions  related  to the course content,  prompting discussions  that  enriched  students   comprehension.  The instructor  actively monitored students   progress  throughout  the  course,  offering  guidance  and  support  within  this online  space.  At course  completion,  students  completed  data  collection  instruments  at the end  of the course.
The aim was to  convert  the  theoretical  knowledge  gained  from course  videos  into practical  skills in the  lab. Students  were  assigned  programming  tasks  relevant  to  the week s topic during  in-person  class activities in the  FC teaching  model. They started  by creating an algorithmic flowchart for the given task, then moved on to coding and making the program functional. Students were allowed to use ChatGPT to assist with coding tasks in the  lab. Access to  the  tool  was browser-based,  as students  used  the  standard  web interface  (https://chat.openai.com) on the lab computers  via their personal  accounts.  No specific software  or application  was installed  for the  experiment.  However, they  were assigned  unstructured  programming  tasks to ensure  that  they engaged  in the problem- solving process  rather  than  relying solely on  ChatGPT for answers.  These unstructured tasks offered  several benefits.  They allowed  students  the  freedom  to  design  their  own solutions,  thereby  enhancing  creativity and  problem-solving  abilities; they  encouraged independent  research,  leading  to a deeper  understanding  of the  subject;  they  required students  to  analyse  problems  and  find  suitable  solutions,  which  strengthens  critical thinking  skills; and  they  built self-confidence  as students  solve problems  on their  own, making them  more  resilient in facing future  challenges.  Additionally, unstructured  tasks provided the opportunity to experiment with different approaches and be flexible, aiding in adapting  to changing  situations.
Instruments
Information  was collected  using  six instruments.  If any of the  scales included  reverse- coded  items, these  items were recoded  prior to the analysis to ensure  that  higher scores consistently  reflected  higher levels of the corresponding  constructs.
Personal information form
To gather  demographic  information  from participants,  the  study employed  a self-report questionnaire.  This questionnaire  included  items  inquiring  about  the  students   gender, department affiliation, and age.
Generative arti cial intelligence attitude scale
The generative  AI attitude  scale developed  by Marengo, et al. (2025), was employed  to measure  students   attitudes  towards  generative  AI applications.  It is a 13 item scale with two factors (positive and negative  attitude)  measured  on a 5-point  scale ranging  from 1 (strongly  disagree)  to  5 (strongly  agree).  Cronbach s  alpha  test  reliability was  used  to assess  internal  consistency.  The recalculated  Cronbach s  alpha  value for the  Generative Artificial Intelligence Attitude Scale was .83 for the total scale, with reliability coefficients of .84 for the  positive  attitude  subscale  and  .83 for the  negative  attitude  subscale, indicating  high  internal  consistency.  Prior to  the  primary  statistical  analyses,  the  data were screened  for accuracy and  consistency.  For the  generative  AI Attitude  Scale, items within the  negative  attitude  factor were  reverse-scored  so that  higher  scores  across all dimensions  of the  scale consistently  indicated  a more  positive attitude  towards  genera - tive AI. This alignment  was necessary  to  calculate  a valid total  attitude  score  for each participant.
Generative arti cial intelligence acceptance scale
Generative  AI acceptance  scale (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Yilmaz, et al., 2024) was employed  to measure  the  university  students   generative  AI technologies  acceptance.  This UTAUT model-based  scale  includes  20  items  measuring  extent  of influence  of variables  on users  behavioural  intention  to use generative  AI tools in higher  education.  It consists of four  factors  including  effort  expectancy,  social influence,  facilitating  conditions  and performance  expectancy.  The items  are  scored  on  a  5-point  Likert scale: 1 (strongly disagree)  to  5 (strongly  agree)  and  score  range  is from  20 to  100, which  reflects  the level of acceptance (higher scores = higher acceptance). Analyses using Cronbach s alpha coefficient indicate that the scale demonstrates strong internal consistency and reliability. 
The recalculated  Cronbach s  alpha  for the  Generative  Artificial Intelligence  Acceptance Scale was .95 for the  total scale, with subscale  reliabilities of .96 for performance  expec- tancy, .97 for effort expectancy, .85 for facilitating conditions, and .96 for social influence, indicating  high internal consistency.
Transactional distance scale
The Transactional Distance Scale, developed  by Zhang (2003) and adapted  into Turkish by  Yılmaz and  Olpak (2016), was  employed  to  measure  perceived  transactional  dis- tance  in  learning  environments.  Conceptually  linked  to  factors  like student-teacher interaction  quality  and  student  autonomy,  the  scale comprises  12 items  across  three factors: student-teacher  interaction,  student-student  interaction,  and  student-content interaction.  Items are  rated  on  a five-point  Likert scale. Importantly,  higher  scores  on this  scale  correspond  to  a  lower  perceived  transactional  distance  and  a  higher  per- ceived  interaction.  Yılmaz and  Olpak (2016) validated  the  Turkish adaptation  on  227 university students,  demonstrating  acceptable  model  fit via confirmatory  factor analy- sis and  strong  internal  consistency  and  reliability with Cronbach s  alpha.  The recalcu- lated  Cronbach s  alpha  for the  Transactional  Distance Scale was .90 for the  total  scale, with  subscale  reliabilities  of  .84 for  student  — teacher  interaction,  .91 for student  — student  interaction,  and  .83 for student  — content  interaction,  indicating  high  internal consistency.
Motivated strategies for learning questionnaire (MSLQ)
Student  motivation was measured  using the MSLQ, adapted by B y k zt rk et al. (2004) from  Pintrich  et  al. (1991). This instrument  utilises  a  7-point  Likert scale  (1 = strongly disagree,  7 = strongly agree) to assess six dimensions:  intrinsic/extrinsic goal orientation, task value, control beliefs, self-efficacy for learning/performance, and test anxiety. Higher scores reflect higher motivation. The recalculated Cronbach s alpha for the MSLQ was .73 for the  total  scale, with subscale  reliabilities of .71 for intrinsic goal  orientation,  .75 for extrinsic goal orientation,  .80 for task value, .72 for control beliefs, .87 for self-efficacy for learning/performance,  and  .79 for test  anxiety,  indicating  acceptable  to  high  internal consistency.  Regarding  the  measurement  of course  motivation,  the  Part A: Motivation  section  of the  Motivational Strategies  for Learning Questionnaire  (MSLQ), developed  by Pintrich et al. (1991), was used. While the full MSLQ contains  81 items related  to motiva- tion and  learning  strategies,  participants  were only administered  31 items from Section A. This choice was made to specifically assess students  motivational orientations (includ- ing intrinsic and extrinsic goal orientation,  task value, beliefs about  controlling  learning, self-efficacy, and  test  anxiety), consistent  with the  research  objectives,  and  also to keep the questionnaire  short to prevent  participant  fatigue.
Student engagement
The Student Engagement Scale, adapted into Turkish by Erg n and Usluel (2015) from Sun and Rueda (2012), was employed to assess student engagement in the online setting. This 19-item Likert instrument measures three dimensions — behavioural (5 items), cognitive (6 items), and emotional  (8 items) — using a 1—5 scale (1= strongly disagree , 5= completely agree ).  Higher  scores  reflect  increased  engagement  levels. In this  research,  the  scale demonstrated  strong  internal  consistency  (Cronbach s alpha = .90), supporting  its relia- bility for measuring  online  student  engagement.  The recalculated  Cronbach s  alpha  for the Student Engagement scale was .92 for the total scale, with subscale reliabilities of .73 for behavioural  engagement,  .85 for cognitive  engagement,  and  .90 for emotional engagement, indicating  acceptable  to high internal consistency.
Data analysis
This  research  investigated  the  relationships  among  the  Generative  AI Attitude Scale,  Generative  AI Acceptance  Scale,  Transactional  Distance  Scale,  MSLQ, and Student  Engagement.  To  analyse  these  relationships,  path  analysis  within  SEM framework  was  conducted  using  AMOS software.  Before  conducting  the  analysis, both  univariate  and  multivariate  normality  assumptions  were  checked  (Ullman,  2013). Univariate  normality  was  confirmed  through  skewness  and  kurtosis  values, which  were  within  the  acceptable  range  of – 2  (Schumacker  & Lomax,  2004). Multivariate  normality  was  assessed  by  examining  Variance  Inflation  Factor  (VIF) and  Tolerance  (T) indices, which  showed  no  significant  issues, thus  supporting  the assumption  of multivariate  normality. Descriptive statistics  were  generated  for each construct.  Pearson  correlations  were  performed  to  explore  the  pairwise  relation- ships  between  the  variables. The proposed  model s  fit was evaluated  using  various indices  derived  from the  path  analysis. Detailed  results  of the  model  are  presented in the  Findings  section.

Discussion
The study s  findings  revealed  that  students   AI acceptance  levels significantly  pre- dicted  their  attitudes  towards  AI technologies.  This  result  aligns  with  previous research  in  the  literature  and  shows  that  integrating  AI into  educational  settings positively  affects  students   perceptions  and  attitudes  towards  these  technologies (Kelly et al., 2023; Park & Woo, 2022). In particular, increased  adoption  and  practical acceptance  of  AI tools  can  be  seen  as  a  driver  that  further  strengthens  positive attitudes  among  university  students.  In  this  sense,  to  effectively  promote  the adoption  of AI technologies,  educators  should  focus efforts  on  designing  interven - tions  that  actively  cultivate  positive  student  attitudes  towards  AI  (Galindo- Dom nguez  et  al., 2024). Enabling  students  to  understand  AI technologies  better and  emphasising  the  potential  benefits  of  these  technologies  in  education  can increase  the  level of general  acceptance  and  strengthen  the  impact  of innovative practices  in education  (Karaoglan-Yilmaz, Yilmaz, et  al., 2024).
The  findings  revealed  students   perceptions  of  transactional  distance  signifi- cantly  predicted  their  attitudes  towards  AI technologies.  The  findings  suggested that  reducing  the  perception  of transactional  distance  acts  as a significant  catalyst, which  in turn  can  enhance  students   positive  attitudes  towards  the  integration  of AI in their learning  process.  Moreover, a decrease  in the  perception  of transactional distance  can  improve  students   ability to  learn  independently  as well as lead  them to  take  more  personal  responsibility  for  their  interactions  with  learning  materials (Kara, 2021;  Shin,  2023).  Taking  this  relationship  into  account,  teachers  must proactively  develop  strategies  to  manage  students   perceptions  of  transactional distance  when  integrating  AI technologies.  This crucial focus  is essential  for opti- mising  student  achievement  and  fundamentally  enriching  the  learning  experience (Chen et  al., 2023). Moverover, the  structural  model  indicated  that  student  motiva- tion  significantly  predicted  AI acceptance  and  transactional  distance.  Consistent with SDT (Deci & Ryan, 2000), when  students  perceive  AI tools  as valuable  for their autonomy  and  competence,  they  are  more  likely to  exhibit  higher  behavioural intention  to  use  these  technologies.  This suggests  that  AI acceptance  is not  merely a  technical  decision  but  a  motivationally  driven  process  where  students   internal energy  and  curiosity  facilitate  the  integration  of  these  tools  into  their  learning process  (Annamalai et  al., 2025).
The  study s  findings  revealed  that  student  engagement  significantly  predicted their  perception  of transactional  distance  and  motivation.  Student  engagement  can be  defined  as  the  degree  to  which  students  are  actively  involved  in  the  learning process,  showing  interest  and  making  an  effort  (Erg n  & Usluel, 2015). Likewise, the  perception  of transactional  distance  and  high  motivation  support  students  to be  more  focused  and  engaged  in lessons  (Doo et  al., 2021). In this sense,  students  closeness  and  personal  attention  while interacting  with  AI technologies  are  essen- tial  in  increasing  their  motivation  and  decreasing  their  transactional  distance perception.  High student  engagement  contributes  to  improved  learning  outcomes and  students  benefit  more  from  their  overall  educational  experience.  Educators should  develop  strategies  to  increase  student  engagement  using  the  interactive and  personalised  learning  opportunities  offered  by  AI technologies.  This  can improve  educational  achievement  by  enabling  students  to  engage  more  deeply in the  learning  process.
The  study  made  a  key  contribution  by  identifying  a  previously  undocumented mechanism  linking  core  psychological  (motivation,  engagement)  and  pedagogical (transactional  distance)  to  AI adoption.  The  findings  revealed  that  enhanced  stu- dent  engagement  in  learning  activities  positively  influenced  course  motivation, which  subsequently  played  a  dual  role.  It  significantly  increased  students   AI acceptance  while  concurrently  reducing  their  perceived  transactional  distance. This  dual  effect  of  motivation  and  the  connection  between  AI acceptance  and attitude  offered  a new  theoretical  lens  for optimising  AI implementation  in higher education.
Limitations and future research directions
This study s  findings  are  primarily  limited  to  university  students.  Future  research should  repeat  this  investigation  with  diverse  student  cohorts  in  order  to  enhance the  generalisability  of the  results.  Examining the  interplay  of AI acceptance,  attitude, motivation,  engagement,  and  transactional  distance  among  individuals from different age  groups  or varying educational  levels such  as high  school  would  provide  a more robust  and  comprehensive  understanding  of these  relationships.  Another  limitation of this study  is the  reliance  on  self-report  measures,  which might  increase  the  potential for participant  bias. Specifically, factors like social desirability bias may have influenced responses,  thereby  compromising  the  overall  accuracy  and  validity  of  the  results. Future investigations  should  mitigate  these  methodological  shortcomings  by incorpor- ating  various  data  collection  techniques  to  triangulate  findings  and  improve  the robustness  of the  findings.  Lastly, the  generalisability  of this  study  is constrained  by its  focus  on  a  single  cultural  and  geographical  context.  Since  cultural  norms  and technological  infrastructure  can  significantly  mediate  how  students  experience  and adopt  new tools. Future research  addresses  this limitation by conducting  cross-cultural comparative  studies.
