A B S T R A C T
Generative AI feedback (evaluative and instructional information produced by large language models in response to student submissions) has become increasingly common in higher education, yet longitudinal evidence on how students’ perceptions of such feedback are associated with academic outcomes remains limited. This three-wave study examined the differential and interactive associations of evaluative and instructional generative AI feed- back  perception  with  academic  achievement  among  6030  Chinese  first-year  undergraduates, assessed at  three time points across consecutive semesters. Cross-lagged panel models showed that evaluative feedback perception was  positively  associated  with  subsequent  achievement  (β =.31  and  β =.28,  p < .001),  whereas  instructional feedback  perception  showed  non-significant direct  associations.  Random  intercept  cross-lagged  panel  models confirmed these  within-person patterns. The evaluative feedback–achievement  association was  partially statis - tically mediated by perceived social support (39.4% of total association), while instructional feedback was fully mediated through this pathway (68.3%). Emotional intelligence moderated these associations in a compensatory pattern,  with  stronger  associations  among  students  with  lower  emotional  intelligence.  The  evaluative 
× instructional feedback  interaction was  positively  associated  with  achievement at  the  second  time  point  (β =.150,  p  = .018),  with  instructional  feedback  showing  significant  associations  only  under  high  evaluative feedback  conditions.  This  interaction  varied  across  disciplines.  These  findings  extend  feedback  theory  to generative AI contexts and offer preliminary recommendations for integrated AI feedback design.

1. Introduction
Feedback is a central component in educational processes that shapes learning outcomes  (Wisniewski et al., 2020;  Bassleer et al.,2025). The rapid expansion of generative artificial intelligence in higher education 
has introduced a new form of feedback: generative AI feedback. Unlike traditional automated feedback systems that select responses from pre- determined templates or apply rule-based scoring rubrics, generative AI feedback refers to evaluative and instructional information produced by 
large  language  models  (LLMs) in  response  to  individual  student  sub- missions.  These  systems  generate  contextually  adaptive  natural  lan- guage responses that vary in content, structure, and emphasis based on 
each  student’s specific  input  (Kasneci  et  al.,  2023).  This  capacity  for individualized, language-based feedback delivery distinguishes genera -
tive  AI feedback  from  both  conventional human  feedback  and  earlier forms of computer-assisted feedback (Bittencourt et al., 2024).
Within  educational  contexts,  feedback  is  typically  classified  as evaluative or instructional, with each type theorized to engage different cognitive  processes  (Narciss  et  al.,  2014).  Evaluative  feedback  (EF) provides  judgments  about  performance  quality,  such  as  grades,  error identifications,  or  rankings.  It  primarily  directs  attention  toward  dis- crepancies  between  current  and  desired  performance.  Instructional feedback  (IF) offers  process-oriented guidance—explaining  underlying principles  or  suggesting  strategic  approaches—without  explicit

performance judgment.  This  type  is theorized  to  promote  deeper  con- ceptual  understanding through  the  integration of strategic  knowledge with existing schemas (van der Kleij et al., 2015). Neuroscience research suggests that these feedback types may engage different cognitive pro- cessing patterns when delivered through AI interfaces (Gkintoni et al., 2025).  However,  how  evaluative  and  instructional  feedback  function when delivered through generative AI systems—particularly regarding 
their  temporal  patterns,  associational  pathways,  and  potential interactions—remains insufficiently examined.
Recent  research  has  begun  to  examine  AI-generated  feedback  in higher education, with findings indicating that AI feedback can match or approach  human  feedback  quality  in specific  domains  such  as writing assessment and statistical learning (Bauer et al., 2025; Yin & Dou, 2025; 
Shi & Aryadoust, 2024). However, this body of work has largely over- looked  two  important  dimensions.  First,  most  studies  employ cross-sectional or short-term designs that capture immediate responses without  tracking  how  associations  between  feedback  perception  and achievement develop  over  successive  academic  terms  (Ouyang  et  al., 2022).  Theoretical  models  suggest  that  evaluative  and  instructional feedback  may  display  different  temporal  patterns,  with  evaluative feedback  potentially  showing  more  immediate  associations  and instructional feedback requiring longer integration periods. Second, the associational pathways connecting AI feedback perception to academic outcomes  remain  underspecified. Feedback  does  not  operate  in  isola- tion;  it  is  received,  interpreted,  and  acted  upon  within  social  and emotional contexts (Winstone et al., 2017). How students’  perceptions 
of  social  support  and  their  emotional  intelligence  shape  the  feed- back–achievement link in AI-mediated settings has not been systemati -
cally  studied  (Er  et  al.,  2025).  Furthermore,  potential  interactions between evaluative and instructional feedback types—which commonly co-occur when students use generative AI—have not been investigated. Emerging perspectives on the reciprocal relationship between artificial and human intelligence in education (Ifenthaler & Schumacher, 2023) underscore the need to understand these feedback processes within their social-cognitive  context.  From  the  learner’s  perspective,  feedback  is only effective if actively received and implemented (Mandouit & Hattie, 2023), making perception-based inquiry a necessary starting point.
This  study  addresses  three  research  questions.  First,  how  do  longi- tudinal  associations  between  generative  AI feedback  types  (evaluative vs. instructional) and academic achievement differ across time? Second, 
how do perceived social support and emotional intelligence function as statistical  mediators  or  moderators  in  these  associations?  Third,  do evaluative and instructional feedback show interactive associations with achievement?
2. Theoretical foundation
2.1. Feedback intervention theory in generative AI contexts
Educational  feedback  mechanisms  have  traditionally been  concep - tualized through Feedback Intervention Theory (FIT), which posits that feedback redirects attention to different levels of a hierarchical cognitive system,  thereby  regulating  the  gap  between  current  and  desired  per-
formance  (Kluger  &  DeNisi，1996;  Schrader  &  Grassinger，2021). Generative AI fundamentally transforms traditional feedback paradigms 
through attentional reconfiguration, psychological distance modulation, and cognitive processing divergence. The computational architecture of large  language  models  enables  algorithmic  differentiation  between evaluative  and  instructional  elements  with  unprecedented  clarity, potentially  enhancing  the  categorical  precision  that  FIT identifies  as critical for attentional direction (Schrader & Grassinger, 2021).
When  applied  to  generative  AI feedback,  this  framework  suggests that  AI-delivered  feedback  may  produce  different  attentional patterns compared  to  human-delivered  feedback.  The  reduced  interpersonal threat associated with AI-generated feedback may differentially benefit evaluative  feedback,  which  contains  explicit  performance  judgments 
that  could  otherwise  trigger  defensive  responses  (Lipnevich  & Smith, 2018). Drawing  on  FIT’s hierarchical framework,  evaluative  feedback primarily targets meta-task processes (outcome evaluation) that can be rapidly integrated, while instructional feedback addresses task-learning processes that require extended integration (Hattie & Timperley, 2007). 
These  differential  processing  requirements provide  a  theoretical basis for expecting distinct temporal association patterns.
2.2. Social-cognitive pathways and emotional intelligence
The  translation  of  feedback  perception  into  academic  outcomes operates through complex social-cognitive pathways that extend beyond simple  information  transmission.  Social  Cognitive  Theory  (Bandura, 2023) positions feedback within triadic reciprocal determinism, where personal  factors,  behavioral  patterns,  and  environmental  factors continuously  interact.  Within  this  framework,  we  propose  that perceived  social  support  functions  as  a  statistical  mediating  variable connecting AI feedback perception to academic achievement. The pro-
posed theoretical chain is: generative AI feedback helps students identify performance  gaps,  which  activates  help-seeking  behavior—a self-regulated  learning  strategy  (Karabenick  & Berger,  2013;  Vilkova & Shcheglova,  2021;  Jiang  & Yu, 2025).  This  recognition  of learning needs  prompts  students  to  seek  assistance  from  peers  and  instructors, thereby  enhancing  their  perception  of  available  social  support.  This theoretical  link  is  relevant  in  the  present  context  because  students voluntarily sought feedback from the AI system—a behavior that reflects a help-seeking orientation conceptually linked to broader social support utilization  (Vilkova  &  Shcheglova,  2021;  Jiang  &  Yu,  2025).  The perceived social support then facilitates the translation of feedback in- formation  into  academic  improvement through  resource  mobilization and collaborative learning. Parental and peer support have been shown to  amplify  the  association  between  growth-oriented  beliefs  and  aca- demic  persistence  across  diverse  cultural  contexts  (Lee  &  Mendoza, 2025),  further  supporting  the  role  of  social  support  as  a  connecting variable in feedback–achievement associations.
For instructional feedback specifically, we propose that the strategic 
guidance  provided  by  AI requires  collaborative validation  and  imple - mentation with peers. Students who receive process-oriented guidance 
may  be  more  likely  to  discuss  and  practice  suggested  strategies  with study  partners,  strengthening  their  perception  of  social  support (Winstone et al., 2017). This social scaffolding pathway is expected to be 
the  primary  route  through  which  instructional feedback  is associated with  achievement.  In  contrast,  evaluative  feedback  is  expected  to operate through both direct and socially mediated pathways.
Emotional  intelligence—the  capacity  to  perceive,  understand, manage, and utilize emotional information (Mayer et al., 2016)—may moderate  these  associations  by  shaping  how  students  process feedback-related  affective  responses.  Students  with  lower  emotional intelligence  may  have  greater  difficulty  managing  affective  reactions triggered by performance evaluation, making external feedback a more important resource for activating support networks. This compensatory pattern  would  manifest  as  stronger  feedback-to-support  associations among  students  with  lower  emotional  intelligence  (MacCann  et  al., 2020).

3. Methods
3.1. Research design
This  study  used  a  longitudinal,  three-wave  panel  design.  Data collection  occurred  at  January  2025  (T1),  May  2025  (T2),  and September  2025  (T3),  corresponding  to  the  conclusion  of  three consecutive  academic  terms.  The  intervals  between  assessments (approximately four months for T1→T2 and three months for T2→T3) 
were selected to align with the natural cycle of academic performance assessment  in  the  Chinese  higher  education  system.  These  intervals, shorter  than  typical  semester-based  designs,  may  capture  feedback integration processes  with  greater  temporal  resolution  (Selig  & Little, 2012).
3.2. Generative AI feedback system and instructional context
The  generative  AI feedback  system  used  in  this  study  was  institu - tionally  deployed  across  all  ten  participating universities. It was  built upon  the  DeepSeek  large  language  model  architecture and  integrated into  each  university’s  learning  management  platform,  providing  a standardized feedback interface accessible to all enrolled students.
Students interacted with the system through a structured submission 
interface. The workflow proceeded as follows: (1) students voluntarily 
submitted  academic  content—including  completed  assignments, learning questions, or examination preparation materials—through the 
platform;  (2)  the  system  generated  feedback  comprising  four  compo - nents:  textual  evaluation of submission  quality,  specific  improvement suggestions,  performance-level  judgments  aligned  with  course  assess- ment criteria, and strategic learning guidance; (3) feedback was deliv-
ered  asynchronously,  typically  within  minutes.  The  system  operated with  complete  autonomy—no  instructor  review,  modification,  or approval was involved at any stage.
The  identical  system  architecture  was  deployed  across  all  partici - pating universities and disciplinary domains, ensuring consistency in the feedback  generation  process.  While  the  system  adapted  feedback  con- tent  to  discipline-specific  terminology,  the  underlying  model,  algo- rithms, and interaction protocols remained uniform.
This system differs from traditional rule-based automated feedback in that it: (a) generates contextually adaptive natural language responses rather than selecting from predetermined templates; (b) produces indi- vidualized  feedback  varying  in  content  and  emphasis  based  on  each student’s  submission;  and  (c)  integrates  evaluative  judgments  and instructional guidance  within  a  single  coherent  response.  These  char - acteristics  distinguish  it  from  conventional  automated  grading  tools (Bauer et al., 2025).
3.3. Participants and sampling
The study employed cluster sampling from ten comprehensive uni- versities  across geographically diverse  regions in China. First-year un- dergraduate students  (2024  cohort)  constituted the  target  population, with  sampling  stratified  across  four  disciplinary  domains:  engineering (40%),  humanities (30%),  sciences  (20%),  and  business/management (10%).  These  proportions were  based  on  the  actual  enrollment distri - bution at the participating institutions, consistent with the disciplinary structure reported in the China Statistical Yearbook of Education.
Participants  were  recruited  through  classroom-based  distribution during  regular  class  hours.  Research  team  members  visited  selected courses,  distributed  survey  materials  to  all  attending  students,  and provided  standardized  instructions  regarding  voluntary  participation and  confidentiality. This  approach  ensured  near-complete coverage  of enrolled  students  within  selected  courses,  reducing  self-selection  bias. Of  6820  students  present  during  T1  sessions,  6500  completed  the assessment  (initial  response  rate:  95.3%).  The  sample  had  balanced gender  distribution (47%  male,  53%  female)  with  mean  age  of 18.37 years (SD = 1.69).
Longitudinal retention resulted in 6030 students (92.8%) completing all  three  waves.  Independent  samples  t-tests  and  chi-square  analyses confirmed non-significant differences between completers and dropouts 
on  gender,  feedback  perception,  and  academic  achievement  (all p > .05). Little’s MCAR test was non-significant (χ² = 142.38, df = 131, 
p = .231), supporting classification of missing data as MCAR.
3.4. Measures
3.4.1. Generative AI feedback perception scale
This  scale  was  developed  following  systematic  psychometric  pro- cedures  (DeVellis & Thorpe,  2021). An initial pool  of 24 items  under - went  expert  review  by  ten  specialists  (five  in  educational technology, five  in  educational  psychology).  Items  with  Content  Validity  Index below.80 were eliminated, yielding 14 items. Cognitive interviews with 
12 students led to further refinements. Exploratory factor analysis with a pilot  sample  (n = 412)  using  principal  axis  factoring  with  oblique 
rotation  yielded  a two-factor  solution  explaining  76.34%  of variance. Items  with  loadings  below.60  or  cross-loadings  above.30  were  elimi- nated,  resulting  in  six  items:  three  assessing  evaluative  feedback perception and three assessing instructional feedback perception, on a five-point  Likert  scale.  All items  were  explicitly  anchored  to  the  insti- tutionally  deployed  AI system  (e.g.,  “When  the  AI feedback  system evaluates  my  work…”).  Complete  item  texts  are  provided  in Supplementary  Materials  S1.  Reliability  was  excellent:  Cronbach’s  α 
= .918  overall,.861  for  evaluative,.921  for  instructional  feedback. Confirmatory factor  analysis  confirmed  configural,  metric,  and  scalar 
invariance  across  all  three  waves  (all  ΔCFI  ≤.01;  Supplementary 
Materials S1).
Academic  achievement  was  operationalized  using  standardized term-end  /  midterm  examination scores,  converted  to  z-scores  within each  course.  This  objective  measurement eliminates  common  method variance  for  the  feedback  perception–achievement association,  as  the predictor  (self-report)  and  outcome  (institutional  scores)  derive  from different measurement methods.
Perceived social support was assessed using the Multidimensional 
Scale  of  Perceived  Social  Support  (Zimet  et  al.,  1988),  adapted  for Chinese  contexts  by  Chou  (2000).  Cronbach’s  α  = .892–.912  across 
waves.
Emotional  intelligence was  assessed  using  the  Schutte  Emotional Intelligence  Scale  (Schutte  et  al.,  1998),  validated  for  Chinese  pop- ulations by Shi & Wang (2007). Cronbach’s α = .893–.901 across waves.
3.5. Analytical strategy
All analyses used Mplus 8.6 (Muth´en & Muth´en, 2017) with robust maximum likelihood estimation (MLR). For the first research question, we  constructed cross-lagged  panel  models  (CLPM) separately  for  each feedback type, supplemented by Random Intercept Cross-Lagged Panel Models  (RI-CLPM) to  separate  within-person from  between-person as- sociations  (Hamaker  et  al.,  2015).  For  the  second  question,  we  used latent  variable  structural  equation  modeling  with  longitudinal media - tion and moderation specifications. For the third, we used latent inter - action modeling with feedback type product terms.
We  controlled  for  socioeconomic  status  and  academic  discipline throughout.  Missing  data  were  handled  through  full  information maximum  likelihood  (FIML).  The  nested  structure  (students  within universities)  was  addressed  through  TYPE=COMPLEX with  cluster- 
robust standard errors.
We  acknowledge  that  while  the  three-wave  longitudinal  design strengthens directional inference relative to cross-sectional designs, and RI-CLPM isolates within-person dynamics, the non-experimental nature of  this  research  does  not  support  definitive  causal  claims.  Regarding statistical  power,  with  N = 6030,  the  study  has  over  90%  power  to detect small associations (f² ≥.01). This large sample ensures detection of meaningful associations but also means very small associations may reach  significance.  We report  and  interpret effect  sizes  alongside  sig-
nificance tests, using education-specific benchmarks (Kraft, 2020).
4. Results
4.1. Preliminary analyses
Prior  to  hypothesis  testing,  we  conducted  comprehensive  pre- liminary  analyses  to  establish  measurement  quality,  assess  potential methodological artifacts, and examine data properties. Both feedback types exhibited significant positive as- sociations with academic achievement across all measurement intervals,

confirming  that  observed  temporal  changes  reflect  true  construct  dy- namics rather than measurement artifacts.
To address potential common method bias resulting from self-report measurement, we implemented a five-pronged strategy. First, Harman's single-factor  test revealed  that  a single  common  factor explained  only 18.75%,  19.65%,  and  24.58%  of  total  variance  at  T1,  T2,  and  T3 respectively—all substantially below the critical threshold of 40% that would indicate problematic common method variance (Podsakoff et al., 2024). Second, we employed the marker variable approach (Lindell & Whitney,  2001),  utilizing  a  theoretically  unrelated  single-item  mea- sure  of  campus  infrastructure  satisfaction  as  the  marker  variable. Correlational  analyses  indicated  non-significant  associations  between this  marker  and  core  study  variables  (r = -.04–.11,  all  p > .05), providing  further  evidence  against  substantial  method  associations. Third,  unmeasured  latent  method  factor  (ULMF)  analysis  showed negligible changes to structural paths after inclusion (average Δβ =.003, maximum  Δβ  =.008;  Podsakoff  et  al.,  2024).  Fourth,  academic achievement  was  objectively  measured.  Fifth,  key  variables  were 
temporally  separated  (EI at  T1; SS at  T2; AA at  T1–T3).  Discriminant 
validity  evidence  further  supports  that  social  support  is distinct  from other  psychosocial  constructs:  its  correlations  with  academic self-efficacy  (r = .21–.23)  were  substantially  lower  than  associations with focal variables, and it showed non-significant correlations with test anxiety (r = .09).
4.2. Temporal dynamics between feedback types and academic achievement
Cross-lagged panel analyses for evaluative feedback (Fig. 2) revealed 
a  systematic  pattern  of  bidirectional  relationships  with  academic achievement  across  both  time  intervals.  Specifically,  evaluative  feed- back at T1 significantly predicted academic achievement at T2 (β =.31, 
p < .001,  95%  CI [.25,.37]), controlling  for  stability  associations  and within-time covariances. This predictive relationship, while remaining significant, showed modest attenuation in the subsequent interval, with 
T2 evaluative  feedback  predicting  T3 achievement at  a slightly  lower magnitude (β =.28, p < .001, 95% CI [.21,.35]). Reciprocal associations 
were also observed, with academic achievement significantly predicting subsequent evaluative feedback perceptions, though at somewhat lower magnitudes  (T1→T2:  β =.24,  p < .001,  95%  CI [.17,.31];  T2→T3:  β =.22,  p < .001,  95%  CI [.15,.29]).  The  overall  model  demonstrated excellent  fit  to  the  observed  data  (χ²/df  = 3.06,  CFI =.97,  TLI =.96, RMSEA =.052).
The  parallel  analysis  for  instructional feedback  (Fig. 3) revealed  a markedly  different  temporal  pattern.  Notably,  instructional  feedback demonstrated  non-significant  prospective  associations  on  academic achievement across both time intervals (T1→T2: β =.08, p = .214, 95% 
CI [-.05,.21]; T2→T3: β =.05, p = .372, 95% CI [-.06,.16]). In contrast, academic  achievement consistently  predicted  subsequent  instructional

5. Discussion
5.1. Research gaps and overview of findings
Despite  growing  adoption  of  generative  AI  in  higher  education, important gaps  remain  in understanding how  students’ perceptions  of AI-generated  feedback  are  associated  with  academic  outcomes  over time. Most existing research relies on cross-sectional designs that cannot address temporal ordering, rarely distinguishes between evaluative and instructional feedback  types,  and  does  not  account  for  the  social  and emotional  contexts  in  which  feedback  is  received  and  processed. Furthermore, the potential for evaluative and instructional feedback to interact—an  everyday  reality  when  students  use  generative  AI systems—has  not  been  empirically  examined.  This  study  addressed these  gaps  through  a  three-wave  longitudinal  design  examining  the differential, mediated, moderated, and interactive associations of eval-
uative  and  instructional  generative  AI feedback  perception  with  aca- demic  achievement  among  6030  Chinese  first-year  undergraduates using  an  institutionally  deployed  AI  feedback  system.  The  overall pattern of findings indicates that evaluative and instructional feedback perception are differentially and complementarily associated with aca-
demic achievement, operating through distinct temporal profiles, social- cognitive pathways, and interactive configurations.
5.2. Temporal patterns and feedback intervention theory
The  differential  temporal  patterns  between  feedback  types  are consistent with predictions from feedback intervention theory (Hattie & Timperley,  2007;  Panadero,  2023;  Bassleer  et  al.,2025).  Evaluative feedback, targeting meta-task processes related to performance evalua - tion,  showed  consistent  direct  associations  with  achievement  that gradually  attenuated  over  time.  Instructional  feedback,  targeting task-learning processes, showed no direct associations but was linked to achievement through social support. This pattern extends FIT’s hierar - chical  model  to  generative  AI  contexts  by  demonstrating  that  the meta-task  versus  task-learning  distinction—originally  developed  for human  feedback—also  applies  when  feedback  is  algorithmically generated. The temporal attenuation of evaluative feedback associations (9.7% reduction between intervals) is compatible with feedback inter - nalization processes described by Panadero (2023): as performance in- formation  becomes  integrated  into  students’  self-regulatory frameworks,  the  incremental  association  of  new  external  evaluative feedback may diminish. This finding has a broader theoretical implica -
tion:  it  suggests  that  generative  AI feedback,  despite  its  non-human origin,  is  processed  through  the  same  hierarchical  attention  system that  FIT  proposes  for  human  feedback,  supporting  the  view  that 
feedback processing is driven by information characteristics rather than source characteristics.
5.3. Social support as a statistical mediator: an AI-to-human bridging function
The  statistical  mediation  by  social  support  contributes  to  social cognitive  theory  (Bandura,  2023)  by  identifying  a  specific  environ - mental  pathway  through  which  generative  AI  feedback  perception connects  to  academic  outcomes.  The  differential  mediation patterns—partial for evaluative feedback (39.4%), full for instructional feedback (68.3%)—have important theoretical implications. Evaluative feedback  appears  to  operate  through  dual  pathways:  a  direct  route, possibly through immediate motivational or attentional regulation, and 
a socially mediated route through help-seeking activation. Instructional feedback, by contrast, appears to require social scaffolding to translate strategic  guidance  into  performance  gains.  This  finding  extends  the help-seeking  framework  of  Karabenick  and  Berger  (2013) to AI-mediated  contexts:  it  suggests  that  AI-generated  strategic  guidance does  not  function  as  a  self-contained  instructional  intervention,  but rather as a prompt that activates collaborative learning behaviors within students’ social  networks  (Vilkova  & Shcheglova,  2021;  Jiang  & Yu, 2025). In theoretical terms, this positions generative AI feedback not as 
a replacement for social learning processes (Xin et al., 2026), but as a catalyst  that  redirects  students  toward  human  support  systems—what we term an “AI-to-human bridging” function.
We acknowledge  that  social  support  is one  of several  possible  sta- tistical mediators. Other variables—including self-regulation strategies, academic  self-efficacy,  and  metacognitive  development—may  also connect feedback perception to achievement. The present study focused on social support because it captures a distinctive interpersonal pathway that is often overlooked in AI feedback research. Future research should construct competing mediation models to compare the relative contri - bution of multiple mediating variables.
5.4. Compensatory moderation by emotional intelligence
The compensatory moderating pattern of emotional intelligence ex- tends  ability  models  of emotional  intelligence  (Mayer  et  al.,  2016) to AI-mediated  learning  contexts.  The  finding  that  feedback  associations were  stronger  among  students  with  lower  emotional  intelligence  sup- ports  the  theoretical  proposition  that  external  feedback  provides compensatory  regulatory  support  for  students  who  lack  internal emotional resources (MacCann et al., 2020). This pattern has a broader implication for how we conceptualize the role of AI in education: rather 
than  being  equally  beneficial  for  all  students,  generative  AI feedback may function as an “emotional scaffolding” resource (Zhu & Lin, 2024) 
that is most valuable for students who are least equipped to manage the affective demands of academic evaluation independently. This finding

5.5. Feedback type interactions and cognitive load theory
The  interaction  between  feedback  types  is  interpretable  within cognitive load theory (Sweller, 2011). Evaluative information appears to provide  cognitive  anchoring  that  helps  students  process  and  apply instructional guidance.  Without  this  anchoring,  students  may  lack  the performance  reference  point  needed  to  direct  the  implementation  of strategic advice. The time-limited nature of this interaction (significant at  T2 but  not  T3)  suggests  that  feedback  synergies  are  most  relevant during initial integration and may diminish as feedback is internalized. From  a  theoretical  standpoint,  this  finding  challenges  the  common research practice of studying feedback types in isolation. It suggests that the  unit  of  analysis  in  feedback  research  should  be  the  feedback configuration—the specific combination of evaluative and instructional elements—rather than individual feedback types. This has implications for  how  feedback  theory  is formulated:  models  should  account  for  in- teractions  between  feedback  components,  not  merely  their  additive contributions.
6. Scope of perception-based measurement and practical implications
An important interpretive boundary  concerns  the  reliance  on  self- reported  feedback  perception rather  than  objective  measures  of feed- back  content.  Students’  perceptions  may  be  shaped  by  recall  bias, satisfaction halo associations, or social desirability. The findings reflect associational  patterns  between  perceived  AI feedback  characteristics and academic achievement, not the objective properties of the AI feed-
back itself.
However, feedback perception is theoretically important in its own 
right.  Winstone  et  al.  (2017) demonstrated  that  learners’  proactive recipience—their  active  engagement  with  and  interpretation  of feedback—is a necessary condition for feedback to be used. Regardless 
of objective quality, if students do not perceive feedback as evaluative or instructional,  it  cannot  function  through  the  proposed  pathways. Perception-based  measurement  thus  captures  a  necessary  link  in  the feedback utilization chain (Mandouit & Hattie, 2023). Future research should  complement  perception  measures  with  behavioral  indicators such as AI system log data.
First,  AI feedback  systems  should  integrate  evaluative  and  instruc - tional  elements  in  a  coordinated  sequence.  The  interaction  pattern suggests  evaluative feedback  should precede instructional guidance  to provide cognitive  anchoring (e.g., “Your analysis addresses 3 of 5 key criteria” followed by strategic suggestions).
Second,  feedback  design  should  account  for emotional  intelligence differences.  Students  with  lower  emotional  intelligence  benefit  more from  supportive  framing  (e.g.,  affirmational  prefixes,  gradual  error disclosure).
Third,  because  instructional feedback  is primarily  associated  with achievement through social support, AI systems should incorporate so- cial learning prompts (e.g., “You might discuss this approach with your study group”).
Fourth,  disciplinary  calibration is warranted. Engineering  and  sci- ences  showed  stronger  feedback  interaction  associations;  humanities and management may benefit from enriched instructional feedback.
7. Limitations and future directions
Several limitations should be acknowledged when interpreting these 
findings.  First,  despite  the  longitudinal design  and  supplementary RI-       CLPM  analyses,  causal  inference  remains  constrained  by  the  non- 
experimental  nature  of  the  research.  The  observed  associational  pat- terns, while temporally ordered, cannot rule out unmeasured confounds such as prior academic motivation, technology familiarity, or instructor quality.  Future  studies  should  employ  experimental  designs—for example,  a  2 × 2  factorial  that  manipulates  evaluative  and  instruc - tional  feedback  configurations  through  AI  system  settings—to  test whether the observed patterns reflect causal processes.
Second, the study relies on self-reported feedback perception for the predictor variables. Although the primary outcome (academic achieve - ment) is objectively measured and temporal separation of key variables reduces common method concerns, perception-based measures may be shaped  by  recall  bias  and  social  desirability.  Future  research  should incorporate  multimodal  indicators,  such  as  AI system  log  data  (e.g., interaction  frequency,  feedback  viewing  duration,  and  response  pat- terns), to complement self-report with behavioral evidence of feedback engagement.
Third,  the  measurement  intervals  (approximately  four  and  three months,  respectively),  while  shorter  than  typical  semester-based  de- signs,  may  still  miss  the  fine-grained  temporal  dynamics  of  feedback processing—particularly for instructional feedback, whose integration is theorized to unfold over shorter cycles. Intensive longitudinal designs, 
such as diary studies or experience sampling with weekly or biweekly assessments, would provide greater temporal resolution for examining within-semester feedback processing.
Fourth,  the  sample  is  limited  to  first-year  undergraduates  at  ten Chinese comprehensive universities, which constrains external validity in two respects. At the cultural level, Chinese students' orientation to- ward institutional authority may amplify the associations observed for evaluative  feedback  from  a  university-deployed  system.  Collectivist educational norms may strengthen social support's statistical mediating role,  as  students  in  such  contexts  may  be  more  inclined  to  seek  peer assistance  when  prompted  by  performance information. At the  devel- opmental  level,  first-year  students  are  in  an  early  phase  of university adaptation, and the observed patterns may differ for more experienced students  who  have  developed  stronger  self-regulatory  capacities. Replication  across  different  cultural  contexts,  educational  levels,  and student populations is needed before these findings can be generalized beyond the present sample.
Fifth,  the  study  does  not  include  comparison  conditions.  Without contrasting generative AI feedback with traditional teacher feedback or rule-based automated feedback, it is not possible to isolate the unique contribution  of  generative  AI-specific  characteristics  (e.g.,  contextual adaptivity, natural  language  generation) to  the  observed  associations. Future  research  incorporating  such  comparison  conditions  would strengthen the specificity of theoretical claims regarding generative AI feedback.
8. Conclusion
Despite the growing use of generative AI for educational feedback, longitudinal  evidence  on  how  students’  perceptions  of  different  AI feedback  types  are  associated  with  academic  achievement  has  been lacking.  Specifically,  it  has  been  unclear  whether  evaluative  and instructional AI feedback show different temporal association patterns, whether  social-cognitive  variables  statistically  connect  these  percep - tions to achievement, and whether these feedback types function inde- pendently or interactively.
Addressing  these  gaps  through  a  three-wave  panel  study  of  6030 Chinese  undergraduates using  an  institutionally deployed  AI feedback system,  this  study  produced  three  principal  findings.  First,  evaluative feedback  perception  showed  consistent  direct  associations  with achievement, while instructional feedback showed no direct association 
but was linked to achievement fully through perceived social support. Second,  emotional  intelligence  moderated  these  associations  in  a compensatory  pattern:  students  with  lower  emotional  intelligence showed  stronger  feedback–support  associations,  suggesting  that  AI 
feedback  may  serve  as external  regulatory  scaffolding  for  emotionally vulnerable learners. Third, evaluative and instructional feedback func- tioned  interactively  rather  than  independently,  with  instructional feedback  showing  positive  associations  with  achievement  only  under moderate-to-high  evaluative  feedback  conditions—a  pattern  that  was time-sensitive and discipline-dependent.
These  findings carry several implications. For feedback theory, the results demonstrate that the evaluative–instructional distinction, origi- nally developed for human feedback, applies to generative AI contexts but operates through different pathway configurations. For educational practice,  the  results  support  designing  AI feedback  systems  that  coor- dinate  evaluative  and instructional elements sequentially, adapt to  in- dividual  emotional  profiles,  incorporate  social  learning  prompts,  and calibrate  to  disciplinary  assessment  structures.  The  compensatory moderation  by  emotional  intelligence  is  particularly  relevant  for educational equity: AI feedback systems may offer the greatest benefit to students  who  are  least  well-served  by  traditional  feedback  environ - ments. These findings provide an empirical foundation for moving from generic  AI feedback  deployment  toward  evidence-informed,  adaptive feedback design in higher education.
