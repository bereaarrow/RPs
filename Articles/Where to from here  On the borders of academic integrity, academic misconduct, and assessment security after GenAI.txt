   academic integrity conducted at an Irish university during the 2023—24 
   academic  year.  Findings  suggest  that  contemporary  Higher  Education 
   learners  tend  strongly  to  associate  the  concept  of  academic  integrity

Introduction
At the Generative Arti cial Intelligence (GenAI) inflection point, it is critically important to work toward multifaceted new understandings of academic integrity, taking careful account of the per - spectives and priorities of both learners and academics. As institutions and policymakers hasten 
to produce and continuously update policy, procedure, and guidance for educators and learners, 
we report in the present paper on a persistent uncertainty among learners at an Irish university regarding the meaning and practice of academic integrity, and through analysing learners  thoughts 
on the concept, we aim to add nuance the literature of academic integrity. Our  ndings show a mounting confusion about academic integrity among students, who often conflate academic integ - rity with the avoidance of academic misconduct and, more speci cally, of assessment misconduct.

We point, too, to ambiguities in the academic and policy literature about the nature of academic integrity, which often de nes the concept, but seldom grounds it in explanation or exempli cation 
of  how  theory  translates  to  practice.  This  discursive  ambiguity  is  reproduced  to  a  considerable degree by our focus group participants, who often equate academic integrity with adherence to assessment  rules.  This  appears  to  restrict  the  perceived  relevance  of  academic  integrity  to  the sphere of summative assessment of learning, but also to restrain learners from thinking holistically about  learning with integrity.
Ultimately, participants frame academic integrity less often as a matter of ethics and more often 
as one of procedure; as a concern for the individual rather than a community; and as an episodic rather than an intrinsic behaviour. This conceptual reduction represents a fundamental challenge 
as  the  technological  landscape  continues  to  evolve  at  a  pace  previously  almost  inconceivable. Although GenAI s disruption of Higher Education has reinvigorated the debate about the practice(s) 
of integrity, we are simultaneously experiencing ethical impasse as the sector has yet to metabolise 
the implications of GenAI s capabilities. As such, what consensus may previously have existed about the ethics of Higher Education has shattered, and the ontological block is yet to be overcome.
Most data on academic misconduct among students currently extant rests upon self-reporting 
from the learner perspective through the medium of surveys (Benson and Enstroem  2023). A key aim of the present work is to enrich our currently limited knowledge of learners  conceptualisation 
of educationally (in)appropriate behaviour, therefore, we investigated students  social negotiation of 
the ideas of academic integrity and misconduct through seven focus group sessions in which a total 
of 38 students participated during the 2023—24 academic year.
Ten questions were posed to each group, eliciting students  views on valid and relevant assess - ment instruments, their concepts and experiences of academic integrity (henceforth AI) and aca - demic misconduct (henceforth AM), and their views on new directions for learning, teaching and assessment  post-GenAI.  Here,  we  focus  on  participants   responses  to  the  two  questions  below, which were posed in the order shown and represented a signi cant thematic pattern in our data: 
. What does the term  academic integrity  mean, in your view? What could be some examples of students acting with academic integrity?
. What does the term  academic misconduct  mean to you? What could be some examples of aca - demic misconduct, in your view?
In their responses, a signi cant majority of participants defaulted to enumerating  rules  on assess - ment  misconduct  avoidance.  However,  as  Dawson  et  al.  (2024)  have  argued,  such  lists  of  rules address cheating management rather than validity, and would need to expand almost ceaselessly 
to keep pace with the ever-increasing number of ethically unallowable behaviours. Nonetheless, our participants mainly frame AI instrumentally as a set of individual actions to do in summative assessment.
A minority of participants, however, did invoke of the need for a sustained integrity in learning      practice throughout the entire student lifecycle. These students described AI as a communitarian 
matter and a moral—ethical disposition which should be an integral facet of being-a-student, includ - ing and extending beyond assessment, and appealing to values such as honesty and responsibility.
That students continue to construct their concept of academic integrity against an obsolescent paradigm betrays a profound conceptual disorientation impacting learners, educators, and policy - makers  alike.  Our  ndings  would  suggest  that  Corbin  et  al.  (2025a)  recent  characterisation  of GenAI as a  wicked problem  in assessment applies also to the broader ambit of Higher Education, touching teaching and learning just as it does assessment. It is logical that under conditions of phi - losophical and practical chaos, learners and scholars would  rst attend to the most immediate and concrete conundrum — that of assessment validity and security. However, secure assessment alone will not be sufficient to ensure the integrity of Higher Education post-GenAI.
Notes on the literature of academic integrity: problem to concept to practice?
 Academic integrity  became a mainstay of education literature as recently as the 1990s when Donald McCabe and colleagues established it as a standalone  eld of enquiry (Christensen Hughes and & McCabe 2006; Cole and McCabe 1996; McCabe and Klebe Trevi o  1993; McCabe, Butter eld, and Klebe Trevi o 2006; McCabe, Klebe Trevi o, and Butter eld  1999; McCabe, Klebe Trevi o, and But- ter eld 2001; McCabe, Trevi o, and Linda 1997). While much of McCabe s scholarship concerned the impact of the presence or absence of institutional  honour codes , the studies generally operate on 
the assumption that integrity results from the control of dishonesty and/or misconduct. However, Dawson  (2021)  notes  that  with  the  proliferation  of  assessment  outsourcing  options,  primarily digital, available to contemporary learners, a will to adhere to an honour code is not likely to be an effective misconduct deterrent. Corbin et al. (2025a) further note that traditional norms of aca - demic integrity and the possibility of detection previously enabled detection and sanctioning of mis - conduct, which is now effectively impossible.
The concepts of academic integrity and academic misconduct continue to be invoked almost 
interchangeably in the literature, however. Davis (2023, 3), for instance, states that academic integ - rity  implies a  rm adherence to a code of moral values and translates into actions that avoid cheat - ing, plagiarism and contract cheating . Lancaster et al. (2025) posit the development of institutional       policy to deal with academic misconduct as an academic integrity imperative. Similarly, Ellis and Murdoch s (2024)  educational integrity enforcement pyramid  is intended to curb cheating beha - viours — albeit restoratively rather than only punitively — by appropriating principles from regulatory theory developed for industries as diverse as mining and residential care. Luo s (2024, 1) analysis of a sample of 20 university GenAI policies reveals a broad consensus that GenAI potentially erodes the 
 originality   of  student  work,  thus  transposing  the  integrity-as-non-plagiarism  paradigm  to  the debate on GenAI ethics in education. Importantly, academic misconduct prevention has also been approached from the theoretical perspective of crime prevention (Birks and Clare  2023; Clare et al. 2022; Hodgkinson et al. 2015), and the facilitation of academic misconduct has been codi ed 
in law as an offence across a number of jurisdictions, including New Zealand and Ireland (Draper and Boland 2024).
While thinking of academic integrity as the opposite of academic misconduct may previously      have been a serviceable approach, the advent of GenAI and the resultant near-impossibility of accu - rate or reliable misconduct detection has problematised this binary (Ardito 2024; Perkins et al. 2024).      Perkins (2023) makes the case that GenAI contributions to student authorship should not, of them - selves, be considered plagiarism, but that a lack of transparent acknowledgement of their use con - stitutes  the  new  academic  misconduct  paradigm.  A  response  to  this  speci c  change  in  the 
assessment landscape is the oft-cited Arti cial Intelligence Assessment Scale or AIAS (Furze et al. 2024;  Perkins  et  al. 2024a;  Perkins,  Roe,  and  Furze 2024b)  which  aims  to  guide  academics  on de ning and communicating to learners the extent to which, and the ways in which they may 
use GenAI when completing assignments. This simultaneously underscores the impracticability of GenAI  bans  and  detection  and  reinforces  the  idea  that  good  assignment  practice  consists  in 
de ning acceptable levels of cognitive outsourcing. Furthermore, there are early indications that requiring students to declare their GenAI use in assessment is broadly ineffective (Gonsalves 2024).
More  recently,  Corbin,  Dawson,  and  Liu  (2025b)  have  identi ed  the  need  to  move  beyond 
 declarative  (4) and  discursive  (5), communication-based guidelines on GenAI usage in assessment such as the AIAS (4), since their effectiveness depends entirely upon students  compliance. Such approaches also risk leaving the  mechanics  (2) of assessment instruments unaltered, and de ne 
the (un)acceptable through linguistic instructions which students may disregard without conse - quence.  Structural  (6) changes which delimit the extent to which GenAI use is even possible are offered as a better solution. Importantly, however, the scope of the article is limited to assessment 
of learning.
Corbin  et al.  (2025c)  call  for assessments  to be  redesigned such  that  the  desired behaviour emerges naturally  from  the assessment  design   (7)  to  mitigate  the  unfeasibility  of  detection.  In short, at least certain elements of the assessment are carried out under conditions where GenAI is inaccessible. They offer the examples of a take-home essay, an online MCQ test, and a lab report, briefly  suggesting  how  these  might  be  altered  discursively  and  structurally,  but  they  justi ably 
 make no claim that the examples [ ] represent perfect assessment security  (7).
The systematic framing of AI as the opposite or avoidance of assessment misconduct is further problematised by policy and educational interventions which invoke integrity in their titles and mis - conduct in their substance. An example is Ireland s National Academic Integrity Network s National Principles and Lexicon of Common Terms, which de nes and offers many detailed examples of plagi - arism,  academic  dishonesty,  contract  cheating,  and  collusion,  but  de nes  and  does  not  furnish examples  of  academic  integrity  (National  Academic  Integrity  Network  2021).  The  European Network for Academic Integrity s (ENAI) glossary of terms de nes a similarly wide range of AI- and AM-related terms without situation in examples of practice (Tauginienė  2018), whereas Australia s Tertiary Education Quality and Standards Agency (TEQSA) enumerates behaviours constitutive of AI, but signi cantly more that  undermine  it (TEQSA  2022).
The  body  of  literature  theorising  AI  is  unexpectedly  scant  (Mej a  and  Garc s-Fl rez  2025). De nitions  and  discourses  of  AI  developed  to  date  represent  a  relatively  narrow,  bounded concept intended to apply across disciplines, rendering them extremely challenging to operationa - lise. Even that most conventional measure of academic misconduct, plagiarism, is problematic: the necessarily intertextual nature of thinking and writing makes comprehensive acknowledgement of textual  borrowings  a  complex  matter  rather  than  a  simple  procedure  (Howard  2000).  GenAI, however, is further enmeshing intertextuality-by-default in a development described as  postplagiar - ism  (Eaton 2023, 1), suggesting a need to refocus our attentions on ethical knowledge construction. Where today,  [u]niversity institutional responses tend to comprise a set of de nitions of misconduct, some moral persuasion, and perhaps a little training for incoming students  (Hodgkinson et al. 2015, 
3), the GenAI era demands co-construction by academics and learners of a contemporary ethics of learning.
GenAI also represents a novel integrity  shock , intensifying the debate about professional and 
educational  standards  in  Higher  Education  as  did  contract  cheating  (Awdry  and  Newton  2017; Curtis and Clare 2017) and Emergency Remote Assessment (Curtis  2024; Newton and Essex 2024) before it. If it is to be addressed meaningfully, novel AI policy and process should ideally reflect with some degree of clarity not only proscribed but also prescribed learner behaviours. For instance, 
we did not speci cally ask participants in our focus groups about GenAI, but they raised their con - cerns about whether ChatGPT use was a line not to be crossed or a legitimate learning and assess - ment aid.
Post-GenAI, then, there is no longer a shared ethics of the appropriate. Does GenAI use in learn - ing, teaching and assessment void learning and demand opposition or is it a potentially assistive tool 
to be thoughtfully integrated? Will it, indeed, revolutionise the effectiveness of education (albeit that 
this argument assumes that  personalisation  of learning is a virtue rather than a dismantling of the inherently social nature of education)? In the sphere of assessment, such questions are irresolvable 
and subject only to better or worse mitigation approaches (Corbin et al. 2025a), but we have not yet assessed their status in  the broader  eld of  academic  integrity. To so do  will require improved insights on evolving learner views on what is and is not important and what is and is not acceptable 
in the experience of being a Higher Education student.
Methodology
Our focus groups are a supplement to a survey (see Grimes et al. 2024) intended to allow us to evalu - ate students  social negotiation of AI and AM. All students of four of the university s campuses were invited to participate via an email containing a link to an expression of interest form. Of the 138 students who expressed interest, 38 were ultimately available and willing to participate. While this effectively constituted a convenience sample, all faculties and all degree stages were represented.
We aimed to facilitate participants to co-construct detailed and nuanced understandings of and positions on AI and AM than would be captured either by survey or through individual interviews. Focus group moderators adopted a minimally directive approach, facilitating development of ideas 
by posing prompts, signalling opportunities for participants to contribute, and elaborating or clarify - ing prompts where requested (Gr¿nkj r et al. 2011).
Conducted with ethics approval from our university s Human Research Ethics Committee (ref. MTU-HREC-MR-23-024-A), all seven focus group sessions were audio recorded, and verbatim tran- scripts were produced. The sessions varied in duration from approximately 40 to approximately       90 minutes, depending mostly on the number of participants. All participants were pseudonymised 
and had given their written informed consent for their transcribed contributions to be included in 
the dataset for analysis.
Thematic analysis of the data was conducted by the two  rst authors. This was guided by the research priorities outlined in the introduction and was, as such, a theoretical analysis. Each coder worked  independently  through  data  familiarisation  with  all  seven  transcripts  and  through  the initial process of open coding of two transcripts using NVivo. Both coders then compared and dis - cussed initial codes,  nding a strong degree of similarity in rationales and outcomes. A  nal set of codes was agreed and was reapplied to the four transcripts already coded and applied to the remain - ing three, again using NVivo.
With  the  nal  coding  frame  applied,  the  coders  organised  codes  into  descriptive  themes. Themes were concept-checked through a visual concept-mapping process to identify overarching patterns  in  the  in  the  data,  establish  connections  between  them,  test  their  validity,  re-test  the assignments  of  codes  to  each  theme,  and  eliminate  any  remaining  redundancy  or  overlap  in code assignment.
Findings
Our participants  concepts of AI and AM closely mirror the literature s frequent conflation of AI with measures to reduce or eliminate misconduct in summative assessment. This highlights an important problem: how, in the era of instant text-extrusion, can we reframe academic integrity as a sustained feature of being-a-student  in a university community instead of as a set of now-unenforceable rules to observe episodically when completing summative assessments? We outline below our partici - pants  views on both conceptions: AI as the episodic avoidance of assessment misconduct and AI as a disposition inherent to being a student.
We begin by outlining the more common conception of academic integrity as non-misconduct achieved  by  adherence  to  traditional  lists   of  rules.  We  capture  participants   thoughts  on  the most important components of academic integrity, namely not cheating on or plagiarising assign - ments, not cheating in exams, referencing,  owning  one s assessed work, not using ChatGPT (exces - sively)  in  assessment  completion,  not  becoming  dependent  on  ChatGPT  and,  tentatively,  using ChatGPT  ethically . A  nal thematic thread involves a wish for more and clearer guidance on the meaning of academic integrity from lecturers and institutions.
Academic integrity as the avoidance of assessment misconduct
Our participants  responses suggest that the conceptual overlap of AI and non-AM is pervasive enough that students have internalised it. It is important to note, however, that this is at least partly a function of to-date incomplete work on theorising academic  integrity comprehensively (Mej a and Garc s-Fl rez  2025). Under this condition, students cannot realistically be expected to articulate a  ne-grained account of an elusive concept. However, achieving an expanded under - standing of learner beliefs about academic integrity would logically pre gure the implementation of capacity-building in terms of arti cial intelligence literacy, which has repeatedly been posited as a potential corrective to growing pressures on the maintenance of academic integrity in recent litera - ture (Barrett and Pack  2023; Saunders, Coulby, and Lindsay 2024; UNESCO 2023).
Our respondents made some 41 distinct references to actions that either should not be done or should be done in their efforts toward de ning what constitutes AI and AM. This contrasts conspicu - ously  with  their  16  distinct  references  to  dispositions  and  values  which  comprise  integrity, suggesting the framing of AI as the opposite of or a reaction to AM is embedded, perhaps even fossilised.
Being able to assert ownership of one s work, or not cheating on or plagiarising assessments is a de nition of AI offered by three students. Work meeting this criterion, they argued, would  actually represent the level that the student is at, and the effort that they put into the assignment or the exam or whatever it was . Achieving this level of legitimate ownership of one s assessed work also involved  actually learning the work yourself ,  studying and not cheating, like, it being your work , and not claiming ownership of work actually produced by another student, a scholar, or, by infer - ence, a chatbot.
A further three participants believe that cheating in an exam or looking at what someone else is writing in an exam setting  obviously  constitutes academic misconduct. One drew parallels between examination misconduct and misconduct in reporting on experimental work, highlighting the possi - bility  in  each  scenario  to  copy   work  from  another  student,  thereby  negating  the  ownership  imperative: 
Being dishonest, plagiarism and, like, copying off someone else, cheating in an exam [ ] And, there are so many examples of that. I  nd in Science labs, people take other people s results a lot and because that s not really, like, punished or anything. [ ] A lot of people don t end up doing the lab properly and then they just go and copy someone else s. I don t know is that cheating or not, but   And then, yeah, in exams, like, or if you submit a piece of work and it s not really your own or anything like that.
13 participants understood AI to consist in referencing (seven references) any secondary material integrated  into  their  own  assignment  text  and  AM  to  involve  plagiarism  (four  references)  or 
 pass[ing]  off  work  that  isn t  your  own   wilfully  deceiving  the  system.   Where  one  participant describes referencing as the act of giving credit to the author of any information found  online, another suggests that using  reliable sources  was core to producing work with integrity. Plagiarism 
is perceived to occur when students  submit a piece of work and it s not really your own,  where its opposite is described by another participant as 
being original, [ ] having a backbone and what you produce is your own and whatever supporting arguments you get, they re quoted, they re referenced so you re not taking somebody else s work and somebody else s intellectual property as your own.
Integrity, therefore, is constructed as the opposite of plagiarism, speci cally. This view is shared by two more participants, the  rst of whom suggests that  academic integrity would be destroyed if someone plagiarises because you ve taken an academic report or document and you ve claimed 
it as your own and therefore the integrity of the document is removed . The parallel between integ - rity and anti-plagiarism is slightly problematised, though, by a participant who believes that  there s 
a big difference between someone who s either not cited or just cited incorrectly, and someone who s just decided that they re going to take a piece of document . A  nal component of the integ - rity-as-non-plagiarism argument is added by a participant who stresses that  putting things into your own words and not using Grammarly  ranks alongside referencing as an example of correct academic behaviour.
As noted above,  ownership  is quite frequently conceptualised by our focus group participants as 
the opposite of plagiarism and the essence of integrity, particularly in assessment, with nine students 
invoking this. Additionally, one student suggested that AI also required academic and disciplinary       competence, and believes that to complete an assignment with AI requires  understanding the ques - tion that s been given and just having knowledge and putting the correct information on the sheet, I suppose, and then the examples . This participant has identi ed  preconditions  to the plagiarism avoidance discussed by other participants by highlighting the need for conceptual clarity and argu - mentation through logical progression of ideas.
Conversely, submitting patchwritten work or plagiarised material was thought by  ve participants 
to be tantamount to AM. This was discussed both holistically, as a breach of the norms of academic life ( going against the grain of what you re taught about in college   cheating and submitting someone else s work that isn t your own and that you just weren t putting any effort into what you were given, like your assignment or your exams ) and technically, as an exercise in keeping one s  plagiarism percentage  below a speci ed maximum.
Turning  to  the  speci c  challenges  of  GenAI,  one  participant  speci cally  names  avoidance  of ChatGPT as core to integrity, indicating at least some belief that eschewing GenAI assistance is as important as cheating avoidance and honest effort. 
Like if you re a really integral student [sic.], I imagine that s a student that studies hard, helps people out, engages with the lecturers, engages in class discussion, doesn t, like, cheat in anything, doesn t use ChatGPT, and just gen - erally puts their best foot forward and is an honest, good student. [emphasis ours]
ChatGPT s  use  to  complete  assessments  was  equated  with  misconduct.  Three  participants       expressed the view that doing this was contrary to correct academic conduct. More particularly,       they  emphasised  that  ChatGPT  compromises  the  ownership  requirement.  Four  believed  that 
using ChatGPT in the assignment process was a clear example of academic misconduct. Conse - quences accruing from using ChatGPT and being  caught  were deemed to be the student s own 
 fault  since  they know that you shouldn t be using it .
Similarly, another participant — noting that lecturers had suggested that they know when text 
is  produced  by  ChatGPT  and  that  the  submission  of  such  text  would  result  in  no  marks  being awarded  —  described  the  choice  to  have  GenAI  produce  academic  work  amounted  to  setting yourself  up  to  fail .  The  same  student  expressed  tacit  support  for  using  GenAI  as  an  aid  to earlier  assignment  stages  such  as  idea  generation,  but  was  insistent  that  the  risk  of  failing  an assignment was  motivation  enough  to  ensure that  all  work  submitted  satis ed  the  ownership  criterion.
Another student, however, suggested that ChatGPT use was commonly masked by light editing 
of the chatbot s text output. Responding to our prompt on AM, they offered the example of      using ChatGPT. Just typing in the question into that and then just changing the odd few words around, which I know is really common. And then, like, just getting fake references as well .
While use of GenAI in the assignment process was generally equated with misconduct, a degree 
of nuance regarding its possible legitimate use in assessment was also observed. Speci cally, two participants perceived a boundary between productive use of GenAI and  dependence  on it, which was perceived as a pathway not only to AM, but also to learning loss. 
I don t know if it s technically a bad thing. I think that being over-dependent and over-reliant on it is a bad thing. It s like everything is evolving so   I just think that being over-dependent is the problem and not the actual software.
However, uncertainty about legitimate and unethical GenAI usage was obvious, further under - scoring the need for greater clarity on AI after GenAI s integration into educational and industrial settings,  as  highlighted  by  the  above  contribution.  A  second  participant  says  of  the  the  links between the two environments: 
Like, if you get into the habit of using and depending on it, you re also kind of stunting your own learning and, like, it prevents you from kind of progressing and, like, it s the whole point of college, like, to be challenging yourself, you know? And so then when you re going into the real world, you might be a bit like,  what do I do?  You need to kind of fend for yourself.
Interestingly, this participant — contrary to the literature s more common urge to integrate GenAI into learning, teaching and assessment (O Dea  2024; Pratschke 2024; Smeaton  2025) to prepare learners for an arti cial intelligence-infused workplace (Chan and Hu 2023; Chan and Tsi 2024; Chiu 2024) — surmises that over-reliance on GenAI could limit not only educational attainment but also readiness to enter the workplace con dently. Similar trepidations about over-reliance were found 
in a survey of Hong Kong-based Higher Education learners by Chan and Hu (2023). Their concerns spanned accuracy of information generated, the ethics of GenAI use, the potential stunting of intel - lectual and personal development, the possible obsolescence of the professional roles for which they 
are preparing, justice and equality implications, and the current policy vacuum regarding acceptable 
use of GenAI within institutional — especially university — contexts.
Although eight participants in our focus groups mentioned ChatGPT usage as an example of mis - conduct, a further three offered nuanced evaluations of potentially  ethical GenAI use. Speci cally, they believed that it is legitimate and ethical to use ChatGPT as an aid in the early stages of assign - ment preparation, particularly as a way of  generating ideas  and helping learners to  understand the 
key words  in an assignment brief. However, they add that standard research and writing processes should follow.
A more detailed, if also somewhat more problematic assessment was as follows: 
I think the keyword there is tool. It s like anything else. Like, if you re looking for information on a topic [ ], in theory, the medium is the same as if you go to the library, check out a book, read up on a speci c theory or topic and you get the information that way. I would probably use AI as a starting point, d you know? I usually still do that. To this day, if I want to look at something really, really quickly, really, really general, despite the fact that it s not the most reliable source, I d check the Wikipedia page  rst just for a general overview and then take it from there. But I think it would be a very useful tool in that regard, but obviously the temptation when something is spat out with such ease on a particular topic, particularly if it s a written submission work or something like that, then obviously the temptation is greater   on that. But yeah, I would say a very useful starting tool, but has to remain that: a tool.
Implicit in this student s narrative of GenAI as a  starting point  is a defence of the stance taken on its status as a  tool . Acknowledgement of issues of authenticity and reliability are countered by a narrative of neutral utility and the awareness of the need for some kind of  fact-checking . It is, however, pertinent that the limit at which the  tool  is abandoned and the process of human research resumes is not identi ed, recalling the difficulty being experienced by learners and educators alike in understanding where the boundary between acceptable and unacceptable use is now located, as reported by Corbin et al. (2025c).
Barrett and Pack (2023), too, found broad consensus between students and teachers on the acceptability of using GenAI to assist in early stages of assessments, particularly where the learner has previously demonstrated competence in the task being outsourced. Similarly, respondents to Johnston et al. (2025) survey largely endorse use of GenAI for the purposes of writing enhancement.
A  nal area in which our participants expressed worry about avoiding misconduct was perceived issues in relation to guidance from their lecturers and university. Four participants directly stated that they do not know what lecturers are asking for when they ask students to act with AI. This may, to some extent, be a function of uncertainty among academics themselves since, as Eaton (2024) has noted,  academic  integrity  policies  seldom  address  themselves  to  teaching  and  research  staff. However, that lecturers and the university are sometimes thought to be offering scant or evasive guidance is more likely a result of increasing uncertainty among educators regarding the changing norms of assessment integrity.
Three  participants  noted  limited  direction  and  explicitness  from  lecturers  or  from  university guidelines. One  nal-year undergraduate student argued that 
the only thing that I ve ever seen on that [GenAI] until my 4th year is lecturers saying  don t plagiarise, make sure you reference properly.  That is the only reference that I ve had to academic integrity. Yeah, the modules are on Canvas. I think it s popped up, but that s been it and I ve been here for four years.
Another claims not be aware of any resources, saying they  haven t seen anything come along the tracks . They go on to add that  maybe something has to come .
Integrity as inherent to being-a-student
As indicated above, some participants invoked values and dispositions intrinsic to the learner-as-per - son(ality) as determinants of AI rather than externally de ned and monitored requirements. These values equated to generally good moral behaviour, with one participant explaining that  when I think about integrity in general, I think about values, morals . There is a not insigni cant overlap between our participants  relatively vague construction of the concept of integrity and the loose 
de nition of academic integrity put forth by the International Center for Academic Integrity (2021, p. 4), which envisions a  commitment to six fundamental values: honesty, trust, fairness, respect, responsibility, and courage .
Our participants  thoughts on the values of AI reflected the incompleteness of the de nitions developed to date. Indeed, much as the ICAI de nition stops short of comprehensively de ning what constitutes academic integrity, so, too, did our students experience some difficulty in situating these value descriptors in a particularly academic context or practice.
Four participants directly link AI with honesty, which, perhaps unsurprisingly, is equated with doing assessments without deception. Two of these four also point to truthfulness with regard to what is submitted. One participant sees  being dishonest  as AM, and another adds that bending 
of rules  brings the whole thing into disrepute . A further two see dishonesty as an affront to  the system ,  stating  that  AM can  be  characterised  as  wilfully  deceiving  the  system   or  gaming  the system .
 Respect for the world of academia  is seen by one student as essential to AI, although they clarify that students should  just act the part [ ] because it is a privilege to be in education and you should show respect back to the institution by being honest . This sentiment is echoed by another student: 
I think integrity refers to you as a student and your self-respect and your kind of, like, pride and ego, but in the good sense and not, like, bad pride and ego, but your kind of   like how you carry yourself in the world of aca - demia and how you present to the world as a student.
Not unlike policymakers, it is at this boundary between theory and practice that our participants struggle to envision how this integrity might be enacted. Amid a sectoral identity crisis, this uncer - tainty on how the general desire to respect one s scholarly community and conduct oneself honestly 
can be achieved represents an estimable threat.
Responsibility was mentioned by two participants, and is of particular interest because, in both 
cases, this is constructed beyond the individual level and beyond the assessment realm. Instead, 
both participants highlight the responsibility of the individual student to the academic collective, 
and to the values and norms of that community as a measure against which the student can test 
their own personal  student integrity . One highlights  that  responsibility as a community; we have to support each other. And where it s, like, OK to support others. Not like supporting someone s       assignments and do it for them or not like that  [emphasis ours], pointing to a developed sense 
of reciprocal support as a component of integrity in an educational setting.
Responsibility to and in the academic community is also understood as a commitment to  be present within that community: 
Responsibility is kind of the integrity because that means you kind of de ne yourself in community: are you an integral person [sic.] or not? So even if you do some misconduct that will harm your integrity; as an example    
let s say you cut lectures   I don t think it s an offence, but let s say if it s an offence and that doesn t mean any - thing about integrity, right? You just don t show up for the lectures, but it can be considered as a misconduct. But then in the larger context, people may see, like, OK, this person is not attending lectures or not in the com - munity of education   that kind of has impact on your integrity as a person in the community. So, it s   it s a tough call.
Read in combination, both dispositions are clearly thought to be about mutual support, commu - nity, presence, and transparency, reflecting the discourse of  belonging  in the student engagement literature (Ahn and Davis 2019; Ajjawi et al. 2025; Pedler, Willis, and Nieuwoudt 2022; Tinto 2015). 
Interestingly, little comment on GenAI permeated the participants  discussion of the broad moral components of AI, although its use by students for social support, to enhance academic success, and/or to enhance engagement retention are now emerging as a sub eld of student engagement scholarship (Allen, Pani, and Cowling 2024; Nguyen et al. 2024).
Discussion and conclusion: toward a new academic integrity?
Mej a and Garc s-Fl rez (2025, 3) envision acting with academic integrity thus: 
[S]tanding before others and with others to protect the integrity of academic practice and pursue the internal goods and values of excellence related to learning and growth, and to knowledge production and sharing, that  de ne  education  and  research  as  worthwhile  activities  whose  value  we  collectively  endorse  and  in whose collective interpretation we actively participate. [Emphasis original]
Renegotiating AI for a fundamentally altered landscape will require sustained attention to displa - cing the outmoded paradigm of AI as the avoidance of assessment misconduct. Our study reveals at least some awareness among learners that integrity is dispositional rather than occasional, and indi - cates an important starting point for the remaking of AI for post-GenAI Higher Education. That the episodic understanding of AI which was dominant in our study pertains to a paradigm of knowledge production which is now all but obsolete indicates the urgency of this reframing. In responding to this, however, we will be addressing just one of the two pillars of AI (Mej a and Garc s-Fl rez 2025, 12):  integrity within academic practice .
The applicability of the detection- and sanctions-based understanding of AI which predominate 
in our participants  constructs will continue to diminish as GenAI grows in sophistication. It is worri - some that students expressed a need for better and clearer direction on  staying inside  the shifting boundaries between  the acceptable and  the unacceptable. These were  already  unstable before GenAI, and thus, it is now crucial that we advance our understanding(s) of how GenAI will remake academic ethics.
The predictable emergence of GenAI as a critical theme in our study is testament to the necessity       of keeping it  rmly in frame as we redraw the boundaries of the acceptable. However, this renego - tiation requires us to think not only about how we assess students, assure standards, validate learn - ing, and award quali cations, but also about which kinds of learning are essential to the satisfaction 
of these outcomes. For instance, does almost frictionless, arguably struggle-free,  personalised  learn - ing meet renewed AI standards? Can the integration of GenAI into the lecture theatre do so? As Kon - stantinidis (2025, 2) has asked,  [w]hat future do we tacitly normalize through the integration of these technologies?  Confrontation of these challenges will represent a response to the second pillar of AI —  the integrity  of academic practice  (Mej a and Garc s-Fl rez 2025, 12).
While Corbin, Dawson, and Liu (2025b) have already demonstrated the need for synchronous checkpoints  in  the  assessment  process  to  ensure  validity,  meaningful  renegotiation  of  AI  will require us to look, too, to the processes of learning and teaching: how can these be assured such that the potential utility of GenAI is not overlooked or constrained, but its use also does not threaten the integrity or the value of Higher Education? Here, it is worth re-emphasising the tension between those of our responses that framed GenAI as a necessary component of teaching and learning to ensure adequate workplace preparation and the countervailing view that cognitive over-reliance 
on it might result in reduced capacity to adapt to the demands of working life.
Perhaps more importantly still, if GenAI is now inherently a component of the debate on AI, more clear-eyed and more extensive debate on the very ethics of the technology and its impact on people 
and planet is critical to a reimagination of the integrity of academic practice. It is especially urgent in      light of emerging discourses of GenAI s utility as a  tool  or as an aid to a personalised — or, perhaps more accurately, individualised — experience of learning, which are reflected by some of our partici - pants. As Konstantinidis (2025, 3) has argued, this focus on utility risks  anthropomorphizing the technology,  suggesting  an  agency  or  empathy  it  does  not  possess ,  but  also  overlooking  the biases it propagates, the problematic power structures it reinforces, and the ethical compromises it necessitates.
While we are con dent that the  ndings outlined above are compelling, and a necessary addition 
to the wealth of survey data which already exists, we note a number of limitations of this study which may open the way for additional research. In the  rst instance, while our data are rich, our sample size was necessarily relatively limited, leaving scope for further qualitative investigation of develop - ing  attitudes  to  academic  integrity  and  misconduct,  particularly  in  light  of  increasing  cognitive offloading to GenAI. We note, too, that our focus group  ndings necessarily reflect the stances of those learners who volunteered to participate, who are likely to be among the university s more engaged learners.
Additionally, since our focus groups were conducted, the debate around the ethics and integrity implications of GenAI, in particular, has broadened. As we have indicated above, now-prominent issues that did not arise in our focus groups included, for example, material and non-material extra - ctionism;  data  privacy  and  (mis)use;  (in)equity  of  access;  energy  and  associated  environmental impacts; vested commercial interests; poor labour practices all along the GenAI supply chain; and intensi cation of biases and discrimination (Coeckelbergh  2022; Crawford 2021; Higher Education Authority 2025; Nguyen et al. 2024; Rudolph, Ismail, and Popenici 2024; UNESCO 2023). More quali- tative research with both learners and academics is therefore merited to measure the evolution of awareness of and interest in these core moral-ethical dilemmas.
