ABSTRACT
The study  of academic  misconduct  in higher  education  spans  generations. Reducing the problem among college students remains a top priority among university  stakeholders.  There are  several  theoretical  explanations  for why students cheat, including the institutional climate around academic integrity, motivational  achievement  goal structures,  peer  norms, and moral attitudes. These  models  have  rarely been  examined  in concert  to  ascertain  a more complete picture of the nuances of academic dishonesty. Understanding the interplay  between  these  theoretical  explanation(s)  would  be  an  important step  in developing  programming  aimed  at  reducing  cheating  in higher education.  The purpose  of this  study  was  toexamine  the  role  of these theoretical  explanations  in  predicting  academic  cheating  as  well  as a combined  model  with all constructs.  Using a sample  of 2,329 undergrad - uates,  we found  that  all  ve  models  were signi cantly  associated  with self- reported cheating, but in unique ways depending on the model. Implications for theory  and practice are discussed.

The study of academic misconduct  spans generations  and  has a long history  in  the  psychology literature  (McCabe et al., 2001). The prevalence of students  engaging in  academically dishonest behaviors is alarmingly high (Rettinger et al., 2024), prompting  a response from the research com- munity  to  better  understand  these behaviors and  to  reduce  their  occurrence.  Although  the  total number  of students  who acknowledge misconduct  has remained  relatively stable, the  amount  of misconduct has grown dramatically (Lee et al., 2024; Newton, 2018). This increase in frequency points to a pressing need to understand the personal and contextual factors that underpin academic cheating. A number of theoretical explanations for cheating have been offered, which help to inform efforts to develop programming, interventions, and policies designed to reduce and prevent cheating, especially the most serious offenses.
These theoretical explanations have included examinations of institutional climate (Scanlan, 2006), motivational achievement goal structures (Anderman  & Koenka, 2017), peer norms (McCabe et al.,  2001), and moral attitudes (Lee et al., 2020). The reason these four theories are of interest is because all share a common assumption that academic dishonesty is inherently wrong and problematic (Murdock 
& Stephens, 2007), that social context plays a role (Macfarlane et al., 2014), and that students can

However, these theories potentially yield differing results because they differ on the psychological mechanisms through  which students  come to the decision to cheat.
Importantly,  little research has been undertaken  to  examine models based on  these differing theoretical  explanations  in  concert.  Often, these models are examined  in  isolation (e.g., Bertram Gallant, 2017; Curtis & Clare, 2024; Macfarlane et al., 2014). However, there is an increasing need to examine academic integrity from a multitheoretical approach. Similar efforts have been proposed in the areas of motivation, personality, and development (Dweck, 2017). Combining  approaches and examining academic integrity from multiple perspectives simultaneously allows for a more thorough understanding  of the antecedents, mechanisms, and consequences of academic misconduct.
As such, the current investigation was an attempt to examine the dynamic interplay of these models of cheating to better capture the nuanced underpinnings for why and when students cheat in college. In concert with recent efforts to update the McCabe survey of academic integrity (Rettinger et al.,  2024), we took a psychometric approach to examining this issue by validating measures associated with the four selected theoretical explanations for cheating and then used structural equation model- ing (SEM) to  assess the  analytical capabilities of each theoretical  approach  both  separately and combined. In the following sections, we outline the four theories we examined and then  describe our goals for the current study.
THEORETICAL EXPLANATION 1: INSTITUTIONAL CLIMATE
An institution  with a strong  climate of academic integrity has a culture  that  supports  academic integrity. Academic integrity climate manifests through both formal policies, such as an honor code (Tatum, 2022), attitudes toward academic integrity, student knowledge and acceptance of academic integrity policies (Jordan, 2001; Young et al., 2018), as well as faculty knowledge and  support  of academic integrity policies (McCabe & Trevi o,  1993). Students  understanding and acceptance of an academic integrity policy is strongly and negatively associated with academic misconduct  (Jordan,  2001), and students  who attend  schools with an honor  code report  more knowledge and a better understanding  of the academic integrity policy and perceive less cheating overall than  students  at institutions without an honor code (McCabe et al., 2012).
Faculty play an integral role in creating and supporting the academic climate surrounding academic integrity (McCabe et al., 2012). Students report  fewer academic misconduct  behaviors when they believe they will face consequences and that the consequences will be significant (Miller et al., 2011). Students are less likely to cheat on homework when they are reminded of an institution-wide honor code and even less so when that reminder is combined with a warning about specific consequences of cheating (Bing et al., 2012). The academic integrity climate at schools with an honor code may explain why there is generally less self-reported cheating at honor  code schools when compared  to those without an honor code (McCabe & Trevi o,  1993).
Campus  climate also encompasses students   beliefs about  the  prevalence of misconduct  at their institution.  Climate is distinct from peer norms,  because climate refers to a nebulous belief about the behaviors of others, rather than specific, experience-based perceptions. Students who believe there is more misconduct are more likely to engage in it themselves (O Rourke et al., 2010). The role of the academic integrity climate in determining students  attitudes toward academic misconduct and misconduct beha- vior in combination with other factors, such as peer attitudes and behaviors, requires further investigation.
THEORETICAL EXPLANATION 2: ACHIEVEMENT GOAL STRUCTURES
Achievement goal theory is an empirically supported framework for examining motivation to engage in various tasks (Kaplan & Maehr, 2007; Urdan & Kaplan, 2020). The theory has been applied widely in many areas, including social psychology (e.g., Chu & Zhang, 2018), sports psychology (e.g., Cury et al., 2006), and  education  (e.g., Anderman  & Wolters, 2006). The theory  posits that  when an individual is engaged with a task (e.g., an academic assignment), engagement is guided by the goal(s) that the individual is pursuing.
Achievement goals have been operationalized  differently by various researchers, but  the  most widely accepted model includes four primary goals, including two types of mastery goals and two types of performance goals (Elliot & McGregor, 2001). A student who holds a mastery-approach goal when engaging with a task has the goal of truly mastering that task, whereas a student  who holds a mastery-avoidance goal is focused on avoiding misunderstanding the task. In contrast, a student who holds a performance-approach goal is focused on demonstrating their ability at a task in comparison to others, whereas a student  who holds a performance-avoidance goal is focused on not appearing  dumb   or incompetent when engaged with a task.
Achievement goals have been investigated with regard to how they are reflected in an educator s instructional practices. Students  perceptions of instructional practices from a goal theory perspective have been conceptualized as goal structures  (Kaplan et al., 2002). Goal structures  have been con- ceptualized in a variety of ways. Students are more likely to report perceiving a mastery goal structure in a class when they perceive the instructor as emphasizing mastery of content such as emphasizing the importance  of effort, taking the time needed to deeply understand  the class material, and  giving permission to embrace making mistakes during learning as an important part of the learning process (Meece et al., 2006). Students are likely to report perceiving a performance goal structure when their instructor is perceived as emphasizing differences in students  abilities (e.g., by publicly sharing exam scores), and making it obvious when students are not doing well in class. Some researchers have more specifically operationalized perceived performance goal structures in terms of extrinsic outcomes (i.e., an extrinsic goal structure); students report  perceiving an extrinsic goal structure  in a class if they perceive the instructor as emphasizing the importance of tests and grades, and spending a lot of time talking about tests and assessments (e.g., Won et al., 2020).
There is now a fairly extensive body of research that indicates that perceived goal structures are related in predictable ways to academic dishonesty (Murdock  & Anderman,  2006). This research indicates that students  perceptions of an instructor s focus on mastery of content and/or  on perfor- mance and grades are related to academic cheating. In particular, research indicates that a perceived mastery goal structure  is negatively correlated with academic cheating (e.g., Anderman  & Midgley,  2004; Anderman et al., 1998; Krou et al., 2021; Murdock et al., 2004). Most research on the relations between achievement goal structures and academic integrity has focused on perceptions of an extrinsic goal structure (as opposed to a performance goal structure), with results indicating a greater likelihood of cheating occurring in classrooms perceived as emphasizing extrinsic outcomes (e.g., Anderman & Midgley, 2004; Anderman et al., 1998; Murdock et al., 2001).
THEORETICAL EXPLANATION 3: PEER NORMS
Peer norms and behaviors, as well as perceptions of peer behaviors and attitudes, are consistently correlated with academic misconduct. Students use examples of their peers  behaviors to regulate their own actions, and when they observe their peers engaging in academic misconduct, they are more likely to engage in similar behavior (O Rourke et al., 2010). Researchers have utilized several theories to explain the influence of peers on academic cheating including social learning theory and neutralization theory. Regardless of the theoretical explanations, students  perceptions of their peers  behaviors are one of the most influential factors related to academic cheating. In a recent meta-analysis, Zhao and colleagues (2022) demonstrated  that  the perception  that  one s peers are cheating is one of the strongest predictors of academic cheating among students. Their analysis of the  perceived  peer  cheating  effect included  38  studies  conducted  over  an  80-year  span  and included multiple countries and over 24,000 students. They found the effect size to be moderate, which is stronger  than  most other  predictors  of academic cheating. The perceived peer cheating effect is stronger  in collectivist cultures, which may be due to how strongly students  value their peers  opinions,  fear disagreement, experience pressure to conform, and demonstrate  respect for authority.
Peer behavior can also have an exponential effect on cheating. In a retrospective study of students from three U.S. military academies, Carrell et al. (2008) established strong effects of peer influence on self-reported cheating. They used four-year cohorts rather than graduating year data to help reduce response bias among participants. Individuals  probability of cheating significantly increased as the number of peer cheaters increased even when they corrected for selection bias, school, and time.
The role of peers is central to the study of academic integrity. When students believe that their peers would disapprove  of academic misconduct,  they are less likely to  engage in  cheating  behaviors (McCabe et al., 2012;). At some institutions,  students  are encouraged  or even required  to report instances of academic dishonesty. Carrell et al. (2008) found  that  the military academies with the highest levels of peer reporting of academic dishonesty had the lowest levels of self-reported cheating. Although  their  sample was derived from  military academies which may have a peer culture  that deviates radically from  other  types of colleges and  universities, their  findings are consistent  with previous studies including McCabe and Trevi o s  (1993) seminal work that established that peers  behaviors, and the certainty of being reported by a peer for cheating, are strong predictors of academic dishonesty.
THEORETICAL EXPLANATION 4: MORAL ATTITUDES
Students  moral attitudes have long been theorized to be important  factors in explaining academic misconduct. Students  beliefs about the morality or ethical acceptability of cheating behavior and their  disengagement  or  neutralization  of responsibility for engagement in it have been two of the most widely studied types of moral attitudes. The former is theorized to oblige a course of action (e.g., if one believes cheating to be morally wrong, then one should not cheat), while the latter serves to reduce or negate culpability for engagement in behavior one believes to be morally wrong (e.g., Ajzen, 1991; Bandura, 1990; Stephens, 2017; Sykes & Matza, 1957).
Research on these moral attitudes has yielded a consistent pattern of results: Students  beliefs about the  immorality  of  cheating  have  been  negatively associated with  self-reported  cheating  (e.g., Anderman  et al., 1998; McCabe et al., 2012; Stephens et al., 2007) while their tendency for moral disengagement a positive predictor of it (Farnese et al., 2011; Haines et al., 1986; O Rourke et al., 2010; Stephens, 2018). Put another way, the more that students believe that cheating is morally unacceptable, the less likely they are to engage in academic dishonesty (Stephens et al., 2007). However, the more that students morally disengage or have neutralizing attitudes about cheating, the more likely they are to engage in those behaviors (O Rourke et al., 2010). These remain powerful indicators of students  tendencies to cheat in college.
THE CURRENT STUDY
The purpose  of the current  study was to examine the role of four analytical models of academic dishonesty based on  the four theories described above in predicting  academic dishonesty among undergraduate  college students.  Importantly,  each theory  was examined  separately and  then  all together.  This was to  ascertain  the  unique  contribution  of each analytical model, and  then  the predictive utility of all models being analyzed together simultaneously. The findings of the present study will help to further explain and hopefully reduce academic misconduct in higher education. We expected the findings to be in line with past studies of academic integrity, though we had no a priori expectations for the combined model as no past studies have examined all four models at once, and therefore, it is unknown how the constructs will operate when controlling for each other. Specifically, we hypothesized the following:
H1: We anticipated that a positive institutional climate of integrity would negatively relate to self- reported cheating and that a negative institutional  climate of misconduct would positively relate to self-reported cheating.
H2: We expected perceptions  of mastery  goal structures  to  negatively relate to  cheating  and perceptions of extrinsic goal structures to positively relate to cheating.
H3: We hypothesized that perceptions of negative peer attitudes related to cheating would negatively relate to self-reported cheating, and conversely, that reports of peers engaging in academic misconduct would positively relate to self-reported cheating.
H4: Finally, we expected the  perceptions  of moral  unacceptability to  negatively relate to  self- reported cheating and moral disengagement to positively relate to academic cheating.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Participants
Data  for  this  study  were collected as part  of a  larger  study  on  academic  integrity  (Rettinger et  al.,  2024).  Participants  were  recruited  from  six  institutions  across  the  United  States  and Canada,  including  a  research  university  in  the  Southeastern  U.S. (n = 1,216); a  public  liberal arts university in the Mid Atlantic (n = 548); a university in the Southwest of the U.S. (n = 192); and  a  community  college in  Ontario  (n = 418). A  total  of  2,374 provided  data,  but  45  were removed  prior  to  analyses for  various  reasons  (e.g. withdrawing  consent,  not  completing  the survey, careless responding, or being under 18 years old), thus leaving a sample of 2,329 students as the analytic sample.
Participants ranged in age from 18 to 81 years old (M = 24.2, SD = 7.6). About 65% of participants identified as female, 29% as male, 2% as non-binary, 1% as more than one gender, and 3% declined to respond. In terms of race/ethnicity, 58% of participants identified as White, 7% as African American/ Black, 6% as Hispanic, 15% as Asian, 10% as Multiracial, 2% as other race, and 3% declined to respond.
Survey design and procedure
This study was approved by the University of Mary Washington IRB board. All survey participants were recruited via e-mail by their institution  and provided informed consent prior to participating. Regarding compensation,  three  of the  institutions  offered entries  to  raffles for varying prizes. Participants  were treated according to the ethical guidelines of APA and data was collected anon- ymously. In line with past studies of academic integrity (e.g., Stephens et al., 2021), three institutions used complete samples and one randomly selected subsets of the student body for participation via simple random sampling. Response rates ranged from 3—10%.
The survey was administered  online in 2021 during  the COVID-19 pandemic. The survey was counterbalanced for all consenting participants. Half received the demographic section first, while half responded to demographic items at the end of the survey. The remaining sections and the items within them were also randomized and presented to participants. Attention-check items such as  Humans eat food  were inserted in the academic misconduct scale and academic integrity climate scales to assess participant attention. Using a single item (Zimmerman  & Langer, 1995), participants were asked to self-report how honest they were in answering the questions on the survey and were then debriefed. The attention-check items and honesty items, in addition to checking for straight-lined responses and multivariate outliers, were used to screen participants such that only participants who answered the items both logically and honestly were retained in the analytic sample.
Materials
The following section describes the measures used in the current study. For all measures, psychometric properties were assessed by calculating reliability estimates (both Cronbach s Alpha and McDonald s omega) and using confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) to assess the fit between the collected data and the theorized factor structures  via commonly used model fit indices including the comparative fit index (CFI), root  mean  square  of approximation  (RMSEA), and  standardized  root  mean  square residual (SRMR). Values greater than  .90 for CFI, less than  .05 for RMSEA, and less than  .08 for SRMR were considered indicative of good model fit (Hu & Bentler, 1999; Kline, 2023). Maximum likelihood estimation was used for all measurement models, and all models were estimated with robust standard  errors  to  account  for  non-normal  response  distributions,  including  zero-inflation  of responses on the academic misconduct scale (e.g., most respondents indicated they had never engaged in academic dishonesty; Satorra & Bentler, 2010). It should be noted  that  RMSEA values tend  to fluctuate as a factor of model complexity (McNeish et al., 2018). Thus, slightly elevated RMSEA values were not  considered indicative of poor  model fit when CFI and  SRMR values were in acceptable ranges.
When models exhibited poor fit to the data, modifications were made, and the respective measure was re-analyzed with CFA using the same sample. Factor loadings of less than .40 and evidence of cross-loading between factors were considered evidence of poor item quality and decisions of item removal were made as warranted. Cronbach s alpha and McDonald s omega were calculated using SPSS version 29, and CFAs were conducted in Mplus version 8.8 (Muth n  & Muth n,  2017).
Academic misconduct behaviors
We used a newly revised version of the McCabe academic integrity measure (The McCabe-ICAI Academic Misconduct  Instrument  [MIAMI]; Rettinger et al., 2024). Participants  were asked to indicate  how often, in  the  past 12 months,  they engaged in  a variety of academically dishonest behaviors. Responses were on a 0—4 scale (0 = Never, 1 = Once, 2 = 2—4 times, 3 = 5—10 times, 4 = 11 or more times) with  not  applicable to my program   as an additional option. There were 24 items included initially. One item ( Offering a professor, teacher or TA money, a gift or a favor in exchange for academic benefit ) was removed due to a lack of response variability. The remaining 23 items were entered into a CFA to assess model fit. Initial findings indicated poor model fit due to low loading and cross-loading  items. Six items  were removed,  resulting  in  the  following three-factor  solution: Collusion (4 items;   = .79,   = .83; e.g.,  Allowing another  student  to copy from you during a test or exam ), misuse of resources and sources (6 items;   = .72,   = .76; e.g.,  Using unauthorized notes or sources during a test or exam ),  and fraud/contract  (7 items;   = .64;   = .63; e.g.,  Changing  or making up lab or research data ). Results from a second CFA confirmed good fit to the data (CFI = .90, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .04], SRMR = .05).
Academic integrity climate
Academic integrity climate was assessed via a measure adapted  from previous literature  (McCabe et al., 2012; Rettinger et al., 2024) that measured students  knowledge of and attitudes toward the academic integrity policy at their  institution.  The original measure included  Likert type response scales as well as dichotomous  options  (i.e. yes/no). We utilized 22 self-report items that  assessed college students  perceptions of the academic integrity climate at their institution on a five-point Likert scale from (0) Strongly Disagree to (4) Strongly Agree. Respondents indicated their agreement with statements measuring the following dimensions: Students  understanding  of what actions constitute cheating, perceptions  of their  peers  attitudes  and  behaviors, perceptions  of student  and  faculty knowledge and support of the policy, and perceptions of the effectiveness of the academic integrity policy in deterring cheating, including the likelihood of facing consequences.
Preliminary analyses indicated that two items needed to be removed due to cross-loading ( I believe that the process for handling cases of cheating here is fair  and  Cheating is a serious problem here ). Once these items were removed, the following two-factor solution was entered into a CFA: climate of integrity (12 items;    = .84,    = .85; e.g.,  The faculty here clearly define what actions are considered to be cheating in their courses ) and climate of misconduct (4 items;   = .74,   = .76; e.g.,  Most students here ignore the academic integrity policy ). The results of the CFA confirmed the good model fit of the two-factor solution (CFI = .91, RMSEA = .05 [.05, .05], SRMR = .04).

Students  beliefs about  the  moral  unacceptability of academic misconduct  were assessed with an adapted version of the McCabe et al. (2012) measure. RESULTS
Data analysis plan
A psychometric approach via structural equation modeling (SEM) was employed such that the five models (one for each analytical model and then one combined version) were each estimated with the same sample of undergraduate college students, who all responded to the aforementioned scales. The model fit statistics, internal consistencies, and proportions of variance explained in the outcomes (e.g., 
R2  values) were generated for the five models and compared. For the remainder  of the paper, for simplicity and  consistency, models will be referred to as follows: institutional  climate (Model A), achievement goal structures  (Model B), peer  norms  (Model C), moral  attitudes  (Model D), and a combined model (Model E). Figure 1 provides a theoretical model guiding our study which outlines these five analytical models.
In each of these analyses, students  age, years of schooling, whether or not  coursework was in students  native language, whether or not students  received Pell grants, gender identity dummy coded (male and third-gender with female as reference category), race/ethnicity dummy coded (Asian, Black/ African-American, Hispanic/Latinx, and  Multiracial with White/Caucasian  as reference category), and status as a first-generation college student were all included as covariates in all models. Whether or not coursework was in students  native language and the dummy codes for third gender, Black/African American, Hispanic/Latinx, and Multiracial were not significant predictors for any outcome variables in any models and were thus removed from the final models reported in the manuscript. All reported coefficients are standardized  estimates. Descriptive statistics and correlations between variables are presented in Table 1.
To confirm that the sample size of the analytic sample was sufficient to detect significant effects, we conducted a Monte Carlo power analysis simulation in Mplus using the path estimates from Model E (the combined model). This was the largest and most complex model, so if sufficient coverage could be found for this model, then there would be evidence that the analytic sample was large enough to detect significant effects across all five models. Measurement models
Prior  to estimating  the  five theoretical models, measurement  models were assessed to confirm that  the various measures were psychometrically sound  and  could operate  simultaneously. The same  model  fit  indices  utilized  for  the  CFAs  were  also  consulted  when  conducting  these measurement  models, with  the  same metrics  for  evidence of good  model  fit being employed. Model  A  (institutional  climate;  CFI = .90,  RMSEA = .03  [.03,  .03],  SRMR = .04),  Model B (motivational  beliefs; CFI = .93, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04), Model C (peer  norms; CFI = .90, RMSEA = .04 [.04, .04], SRMR = .05), Model D  (moral  attitudes;  CFI = .93, RMSEA  
= .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04), and Model E (combined  model; CFI = .90, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04) all fit the data well. Model C includes three residual covariances between items in the  peer  observed behaviors scale and  the  MIAMI. These items  were essentially duplicates in content and phrasing, with the former asking about peer behavior, and the latter asking for first- person report.  The three items refer to  receiving  unauthorized  assistance from another  person while completing an assignment,   paraphrasing  or copying a few sentences or more  from any source without  citing it in  a paper  or  assignment,   and  using  unauthorized  notes  or  sources during  a test or exam. 
Primary results for  ve  models
In the following paragraphs, the results for each model are described in detail. Table 2 provides the model fit information for all five models. Additionally, Table 3 provides the variance explained in the three outcome variables across the models.
Model A
In Model A, institutional  climate variables (culture of integrity and misconduct) were entered as independent  variables  relating  to  the  three  types  of  academic  misconduct.  Although  overall model  fit  for  this  model  was  below  conventional  thresholds  (CFI  =  .89),  other  indicators 
indicated  acceptable fit to  the  data  (RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04). R2  values indicated that the variance explained in the outcomes was 16% for collusion, 22% for misuse of resources, and 10% for fraud/contract.
Associations between perceived institutional  climate and  self-reported  cheating indicated  that a climate of integrity did not  correspond  to academic misconduct  including collusion (  = .05, p  
= .25), misuse of resources (  =  .01,  p = .74), and  fraud/contract  (  =  .02,  p = .77). However, a culture of misconduct significantly and positively related to all three types of self-reported cheating including collusion (  = .35, p < .01), misuse of resources (  = .36, p < .01), and fraud/contract (  = .25, p < .01). In  other  words, the  more  participants  perceived an  institutional  climate that  promoted academic dishonesty, the more likely they were to report  engaging in academic misconduct. Thus, despite minor model fit limitations, hypothesis 1 was partially supported.
Model B
Mastery and extrinsic achievement goal structures were entered as independent  variables relating to self-reported cheating in Model B. Model fit statistics for this model showed good fit to the data (CFI  
= .92, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04); however the  R2  values for collusion (.10), misuse of resources (.13), and fraud/contract  (.06) cheating were relatively small.
Findings for this model indicated that perceptions of extrinsic goal structures were not significantly related to cheating including collusion (  =  .01,  p = .84), misuse of resources (  =  .04,  p = .12), and fraud/contract  (  = .002, p = .95). However, perceptions of mastery goal structures were significantly and negatively related to self-reported cheating including for collusion (  =  .17,  p < .01), misuse of resources (  =  .20, p < .01), and fraud/contract (  =  .15, p < .01). In other words, the more students perceived their professors were focused on learning and mastery, the less likely they were to engage in academic misconduct. As such, hypothesis 2 was partially supported.
Model C
Exogenous variables in Model C included peer norms (peer attitudes and peer observed behaviors), and self-reported academic misconduct variables were endogenous. Similar to model A, overall model fit was below conventional thresholds (CFI = .89), but the other model fit indices showed acceptable fit 
to the data (RMSEA = .04 [.03, .04], SRMR = .04). The R2 values for this model were quite strong, with 42% of the variance in collusion, 46% of the variance in misuse of resources, and 21% of the variance in fraud/contract  behaviors explained by the peer norms variables.
Regarding the associations between constructs, both peer attitudes and peer observed behaviors were significantly related to self-reported cheating. Peer attitudes were negatively related to collusion (  =  .24,  p < .01), misuse of resources (  =  .31,  p < .01), and fraud/contract  (  =  .14,  p < .01); and peer observed behaviors were positively related to collusion (  = .49, p < .01), misuse of resources (   
= .44, p < .01), and fraud/contract  (  = .37, p < .01). In other words, the more participants perceived that their peers thought  cheating was morally wrong, and the less they saw their peers engaging in academic misconduct, the less likely they were to engage in it themselves. Accordingly, albeit with minor model fit challenges, hypothesis 3 was fully supported.
Model D
Model D  included  moral  attitudes  (moral  unacceptability  and  moral  disengagement)  as inde- pendent  variables  relating  to  academic  misconduct,  which  again  was  represented  by  latent constructs  corresponding  to  three  different  types  of  cheating.  Model  fit  statistics  indicated 
good fit to the data (CFI = .93, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], SRMR = .04), and  the R2  values for the outcome variables in this model were strong (collusion: .31, misuse of resources: .41, and fraud/ contract: .16).
Findings for this model indicated that moral unacceptability significantly and negatively related to collusion (  =  .10,  p < .01) and misuse of resources (  =  .09,  p < .01), but not fraud/contract  beha- viors (  =  .02, p = .51). In other words, the more students thought that cheating was morally wrong, the less likely they were to collude with their peers or plagiarize material, but they were no less likely to engage in fraud or contract cheating. Conversely, moral disengagement significantly and positively related to all three types of cheating including collusion (  = .47, p < .01), misuse of resources (  = .55, p < .01), and fraud/contract  (  = .36, p < .01). In other words, the more participants disengaged from the  moral  responsibility of academically dishonest  behavior, the  more  likely they were to  report engaging in  it. With  the  exception of the  non-significant  relation  between moral  unacceptability and fraud/contract  behaviors, hypothesis 4 was supported.
Model E
Finally, we fit the  data  to  a combined  model in  which all of the  aforementioned  antecedents  of academic misconduct were included simultaneously. We did not have a priori expectations for this model. The model fit for this combined  model was acceptable (CFI = .90, RMSEA = .03 [.03, .03], 
SRMR = .04) and the R2 values for self-reported cheating were very strong (collusion: .49, misuse of resources: .58, and fraud/contract: .24).
In line with theoretical expectations, peer norms and moral attitudes remained significant predictors of self-reported cheating. In particular, peer attitudes negatively related to collusion (  =  .10,  p = .01) and misuse of resources (  =  .08, p = .03), but did not significantly relate to fraud/contract (  =  .01, p  
= .72). Peer observed behaviors remained a significant predictor of all three types of cheating including collusion (  = .51, p < .01), misuse of resources (   = .48, p < .01), and fraud/contract  (   = .34, p < .01). Moral unacceptability, much  like it did before, negatively related to collusion (  =  .08,  p < .01) and misuse of resources (  =  .07,  p < .01) but not  fraud/contract  (  =  .01,  p = .80). Additionally, moral disengagement positively related to all three  types of cheating including  collusion (   = .28, p < .01), misuse of resources (  = .37, p < .01), and fraud/contract  (   = .24, p < .01). Climate of integrity signifi- cantly and positively related to self-reports of collusion (  = .10, p = .01), which was opposite of the findings from Model A. Culture of misconduct, mastery goal structures, and extrinsic goal structures did not significantly relate to any of the self-reported cheating constructs in this combined model.
Overall, the findings from  Model E indicate that  peer norms  and  moral  attitudes  consistently emerged as consistent predictors of self-reported cheating when examined simultaneously.
DISCUSSION
The present study was the first to simultaneously analyze five analytical models based on theoretical approaches of understanding  academic misconduct. We examined the roles of institutional climate, achievement goal structures, peer norms, moral attitudes, and a combined approach in explaining self- reported cheating behaviors among college students. Our findings indicate that moral attitudes and peer norms are consistently related to self-reported academic misconduct in theoretically anticipated ways. The findings of these models indicate that the more students perceive cheating as a moral wrong and that their peers disapprove of the behavior, the less likely they are to engage in academic cheating. Conversely, the more students disengage from their perceived moral responsibility for cheating and perceive that their peers are engaging in academic dishonesty themselves, the more likely they are to self-report  that  they cheated. The  current  findings advance the  academic integrity  literature  by synthesizing decades of research across four theoretical approaches, along with testing the robustness of a combined model with all constructs included. The implications of these findings are vast, and we discuss them in detail below.
Contributions of each theoretical model
Each theoretical model offers some explanatory power for student academic misconduct and tangible steps for reducing it. First, institutions should work to develop a culture of academic integrity. Though lacking in  explanatory power, Model A shows that  an  institutional  culture  that  is perceived as promoting  academic integrity is tied to low rates of academic misconduct.  This result aligns with existing research on the presence of an honor code (McCabe et al., 2003; Tatum, 2022), faculty support of that code (Burrus et al., 2007), and the belief that violators will be reported, face sanctions, and that those sanctions will be severe enough to deter future behavior (McCabe & Pavela, 2004; Whitley,  1998). Similar results occur in each of the other theories: achievement goal structures, peer attitudes and behaviors, and moral attitudes.
Within the classroom, fostering a culture of mastery decreases the likelihood that a student will self- report academic misconduct (Anderman  & Koenka, 2017). Although Model B failed to tie extrinsic motivation  to academic misconduct  in a meaningful way, and  neither  goal structure  significantly related to  self-reported  cheating in  the  combined  model, Model B did  show the  importance  of a mastery orientation. Even without explicitly building a culture of academic integrity, an institution that  supports  and  develops faculty who promote  a mastery goal structure  in their  classrooms are contributing to less academic misconduct.
Similarly, faculty can support and develop students that are dedicated to their own academic work. If students that commit academic misconduct not only get away with it, but are rewarded with higher scores, positions of leadership, and internship or research experiences, the faculty and institution are promoting a culture opposite to academic integrity. Students watch their peers and want their peers to think highly of them. If academic misconduct is rampant, the student has no concern that their peers will be disappointed  in them.  This finding supports  existing research from  McCabe et al. (2012) indicating  that  students  concerned  with peer  disapproval  are less likely to  engage in  cheating behaviors. In addition, our findings support previous literature indicating that students that observe academic misconduct by their peers are more likely to engage in similar behaviors (Haines et al., 1986; Jordan, 2001; O Rourke et al., 2010).
Finally, developing moral attitudes that align with a culture of academic integrity should take place in every corner of campus. This work starts with helping students realize that engagement in academic misconduct is not just a violation of university conduct codes but also morally wrong — that is, always dishonest, often unfair, and occasionally harmful (e.g., Stephens, 2017, 2018, 2019). However, efforts to promote academic integrity must not only help students appreciate academic misconduct as a moral violation, they must also foster students  sense of personal responsibility for refraining from cheating. As evidenced in the  present  study and  many  others,  moral  disengagement (the  neutralization  of personal responsibility) can override and  undercut  moral  judgment:  many students  cheat despite believing it is morally wrong or unacceptable (e.g., Anderman et al., 1998; Stephens, 2018; Stephens et al., 2007). The past decade has seen a significant increase in the number  of universities requiring students  to  complete  (typically online)  courses or  tutorials  related  to  academic integrity  upon matriculation.  Research on  these approaches  has indicated  that  they can be effective not  only in increasing knowledge and attitudes (e.g., Cronan  et al., 2017; Curtis et al., 2013), but also reducing dishonesty (e.g., Dee & Jacob, 2012; Owens & White, 2013).
Bene ts  of a combined approach
Model E confirms the importance of each of the theories as not completely separate but intertwined. Institutional climate can influence and be influenced by classroom context and peer culture. Similarly, moral attitudes can be shaped by institutional climate as students learn the social norms from their faculty and peers. The combined approach accounts for the multitude of reasons that lead students to decide to engage in academic misconduct. Further, the combined approach accounted for the most variation in self-reported cheating behaviors. Additionally, the combined  model also produced  an unexpected finding, as climate of integrity positively related to collusion. This atheoretical finding may be a symptom of multicollinearity among all these related constructs or an unmodeled  interaction. However, future efforts should continue to integrate multiple models of academic integrity to ensure that a full understanding  of the antecedents of cheating is being captured in research.
Implications for research and practice
Most existing research studies examining academic misconduct among college students have focused on  either  one  or  a small number  of individual  predictors.  These previous findings have laid the groundwork  for our  study by narrowing  down  the  reliable and  strong  individual  antecedents  of academic cheating and other related behaviors. We have taken these empirically derived constructs and examined their ability to predict, both uniquely and in combination with each other, self-reported academic misconduct. Rather than relying solely on theoretical approaches such as those derived from criminology, psychology, and behavioral economics, we have taken a pragmatic approach supported by decades of empirical research (McCabe et al., 2012; Zhao et al., 2022). Further, we have presented further evidence of a measure of academic misconduct that can be used across studies (Rettinger et al.,  2024). Most importantly,  we have provided  an  examination  of which combined  factors predict misconduct which, in turn, provides practitioners with theoretical evidence to support specific types of interventions to promote academic integrity and prevent misconduct.
These findings have important implications for practice in prevention and deterrence of academic misconduct. We highlight the importance of moral and neutralizing attitudes in theoretical terms and recommend the same for practice. Interventions that help students to understand the harm to others that cheating causes can be expected to influence moral values and ultimately reduce misconduct. Models D and E point out the critical influence of moral disengagement on misconduct. Even students who disapprove of a particular cheating behavior report engaging in that behavior in some instances. Neutralizing theory proposes that students can exempt particular situations from moral scrutiny and act in their short-term interest by cheating. This finding indicates that any communication or training strategy that focuses on high-risk situations can help students to map their moral values onto real situations as they occur. For example, a training that uses role plays or discusses scenarios should go beyond the question of  is the behavior right or wrong,   but prompt  participants with resources to prevent  neutralization  as well. In  doing  so, students  can develop their  internal  moral  situation detector  that can prevent moral disengagement for a particular situation.
Limitations
The current study is not without limitations. First, the data were all self-reported from participants. Reporting bias is possible at the best of times, and in studies of academic misconduct, it is logical to argue that participants  may have underestimated  how often they engaged in cheating behaviors to avoid embarrassment, shame, or possible disciplinary consequences of reporting such behavior. They may have also overstated their honesty on our honesty item to compensate (despite our assurances of the confidentiality of responses), though  past research has shown the honesty item is effective in reducing participants  tendencies to underreport  sensitive behavior such as cheating (Zimmerman & Langer, 1995). Still, it is logical to argue that students who perceive cheating as more morally wrong may be less inclined to report that they do so at the same rates as other students. However, we contend that underreporting cheating behaviors would ultimately make it harder to find statistically significant effects, and our study clearly shows significant and strong relations between many of our theoretical constructs.
Relatedly, our second limitation has to do with the low response rate across institutions (3—10%). It is possible that nonresponse bias affected our pattern of findings. We did not conduct a nonresponse bias assessment which could illuminate the possibility of biased estimates of misconduct among the samples. It is possible that  those students  who responded  to the survey were different from non- responders. Participants  at all institutions  were contacted by mass e-mails via their student  e-mail accounts and given three weeks to respond to the survey. Only three of the universities offered to enter participants into a raffle to bolster recruitment efforts. Establishing a high response rate is considered an acceptable approach to obtaining unbiased estimates. Although our overall response rate was low (3—10%), our sampling frame was rather  large. Fosnacht et al. (2017) suggest that researchers can produce reliable estimates in low response rate conditions (5—10%) when the sampling frame includes at least 500 students. We far exceeded that sampling frame in our study.
Our third limitation is that the research was conducted prior to the advent of modern generative artificial intelligence (AI) programs such as ChatGPT, the use of which has increased exponentially in recent years (Lee et al., 2024). Thus, none of our measures of cheating take generative AI into account. Future studies would do well to include generative AI as part of models of academic misconduct to help understand  what roles, if any, traditional theoretical approaches to studying academic integrity have in explaining decisions to cheat via these means.
Another limitation of the present study was that the overall model fit (CFI) for Models A and C fell below conventional thresholds, though RMSEA and SRMR estimates were acceptable for both of these models. Therefore, the findings and support for our hypotheses associated with these models should be interpreted with caution.
Finally, our data were collected from institutions across the United States and Canada, limiting the generalizability of our study cross-culturally and internationally. Indeed, there have been recent calls for academic integrity research to account for cultural differences in college students, with a specific focus on racial and  ethnic differences in cheating (Shamoa-Nir, 2024). While our  study does not capture cultural influences on academic misconduct, such as perceptions of collectivism or individu- alism in the environment, results from several cross-cultural studies indicate similar factors to those we included in our models predict academic dishonesty in other  cultural groups. For example, in interviews with Arab college students in Israel, Shamoa-Nir (2024) identified three themes related to academic dishonesty: peer acceptance of cheating, moral justifications (e.g. neutralization)  for aca- demic dishonesty, and an institutional climate characterized by low likelihood of detection, a lack of consequences for dishonesty, and institutional culture that does not uphold academic integrity. Future research should incorporate the contextual factors that might influence academic dishonesty, includ- ing cultural differences and external pressures on students that may vary by group membership.
CONCLUSIONS
Overall, the findings of this study indicate that all four theoretical models of cheating are predictive of self-reported academic cheating, both in isolation and in concert, and that moral attitudes and peer norms  provide reliably consistent  findings across models. We therefore  suggest to institutions  of higher learning that they consider engaging students in exercises that make clear the moral short- comings of academic misconduct, how best to steer clear of rationalizing away the moral implications of deciding to behave in this manner, and to foster peer interactions that promote academic integrity rather  than  dishonesty. Doing so will hopefully reduce additional  violations of academic codes of conduct and further advance our understanding  of academic misconduct in higher education.
