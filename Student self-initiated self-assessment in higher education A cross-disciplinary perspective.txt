Student self-initiated self-assessment in higher education: a cross-disciplinary perspective
Ke Jiang , Gavin T. L. Brown  and  Lawrence May 
Faculty of Art and Education, The University of Auckland, Auckland, New Zealand

ABSTRACT  
Student  self-assessment  is  an  important  component  of  self- regulated learning. In higher education, the autonomy of young adults  suggests  that,  without  direction  by  curriculum  or instruction,  students  will  engage  in  self-initiated  self-assessment (SISA).  A  cross-disciplinary  semi-structured  interview  study  was conducted at a research-intensive university in New Zealand with 36 students — 12 each from initial teacher education, nursing, and 
 ne arts. Content analysis revealed a seven-step cycle — intention setting,  criteria  seeking,  criteria  calibration,  feedback  seeking, feedback deliberation, decision and revision, and submission and result — constituting the full process of SISA, labeled as Informed SISA. Based on the degree to which students engaged with the seven-step  cycle,  two  further  types  corresponding  to  partial processes  were  identi ed:  Intuitive  SISA,  driven  by  internal judgment without explicit criteria or external feedback; and Hasty SISA,  marked  by  minimal  engagement  and  a  rapid  progression from  intention  to  submission.  Disciplinary  differences  were  also observed: while nursing and teaching students typically enacted informed  SISA  grounded  in  professional  standards,  art  students were more likely to rely on intuitive SISA guided by self-de ned criteria.  The  ndings  provide  a  framework  for  educators  to recognize  and  support  student-led  evaluative  practices, highlighting  the  role  of  disciplinary  context  in  fostering  self- regulated learning.

Introduction
Student self-assessment is a process in which students evaluate the quality of their own work (Andrade & Brown, 2016; Yan & Brown, 2017). Previous studies in K—12 and higher education have reported positive effects of self-assessment on academic perform- ance and attitudes toward learning (Atrash et al., 2023; Brown & Harris, 2013; Yan, 2020; Yan et al., 2020; Yan & Brown, 2017). However, research into student self-assessment is normally based on the premise that the task of student self- assessment is required by the curriculum or the instructor. Thus, less is known about how students enact self-assessment when it is not formally required. This paper provides insights into how students report engaging in self-assessment when they initiate that process themselves. This perspective connects with the growing body of work on evalua- tive judgment — the capability to make decisions about the quality of one s own and others  work — which positions self-assessment as a core disciplinary and developmental practice (Boud et al., 2018; Tai et al., 2018).
Self-Regulated  Learning  (SRL)  encompasses  a  range  of  learning-related  concepts, including goal orientation, task-speci c strategies, metacognitive strategies, attribution theory, self-reinforcement, self-recording, and self-instruction (Panadero et al.,  2017; Zimmerman,  2015). Among the various models of SRL, Zimmerman s (2000) cyclical model  is  the  most  widely  cited  (e.g.,  Panadero  et  al., 2017).  Zimmerman  (2000) de ned SRL as  self-generated thoughts, feelings, and actions that are planned and cycli- cally adapted to the attainment of personal goals  (p. 14). Drawing upon and extending Bandura s social cognitive theory (Schunk, 2012), Zimmerman s model outlines SRL as a cyclical  process  involving  three  interconnected  phases:  forethought  (learners   prep- aration before performance), performance control (attention and effort during learning), and self-reflection (evaluation of learning processes and outcomes). Speci cally, the self- reflection phase includes self-judgment and self-evaluation (Zimmerman, 2002), which are synonymous with self-assessment. The SRL perspective views learning as a proactive, self-directed activity that students undertake for themselves (Zimmerman, 2015).
There is a strong theoretical connection between SRL and self-assessment, which is present throughout all phases of the SRL cycle (Panadero et al., 2017). SRL relies on self-assessment  through  self-monitoring  and  self-evaluation,  and  self-assessment,  in turn, positively influences students  self-regulation (Panadero et al., 2017). While self- assessment is most prominent in the  nal phase of SRL — after the learning task is com- pleted — it occurs at every phase if de ned broadly as the act of monitoring and reflecting (Yan, 2020). Conceptualizing self-assessment as an essential element of self-regulation, Brown and Harris (2014) proposed positioning it as a core competence within the cur- riculum. Harris and Brown (2018) further argued that all three stages of Zimmerman s cycle — forethought, performance control, and self-reflection — require self-assessment. As a key operational component of SRL, self-assessment enables students to regulate their thoughts, emotions, and behaviors across all aspects of a learning task (Brookhart, 2016; Harris & Brown, 2018, 2022; Panadero & Alonso-Tapia, 2013).
SRL and self-assessment are conceptually inseparable and mutually influential (Pana- dero et al., 2017). Although little is known about how self-assessment aligns with the speci c phases of SRL, researchers generally agree that self-assessment  is not only one aspect of SRL but also contributes to it by acting as a fundamental skill underlying SRL  (Yan, 2020, p. 226). As a form of self-assessment that depends on students  self- initiation  and  self-monitoring,  SISA  may  have  an  even  stronger  connection  to  SRL than formal, externally prompted forms of self-assessment. SISA is closely linked to agency and autonomy — qualities considered essential in higher education. This view of self-assessment aligns with Yan et al. s (2020) notion of  agentic and autonomous  self-assessment, in contrast to teacher-directed approaches.
Sargeant et al. (2010) presented a multidimensional model of informed self-assess- ment, emphasizing that it is not solely a cognitive act but a socially and contextually influenced  process.  The  model  highlights  how  learners  integrate  internal  judgments with external feedback from peers, mentors, and institutional cues to construct a more accurate and developmental understanding of their performance. Informed self-assess- ment, in this view, is an iterative, interactive process that supports ongoing professional growth and adaptive learning. Epstein et al. (2008) proposed the concept of  informed self-assessment  which emphasizes the importance of both internal and external data for  self-assessment and the integration of the two types of information for effective self-assessment. According to Epstein et al., useful internal data include self-awareness of thoughts, feelings, emotions, and sensations, and sources of external data include people and processes. Epstein et al. (2008) de ned informed self-assessment as a power - ful type of self-assessment resulting from  the integration of high-quality external and internal  data  to  assess  current  performance  and  promote  future  learning,  and  the capacity for ongoing self-monitoring  (p. 11). McDonald et al. (2022) explored  informed self-assessment  among medical students and identi ed a greater sense of belonging and evidence of progress as the most important factors leading to informed self-assessment. Yan and Brown (2017) formulated a cyclical self-assessment process model that consisted of three actions (i.e., determining performance criteria, self-directed feedback seeking, and self-reflection), addressing typical actions and scenarios involved in self-assessment as a holistic, dynamic, and continuous process.
The research gap and research question
Existing influential models of self-assessment provide robust frameworks but assume the task is formally required or curriculum embedded. Yan and Brown s (2017) cyclical model effectively captures self-assessment as a holistic process, yet it was developed in contexts where self-assessment is a structured, externally prompted activity. Similarly, Sargeant et al. s (2010) model of informed self-assessment illustrates how learners inte- grate internal and external feedback, but it focuses on contexts where benchmarks and feedback  are  systematically  provided.  While  invaluable  for  formal  self-assessment, these models may not fully account for the processes students use when they indepen- dently  evaluate  their  work.  Teacher-designed  self-assessment,  though  essential  for scaffolding skills, risks becoming compliance-oriented unless it fosters the autonomous evaluative  agency  central  to  self-regulated  learning  (Etel pelto  et  al., 2013).  This agency is what we term student-initiated self-assessment (SISA).
Furthermore, the literature on evaluative judgment and disciplinary epistemologies indicates that self-assessment is not generic but shaped by  eld-speci c norms (Becher & Trowler, 2001; Boud et al., 2018). Professional disciplines socialize students toward explicit,  communal  standards,  while  creative  disciplines  often  emphasize  subjective, internal criteria (Knight,  2002; Sadler, 2014). However, prior work has not examined how these disciplinary contexts influence self-initiated — rather than assigned — assess- ment practices.
We argue that a comprehensive understanding of self-assessment must encompass not only assigned tasks but also the self-initiated processes students undertake independently to evaluate and improve their work. This form, SISA, represents a key manifestation of learner agency. Given its likely high frequency and fundamental connection to self-regu- lated learning, SISA warrants greater attention. It remains unclear how students engage in SISA, what forms it takes, and how disciplinary contexts shape these voluntary pro- cesses. This study addresses that gap by investigating how New Zealand university stu- dents engage in SISA.
This study focused on student-initiated, unreported, and ungraded self-assessment processes  used  to  evaluate  and potentially  improve  the  quality of  students  work.  It was conducted with university students from two professional disciplines and one crea- tive discipline. The aims were to identify the key processes of SISA, develop a typology of its enactment, and explore how these differ between professional and creative disciplines. This study therefore positions SISA as a critical, under-examined component of evalua- tive expertise — a core graduate capability for navigating complex professional and crea- tive landscapes (Boud & Molloy, 2013). The overarching research question is: How do New Zealand university students engage in SISA?
Methods
This study employed semi-structured interviews to capture the nuanced, self-initiated processes  of  SISA.  This  method  was  chosen  for  its  interactive  and  flexible  nature, which is well suited to exploring students  internal experiences, meanings, and undocu- mented  practices  that  are  not  easily  accessed  through  surveys  or  observation  alone (Maxwell, 2012). The interviews aimed to uncover participants  inner experiences and meanings  of  SISA  (Corbin  &  Strauss, 2014).  It  was  hypothesized  that  SISA  would exhibit different patterns between creative practice-oriented disciplines and profession- oriented  disciplines.  Since  e-portfolios  are  popular  assessment  tools  used  across various academic disciplines in higher education (Deneen et al., 2018; Scartabello et al., 2018),  e-portfolio  work  was  selected  as  the  context  for  exploring  SISA  in  this study. For practical reasons, the study included student work submitted through all e- portfolio platforms adopted across different courses, as each course or discipline used its own designated platform. To maintain focus, the study excluded consideration of how the design and functionality of individual e-portfolio platforms might affect stu- dents  SISA activities.
Participants
Participants were recruited from a large (N ≈ 40,000), publicly funded, research-inten- sive university located in New Zealand s largest metropolitan region, home to approxi- mately one-third of the national population. The sample comprised 36 students — 12 each from two profession-oriented disciplines (nursing and teaching) and one creative practice-oriented discipline (the arts) — all of whom were required to submit an e-port- folio at one or more stages of their program. This sample size was sufficient to achieve qualitative data saturation (Hennink & Kaiser, 2021). Participants were assigned coded identi ers: T01—T12 for teaching, N01—N12 for nursing, and A01—A12 for the arts.

Data collection
The interview questions were open-ended and aimed to generate conceptually indepen- dent categories and themes. The interview questions asked whether and how students self-assessed the quality of their work before submitting it, how they decided if their work was good, how and where they got feedback if they thought it was needed, and what  challenges  they  faced  in  judging  how  to  improve  their  work.  The  individual interviews  were  in-depth,  taking  between  35  and  90 min  so  that  the  categories  and themes  were  substantiated  by  the  participants   thick,  nuanced  descriptions  of  their inner thoughts, feelings, and experiences (Kruger et al., 2019).
In accordance with institutional review board approval (Ref: UAHPEC27708), access permission from each program s Dean was obtained. Advertisements for the study were disseminated with the assistance of instructors for courses using e-portfolios. Partici- pants volunteered through email to the  rst author and were encouraged to disseminate the opportunity to their colleagues. The  rst author conducted all interviews face-to-face or online according to the participants  preference. All interviews were audio-recorded with the participant s permission.
Data analysis
Data were analyzed using qualitative content analysis, a systematic technique for identi- fying categories and patterns through explicit coding rules (Krippendorff & Bock, 2008; Weber, 1990). The analysis followed an iterative, primarily inductive process, informed by concepts from self-regulated learning theory. In the initial phase, all interview tran- scripts were read multiple times for immersion, followed by line-by-line open coding to generate descriptive, low-inference codes (e.g.,  checking the rubric ,  comparing to my last submission ,  asking a flatmate for opinion ). These codes were then iteratively compared, grouped, and abstracted into broader conceptual categories (e.g.,  Sources of  Criteria ,  Feedback-Seeking  Actions ,  Emotional  Dimensions  of  Judgment ) through constant comparison within and across disciplinary groups.
Through axial coding, relationships between categories were examined, leading to the abstraction of two core themes: a sequence of seven recurring steps constituting a full SISA cycle, and distinct patterns of engagement forming the basis for the three SISA types (Informed, Intuitive, Hasty). Following this inductive phase, a structured coding scheme was developed, de ning each step and type, and applied systematically to all 36 transcripts to ensure consistent identi cation of processes and to enable quantitative summaries of frequencies across disciplines (Table 2). To enhance reliability, an indepen- dent rater trained on the coding scheme coded a randomly selected subset of  ve tran - scripts  (approximately  14%  of  the  data).  Inter-rater  agreement  was  strong  (average Cohen s κ = .90; Stemler, 2004), supporting the consistency of the analytic framework.

Seven steps of the full SISA process
SISA emerged as a dynamic cycle of interrelated steps through which students regulate assessment-related thinking and actions. The process begins with intention, followed by criteria seeking (identifying standards), criteria calibration (gauging work against standards), feedback seeking (gathering input from others), and feedback deliberation (interpreting and reconciling external and internal feedback). It then moves to decision and revision (acting on judgments) and concludes with submission and result. Together, these steps form an iterative, reflective, and intentional process.
Step 1: intention
SISA begins with students  intention to self-assess, shaped by cognitive, emotional, and situational factors. Four key drivers emerged: outcome orientation, openness to self-cri- ticism, time availability, and learned habit. Outcome orientation, common in nursing and education, motivated students to self-assess to improve grades. As nursing student N02 said,  De nitely, I will assess myself  rst because the portfolio is one of the main jobs   to be sure that I can get a good grade.  Teaching student T06 echoed,  a necessary step before submitting anything important.  Others were driven by openness to self-criti- cism — an emotional readiness to engage with their weaknesses. Nursing student N11 reflected,  The  fear  of  self-criticism  also  plays  a  role  in  my  hesitancy   it s  like opening a can of worms that I m not sure I m prepared to deal with.  Art student A06 shared,  I used to avoid looking too closely at my own work, but now I see it as just part of growing.  Time availability also affected the ability to self-assess. Art students cited competing demands. A12 noted,  Balancing school, work, and personal life leaves limited time   only small pockets here and there.  Finally, some described SISA as a learned  habit  developed  through  repeated  reflection.  Art  student  A11  remarked,  I de nitely self-assess my e-portfolio work before submitting it. It s like a natural instinct for me now.  Others viewed it as a normalized part of their creative process. These drivers show that intention in SISA is multifaceted — shaped by goals, emotions, context, and disciplinary norms. Recognizing this complexity is crucial for designing supports that foster authentic self-assessment across contexts.
Step 2: criteria seeking
Criteria seeking is the active process by which students identify benchmarks for judging their work. This marks a shift from internal motivation to anchoring assessment in stan - dards — professional, classroom-based, or self-de ned. Teacher education students often referred to professional standards, drawn from curriculum or  eld guidelines, which they saw as objective and authoritative. Teaching student T07 explained:  As I assess the quality of my e-portfolio work, I pay close attention to the alignment with curriculum standards and educational best practices   to ensure they adhere to recognized guide - lines.  In contrast, classroom standards were sometimes unclear. Nursing student N05 said,  I wish there were clearer guidelines or examples to follow so that I can have a 
better sense of what constitutes excellent work.  Vague or implicit standards led to uncer- tainty in aligning self-judgments with institutional expectations. Art students most often cited self-de ned standards rooted in personal aesthetics, goals, and expression. Their criteria balanced technical precision and emotional impact. Art student A04 shared:  First, I look at the technical aspects   Then, I focus on the emotions and impact   If 
my images are dull or lack meaning, I consider them inadequate.  These variations high- light disciplinary differences: while professional and classroom standards offer external structure,  self-de ned  criteria  reflect  internalized,  reflective  evaluation.  Across  all cases, criteria-seeking legitimizes SISA by transforming it from vague reflection into standard-based judgment.
Step 3: criteria calibration
Criteria calibration is the reflective process of applying selected standards to evaluate one s own work. It involves two strategies: benchmarking and balancing. Benchmarking compares current work to references — past submissions or peer exemplars. Art student A04 said,  I compare it to my previous projects. I ask myself if I ve improved     Nursing student N07 emphasized,  We got to have some comparisons of previous portfolios or existing  portfolios      highlighting  the  value  of  external  models.  Balancing  captures the emotional effort of judging fairly. Teaching student T09 shared:  One of the main challenges   is  striking  the  balance  between  being  self-critical  and  recognizing  my achievements.  High standards drove improvement but risked perfectionism. Thus, cali- bration is not just rational judgment. It involves tracking growth over time (benchmark - ing), managing emotions (balancing), and aligning values with expectations. As a bridge between identifying and acting on standards, calibration is a complex, crucial phase of SISA.
Step 4: feedback seeking
Feedback seeking is the intentional effort students make to gain external perspectives on their e-portfolio work. While self-assessment starts as a solitary act, it is often enriched through social interaction. In SISA, feedback seeking marks a shift from internal cali- bration to dialogic engagement, involving three dimensions: perceived importance, bar- riers, and preferred channels. Students strongly recognized feedback s role not just as correction but as a way to deepen their evaluative insight. Art student A04 said,  Their fresh eyes can catch things I might have missed. Their critiques help me understand my strengths and weaknesses , highlighting awareness that self-assessment alone has limits. Similarly, teaching student T06 noted,  Without objective input from experienced educators, it becomes difficult to identify blind spots and areas that require improve- ment.  Feedback is thus actively sought to expand the student s evaluative capacity, rein- forcing learner agency. Despite this, barriers like time constraints often limited feedback seeking. Nursing student N04 admitted,  I don t actively seek feedback, to be honest. I know I probably should, but I feel like I m too busy with everything else.  This tension between valuing feedback and lacking resources is a major challenge for SISA practice. Students used a mix of formal and informal feedback channels, influenced by accessibility and discipline. Nursing student N02 preferred asynchronous digital feedback:  Email is very convenient; it s better for my colleagues to just email and give me feedback.  In con- trast, creative students favored peer and social media platforms. Art student A04 shared,  I usually share my photos on social media platforms like Instagram and Facebook, where I can get feedback from friends, family, and other photographers.  This shows feedback seeking  varies  by  disciplinary  norms,  networks,  and  technology.  Overall,  feedback seeking in SISA is a deliberate, context-sensitive strategy. Its success depends on students  willingness, time, and resources, and their ability to navigate diverse channels. It marks a key moment where individual reflection grows into collective dialogue.
Step 5: feedback deliberation
Feedback deliberation is a crucial reflective stage where students critically evaluate feed- back s relevance, accuracy, and usefulness. Students act not just as recipients but as active judges, deciding which feedback to accept, reinterpret, or reject. Their approaches often focused on negative feedback (tone), biased feedback (credibility), unspeci c feedback (clarity), and how feedback could be implemented (utility), all influencing their willing- ness to make changes. For some, negative feedback wasn t discouraging but a growth opportunity. Nursing student N03 reflected,  I wouldn t consider people s feedback as negative because giving feedback is a way to improve your work, so I wouldn t take that as a challenge.  This shows emotional readiness and a growth mindset essential for transformative SISA. However, feedback wasn t always accepted uncritically. Students worried about bias, especially from familiar or anonymous sources. Art student A07 said,  I  nd it difficult to get unbiased opinions. Friends and family might be too nice   and strangers online can be harsh and unhelpful.  This reveals a dilemma: peer and public feedback is frequent but often inconsistent or partial. Students must discern feedback value  against  the  giver s  motives  and  expertise.  Unspeci c  feedback  was  another problem. Some found comments lacked actionable detail, causing uncertainty. Nursing student N10 said,  Sometimes, the feedback from instructors is not very speci c, and I feel lost on how to improve.  This highlights how vague feedback stalls effective delibera- tion. Students also struggled to implement diverse or conflicting suggestions. Art student A03 expressed,  Integrating multiple ideas may not be feasible. The biggest challenge   is how to convince myself to let go of certain ideas.  This shows the intrapersonal tension of balancing external opinions with personal vision, underscoring SISA s selective nature, where feedback is negotiated, not blindly followed. In sum, feedback deliberation is a dynamic, demanding process of interpretation, judgment, and self-regulation. It re-inte - grates external input into self-assessment, requiring students to decide how or whether to act on feedback. This deliberative skill is key to sustaining effective SISA practice.
Step 6: decision and revision
Decision and revision is a pivotal SISA stage where internal reflection shifts to outward action. Students move from deliberating their e-portfolio s quality to making concrete choices about what, how, and whether to revise. Unlike earlier diagnostic stages, this step is decisive and transformative, showing the learner s ability to turn self-assessment into real improvements. Decisions involve both cognitive judgment and emotional readi- ness. Art student A07 captured this well:  If spontaneous self-assessment is merely a fleeting thought in one s mind and ends after introspection, then I don t think it holds much value. I don t believe that someone who lacks the ability to put thoughts into action  can  bene t  signi cantly  from  constant  self-assessment  without  taking  any action.   This  highlights  the  difference  between  reflective  awareness  and  actionable agency, emphasizing that insight without implementation risks making SISA hollow or performative. Revision itself wasn t always straightforward. Students described it as nego- tiation — balancing competing feedback, personal or professional judgment, and time constraints. Revising could mean meeting formal criteria but also required emotional labor, such as letting go of cherished ideas or restructuring  nished work. Strategic decision-making was key: deciding what to change, what to preserve, and how much effort to invest. When feedback or criteria were vague or conflicting, revision became hesitant or ad hoc. Ultimately, decision and revision synthesize all prior SISA steps — intention, criteria, calibration, and feedback — into a tangible, assessable outcome. This step sets SISA apart from passive reflection or routine editing by being deliberate, evi- dence-based, and learner-driven.
Step 7: submission and result
Submission and result is the  nal SISA stage, marking both the culmination of effort and the start of a new evaluative cycle. This phase is emotionally charged, as students reconcile their internal judgments with external assessments, shifting from self-directed evaluation to  institutional  validation.  Submission  signals  a  moment  of  closure  where  students decide their work meets acceptable standards. Nursing student N11 explained:  After I have made up my mind to submit, I submit with caution, and then I look forward to receiv- ing a good result.  This reveals a mix of cautious con dence and lingering doubt, showing the tension between self-trust and external validation inherent in SISA. After submission, students move from active agency to passive waiting as institutional judgment takes over. Results — grades or feedback — reflect not only work quality but also the accuracy of stu- dents  self-assessment. Emotional reactions vary from pride to disappointment, influencing future engagement with SISA. Positive outcomes reinforce belief in self-assessment; poor or unexpected results may shake con dence. Thus, submission and result form a feedback loop fueling the next self-assessment cycle. This stage also highlights differences in risk tol- erance  and  self-perception.  Some  submit  only  after  meeting  high  personal  standards; others submit pragmatically, balancing time and adequacy. Submission is a strategic act where internal evaluation meets external accountability.
Three types of SISA
Analysis of student accounts revealed notable variation in how self-assessment processes were enacted. To capture this variation, we developed a typology based on the degree of completion and coherence across the seven steps of the full SISA process (see Figure 1). This typology comprises three types: Informed SISA, Hasty SISA, and Intuitive SISA. The types are distinguished by the extent to which students engaged in deliberate, reflective, and criteria-informed practices, particularly within the core loop of Information-Driven Self-Regulation (IDSR) — involving criteria seeking, calibration, feedback seeking, and deliberation. Each type reflects a qualitatively different mode of self-assessment with implications for learning depth and metacognitive development.
Informed SISA
Informed SISA represents the most complete and reflective form of student-initiated self- assessment. It follows a dynamic, seven-step process: beginning with intention, students

proceed to criteria seeking, deliberation, feedback seeking, calibration, decision and revi- sion, and  nally submission and result. Central to this process is sustained engagement with the IDSR core, which fosters coherence between internal judgments and external standards. Students demonstrating informed SISA actively consult rubrics or bench- marks, seek and evaluate feedback, and make strategic revisions. This process is recursive and adaptive, enabling the learner to align performance with evolving understanding. For example, teaching student T08 described: 
Before  nalizing my portfolio, I went back to the rubric to double-check whether all the learning outcomes were addressed. I also asked a peer from my class to review it and give me  honest  feedback.  Based  on  her  suggestions,  I  reorganized  the  structure  and  added more classroom examples to strengthen my reflection.
In this case, T08 intended to meet professional registration standards and actively sought relevant criteria by reviewing the official rubric and curriculum documents. He then cali- brated his work section by section against these standards, deliberated on peer feedback, and  made  targeted  revisions  such  as  reorganizing  content  and  adding  classroom examples. This complete and criteria-driven process illustrates the iterative nature of informed SISA.
Informed SISA typi es a systematic and feedback-rich approach to self-assessment, contributing to higher-order thinking and academic agency. Its coherence and deliber - ateness make it the closest match to the theoretical model in Figure 1.
Hasty SISA
At the other end of the spectrum is Hasty SISA, characterized by minimal or fragmented engagement with the self-assessment process. This type often arises from time pressure, low motivation, or lack of awareness, leading students to move directly from intention to submission without engaging with the IDSR loop. Criteria and feedback are typically overlooked, and revision is either skipped or super cial. Hasty SISA lacks the deliberative and adaptive qualities of informed SISA, reducing self-assessment to a procedural or per- functory act. As art student A10 admitted:  For various reasons, sometimes I don t give my work a second thought before I submit it, although I do want to self-assess. 
Such situations illustrate how the intention to self-assess may be followed almost immediately  by  submission,  particularly  when  students  experience  time  pressure  or perceive the task as low-stakes. In these cases, core processes such as seeking criteria, cali- bration, and feedback are bypassed entirely.
This type reflects a reactive or compliance-oriented approach, with limited develop- mental value. It underscores the need for scaffolding and time management support to foster deeper engagement.
Intuitive SISA
Intuitive SISA occupies a middle ground between informed and hasty approaches. Stu- dents in this category demonstrate some engagement — typically in decision and revision — but rely primarily on instinct or personal judgment rather than articulated criteria or external input. While they may value self-assessment, their processes remain internalized and loosely structured, bypassing key steps of the IDSR loop such as feedback seeking and calibration. Their revisions are often based on perceived   t  rather than evidence-based comparison with standards. Art student A12 explained:  I self-assess my work no more than my gut feeling, although I always do a bit of polishing before submission. 
For instance, when preparing her digital art portfolio, A12 described relying on intern- ally de ned standards such as  emotional impact  and  visual cohesion  rather than formal criteria. Her evaluation involved reviewing the overall collection to determine whether it  felt right , and revisions consisted mainly of intuitive polishing. Although she occasion- ally  sought  friends  general  impressions,  these  were  rarely  used  as explicit  reference points for revision.
Intuitive SISA reflects an implicit and habitual form of evaluation that, while poten- tially useful, lacks transparency and rigor. It suggests a developmental opportunity: to help students articulate and externalize the criteria they already apply informally.
In sum, this typology captures a continuum of self-assessment enactment, from stra- tegic and standards-aligned (informed SISA) to rushed (hasty SISA) and intuition-based (intuitive SISA). The typology clari es not only what students do during SISA, but how and why they differ — pointing to differentiated pedagogical strategies for supporting metacognitive growth.
Disciplinary differences in SISA
Patterns of SISA engagement varied signi cantly across disciplinary contexts, reflecting the influence of epistemological orientations and professional norms on self-assessment behav- ior. As shown in Table 2, analysis of category frequencies revealed two major disciplinary differences, particularly between students in professional programs (Nursing and Teach- ing)  and  those  in  the  Arts.  First,  clear  contrasts  emerged  in  the  category  of  criteria seeking. Students in Nursing and Teaching were considerably more likely to reference external professional standards — such as competency frameworks, curriculum outcomes, or clinical guidelines — when evaluating their e-portfolio work. In contrast, Arts students relied almost exclusively on self-de ned standards, drawing on personal aesthetic judgment or tacit notions of quality. This difference was statistically signi cant (χ2 (4) = 24.10, p  < .001), highlighting the role of disciplinary expectations in shaping how evaluative criteria are sourced and applied. The professional disciplines encourage alignment with institutio- nalized norms of performance, whereas creative disciplines foreground subjective coher- ence and originality, often at the expense of articulated benchmarks.
Second, the distribution of SISA types also differed markedly across disciplines (χ2 (4)  
= 23.39, p < .001).  Arts  students  were  predominantly  classi ed  under  Intuitive  SISA, characterized by intuitive and self-referential evaluation. Their self-assessment processes emphasized internal alignment and expressive clarity but showed limited engagement with feedback or calibration. In contrast, students in Nursing and Teaching more fre - quently  demonstrated Informed SISA,  involving structured, criteria-based evaluation and active participation in feedback loops. Very few students in the professional pro- grams engaged in Hasty or Intuitive SISA.
These  ndings suggest that disciplinary cultures and epistemologies play a critical role in mediating self-assessment practices. Professional programs foster a culture of accountability and alignment with shared standards, making informed SISA a more accessible and expected norm. By contrast, creative disciplines valorize autonomy, affect, and individual voice, which may predispose students toward intuitive forms of SISA. This highlights the importance of contextualizing pedagogical support: while students in all disciplines bene t from clearer articulation of criteria and feedback engagement, the mode and framing of such support must resonate with the underlying values and epistemic assumptions of each  eld.
Discussion
This study introduces and theorizes self-initiated self-assessment (SISA) as a student- driven evaluative process that is conceptually and functionally distinct from teacher- directed or externally prompted self-assessment. While prior research typically presents self-assessment as a response to instructional cues (e.g., Yan & Brown, 2017), SISA is initiated  independently  by  students,  without  immediate  external  requirements.  This reconceptualization  aligns  more  closely  with  self-regulated  learning  (SRL)  theories, which highlight learner autonomy in goal setting, monitoring, and revision (Panadero et  al.,  2017).  It  shifts  the  analytical  lens  from  teacher-led  instructional  design  to student  agency,  thereby  extending  existing  models  of  self-assessment  and  reframing how evaluative behavior is theorized in higher education.
One key insight from the study is the articulation of a seven-step process that charac- terizes informed SISA. This sequence captures how students initiate, sustain, and reflect on their self-evaluative efforts — from early intention-setting and engagement with assess- ment criteria, to calibration, feedback use, and revision. The model highlights the recursive, metacognitive nature of informed SISA, distinguishing it from more reactive or super cial forms of self-assessment documented in prior work (e.g., Sargeant et al., 2010).
Building on this processual model, the study offers a typology of three distinct forms of SISA: informed, intuitive, and hasty. These forms vary not only in completeness but also in coherence and evaluative depth. Informed SISA represents the most deliberate and  strategic  engagement,  involving  purposeful  criteria  application  and  active  inte- gration of feedback. Intuitive SISA, by contrast, is guided by internal judgment and affective cues but lacks explicit reference to external benchmarks. Although less struc- tured, it reflects a form of tacit expertise that is particularly valued in artistic disciplines. Hasty SISA, meanwhile, involves minimal evaluative engagement, often arising from time pressure, low motivation, or unclear expectations.
The typology underscores that self-assessment practices are neither uniform nor consist- ently strategic. Our  ndings provide strong evidence that they are profoundly shaped by disciplinary culture, which socializes students into particular evaluative logics — some favor- ing compliance with external standards, others privileging personal coherence and creative risk (Becher & Trowler, 2001). While our data contain hints of the influence of individual student experience (e.g., references to self-assessment as a  learned habit ) and task con- straints  (e.g.,  time  pressure),  the  most  salient  and  clearly  substantiated  factor  emerging from this study is the overarching disciplinary context. Notably, the disciplinary analysis revealed distinct patterns: professional programs such as Nursing and Teaching emphasized alignment with formalized standards and accreditation outcomes, often supporting informed SISA through structured feedback cycles and criteria-rich environments. In contrast, Art stu- dents relied more on internal standards and personal vision, fostering intuitive SISA within contexts that value originality and expression over conformity to prescriptive rubrics.
Contribution and implication
This study establishes Student-Initiated Self-Assessment (SISA) as a critical, yet under- examined, component of the student experience in higher education. It demonstrates how the informal evaluative practices students engage in daily — distinct from mandated curricular tasks — fundamentally shape their development as agentic learners and their readiness for professional and creative practice. By shifting the analytical focus from instructor-led design to student agency, the study reframes self-assessment theory to account for autonomous evaluative behavior.
The theoretical contribution is twofold. First, the seven-step SISA model provides a struc- tured framework for understanding how informed self-assessment operates as an iterative, metacognitive cycle when student-driven. Second, the three-part typology (Informed, Intui- tive, Hasty) advances beyond binary conceptions of self-assessment by explaining variation in evaluative engagement — capturing differences in deliberateness, criteria use, and feed- back integration. This typology enables educators to diagnose not merely the presence of self-assessment, but its qualitative nature and developmental potential.
The practical implications are signi cant and layered. For instructional design, the informed  SISA  model  offers  actionable  levers  for  intervention:  fostering  intention- setting, scaffolding criteria-seeking, and structuring feedback dialogue. For curriculum development, the  ndings argue for a developmental trajectory: early training in struc - tured,  formal  self-assessment  can  build  essential  skills  that  later  empower  effective SISA.  Crucially,  this  support  must  be  contextualized;  pedagogical  strategies  should align with disciplinary epistemologies — validating intuitive judgment in creative  elds while  reinforcing  criteria-based  alignment  in  professional  programs.  Ultimately,  this research advocates for a reconceptualization of assessment literacy. By recognizing and fostering  SISA,  educators  can  move  beyond  compliance-oriented  tasks  and  cultivate the evaluative agency that underpins lifelong, self-regulated learning.
Limitations and future directions
This study has several limitations. First, it draws on a non-random volunteer sample from three disciplines. Participants may have been more reflective or motivated than the general student population, limiting the generalizability of the  ndings. Second, while e-portfolios provided a consistent context, we did not control for differences in assessment typologies (e.g., iterative vs. summative). The developmental nature of arts portfolios may inherently foster more SISA opportunities than one-off tasks in other  elds, and our design could not separate assessment type from disciplinary culture. Third, although the study examined auth- entic e-portfolio work and interviews, the digital platforms used varied by discipline. Differ- ences in platform design, usability, and institutional emphasis may have influenced how students engaged in self-assessment. Fourth, while the SISA typology outlines three distinct forms, students may shift between types within a single task or across time, depending on workload, feedback availability, or con dence levels. These transitions remain underexplored.
Future research should disentangle assessment design from disciplinary culture by comparing SISA within the same discipline across different assessment types (e.g., port- folio vs. exam) or similar assessments across disciplines. Also, future research should examine the mechanisms underlying intuitive SISA. Unlike informed SISA, which is traceable through observable practices, intuitive SISA relies on tacit, internal criteria. Further studies could investigate how such internal standards develop, how they relate to  disciplinary  norms,  and  how  they  influence  learning  outcomes.  Additionally,  the role of technology deserves closer attention. Digital tools may scaffold or inhibit SISA in  discipline-speci c  ways.  Comparative  research  across  platforms  could  inform  the design of systems that support both structured and intuitive self-assessment. Finally, future studies should explore whether SISA is transferable across contexts. Can students who develop informed SISA in one discipline carry these habits into others? How do these  evaluative  strategies  evolve  in  professional  or  creative  practice?  Longitudinal research could illuminate SISA as a developmental and lifelong learning skill.
Conclusion
This study conceptualizes self-initiated self-assessment (SISA) as a critical, yet under-the- orized, component of self-regulated learning in higher education. By distinguishing SISA from  externally  prompted  forms,  the  research  reframes  self-assessment  as  a  deeply autonomous process that takes different forms depending on student motivation, evalua - tive  strategy,  and  disciplinary  context.  The  seven-step  model  provides  a  structured framework for understanding informed SISA, while the typology and disciplinary analy- sis show that self-assessment is a complex, situated practice shaped by cultural, epistemic, and curricular factors. Rather than advocating a singular best practice, this study calls for a flexible, contextualized approach to fostering evaluative capacity. Educators should support students in developing adaptive repertoires that combine structured, criteria- based engagement with intuitive, experience-based reflection.
