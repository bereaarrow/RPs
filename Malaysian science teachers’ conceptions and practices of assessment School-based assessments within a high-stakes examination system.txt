Malaysian science teachers  conceptions  and practices of assessment:  school-based  assessments  within a high-stakes examination system
Dayana Sirri a, Amanda Harper b  and Gavin T. L. Brown a
aLearning, Development and Professional Practice, Faculty of Arts and Education, The University of Auckland, Auckland, New Zealand; bSchool of Biological Sciences, Faculty of Science, The University of Auckland, Auckland, New Zealand

ABSTRACT
After a prolonged  reliance  on external  exams, the  Malaysian gov- ernment  eliminated  two  standardised  exams,  favouring  school- based,  teacher-made  assessments.  A survey  was  conducted  to determine  if their beliefs and practices  of assessment  aligned  with this responsibility. Four primary conceptions  and  five practice  fac- tors were identified  using the  Teacher Conceptions  of Assessment (TCoA-III) and  Practices  of Assessment  (PrAI) inventories.  Analysis was limited to 142 participants with complete, paired datasets from 236 teachers  who  provided  some  data,  as  this  requirement  pre- cluded  imputation.  Structural equation  modelling  found  six statis- tically significant paths  from TCoA-III to PrAI factors: improvement conception  robustly  predicted  diagnostic  and  improvement  prac- tices; the  student  accountability  predicted  exam  preparation  and ignoring  assessment  practices;  the  irrelevance  conception  pre- dicted  ignoring  assessment  practices;  and  school  accountability predicted  equivalence  practices.  These results had  strong  similari- ties to Chinese teachers  who work in a public exam-oriented  sys- tem, suggesting  that  Malaysian teacher-made  assessments  are not strongly formative.

Background
Assigning a summative  evaluation grade to  students  (e.g. A to  F; Excellent to  Not Achieved, etc.) requires a complex judgement about the quality of student work which is often influenced by non-academic factors, such as attitude, attendance, or perceived effort (Brookhart et al., 2016). Such approaches create potentially invalid achievement reports that fail to communicate appropriately to parents or administrators (Friedman & Frisbie, 1995; Waltman & Frisbie, 1994). Evidence exists that teacher overall grades or judgements reflect internal biases relative to tested performance about the competence of student demographic groups (Meissel et al., 2017). Nonetheless, in some jurisdictions, it is possible that teacher grading constitutes a good proxy for eternal exams and generates more  sophisticated  insights about  learning. For example, in  Norway, the  correlation between teacher-assigned grades and  external exam results was moderate  to  strong, indicating  that  teachers  understanding  of their  students   learning  capabilities made school assessment grades trustworthy (Arencibia Alem n  et al., 2024).
The Malaysia qualification system has recently removed standardised exams at the end of primary (first 6 years) and lower secondary levels (schooling years 7 to 9) in order to strengthen school-based assessments and raise overall achievement (BERNAMA, 2024). However, when teachers are required to create substitutes for formal testing, they often lack the experience and knowledge to create highly valid and reliable tasks. So, unless teachers and  schools can guarantee quality instruction  and assessment, teacher made assessments can  be problematic  for individual  students  and  families, teachers and schools, and  for society as a whole. One  way to explore the  concerns  teachers have about this reform pressure is to examine how teachers conceive of the purpose and use of assessment (i.e. their conceptions) and how they claim to practice assessment.
The Malaysian assessment  context
The Malaysian education system has historically prioritised examinations (Heng et al.,  2015; Idris, 2016; Ong, 2010), with standardised  exams being conducted at the end of every schooling level: primary level (Standard 1 to 6), lower secondary level (Form 1 to 3), and  upper  secondary level (Form  4 and  5). Teacher and  school quality are generally judged based on examination  results, assuming that teacher success is defined by the number  and  proportion  of students  who pass these exams (Mohamed  et al., 2011). However, the Ministry of Education (MOE) has introduced assessment reforms because of the increasing spread of student achievement scores on national exams (MOE, 2013). This has led to the introduction  of a standards-based  curriculum  and  an assessment system that evaluates students against performance standards that describe hierarchical mastery levels (MOE, 2016).
The  implementation  of  assessments  aligned  to  a  standards-based  curriculum enables  teachers  to  acquire  valuable insights  into  the  capabilities  and  needs  of their students (Brown, 2018). Teachers are encouraged to use multiple and diverse assessment methods, both informal (e.g. peer and self-assessment) and formal (e.g. tests  or  quizzes),  to  obtain  detailed  information  about  student  learning  (MOE,  2017).  Teachers  are  expected  to  implement  low-stakes  formative  assessments throughout  teaching  and  learning  process,  as  well as  administering  summative assessments  after  lesson  units,  months,  or  academic  years. Ideally, these  assess- ments  complement  each  other,  generating  information  to  diagnose  and  address student  learning  needs  and  to  reliably  evaluate  the  overall  quality  of  student achievement.
Nonetheless, teacher-generated  assessment information  within  schools transcends external purposes like qualifications or certification, because it is a primary resource by which teachers make informed classroom instructional decisions (Hosp, 2011). However, the Malaysian reform expects teachers to act as assessors for qualifications while simul- taneously designing and administering  formative assessments to support  the develop- ment  of student  learning. Therefore, Malaysian teachers need  to  be able to  design, administer,  score, and report  on all kinds of assessments during  the school year and generate robust, valid data to contribute to official qualifications.
When the Malaysian government announced the abolition of standardised exams for the  primary  and  lower secondary levels, parents  were worried  about  their  children s futures (Muhammad  & Alias, 2023). Such a reaction was expected from parents, pri- marily because they had  experienced a schooling system that  accentuated  success in public exams. Hence, parents  tended  to judge their  children s success based on their examination  performance  and used these results to form opinions  about the school s quality (Harris & Brown, 2016). Parental perspectives may pressure teachers to prioritis- ing beliefs and practices focused on preparing students for examinations. However, given that external examinations no longer exist at the level of schooling in this study, it is expected that  teachers will demonstrate  some tension  around  how assessments are perceived.
Teacher conceptions  of assessment
Teacher attitudes towards assessment are profoundly influenced by their experiences of assessment as school and university students prior to their becoming teachers (Pajares,  1992). Both personal and professional experiences typically shape teachers  core beliefs about education. These beliefs impact teachers  instructional decisions, classroom activ- ities and practices, and may vary depending on external factors like educational policies, student development, and teacher characteristics (Bonner, 2016).
Interest  in teachers  conceptions  or beliefs about assessment is widespread. Brown (2006, 2008) developed the  Teachers Conceptions of Assessment (TCoA-III),  which identified four inter-correlated  assessment conceptions: improvement, school account- ability, student accountability, and irrelevance. The Improvement  conception refers to using assessment to inform teaching and practices and enhance student  learning. The School accountability conception  involves evaluating the  quality of the  schools and teachers. The Student accountability factor pertains to grading, evaluating, and assessing student performance. Lastly, the Irrelevance conception refers to viewing assessments as irrelevant, inaccurate, and bad for students.
This instrument  is applicable for cross-cultural studies after localised adaptations, as teachers  conceptions are likely to change depending on the local context and educational policies (Brown et al., 2019). As Brown and Harris (2009) pointed out, teacher concep- tions and practices are likely to be highly coherent  with the policies implemented  by educational authorities, as well as cultural norms. A key lever on teacher conceptions seems to be the tension between summative, accountability-oriented policy frameworks in which teachers are employed versus the formative, improvement-oriented  beliefs most teachers have as to their role in education (Bonner, 2016).
Low-stakes assessment jurisdictions
Low-stakes assessment jurisdictions  emphasise the  use of assessments, by teachers themselves, to  guide instruction,  monitor  student  progress, and  report  on  learning achievement to students,  parents, and administrators.  This is coherent  with Scriven s (1967) formative evaluation framework in which teachers gain information, so they can improve how they teach and how and what students learn before the end of a course, unit, or year (Bloom et al., 1981). Teachers working in a low-stakes examination system, like New Zealand, generally viewed assessments as tools for improving  teaching and learning (Brown, 2004). The concepts of improvement  and  school accountability are positively correlated,  apparently  because schools and  teachers fulfill accountability expectations by teaching in such a way that students have improved learning outcomes. In contrast, the conceptions of improvement  and irrelevance were strongly and nega- tively correlated, indicating that assessment matters to improving teaching and learning. A sample of Cypriot teachers endorsed most strongly assessment for student learning and teacher improved teaching (i.e. Improvement)  and rejected the Irrelevance conceptions (Brown & Michaelides, 2011). Queensland  primary  school teachers had  very similar views to those of New Zealand primary  teachers (Brown & Lake, 2006). Conversely, secondary teachers in  both  contexts  (Brown, 2011; Brown, Lake, et al., 2011) had a stronger endorsement of the student accountability factor because teacher assessment was part of the school qualifications system.
High-stakes assessment jurisdictions
In contrast, high-stakes assessment systems rely heavily on end-or-course, end-of-year, and end-of-schooling public examinations to award not just qualifications but also to rank  candidates  for competitive opportunities  (e.g. scholarships, admission  to  elite programmes).  This aligns with Scriven (1967) notion  of summative evaluation, which refers to the assignment of merit, worth, or value at the end of a process. Typically, administered  by external agencies (e.g. the  Malaysian Examination  Syndicate), these examinations carry significant consequences and thus shape teaching priorities, student motivation, and even curriculum design.
Teachers  conceptions of assessment are often shaped by the broader  sociopolitical and educational contexts in which they work, particularly in high-stakes or examination- driven system. Jordanian science teachers perceived assessment as  useless , similar to the conception  that  assessments are irrelevance (Mahasneh  & Al-Zou bi, 2021, p. 395). Iranian mathematics teachers believed that assessments made both students and schools accountable because the results could significantly affect the reputations of teachers and schools (Mirian  & Zulnaidi, 2020). Although  Egyptian teachers believed assessments were for improvement, results were still influential concerning school reputations (Gebril 
& Brown, 2014). In both  Hong  Kong and  China, teachers agreed with improvement conceptions but these were strongly correlated with student accountability conceptions, suggesting that to help students improve as individuals, they need to be assessed (Brown 
& Gao, 2015; Brown et al., 2009; Brown, Hui, et al., 2011).
Malaysia itself, as bequeathed by the British colonial system, has a long tradition  of regular high-stakes examinations used for promotion  and selection, as well as frequent within school high-stakes tests (Ong, 2010). More recently, the Malaysian system has tried to emphasise formative, school-based assessment for improved teaching and learn- ing, something most Malaysian teachers have embraced (Mat Yusoff et al., 2024; Mat Yusoff, Abdul Razak, et al., 2023). Nonetheless, tensions remain among Malaysia teachers because assessments may not fairly represent students  and teachers  actual capabilities (Mat Yusoff et al., 2024). Overall, in highly selective systems that  use examinations, teachers  conceptions of assessment seem to be driven primarily by the need to sustain their  credibility and  that  of their  schools by holding  students  accountable  for their performance.
Teacher practices of assessment
The practice of assessment in schools reflects a tension between what teachers might believe in (e.g. formative uses) versus what administrative policies might require (e.g. summative ranking; Bonner, 2016). Change in policy can be difficult to implement since teachers can either  implement  or  ignore  the  reform  (Walland  & Darlington,  2021). Despite endorsing the importance of formative assessment, Singaporean teachers indi- cated that  they lacked skills and  knowledge to implement  such assessments (Deneen et al., 2019). Likewise, Indonesian teachers recognised the importance of assessments in enhancing  student  learning  but  tended  to  prepare  students  for exams (Azis, 2015). Hence, the challenges Malaysian teachers face may not be unique.
Despite the freedom to conduct appropriate student assessments, Malaysian teachers seem to prefer traditional  testing methods  (Idris, 2016). Instead of describing student performance levels and providing constructive feedback, teachers tended to grade and give marks to student work during the classroom teaching and learning processes (Mat Ali & Jamaluddin, 2007). Despite the policy reform, Malaysian teachers tended to teach students  for upcoming  exams and  conducted  little student-centred  activities (Jing & Saleh, 2020).
Teachers  preference for conducting testing as the primary assessment method could be attributed to the significant consequences of high-stakes examinations. For instance, the results from the Form Three Assessment (PT3), administered  to students aged 15  years old,  are  used  for  streaming  and  screening  students  for  admission  to  high- performing  schools (Noh et al., 2020). Naturally, teachers focus more on the content that  will appear  in  the  external exams. Science teachers tended  to  emphasise exam- focused scientific content  while neglecting less frequently assessed topics (Sembak & Abdullah, 2017). Hence, teachers continue  with the practices focusing exclusively on exams, even when the new policy is enforcing school assessments.
Since the  increased  role  for  school-based  assessments, Malaysian teachers  have struggled to adjust their conventional practices to meet the new policy s requirements. When  Malaysian teachers were asked about  their  understanding  of assessments for learning, they claimed that assessments were mainly for summative purposes, such as student testing and grading (Arumugham et al., 2017). When Malaysian teachers were asked about  their  concerns  for  implementing  school-based  assessment, one  of the major  concerns  was the  difficulty in  developing items for tests, as well as scoring, analysing, and  reporting  results  from  their  own  tests  (Ab  Khalid  et  al., 2015). Examining these concerns  closely, Malaysian teachers predominantly  viewed assess- ments  as  purely  summative,  reflecting  the  belief  that  assessments  are  for accountability.
Objectives of the study
The  literature  reveals a scarcity of studies exploring Malaysian teaching  practices, particularly in assessment (Meng & Samuel, 2017). This study explores the relationship between teachers  conceptions and their self-reported practices of assessment with two instruments previously used in Hong Kong. The instruments were Teacher Conceptions of Assessment (TCoA-III) (Brown, 2006) and Practices of Assessment Inventory (PrAI) (Brown et al., 2009). Therefore, the main objectives were:
(1) To evaluate the validity of the TCoA-III and PrAI for use with Malaysian science teachers
(2) To investigate the  relationship  between teachers  conceptions  and  their  self- reported practices in the Malaysian context
Method
Study design
The study used an online survey among volunteers who were members of PT3 science teacher groups on Telegram. The collected data were analysed using Amos version 20, employing confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) to validate the measurement models and structural equation modelling (SEM) to examine the relationship between the constructs.
Participants
The survey experienced substantial  non-completion  attrition,  which is common  in lengthy  online  instruments  (Couper  et  al.,  2007).  Of  the  236  participants  who completed  at  least half of the  items,  142 (60.2%) completed  both  instruments  in full. This  pattern  is consistent  with  established evidence that  longer  online  ques- tionnaires  impose  a  substantial  respondent  burden,  which  in  turn  predicts  lower completion rates (Fan & Yan, 2010). Moreover, preliminary response-pattern  checks indicated  that  attrition  was not  entirely random.  Participants  with  more  than  10  years of teaching experience appeared  more  likely to  discontinue  the  survey mid- way, aligning  with  existing  literature  showing  that  older  or  more  experienced teachers tend  to respond  at lower rates or disengage earlier in web-based or time- intensive  surveys  (Shih  &  Xitao,  2008).  This  suggests that  non-completion  may reflect differential  burden  rather  than  flaw in  instrument  design,  particularly  for respondents  with  heavier  workloads  or  lower  sustained  engagement  with  long online formats.
Most participants  were female, and  over 85% were older  than  30 years old. Most participants  had  a bachelor s degree (73.9%), and  the rest had  a teaching certificate, masters, or PhD (26.1%). About three-quarters  of the participants  had more than  10 years of teaching experience, with most participants teaching mainly Science at school (62.7%), with the balance teaching science as a minor subject. Half of the participants had attended at least two higher-order  thinking skills and item writing training sessions as part of their professional development (Table 1).

Teacher conceptions of assessment inventory (TCoA-III)
The TCoA-III is an abridged version of the Teacher Conceptions of Assessment inventory, consisting of 27 items representing nine first-order factors and two second-order factors (Brown, 2006). The inventory was designed to capture teachers  preferences for the main purposes of assessment: (1) Improvement, (2) School accountability, (3) Student account- ability, and  (4) Irrelevance. These four  main  factors are inter-correlated  and  both Improvement and  Irrelevance had  four  and  three  subordinate  first-order  factors, respectively.
This inventory has been applied in various cultural contexts, including New Zealand, Australia, Hong  Kong, Malaysia, Jordan, Egypt, Sweden, Iran,  and  other  nations.  Its adaptability to local contexts and educational policies makes it a robust instrument  for cross-cultural-context research (Brown et al., 2019). Given its successful application in Hong Kong, which shares similarities with Malaysia in terms of promoting school-based assessments to reduce the over-reliance on examinations  (Brown et al., 2009; Samuel et al., 2017), this abridged version was deemed suitable for this study. Previous studies have also validated the use of TCoA-III in the Malaysian context (Mat Yusoff et al., 2024; Mat Yusoff, Abdul Razak, et al., 2023; Mat Yusoff, Chin, et al., 2023; Mohamad Mustafa 
& Abdul Manaf, 2019).
Practices of assessment inventory (PrAI)
This inventory consists of 33 self-reported items related to five aspects of how assess- ments are used in practice. The five factors are subordinate to an overall factor capturing the commonality of assessment practices (Brown et al., 2009).
(1) School accountability refers to using assessments to prove the quality of teachers and schools.
(2) Diagnosis refers to the practice of using assessments to identify student learning needs.
(3) Improvement describes teachers  practices of using assessments to improve stu- dent learning and performance.
(4) Examination construct focuses on the practices of preparing students for exams.
(5) Irrelevance generally refers to the practices that disregard the assessment process and information.
This inventory had hierarchical structure in which a second-order factor of Practices of Assessment regressed onto  five first-order  factors with just 23 items  and  with an acceptable fit (Brown et al., 2009). Because this  inventory  has not  been studied  in Malaysia, this study sought to explore whether teachers  self-reported assessment prac- tices aligned with their conceptions of assessment, thus extending its use in the Malaysian educational context.
Analysis
Survey responses were prepared for analysis by first identifying cases with more than 10% skipped or  Uncertain  answers. The primary analyses were conducted using data from  the 142 participants  who provided  complete responses to both  inventories to ensure the credibility of individual responses (Little & Rubin, 2020). This decision was motivated by the pattern of missingness observed in the data. Approximately 40% of respondents (n = 94) answered only one of the two instruments, resulting in unit-level nonresponse for one of the core constructs rather than sporadic item-level missingness. Although modern estimation method such as Full Information  Maximum Likelihood (FIML) are widely regarded as appropriate  for handling substantial item-level miss- ingness, their application is less straightforward when entire scales are missing (Xiao & Bulut, 2020). In  the  present  study, imputing  or  model-based  estimating  values for a fully missing instrument  would rely heavily on statistical assumptions  rather  than respondents  expressed beliefs or  practices, thereby  undermining  the  psychological validity of the constructs. Because the aim of the study is to examine the relationship between teachers  assessment conceptions and their self-reported practices, complete paired data across both constructs are essential for further analysis. A complete-case approach  was therefore  adopted  as a principled  analytic decision aligned with the study s substantive focus. This choice necessarily reduced  the sample size and  may limit generalisability. However, it ensured that all estimated relationships were based on observed responses to both instruments rather than on model-imputed values for an entire construct.
For model development, CFA using maximum  likelihood in Amos version 20 was conducted  to  test Malaysian responses against the  pre-existing measurement  models from New Zealand for TCoA-III and Hong Kong for PrAI. Adjustments to the models were implemented following the well-established criteria in model development: (1) the factor loading of the item must be  .30 and each factor should have at least three items (Bandalos & Finney, 2010), (2) an item must exhibit simple structure  by belonging to only one  factor (Revelle & Rocklin, 1979), and  (3) the  item  should  not  violate the assumption  of independent  error  residuals assumption  (Barker & Shaw, 2015). An iterative process of factor refinement was conducted to enhance construct validity and parsimony of the measurement models. Items with modification indices (MI) exceeding 10 were flagged for evaluation (Raykov & Marcoulides, 2007), though their exclusion was ultimately determined through a theoretical evaluation of each item s contribution to the construct  and  its appropriateness  for the  Malaysian context.  For example, COA08 ( Teachers conduct assessment but make little use of the results ) was flagged for having high MI with multiple items, suggesting local misfit and indicating the item may cross- load onto  unintended  factors. This statistical issue points  to theoretical ambiguity or redundancy in its interpretation.  Furthermore,  COA08 demonstrated  significant covar- iance residuals with other items, suggesting that the item is likely to be repetitive in terms of semantic value (Cattell & Tsujioka, 1964). The negatively worded phrasing is a known methodological concern that may have introduced response bias or confusion. Thus, the item was removed to enhance model fit, improve construct clarity, and ensure alignment with the Malaysian context. Nonetheless, careful consideration was given before remov- ing items from the model to ensure conceptual validity across the model.
To evaluate whether the high item attrition  influenced the stability of the measure- ment and structural models, a sensitivity was conducted using the Akaike Information Criterion  (AIC). AIC estimates the relative information  loss of a model by penalising unnecessary complexity, with lower values indicating a better balance between goodness of fit and parsimony (Akaike, 1974; Burnham & Anderson, 2002). In this context, AIC was used to evaluate whether the removal of misfitting items in the simplified models provided a more coherent  representation  of the constructs than  the full-item models. Since AIC penalises overfitting and  is suitable for predictive modelling applications (Cavanaugh & Neath, 2019), it was used to determine the preferred model. The analysis compared the AIC values of full models (TCoA-III: 27 items; PrAI: 33 items) against the simplified models (TCoA-III: 21 items; PrAI: 20 items) across two CFA models and one SEM model linking TCoA-III and PrAI. The simplified versions demonstrated  superior fit and parsimony for this dataset compared to the full models (i.e. wi = 1.00). Thus, the simplified versions were used to best represent the true covariance relationship for this sample (Supplementary File Table 2).
To assess the model fit, a combination of indices that are less affected by sample size, model  complexity, and  model  specification were employed (Fan  & Sivo, 2007). An acceptable fit was inferred  when gamma  hat > .90, comparative  fit index (CFI) > .90 (Fan et al., 1999), while the root mean square error  of approximation  (RMSEA) < .08 (Maccallum et al., 1996), and  standardised  root  mean  residual (SRMR) < .08 (Hu  & Bentler, 1999). Because the chi-squared value for the model fit is overly sensitive with large degrees of freedom (Wheaton  et al., 1977), a p-value > .05 for the normed  chi- squared  value (i.e. x2/df)  was used  to  identify  models  with  acceptable model  fit. Unfortunately, the CFI reduces as models become more complex (i.e. more than three factors), whereas the RMSEA decreases with model simplicity (i.e. less than three factors) (Fan & Sivo, 2007). Hence, greater weight is put on the normed chi-square p-value, the SRMR, and gamma hat indices. Construct reliability for the CFA models was evaluated using coefficient H, a more  robust  and  consistent  measure  than  Cronbach s  alpha, particularly  in  CFA, as it is unaffected by the  sign or  direction  of factor loadings (Hancock & Mueller, 2001).
Structural equation modelling, using the logic of path analysis (Shipley, 2016), treated conceptions of assessment as predictors of practices of assessment. Hence, each concep- tion  of assessment was regressed onto  its matching  practice. Additional  paths  were introduced  and retained, provided they were statistically significant. Model fit used the same standards as for CFA.
Results
Model development
Teacher conceptions of assessment (TCoA-III) in the context of Malaysian science teachers
Given the small sample size and overall dataset characteristics, the New Zealand TCoA- III model was refined to ensure alignment with theoretical expectations and statistical assumptions. Modifications to address issues related to low loadings, violations of simple structure or independence of error residuals assumptions were made to establish the best model  that  fit the  collected responses (Appendix  A). All sub-factors  were removed resulting in a correlated four-factor structure of the validated TCoA-III instrument.  In this streamlined model, all items are loaded directly onto their respective primary factors without subordinate dimensions. Previous research had validated this model structure, showing that it remains theoretically grounded  and psychometrically sound (Brown & Michaelides, 2011; Fernandes et al., 2024).

The removal of sub-factors enhanced conceptual clarity and parsimony without compromising psychometric soundness.
The Irrelevance factor had negative correlations with all the other factors (i.e. Improvement r = −.66; Student accountability r = −.53; and School accountability r = −.34). These negative associations suggest that teachers who reject the value of assessment tend to disengage from both its pedagogical and accountability functions. In contrast, Improvement  was strongly correlated with both  Student  accountability (r = .86) and  School accountability (r = .72), suggesting that teachers who view assessment as a means to enhance learning also perceive it as a mechanism for promoting student responsibility and evaluating school performance. The strong correlation between Student accountability and School accountability (r = .77) further  supports  this integrated  perspective, suggesting that  teachers associate individual student effort with the broader reputation and success of the school.
Together, these patterns reveal a coherent belief system in which assessment is seen as serving both instructional and institutional goals. In high-stakes educational settings like Malaysia, where test results carry significant consequences for students and schools, such alignment reflects the complex and multifaceted role of assessment as teachers strive to balance its function as a tool for learning and a mechanism of accountability.

Discussion
This  survey study  replicated  a  Hong  Kong study  of how  teacher  conceptions  of assessment related  to  their  practices of assessment. To  achieve acceptable model fit and  construct  clarity, some items were removed based on  combined  statistical and  substantive  considerations.  These items  largely overlapped  semantically with retained  indicators  or showed ambiguous alignment  with the intended  constructs, suggesting  redundancy  rather  than  substantive  loss. The  final  models  therefore prioritise  the  interpretability  of  the  latent  constructs  while  acknowledging  that some  nuances  in  item  wording  may  not  be  fully  represented.  Data  from a relatively small sample of Malaysian science teachers conformed, with acceptable fit, the factor structures  and  structural  relations reported  among  Hong  Kong and Chinese teachers (Brown et al., 2009, 2011). Like Malaysia, assessment in China is embedded  within  high-stakes qualification examinations  used to select candidates for  opportunities.  This  alignment  suggests a broader  regional  assessment culture characterised  by  accountability,  competition,  and  standardisation.  The  resem- blance can  potentially be traced  to  a shared  historical  background,  because both Malaysia and  Hong  Kong  inherited  English  models  of  schooling  and  qualifica- tions, which were in turn  influenced by the Chinese imperial examination  system (Kim  &  Massey, 2025).  This  long-standing  tradition  of  merit-based  selection continues  to  influence  Malaysian education  by  reinforcing  the  belief that  exam results reflect both  student  achievement and  school quality.
Tensions between  improvement and accountability
The  structural  model  revealed a  significant interconnection  between logically con- nected conceptions of assessment and practices of assessment. In terms of practices, the  teacher  strongly  focused  on  improvement  and  diagnostic  purposes  that  were strongly  predicted  by  endorsement  of  assessment  for  improvement.  However,  the diagnostic  and  improvement  response  to  assessment  seems  to  be  undermined  by teachers reporting  that  they did not  use assessments to modify their  teaching prac- tices. Six items  in  the  Irrelevant  practices  factor  suggest that  teachers  disregard assessments  either  because  they  view them  as  imprecise,  unfair,  and  overlooked. This is less about rejecting assessment and more about surrendering  to the pressure to progress, even if it means ignoring diagnostic information.  Making use of assess- ment  information  requires  the  ability to  pause instruction  and  revisit instructional practices  in  order  to  attend  to  students   learning  needs.  Given the  relatively large classes (i.e. 35 to 45 students), it is understandably  challenging for teachers to assess all students  diagnostically and  implement  appropriate  instruction  (Muhammad  & Alias, 2023). Nevertheless, inability to adjust teaching based on diagnosis of student learning needs or challenges means that the formative function  of assessment is not fully accomplished.
Further, assessing students for accountability was strongly correlated with the view that  assessment improves teaching and  learning (Figure 1). Simultaneously, assessing students is also robustly correlated with a way to show that schools are high quality. Good schools are those that assess students to get good marks and the use of those assessments helps students improve. This study affirms the persistent emphasis on exam preparation in Malaysian education  (Idris, 2016), especially since only the conception  of student accountability significantly predicted  the practice of preparing  students  for examina- tions. This suggests that teachers  efforts to prepare students for exams are shaped by the belief that students  must be graded and assessed in accordance with the qualification system. The existence of these high-stakes exams has profoundly shaped school assess- ment  practices, which is geared towards exam preparation  (Jing & Saleh, 2020; Ong,  2010). Indeed,  this belief may arise from  the  practical conditions  of employment  in Malaysian schools.
The strong public endorsement of assessment as a legitimate and expected practice in education continues to reinforce its use for school accountability (Buckendahl, 2016). Despite extensive research highlighting the adverse effects of high-stakes testing (Nichols 
& Harris, 2016), there remains widespread public acceptance of the consequences tied to formal education  (e.g. university admission,  job opportunities,  and  long-term  career paths) arising from formal qualification systems. This enduring support may arise from the simple and straightforward measure of school  quality , (i.e. proportion  of students passing each level of the exam) that resonates with both parents and politicians (Carnoy et al., 2003).
Together, these conflicting tensions  suggest a complex idea that  may characterise Malaysian teacher thinking. If assessment is about judging students, then it seems the information contained in such assessments is ignorable because (1) it is wrong, (2) it is about the student as opposed to the teacher, and (3) there is no time to respond in class teaching to diagnostic insights. The strong correlation between the student accountability conception  with the  improvement  conception  (Figure 1) has been attested  in  both Hong  Kong (Brown et al., 2009) and China (Brown, Hui, et al., 2011). It seems that believing in improvement  does not  mean  rejecting accountability, an indication  that teachers consider  outright opposition to accountability  as unjustified (Lingard & Lewis,  2016, p. 400).
The  current  results  are  quite  different  to  those  of teachers  in  jurisdictions  that prioritise low-stakes assessments. There teachers reject the notion that student results are a good way to evaluate schools and  teachers (Brown, 2004; Brown, Lake, et al.,  2011). Hence, a clear tension  exists, as Bonner (2016) highlights, between personal beliefs and institutional  pressures. The results of this study reveal a tension between improvement  and diagnostic beliefs and the use of assessment to judge students and evaluate schools. Although using student assessments to determine school quality may seem  straightforward,  the  experience  is often  more  complex  and  burdensome  for teachers.  Unfortunately,  the  introduction  of  school-controlled,  standards-based assessments has not yet changed how this sample of Malaysian teachers conceives of or practice assessment.
The ambition  of the Malaysian government  to introduce  greater teacher involve- ment in qualifications and formative assessment is clearly challenged by this complex set  of tensions  under  which  teachers  work.  Drawing  on  the  insights  identified  in Hong  Kong, it is highly likely that  as long as external examinations  dominate  the policy landscape of Malaysia, they will function as a  hard  policy that shapes educa- tional practices, despite the lofty but  soft  policy of formative assessment (Kennedy et  al.,  2011).  Efforts  to  import  western  ideas  of  assessment  for  learning  into Hong Kong s examination system have largely failed because of the continued empha- sis on  selective public  examinations  (Yan  & Brown, 2021).  Hence,  considerable challenges exist for Malaysia s qualification system as evidenced by the tensions report in their conceptions and practices of assessment.
Implications for teacher-made  assessments
This tension-filled belief and practice system of Malaysian science teachers suggests there will be problems in teachers making their own formative and summative assessments. The current low level of training and support for teachers to create their own valid and reliable assessments (Ismail et al., 2017; Khalil & Awang, 2016) has produced relatively negative perceptions of assessment. What training there is has been described as overly general (Hassan et al., 2017). A lack of time to create new tests and excessive teaching workloads already make teachers perceive assessment as something ignorable and unable to exploit in their classrooms (Mohd nor & Haron, 2023; Salleh et al., 2017). The results here suggest that when teachers must give tests that hold students accountable, they are likely to perceive such assessments as ignorable in so far as their own teaching goes. This would undermine  the formative expectation of the policy.
These implications that we have drawn from this study need to be taken cautiously. The small sample size limits generalisability about  the thinking  of Malaysian science teachers, let alone Malaysian teachers of all subjects. Future  research with larger and more  diverse samples across subjects and  educational  levels would provide  stronger evidence and broader applicability.
Conclusion
The strong relationships between the conceptions of improvement  and accountability of students and schools suggest that teachers view assessments as a means of student learning improvement  while also holding  them  accountable, with results impacting  the school s reputation rather than their own classroom practices. Even though school-based assessments have been implemented  for almost a decade, teachers thinking  about  assessment seem entirely coherent with the influence of the previous high-stakes, external examination system. Teachers hold  their  students  accountable, focusing on  preparing  them  for exams while struggling with the demand to implement changes in their teaching that respond to assess- ment results.
While the policy encourages the use of diverse assessment methods to provide more opportunities  for students  to showcase their potential, the external examination  system seems to overhang the profession. As has been pointed out in Hong Kong, assessment for learning, school-based assessments are a good thing, but the real power of the system lies in formal, external examinations (Kennedy et al., 2011). As employees of a system that does not seem to have invested much in reducing the stakes of assessment, improving teacher professional development in classroom assessment or developing a variety of high-quality assessments for school use, it is unlikely that teacher conceptions will change in the near future. Given that the Malaysia Ministry of Education is adamant about enforcing school- based assessments, this study offers important  insights into  teachers  conceptions  and practices emphasising the need for targeted professional development to support  better policy implementation.  Requiring teachers to become assessors without addressing tea- chers  knowledge and beliefs or their practice environments (Xu & Brown, 2016) is unlikely to produce meaningful or sustainable assessment reform.
