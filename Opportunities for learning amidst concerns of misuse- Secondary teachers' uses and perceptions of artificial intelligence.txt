Abstract
Artificial intelligence (AI) has a growing influence on planning, teaching and assessment practices in ed- ucation. In New Zealand, the Ministry of Education (2024)  has  acknowledged  AI's  expanding  role  in schools; nevertheless, limited data exist on teachers' practices and perspectives regarding its implemen- tation. To address this gap in the literature, the pre- sent study employed an online survey and thematic analysis to investigate how secondary school teach- ers in NZ (N = 99) used AI tools and to examine their perceptions of these tools in educational contexts. Quantitative analysis of the survey results revealed that teachers employed AI for various practical pur- poses, including planning (66.7%), teaching (37.4%) and assessment (34.3%). Most respondents agreed, however,  that  there  were  limited  policies  (63.6%) and a lack of knowledge (59.6%) relating to AI use in schools. Qualitative data from the survey suggested that teachers acknowledged the opportunities asso- ciated with AI use and recognised its potential for fos- tering student engagement; nevertheless, they raised a number of significant concerns, including students' misuse of AI tools, the potential erosion of students' critical thinking skills, the lack of professional devel- opment and clear guidelines for teachers and chal- lenges associated with assessment practices. Key insights
What is the main issue that the paper addresses?
This paper investigates the multiple ways New Zealand secondary school teachers use AI tools for teaching and learning. It also surveys teachers' perceptions of the opportunities associated with AI use, the role of AI in student engagement and the challenges that can arise when AI is integrated into educational environments.
What are the main insights that the paper provides?
The authors found that teachers used AI tools for planning, teaching and assess- ment. Although the study participants recognised the benefits of these tools, they also acknowledged several challenges pertaining to both teachers' and students' engagement with AI in classroom contexts.
INTRODUCTION
Artificial intelligence (AI) technology is set to bring profound changes to workforce struc- ture, the economy and the skills needed for future success (Marrone et al., 2024). The integration of AI into educational systems has prompted a significant transformation in teaching and learning, particularly in middle and high schools. AI technologies such as large language models (LLMs), chatbots, intelligent tutoring systems and adaptive learn- ing platforms are reshaping instructional planning, pedagogical practices, student en- gagement and assessment. For example, Singapore's education system has taken steps to introduce AI to students (Park et al., 2023). Similarly, the UK government has acknowl- edged that AI is here to stay and is already making a positive impact across various sec- tors, including education (Department for Education, 2025). Meanwhile, the European Commission has identified AI as an outstanding and strategic technology for the 21st century and predicts that many future jobs will require employees to have AI competency (Alonso, 2020).
However, the integration of AI tools into teaching and learning has been accompanied by implementation challenges as well as ethical and equity concerns (Barrett & Pack, 2023; Tyson & Sauers, 2021). In response, UNESCO (2021) has called for the ethical and re- sponsible use of AI tools in education to encourage critical thinking, promote design-based learning and equip learners with new digital skills.
In New Zealand (NZ), the Ministry of Education (2024) stated that AI should improve teaching and learning by supporting teachers' knowledge and skills; however, teachers must remain central in students' education and take responsibility for students' learning. This echoes Chiu's (2023) study, which emphasised the need for teachers to play a key role in supporting teaching and learning through the use of technology. But although the Ministry of Education (2024) acknowledges AI's growing role in schools, but limited data exist on NZ teachers' practices and perspectives regarding its implementation. To address this gap in the literature, the present study employed a mixed-method survey to investigate NZ second- ary school teachers' uses and perceptions of AI tools in teaching and learning environments. The survey design was developed following a literature review to identify current trends and debates regarding AI use in the classroom.
LITERATURE REVIEW
This literature review synthesises international research to identify some of the uses, ben- efits and challenges that educators may encounter when using AI tools in their teaching practices. The review also surveys some studies that have highlighted AI's capacity to foster student engagement.
Uses and benefits of AI tools for educators
Previous studies have identified that educators use AI for a range of tasks, including in- structional planning and lesson design (Akgun & Greenhow, 2021; Zhu & Ren, 2022), per- sonalised learning (Chen et al., 2020; Kamalov et al., 2023; Kuleto et al., 2022; Marrone et al., 2024), assessment and feedback (Chen et al., 2020; Kumar, 2023; Owan et al., 2023; Stevenson  et  al.,  2024;  Yu  et  al.,  2022),  workload  reduction  (Holmes  &  Tuomi,  2022; Marrone et al., 2024) and the organisation of content delivery (Boateng, 2024; Şahin & Yurdugül, 2020). AI facilitates these processes through tools embedded in learning manage- ment systems (LMS) and intelligent agents that support teacher decision-making (Şahin & Yurdugül, 2020).
As indicated in the introduction, AI tools have been adopted in classrooms in various global contexts. For example, Kuleto et al. (2022) found that Serbian teachers viewed AI as a way to personalise instruction, automate administrative tasks and increase time for profes- sional development. Australian school leaders interviewed by Marrone et al. (2024) reported that AI could reduce teacher workload and support personalised learning. And across the African continent, AI has addressed teacher shortages by delivering science tutoring at scale (Boateng, 2024). This aligns with Wang et al.'s (2024) findings regarding intelligent tu- toring systems (ITS), which adapt content based on students' knowledge and learning style. These systems offer feedback, identify learner needs and help teachers move beyond rote instruction.
Despite these benefits, studies highlight that educators and policymakers must navigate ethical, social and pedagogical concerns about the use of AI in diverse classroom contexts (Holmes & Tuomi, 2022; Kamalov et al., 2023; Ogugua et al., 2023). Educators are also tasked with redefining their roles in an increasingly AI-rich environment (Zhu & Ren, 2022). It is therefore crucial to balance AI's capabilities with human judgement in order to ensure AI remains a tool that complements—rather than replaces—the human elements of education (Marrone et al., 2024). As AI continues to evolve, ongoing research and thoughtful imple- mentation will be key to realising its full potential for creating inclusive, efficient and future- ready learning environments.
AI tools and student engagement
Studies have also identified ways that AI tools can potentially foster students' engagement and personalised learning across a range of subjects and disciplines, including languages, science and music. According to Yau et al. (2022), AI challenges traditional teacher-centred models and allows for more active student participation. Generative AI (GenAI) tools, such as Quillbot, ChatGPT, DALL-E and Synthesia, can encourage students' idea generation, enhance their understanding of abstract concepts and improve the structure and logical co- hesiveness of their writing (Barrett & Pack, 2023; Kamalov et al., 2023; Marzuki et al., 2023; Rahman & Watanobe, 2023). Other AI resources, such as language tools and intelligent tutoring systems, can promote students' independence, offer them personalised feedback and tailored support and encourage collaborative learning (Lameras & Arnab, 2022).
Some AI tools can also assist students with diverse learning needs, thus enhancing classroom inclusivity. Gupta and Chen (2022) identified the capacity of chatbots to provide accessible and confidential support for disadvantaged students, including those with learn- ing disorders or caregiving responsibilities. And Toyokawa et al.'s (2023) study found that AI-supported learning dashboards helped Japanese students with reading difficulties track their progress and visualise their learning processes.
Challenges of using AI tools in the classroom
While AI can offer various benefits to educators and students, research has revealed sev- eral challenges as well. Four challenges that are pertinent to this study are outlined here. First, studies have warned that AI tools can inhibit critical thinking if students lack the skills to evaluate AI-generated content (e.g., Boateng, 2024; Chiu, 2023). Lan and Chen (2024) therefore recommended that students be trained to verify AI outputs against trusted sources. Moreover, there is a risk that students' overreliance on AI could weaken their engagement with content and diminish their creativity and problem-solving abilities (Boateng, 2024; Campino, 2024; Fleckenstein et al., 2024).
A second concern that has been raised relates to the ethics of using AI tools in education settings, given the propensity of AI models to mirror and replicate societal biases regarding gender, race, disability, religion and class (Allen & Kendeou, 2024; Farkas & Németh, 2020; Ogugua et al., 2023; Perrotta & Selwyn, 2020). Perrotta and Selwyn's (2020) The study chal- lenged the perception that AI is ‘an objective scientific endeavour’ (p. 251) and both Allen and Kendeou (2024) and Weerts et al. (2024) suggested that students should be taught to identify potential bias in the AI content with which they engage.
A third concern that has been raised relates to the use of AI tools in student assessments. Studies have highlighted that AI-enabled assessments and exams may increase the risk of cheating and threaten exam validity (Aloisi, 2023; Boateng, 2024; Hill & Hargis, 2025; Ogugua et al., 2023). This problem is compounded by the fact that both novice and experi- enced teachers often struggle to identify AI-generated text and they may be overconfident in their capacity to make such judgements (Fleckenstein et al., 2024). Moreover, detection tools (such as Turnitin) remain unreliable (Campino, 2024; Ibrahim, 2023). Various solutions have been proposed, such as ethics-focused coding lessons and academic integrity train- ing (Boateng, 2024; Ogugua et al., 2023). Studies have also focused on the need to design assessments that minimise the likelihood of AI-enabled cheating, such as oral presenta- tions, classroom debates, project-based work and critical thinking exercises to critique AI- generated content (Fleckenstein et al., 2024; Khlaif et al., 2025; Ogugua et al., 2023).
The fourth issue identified in previous studies relates to teachers' competencies in AI use. School leaders and teachers often lack clear direction or training in AI; as a result, their unfamiliarity can breed anxiety, which further impedes their effective use of AI in teaching and learning (Barrett & Pack, 2023; Marrone et al., 2024; Tyson & Sauers, 2021). Several studies have offered solutions to upskill teachers in this area, including iterative professional development and peer mentorship (Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Teachers' prepared- ness for using AI tools, therefore, appears to be a significant issue; educators need to have the skills to participate in the development and implementation of AI-supported teaching practices (Luckin, 2018; Zhu & Ren, 2022).
A fifth and final challenge of using AI tools pertains to students' inequitable access to AI technologies and devices. Several studies have raised concerns about the creation of a ‘digital divide’, whereby schools in lower socioeconomic areas may lack the resources and infrastructure to ensure all students have access to AI tools in the classroom and at home (Holmes & Tuomi, 2022; Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019). These studies also warn that schools' adoption of AI-mediated learning may unintentionally widen educational inequalities if it is not implemented with adequate support structures.
RESEARCH AIMS AND QUESTIONS
This study investigated the extent to which middle and high school teachers in New Zealand used AI tools in their school-related activities, as well as the challenges and opportunities they perceive or have encountered in doing so. Based on the literature review, a detailed Qualtrics survey was constructed to examine teachers' perceptions of integrating AI into their teaching practices. The specific research questions are as follows:
1. How  are  teachers  in  New  Zealand  secondary  schools  using  AI  tools  for  planning, teaching  and  assessment?
2. What are some of the opportunities teachers associate with the use of AI tools in New Zealand secondary schools?
3. What are some of the challenges teachers associate with the use of AI tools in New Zealand secondary schools?
METHODS
As detailed below, the present study employed a mixed-method research design to investi- gate teachers' uses and perceptions of AI tools.
Participants and procedures
After the authors received approval from their university's ethics committee, they used the Education Counts (2024) public database to compile a list of all public middle and high schools in New Zealand (N = 374). These schools were located in different regions on both the North and South Islands and varied in terms of their Equity Index ranking (Education Counts, 2025). The authors then searched each school's website and retrieved all the pub- licly available email addresses of teaching staff (full-time and part-time) across all subject areas. Towards the end of the school year, an email was sent to each address, which in- cluded a description of the current study, a participant information sheet (PIS) and an anony- mous link to the online survey (hosted on Qualtrics). Participants were reassured that the survey link was anonymised to protect their privacy and that they retained the right to with- draw from the research prior to submitting their survey responses. Recipients were informed that, by submitting the survey, they were consenting to participate in this study and that they retained the right to withdraw from the study prior to submitting the survey responses.
Of the 554 teachers who received the email, 148 (26.7%) clicked on the survey link and 99 of those respondents (66.9%) completed the survey. With respect to demographic char- acteristics: 56.6% of the sample identified as female and 41.4% identified as male; 69.7% identified as New Zealand European and the remaining sample, a small number (none more than 5.1%) of Maori, Pasifika, Asian and ‘Other’. In terms of their professional details, most of the sample (66.7) indicated that they taught Years 11–13, followed by Years 9–10 (19.2%) and the Years 7–8 (14.1); English and science were the most common subjects taught (26.3% and 18.2%, respectively) with PE/health, geography, commerce, art and music the least common (at 2% each). Finally, most teachers indicated that they taught in a traditional classroom (86.9%) and in a school located in a city (54.5%).
Measures
The survey used in the study comprised original measures and was divided into two sec- tions. The first section comprised two closed-ended questions that asked participants to indicate (where 0 = No and 1 = Yes) their perceptions related to the use of AI in their school. The first question presented participants with a list of eight possible uses (plus an ‘Other’ option that allowed for text entry) of AI and asked participants to indicate (by selecting all that applied) the current ways in which they saw AI was being used in their school (see Figure 1). The second question was formatted the same way (i.e. with an ‘Other’ option and ability to select multiple options) but asked participants to indicate the reasons why their school might not be incorporating AI into teaching and learning (see Figure 2).
The second section comprised three prompts or questions that asked participants to describe (in their own words) their experiences and perceptions related to using AI in sec- ondary school settings:
1. If  you  use  AI  in  your  planning,  teaching  and/or  assessment,  please  provide  a  de- scription  of  how  you  use  AI  and  for  what  purpose
2. What are some opportunities you see for the use of Artificial Intelligence in secondary schools?
3. What are some challenges you see in the use of Artificial Intelligence in secondary schools?
Analysis
The data analysis for this study employed a mixed-method approach. Quantitative analysis was used to provide an overview of teachers' different uses of AI tools and the challenges they faced when attempting to integrate these tools into their teaching. These results then informed the subsequent qualitative analysis of the open-ended responses to the survey questions. This step of the analysis considered teachers' use of AI tools for planning, teach- ing and assessment; the opportunities and challenges that teachers associated with the use of these tools.
The qualitative data gathered from the 99 completed teacher surveys were coded by following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-step thematic analysis method. The authors worked collaboratively to familiarise themselves with the data, generate initial codes, identify themes, review and refine those themes, name and define them and produce the final report. A well- structured codebook, which distinguished between numeric and string variables, helped to organise the data and align it with relevant themes. Inductive coding allowed patterns to emerge directly from the data, leading to the identification of themes that were pertinent to the four research questions, such as instructional planning, engagement, feedback and challenges around authenticity and teacher preparedness. Coding results were visualised through diagrams (see Figure 3 thru 5), which enhanced the clarity of data interpretation. Two coders independently coded 20% of responses. Initial agreement was κ = 0.67. After discussion and codebook refinement, agreement was reached, κ = 0.85 for the full dataset. All quantitative analyses, including the computation of frequencies, confidence intervals and crosstabulations with Chi-square analyses, were conducted in IBM SPSS Statistics version 30.0.
RESULTS
The results from this study are presented in three sections. The first section describes the results from the two closed-ended questions related to school- level perceptions of AI. These results help provide context for the latter two sections that detail the results from the three open-ended questions that allowed participants to give voice to their own experiences using AI (Section 2) as well as the opportunities and challenges they believe AI presents in sec- ondary school contexts (Section 3). Specifically, the second section depicts the responses to the first question.

As detailed in Figure 1, ‘Teachers using AI for planning’ was the most common use, with two-thirds of respondents (66.7%) indicating that they had seen AI being used for the pur- pose. Section 2: Participants' use of AI
The first open-ended question asked participants to describe how and for what purposes they used AI in their planning, teaching and/or assessment. As illustrated in Figure 3, results from thematic analyses yielded several codes under the three broad categories of planning, teaching and assessment. As indicated by the n's and percentages, ‘Planning’ was the largest or most populated category with 40% of participants producing a response that was associated with one or more of its three codes. ‘Teaching’ and ‘Assessment’ followed with 25% and 20%, respectively. The remaining 15% of participants indicated that ‘Not applica- ble’, ‘Not relevant’ or ‘Not sure’. Each of the three categories and the codes under them is described below in greater detail.

Within the category of planning, three distinct codes emerged from the data: writing lesson plans and/or objectives (32%), finding resources (4%) and using AI tools to create resources

Teaching
As shown in Figure 3, 25% of the teachers used AI tools as resource assistants in class. As one of the respondents stated, ‘AI can provide a great starting point for design and idea generation’. Likewise, another respondent wrote that ‘AI is a useful starting point for a lot of the work students do—finding research, readings, asking questions, grammar support’. Teachers also identified some AI tools that supported students in their class who had learn- ing needs. One teacher wrote that the tools were effective in ‘supporting neurodiverse stu- dents in particular, students who are struggling and need support getting started on learning activities’ (Gateway teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school).
Further analysis showed that the teachers used AI tools more often for generating lesson ideas than for creating actual activities. According to one participant, AI tools were helpful.
purely for brainstorming. If I can't think of an idea for a classroom activity to fill a gap then I use AI to complete a lesson plan—majority of the time I don't use their plan and just take small suggestions. 
(Language teacher, Years 9–10, traditional classroom, city fringe school).
This response highlights that the teachers did not always adopt the information provided by AI tools comprehensively or uncritically; rather, they drew on some of the generated ideas or inspirations while dismissing others. This was also captured in an aforementioned participant's quote, which mentioned that AI-generated ideas for planning were often ‘too broad or non-applicable’.
Assessment
Similar to the results from the first closed-ended question, only 20% of participants men- tioned using AI tools for assessment purposes. Three-quarters of these teachers (15.2% of all participants) used AI to provide students with more immediate feedback. As one teacher commented, AI tools were helpful when ‘giving immediate feedback for writing samples so students can continue writing rather than wait until their turn with the teacher’ (teacher aide, working in a city school). Another teacher wrote:
Feedback is also broken down so students can understand. I can also run it through Quillbot for an even more simplified version of the feedback that was given. 
(Social studies teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
Only one teacher (1.0% of the sample) used AI tools to create formative assessments and only four (4.0%) mentioned using AI tools to offer students clearer information about their assessments. For example, one respondent reported that they used AI tools to write ‘rubrics’ and ‘instructions’ for assessments (English teacher, Years 11–13, innovative learning envi- ronment, farming community school).
Section 3: Opportunities and challenges Opportunities associated with using AI
As depicted in Figure 4, thematic analysis of participants' comments related to the opportu- nities they believed were associated with AI yielded two distinct themes: instructional plan- ning and feedback and assessment. These themes and codes associated with them are described below. Before doing so, it is worth noting the relatively high percentage of partici- pant responses that were classified as ‘Other’ (29%). In brief, these participants were either missing (i.e. no response at all) or not relevant to the question (i.e. they wrote about some- thing other than possible opportunities associated with AI use). Combined with participants who responded ‘Not sure’ (1%) or ‘Not applicable’ (9%), nearly four of every ten teachers did not contribute a codable ‘opportunity’ for AI use.
Opportunities for instructional planning
As illustrated in Figure 4 47% of teachers regarded AI tools as a useful approach to in- structional planning: 20% to create resources and plan their lessons; 11% to give students inspiration and generate ideas; and 16% as teaching/research assistants. Many of the responses related to resource creation and lesson planning echoed the time-saving theme noted in the foregoing section related to ‘planning’. Participants reported that AI tools not only helped them generate resources and prepare instructional activities, but that they did so in ways that participants say they would not have thought of on their own and in far less time than they could have completed by themselves. As one respondent wrote
[AI tools] help teachers plan and create resources in much less time than it cur- rently takes plus differentiation of lessons and activities is more accessible for teachers to include in teaching content. 
(Social Studies teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, town school)

Opportunities relating to feedback and assessment
The theme of timesaving came up again when participants reflected on feedback and as- sessment. A number of participants noted that AI tools gave them the opportunity to spend less time marking and more time having one-to-one engagement with their students. The survey data also revealed that teachers used AI tools to analyse data and prepare assess- ments, which again created more time to engage with students. One teacher wrote, ‘Less time spent marking for teachers can mean more 1–1 face time with students’ (teacher of multiple subjects, Years 11–13, innovative learning environment, urban school).
The time-saving benefits of AI tools were also mentioned in relation to the quality of teachers' feedback. One participant wrote:
I use AI to reduce time and workload. I still have to follow through and make corrections, but it saves me so much time while writing far more succinctly and professionally. 
(Design and communications teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city 
school)
This quote also illustrates that teachers were still using their own judgements and making alterations while using content generated by AI tools. However, the respondent recognised that these tools provided the opportunity to save time and improve the quality of their feed- back to students.
Some participants mentioned that AI tools could be used effectively for teaching other languages and were helpful as translation tools. Others indicated that AI tools were used to differentiate texts and create scaffolded assessments for ESOL (English speakers of other languages) students. According to one participant,
I sometimes use ChatGPT to simplify texts for my lower- level students and my ESOL students. I've also used it before for ideas about how to teach units when I've hit a creative block; however, I rarely find the suggestions interesting or age appropriate. 
(English Teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, town school)
The participant's final remark again highlights that teachers appear to make use of AI tools in a carefully curated way, rather than integrating their content uncritically into their feedback and assessments. While this teacher appreciated that ChatGPT could help them overcome their ‘creative block’, they were also aware that the opportunities provided by this AI tool were somewhat limited.
Challenges associated with using AI tools
As depicted in Figure 5, thematic analysis of participants' comments related to the challenges they believed to be associated with integrating AI tools in their professional practice: concerns about student learning and shortcomings in teacher preparedness. These themes and codes associated with them are described below. Before doing so, it is worth noting the very low ‘Not sure’ (1%) or ‘Not applicable’ (4%). Put another way, 95% of participants had something of substance to say about the challenges of using AI tools.
Challenges related to student learning
As shown in Figure 5, 68% of participants experienced or expressed two types of chal- lenges pertaining to student learning: the misuse of AI and the compromising of critical thinking.
Student misuse of AI.  With 58% of all participants giving voice to concerns about student misuse of AI tools, this was—by a large margin—the most common challenge for teachers. Specifically, teachers expressed concerns about students misusing AI tools in ways that undermined their learning and/or misrepresented what they had done. The latter concern included using LLM's to ‘plagiarise’ material (i.e., claiming GenAI output as their own ideas or writing). One participant summarised these issues

Many teachers expressed this concern in terms of ‘authenticity’ and ‘validating’ the work being submitted as that of the student submitting it. These concerns were often coupled with concerns about students becoming increasingly reliant on AI tools to generate ideas for them. One respondent observed that this dependence on AI negatively impacted students' reading and writing skills as they used information from these tools without creating or de- veloping original ideas by actively engaging in the research process:
Checking for authenticity, validating information. Students will likely take what- ever responses from AI as true so teaching them about that will be crucial. Keeping up with it, as more people are using it, it keeps changing and getting better. 
(English Teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, town school)
Other respondents noted that students struggled with basic research and writing skills, such as summarising, paraphrasing and critically analysing information generated by AI tools. Overall, teachers' comments highlighted their concerns that students lacked the skills to effectively use AI tools and often resorted to copying and pasting content from platforms like ChatGPT.
Compromising critical thinking
The second code or subtheme that teachers expressed under student-related challenges was critical thinking. Specifically, 10% of participants believed that AI tools undermined critical thinking among students. A few noted that it was a challenge to remind students that AI should be used as a supportive tool rather than as an alternative for thinking and creating. Others argued that students' reliance on AI tools may undermine their ability to develop critical thinking and analytical skills and they expressed concerns that students may be unable to identify misleading information that is sometimes generated by these tools. One respondent summed up these concerns when they wrote that they were chal- lenged by
the fact that AI is built on plagiarism and the work of writers, artists and creators without their consent or permission. That AI is currently a language model pro- gram and not genuine artificial intelligence, which has been bolstered by tech media hype. Reliance on this version of AI can potentially lead to diminished ability to develop critical thinking or analysis, as well as difficulty ascertaining real facts from the false facts sometimes generated by AI. 
(English teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school).
Challenges related to teacher training and teaching
Over a quarter (27%) of participants gave voice to three types of challenges with AI imple- mentation related to teaching and teacher training: lack of guidance and training, inability to detect AI use and modification of assessment practices.
Lack of guidance and training for teachers.  Two-thirds of them (18% of all respondents) were concerned about a lack of guidance and training for teachers who used or wanted to use AI tools in the classroom. Many participants noted that the lack of guidance and training left them unsure about policies concerning privacy and age registration and unprepared to effectively use and ethical use of AI tools for instructional and assessment purposes. One respondent mentioned:
[the] lack of training and understanding on how it works. What are its limitations? What are the data and privacy issues we need to be aware of when working with real students? 
(Science teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
Issues pertaining to privacy laws were raised by another participant, who also expressed concerns about students' equitable access to AI tools in the classroom:
Making sure all staff are aware of privacy law and don't put anything into AI/use AI in a way that could breach privacy. Another big issue is getting staff not to try to “ban” AI use for research/report style assessments (as this is impossible and the kids will just use it anyway). So getting staff to fully embrace AI and un- derstand its pros and cons is key. This is a requirement to ensure assessment authenticity. 
(History teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
A wider issue around access was noted by participants in light of schools not having poli- cies on the use of AI tools. Specifically, they mentioned that certain AI tools require parental consent before children can use them:
The majority of AI systems have age restrictions that are above the level that students are being taught. Students would need parent permission to be taught using these tools. This is not a decision to be taken lightly. ChatGPT, for exam- ple, requires parental consent for 13–18-year-olds. 
(English teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
Related to the theme of access, one participant commented that the use of AI tools had equity-related implications, which current school policies and guidelines did not address:
Students who have ready access to devices at home are much more confident in using AI tools than students who don't have this opportunity. 
(Gateway teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
Teachers' inability to detect AI use.  The second most significant challenge related to teacher training and teaching concerned the inability to detect student misuse. Given the high percentage of participants who expressed concern about student misuse of AI (58%), it is remarkable that only 7% expressed concerns about the detection of misuse. Nevertheless, for this minority of participants, detection of misuse was a clear challenge and one that was often compounded by the difficulty of proving misuse once detected. One participant wrote
Assessments—it's hard to prove that a student has cheated even if the software is saying so. 
(Science teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city fringe school)
Others expressed a concern about AI detection devices. As one participant wrote:
Plagiarism is the big one, with it becoming increasingly difficult to “prove” which work is authentic or not. AI checkers are very unreliable and are not keeping pace with chat bots such as Chat GPT. 
(English teacher, Years 11–13, innovative learning environment, city fringe 
school)
Modification  of  Assessment  Practices.  Only  2.0%  of  the  participants  mentioned  that teachers should modify assessment practices when using AI tools in the classroom because students  were  making  considerable  use  of  these  tools  for  their  writing  assignments. Moreover, because students were making such extensive use of AI tools, teachers needed to ensure that these tools were fit for purpose. One teacher wrote
Students are using [AI tools] extensively for writing tasks, so we may need to modify how we assess. 
(Science teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city fringe school)
Another teacher, who found AI-detecting tools to be unhelpful, mentioned that teachers had to find alternative ways to check that students were not misusing AI to complete their assessments:
We have to use other tools, such as document and revision history, and our own intuition and knowledge of students' abilities. 
(English teacher, Years 11–13, innovative learning environment, city fringe 
school)
The use of ‘document and revision history’ as an assessment method requires students to submit several written drafts, which would show the progress of their work and demonstrate they had worked iteratively on a piece of writing over time. Other teachers mentioned similar strategies to assess the quality and integrity of students' work:
Assessment requires the production of many more written tasks in the early part of the year to establish baseline writing references. Alternatively, requires tasks to have clear research documents where students are required to process found information. Other responses to this question included inauthentic work submis- sion that leads to assessments being not achieved. 
(English teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
It's had a negative impact on assessment this year. To the point where we have gone back to using pen and paper to avoid the authenticity issues with digital assessments. 
(Commerce teacher, Years 11–13, traditional classroom, city school)
DISCUSSION
Summary of findings
This study set out to examine how teachers in NZ secondary and middle schools perceived and engaged with AI tools in their teaching practice. The findings reflect a dynamic and evolving educational landscape, in which AI technologies are both welcomed for their poten- tial to support learning and approached with caution due to concerns about ethics, student agency and pedagogical integrity. Through both quantitative and qualitative analysis of 99 teachers' survey responses, clear patterns emerged around the opportunities AI presents, as well as the significant challenges that must be addressed to ensure its equitable and criti- cal use. The findings are now summarised in light of the three research questions.
Teachers' use of AI tools in the classroom
Many teachers reported using AI to support their work around planning, teaching and assess- ment, and they identified these uses as some of the most beneficial aspects of AI integration. Teachers employed AI tools to generate lesson ideas, create worksheets or comprehension questions, offer clear feedback in a timely manner and simplify complex content for different student levels. Their responses align with findings from existing literature that have identified educators' use of AI for instructional planning (Akgun & Greenhow, 2021; Zhu & Ren, 2022), content  delivery  (Boateng,  2024;  Şahin  &  Yurdugül,  2020),  personalised  learning  plans (Kamalov et al., 2023; Kuleto et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) and assignment design and feed- back (Kumar, 2023; Owan et al., 2023; Stevenson et al., 2024). The teachers in this current study also highlighted that AI tools offered more time-efficient ways to plan and create teaching material, as well as complete administrative tasks. This likewise reflects previous studies that have noted AI's potential to alleviate educators' workload (Akgun & Greenhow, 2021; Holmes 
& Tuomi, 2022; Kuleto et al., 2022; Marrone et al., 2024; Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019). Although many of the teachers who participated in this study saw the value of using AI tools for planning, teaching and assessment, they did not use these tools uncritically; 
several respondents mentioned that they were selective in their use of AI-generated con- tent and were aware that AI tools could sometimes offer teachers limited support. This may reflect one of the challenges of using AI tools, which has been discussed in previ- ous studies. AI-generated content may not be context- or age-appropriate for its use in school environments, or it may encode societal biases that teachers find unhelpful (Farkas 
& Németh, 2020; Ogugua et al., 2023).
Opportunities teachers associate with the use of AI tools
In terms of opportunities, the survey respondents reported that AI tools helped them with instructional planning and with feedback and assessment. Specifically, they mentioned that some tools enabled them to be more creative in terms of their lesson content and assignment styles that catered to individual students' needs, including students with particular learning challenges. These findings align with previous studies, such as Gupta and Chen (2022) and Toyokawa et al. (2023), which noted the capacity for AI tools to assist students with diverse learning needs and enhance classroom inclusivity.
The time-saving opportunities provided by AI were also raised by several participants, who mentioned that their use of AI tools gave them more time to build relationships with stu- dents. This echoes Chen et al.'s (2020) study, which argued that, when used effectively, AI can free up cognitive and emotional space for teachers to engage in more complex, human- centred aspects of pedagogy.
The challenges of using AI tools in schools
Despite these advantages, participants identified several challenges related to the use of AI tools. Many teachers expressed unease about whether students were thinking for them- selves or merely relying on AI to do the intellectual work. This reflects previous findings that overreliance on AI tools can undermine opportunities for critical thinking, especially when students lack the evaluative skills to question AI-generated outputs (Boateng, 2024; Chiu, 2023; Fleckenstein et al., 2024; Lan & Chen, 2024).
The issue of plagiarism and academic integrity also featured prominently in partici- pants' responses. Some teachers admitted that they found it hard to determine whether student work had been AI-generated, particularly in subjects requiring written expression. These concerns align with the findings of Bulut et al. (2024), Ibrahim (2023) and Ogugua et al. (2023), among others, who highlighted the growing challenges of identifying students' misuse of AI-generated content. Some survey participants outlined their efforts to find new assessment methods, such as ‘document and revision history’, which minimised the likeli- hood of AI-enabled cheating. This approach has also been captured in recent studies by Khlaif et al. (2025), Ogugua et al. (2023) and Fleckenstein et al. (2024), who likewise em- phasised the need to design assessment formats that discourage or prevent the (mis)use of AI. This could be a fruitful avenue for teachers to explore in their ongoing training and professional development.
Participants also expressed their concerns that AI may cause students' learning to be- come superficial and discourage their engagement with critical thinking. Teachers noted that, while AI could quickly summarise, explain or rephrase content, it did not always promote deeper learning. As one respondent observed, ‘It gives them an answer, but not the reasoning behind it’. This echoes Perrotta and Selwyn's (2020) critique of AI as a ‘black box’ that potentially limits metacognition and student agency. Teachers' concerns about students' limited capacity for critical thinking also reflect Ogugua et al.'s (2023) observation that the value placed on students' capacity for critical thinking is ‘at risk of being lost’ (p. 4; also Boateng, 2024; Campino, 2024; Chiu, 2023; Fleckenstein et al., 2024; Lan & Chen, 2024).
Several teachers pointed to teachers' limited preparedness and confidence as barriers to more widespread AI integration. Many expressed uncertainties about how to use AI eth- ically or pedagogically, and they often relied on trial and error. There were also concerns about age limits imposed by AI tool creators and the need to secure parents' consent. These issues are by no means unique to the NZ school context. A number of international studies have emphasised the need for teacher training and professional development—particularly in areas such as digital literacy, AI ethics and critical media use—to ensure teachers feel more confident about using AI tools in a safe and effective way (Allen & Kendeou, 2024; Barrett & Pack, 2023; Marrone et al., 2024; Tyson & Sauers, 2021).
Questions regarding equity were also raised by survey respondents, especially around access to AI tools and devices. Some noted that not all students had digital access at home, thus raising concerns about the creation of a ‘digital divide’. Such challenges mirror those identified by Holmes and Tuomi (2022) and Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019), who warned that increased pressures on schools to use AI technologies may unintentionally widen educa- tional inequalities if the implementation of these technologies is not adequately supported.
Limitations and future directions
This study provides a unique perspective on NZ teachers' perceptions of AI use in the class- room; it also contributes meaningfully to the growing literature on this topic. Nevertheless, the study's limitations should be acknowledged. First, while the completion rate of the survey was acceptable (66.9%), the sample size was small (N = 99). This may have been due in part to the survey being sent out near the end of the school year, when teachers tend to be especially busy with end-of-year activities and administration. There may also have been some sample bias, given that the invitation could only be sent to teaching staff with a pub- licly available email. The confounders in any study on AI include teacher AI literacy, school resources and subject-specific assessment modes.
A larger sample would have allowed for more robust statistical analyses and perhaps more refined results. For example, future larger scale studies could explore differences in how teachers implement AI across different academic subjects or settings. The present study suggested that mostly English and science teachers were using AI. It would be ben- eficial to get specific feedback from other subjects, such as maths, music, art and physical education, to identify their various uses of AI tools and/or the reasons why these tools are not being utilised.
Second, the present study focused on New Zealand school teachers, which may limit the generalisability of the findings. Sampling bias, unknown response rate, selection bias from public email scraping, self-report bias and timing (end of school year may influence responses). Nevertheless, as highlighted in the ‘Discussion’ section, the findings were in keeping with earlier studies conducted in other countries in the Global North and South. The range of subjects taught by participants was diverse, but given the higher numbers of English and science teachers, the results may reflect their perspectives more prominently than the perspectives of teachers from other subject areas. There was a higher representa- tion of city-based teachers and teachers working with Years 11–13. Given that only teachers were surveyed, future research in New Zealand could also include student perspectives on the use of AI and its impact on their learning. Combining the perspectives of both teachers and their students would not only enhance the credibility of the research but would also yield a more holistic and nuanced understanding of AI use in educational settings.
Finally, the present study was limited in terms of the research methods and types of data collected. Future research could go beyond an online questionnaire and include interviews, focus groups and/or classroom observations. A multi-method approach would enable the triangulation of data sources and strengthen the validity of the research findings and their implications. Given the rapid development of AI technologies and their uptake in education, a longitudinal study design could allow researchers to evaluate how AI integration shapes classroom practices over time. Finally, this study did not inquire about or account for teach- ers' level of AI literacy; future studies should do so.
CONCLUSION
Taken together, the findings of this study highlight the need for a critical pedagogy of AI— one that empowers teachers and students to engage with AI thoughtfully, reflectively and ethically. AI is not simply a tool to be used but a sociotechnical system that shapes how knowledge is accessed, produced and assessed. As Luckin (2018) argued, educators must actively participate in shaping how AI is embedded into teaching practices rather than being passive adopters of this new technology.
There is a pressing need for professional development, clear policies and curriculum- aligned resources that enable AI integration to enhance rather than replace students' ca- pacity for critical thinking. It also includes designing assessment practices that foreground process over product, thereby ensuring that students' learning journeys are visible even when AI is used as a support tool. While this study was based on a relatively small sam- ple size, the richness of qualitative responses provides valuable insights into how New 
Zealand teachers are navigating a rapidly changing and increasingly tech-driven educa- tional terrain.
