The Impact of AI Literacy on Academic Misconduct: The Mediating Role of Conceptions of Assessment and Academic Integrity

Abstract
As generative AI (GenAI) tools become increasingly embedded in higher education, concerns about AI-facilitated academic misconduct have escalated rapidly. This study addresses a significant gap by investigating the impact of AI Literacy (AIL) on Academic Misconduct (AM) among Chinese university students, with Conceptions of Assessment (CoA) and Academic Integrity (AcInt) as serial mediating variables. Grounded in Brown's (2011) Self-Regulation Model of Conceptions of Assessment and Rest's (1999) Four-Component Model of Moral Functioning, the proposed model posits that AI-literate students develop more adaptive beliefs about the genuine purpose of assessment, which in turn strengthen their commitment to academic integrity and ultimately reduce engagement in academic misconduct, including AI-facilitated forms. A quantitative cross-sectional survey design is employed, with data to be collected from Chinese university students and analyzed using partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM). Findings are expected to illuminate the cognitive and moral pathways through which AI literacy shapes ethical academic behavior, providing empirically grounded guidance for universities seeking to foster cultures of integrity in the GenAI era.

Keywords: AI Literacy; Academic Misconduct; Conceptions of Assessment; Academic Integrity; Higher Education; PLS-SEM

1. Introduction
As an emerging field of computer science, artificial intelligence (AI) has profoundly impacted education (Celik, 2023; Montebello, 2018). Recently, there has been growing interest in exploring the interactions between people and AI and AI education systems. However, much of this interest has concentrated on AI's instructional and technical affordances, with comparatively little attention paid to the integrity- and assessment-related consequences of students' growing access to generative AI (GenAI) tools such as ChatGPT (Gruenhagen et al., 2024; Perkins et al., 2024). The rapid diffusion of GenAI into higher education has unsettled the traditional assumptions underlying assessment design, evaluation, and academic honesty (Gruenhagen et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024). Indeed, more than a third of university students surveyed in Australia reported having used a chatbot for assistance with an assessment, and many of them did not regard this as a breach of academic integrity (Gruenhagen et al., 2024). This finding suggests that students' understanding of what counts as legitimate versus dishonest AI use—that is, their AI literacy—may be a critical yet underexamined antecedent of academic misconduct in the GenAI era.

The AI shift has generated challenges and opportunities for teaching and learning (Ding et al., 2024; Pan & Wang, 2025; Wijaya et al., 2024). Although AI has great potential to facilitate learning, its integration into higher education settings generally requires students to navigate multilayered ethical decisions about legitimate and illegitimate AI use (Al-Abdullatif & Alsubaie, 2024; An et al., 2023). Academic misconduct—broadly defined as behavior that violates the standards of honesty and fairness expected in academic work—has long been recognized as a pervasive problem in higher education, with the majority of students in most countries admitting to having cheated at least once during their studies (Stephens, 2019; Stephens et al., 2024). A nationwide survey of 4,493 undergraduates in New Zealand found that approximately two-thirds reported engaging in at least one form of academic misconduct in the preceding year, and that students' moral attitudes toward cheating were significantly associated with their behavior (Stephens et al., 2024). At the same time, assessment scholars have long argued that how students conceive of the purposes of assessment shapes their self-regulatory responses and, by extension, their academic behavior (Brown, 2011, 2021; Brown & Wang, 2016; Zhao et al., 2020).

First, although the potential for AI-enhanced learning has grown rapidly, there is a notable lack of both theoretical and empirical research on AI Literacy in the context of academic integrity in higher education (Lu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). While AI literacy has been theorized as encompassing an explicit ethics dimension (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021b), empirical research has rarely tested whether this ethical-evaluative competence actually translates into more honest academic conduct when students use AI tools for their academic work (Lund et al., 2025; Perkins et al., 2024). Few studies have systematically examined how the ethics dimension of AI literacy shapes students' assessment beliefs and moral attitudes toward academic dishonesty (Nabhan & Habók, 2026).

Second, although interest in AI Literacy is increasing, the precise relationship between AI literacy and academic misconduct remains largely unexplored. While conceptions of assessment have been extensively studied as antecedents of self-regulated learning and academic performance (Brown, 2011, 2021; Brown & Wang, 2016), and academic integrity has been studied as a predictor of academic misconduct (Perry et al., 2026; Stephens, 2018; Stephens et al., 2024), few studies have integrated these two well-established but largely separate literatures with the emerging literature on AI literacy into a single research framework capable of explaining the cognitive and moral pathways through which AI literacy shapes (dis)honest academic behavior. This integration is vital for understanding how students' AI-related competencies interact with their assessment beliefs and moral orientations to produce academic conduct decisions (Wu et al., 2024).

Although China has actively integrated AI tools into its higher education landscape, systematic empirical research on how Chinese university students' AI literacy relates to their academic conduct remains scarce (A. Zhao et al., 2020). Most existing studies have focused on students' technical AI competencies or pedagogical outcomes, leaving a notable gap regarding how AI literacy shapes assessment beliefs and integrity behaviors in a population where Confucian-heritage assessment norms and high-stakes institutional cultures further complicate the dynamics of academic misconduct (Brown & Wang, 2016; Zhao et al., 2020).

To address these identified research gaps, this study investigates the impact of AI Literacy (Independent Variable) on Academic Misconduct (Dependent Variable) through the mediating effects of Conceptions of Assessment and Academic Integrity (Mediator Variables) in a serial mediation research model. Based on Brown's (2011) Self-Regulation Model of Conceptions of Assessment and Rest's (1999) Four-Component Model of Moral Functioning, this study aims to bridge these gaps by investigating the interrelationships between these constructs among Chinese university students.

2. Literature Review and Hypothesis

2.1. AI Literacy (AIL)

The increasing incorporation of AI into teaching and learning environments has sparked interest in AI literacy (Al-Abdullatif & Alsubaie, 2024; Wang et al., 2023). One approach to AI education is to improve AI literacy (Casal-Otero et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2025). Nevertheless, the effective classroom integration of AI demands that teachers be AI-literate, encompassing understanding, utilization, monitoring, and critical reflection on AI applications in teaching (Ding et al., 2024).

AI literacy is a newly emerging concept that seeks to define, teach, and evaluate knowledge and skills related to AI. AI literacy refers to an individual’s ability to comprehend, utilize, monitor, and critically evaluate AI applications, even without the ability to develop AI models. Furthermore, it encompasses the ability to observe, ethically use, and critically evaluate AI applications and AI-generated content and their implications without necessarily having programming knowledge. AI literacy encompasses the competencies that enable individuals to critically evaluate AI technologies and leverage them for effective communication and collaboration (Long & Magerko, 2020; Wu et al., 2024). This concept entails the design and implementation of AI learning activities, tools, applications, and pedagogical models (Wu et al., 2024; Yim & Su, 2024).

The term “AI literacy” was first coined by Burgsteiner et al. (2016) and Kandlhofer et al. (2016), who described the competencies required to understand the basic knowledge and concepts of AI and to use AI technologies. In addition, Long and Magerko (2020) defined it as a set of competencies that enables individuals to critically evaluate, communicate, and collaborate effectively with AI and use it as a tool, online, at home, and in the workplace. Furthermore, Ng et al. (2021b, 2021a) added AI to every student’s AI literacy in work settings and everyday life and proposed it as a fundamental skill for everyone, not just computer scientists. He incorporated the model of Bloom’s Taxonomy, Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge (TPACK), and AI concepts, practices, and perspectives into the instructional design of AI literacy education (Ng et al., 2021b, 2021a).

Carolus et al. (2023) stated that AI literacy covers “competencies needed to interact with AI technology in a self-determined and rational manner.” Pinski and Benlian (2023) provided a definition of General AI literacy, describing it ashumans “socio-technical competence consisting of knowledge regarding human and AI actors in human-AI interaction, knowledge of the AI process steps, that is, input, processing, and output, and experience in AI interaction.” Laupichler et al. (2023) define the concept of AI literacy as “competencies that include basic knowledge and analytical evaluation of AI, in addition to the critical use of AI applications by ordinary non-expert individuals.” They did not include (programming skills) in their definitions because they considered them a separate set of competencies beyond AI literacy (Laupichler et al., 2023).

Recently, several studies have developed AI literacy frameworks for education (Kong et al., 2023; Ng et al., 2021b; Wong et al., 2020). AI literacy does not require detailed technical expertise in AI applications. However, it spans four dimensions: understanding and comprehension of AI, practical application of AI, assessment of AI applications, and ethical considerations of AI (L. Zhao et al., 2022).

Long and Magerko (2020) provided a conceptual framework of AI literacy composed of five overarching themes derived from the literature (Long & Magerko, 2020).Wang et al. (2023) proposed a theoretical framework to conceptualize AI Literacy, comprising four constructs: Usage, defined as the ability to exploit AI technology to proficiently perform tasks; Awareness, the ability to identify and comprehend AI systems during interactions; Evaluation, the capacity to critically analyze and assess AI applications and their outcomes; and Ethics, which involves recognizing responsibilities and risks associated with using AI systems. Kong et al. (2023) designed an AI literacy framework, including cognitive, affective, and sociocultural. Ng et al. (2021b) developed an AI literacy TPACK framework that includes four dimensions: (1) knowing and understanding AI (knowing the basic functions of AI and how to use AI applications); (2) using and applying AI (applying AI knowledge, concepts, and applications in different scenarios); (3) evaluating and creating AI (higher-order thinking skills with AI applications); and (4) AI ethics (human-centered considerations). These dimensions offer a comprehensive framework that supports the development of AI literacy and enables AI integration into education (Su & Yang, 2024).

Despite the growing body of research on AI Literacy, significant gaps remain (Lu et al., 2024). Most studies have relied on qualitative methodologies or small-scale quantitative analyses (Ng et al., 2021b, 2023; Schiavo et al., 2024; Su et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), limiting our understanding of the broader implementation and effectiveness of AI education (Ng et al., 2021b, 2023; Schiavo et al., 2024; Su et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). A significant majority (75 %) of the papers pertaining to AI literacy have focused on students (Al-Abdullatif, 2024; Almatrafi et al., 2024; Ng et al., 2023), while few have focused on teachers (An et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023).

Therefore, teachers must understand what AI is and what it can do inside and outside the classroom, and engage with AI technologies in a self-determined manner. These sets of skills, including understanding, utilizing, and integrating various AI technologies to enhance teaching methods, facilitate student learning, and improve overall educational outcomes, are commonly referred to as AI literacy (Carolus et al., 2023).

2.2. Academic Misconduct (AM)
Academic misconduct refers to behavior that violates the standards of honesty, fairness, and responsibility expected in academic work (Stephens, 2019). It has been operationalized in the literature as a multidimensional behavioral construct spanning collusion (e.g., unpermitted collaboration on individual assignments), misuse of resources (e.g., plagiarism, unauthorized use of materials), and fraud and contract cheating (Perry et al., 2026; Rettinger et al., 2024; Stephens et al., 2024). The emergence of GenAI tools has introduced new and complex forms of misconduct—including submitting AI-generated content as one's own work and using AI paraphrasing tools to disguise plagiarism—that blur the lines between legitimate AI assistance and academic fraud (Gruenhagen et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024).

Scholars have approached academic misconduct from multiple perspectives. Stephens (2019) characterized the problem as epidemic in scale, noting that the majority of tertiary students globally report having cheated at least once during their studies, with this rate remaining remarkably stable across five decades and numerous countries. From a moral-psychological perspective, Stephens (2018) demonstrated that misconduct is driven not primarily by moral ignorance but by moral disengagement—students who cheat frequently possess accurate moral knowledge but use self-exculpatory rationalizations, such as blaming the instructor or normalizing the behavior among peers, to neutralize their ethical standards in specific situations. At a multi-level theoretical level, Perry et al. (2026) found that moral attitudes, institutional climate, and peer norms each contribute unique variance to misconduct, and that no single explanatory factor is sufficient on its own, confirming that academic misconduct is a multiply determined behavioral outcome.

Prevalence data underscore the scale of the problem. A nationwide New Zealand survey of 4,493 undergraduates found that approximately two-thirds (64.8%) reported at least one act of academic misconduct in the preceding year, with unauthorized collaboration being the most common behavior (Stephens et al., 2024). That survey incorporated the first items capturing AI-facilitated misconduct and found an initial 14.8% self-reported prevalence rate—likely a significant underestimate given that data collection concluded just two months before ChatGPT's public release (Stephens et al., 2024). Subsequent studies have confirmed that AI-facilitated misconduct is rapidly increasing and that many students do not recognize unauthorized AI assistance as a breach of academic integrity, highlighting the urgent need for research on the individual factors that shape these misconduct decisions (Gruenhagen et al., 2024; Lund et al., 2025).

Despite extensive research, significant gaps remain. Most existing studies predate the widespread availability of GenAI tools and therefore do not capture its most emergent forms of dishonest behavior (Lund et al., 2025; Perkins et al., 2024). Moreover, while moral attitudes and peer norms have been identified as predictors, the role of AI literacy—specifically whether higher AI literacy leads to more ethical decision-making around GenAI use—has not been empirically tested (Ng et al., 2021b; Stephens et al., 2024). Research from Chinese higher education contexts is particularly sparse, leaving open how cultural assessment norms and institutional factors moderate the AI literacy–misconduct relationship (Brown & Wang, 2016; Zhao et al., 2020). The most psychometrically validated instrument for measuring academic misconduct is the McCabe-ICAI Academic Misconduct Instrument (MIAMI; Rettinger et al., 2024), which assesses three behavioral factors—Collusion, Misuse of Resources, and Fraud/Contract Cheating—validated in large-scale multinational samples and supplemented with AI-specific misconduct items in recent adaptations (Perry et al., 2026; Stephens et al., 2024).

2.3. Conceptions of Assessment (CoA)
Conceptions of assessment (CoA) refer to the beliefs students hold about the purposes, nature, and consequences of educational evaluation practices they encounter during their studies (Brown, 2011, 2021). These beliefs function as cognitive-motivational filters that mediate between the objective features of an assessment task and a student's behavioral response: the same assignment can elicit very different reactions depending on whether the student views it as a genuine opportunity to develop and demonstrate authentic learning or as an arbitrary, irrelevant, or threatening hurdle to be circumvented by any available means (Brown, 2021; Brown & Wang, 2016).

Scholars have approached conceptions of assessment from multiple perspectives. Brown's (2011, 2021) Self-Regulation Model draws on Boekaerts' (1996) dual-pathway framework to identify two broad self-regulatory orientations: an adaptive growth pathway, in which assessment is treated as a resource for learning improvement, and a maladaptive ego-protective pathway, in which assessment is viewed as irrelevant or externally imposed to shield self-worth from the threat of poor performance. Students who endorse growth-oriented conceptions exhibit greater metacognitive engagement, higher self-efficacy, and better academic performance, while those who endorse ego-protective conceptions are more prone to disengagement and avoidance (Brown, 2021; Zhao et al., 2020). A systematic review of 22 higher education studies spanning 11 jurisdictions confirmed this pattern consistently (Brown, 2021). In the Chinese higher education context, Brown and Wang (2016) developed the culturally validated Chinese Student Conceptions of Assessment (C-SCoA) inventory, identifying eight culturally grounded belief factors—including Societal Uses, Class Benefits, Accuracy, Teacher Use, Family Effects, and Competition—and demonstrating that jurisdictional and institutional factors, rather than shared cultural values alone, explained the most variation in how students conceptualized the purpose of assessment.

Despite strong evidence linking assessment conceptions to effort and motivation outcomes, significant gaps remain. First, their relationship to integrity-specific behavior has not been empirically tested; specifically, whether maladaptive assessment conceptions supply the motivational premise from which students construct moral disengagement reasoning about unauthorized AI use (Brown, 2022). Second, no study has examined whether AI literacy shapes how students conceive of assessment in an AI-enriched environment, or whether such understandings promote more adaptive beliefs. Third, the role of assessment conceptions as a mediating variable between individual AI-related competencies and ethical academic behavior remains entirely untested.

Brown's (2011, 2021) SCoA inventory provides the most extensively validated framework for operationalizing this construct, distinguishing four superordinate beliefs: assessment improves teaching and learning (adaptive), assessment has socio-emotional benefits, assessment evaluates schools and individuals (external accountability), and assessment is irrelevant (maladaptive). In the proposed model, conceptions of assessment function as the first mediating variable: AI-literate students who better understand what assessment genuinely evaluates may develop more adaptive beliefs that reduce their motivation to engage in academic misconduct (Brown & Wang, 2016; Ng et al., 2021b).

2.4. Academic Integrity (AcInt)
Academic integrity refers to students' commitment to fundamental values of honesty, trust, fairness, respect, and responsibility in their academic work (Stephens, 2019). It is most usefully understood not as the simple absence of misconduct but as a positive motivational orientation—a set of moral beliefs and self-regulatory dispositions that determine whether a student chooses to engage authentically with academic demands, including decisions about legitimate versus unauthorized AI use (Perry et al., 2026; Stephens, 2018).

Scholars have approached academic integrity from complementary theoretical perspectives. Rest's (1999) Four-Component Model of Moral Functioning is the most influential framework, decomposing ethical academic action into four components: moral sensitivity (recognizing that a situation has a moral dimension), moral judgment (determining the right course of action), moral motivation (prioritizing integrity values over competing self-interests such as grade pressure), and moral character (the self-regulatory capacity to act honestly despite obstacles). Critically, the model predicts that knowing the right thing to do is necessary but not sufficient to produce ethical behavior. Stephens (2018) confirmed this empirically, finding that moral judgment had no significant direct effect on academic dishonesty; instead, the effect operated entirely through moral disengagement—self-exculpatory rationalizations that allow students to cheat while privately acknowledging that doing so is wrong. Perry et al. (2026) replicated this pattern across 2,329 North American undergraduates, finding that moral attitudes—combining moral unacceptability judgments with resistance to moral disengagement—were the most robust predictors of academic misconduct across five competing theoretical models.

Significant gaps remain. The relationship between AI literacy and academic integrity has not been empirically examined; specifically, whether AI literacy's ethics dimension (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021b) functions analogously to moral judgment in Rest's (1999) model—necessary but insufficient on its own to prevent dishonest behavior. Additionally, no study has tested whether conceptions of assessment predict academic integrity values, despite the theoretical link between ego-protective assessment beliefs and moral disengagement (Brown, 2021; Stephens, 2018). Existing moral attitude measures have also not been widely adapted for AI-specific misconduct scenarios, limiting their applicability to the GenAI context (Stephens et al., 2024). The Moral Attitudes scale validated by Perry et al. (2026) and Stephens et al. (2024) provides the most psychometrically rigorous operationalization of academic integrity currently available, combining a Moral Unacceptability subscale (5 items) and a Moral Disengagement subscale (7 items, reverse-scored). In the proposed model, academic integrity functions as the second mediating variable, positioned downstream of conceptions of assessment, through which adaptive assessment beliefs translate into reduced academic misconduct.

2.5. Brown's Self-Regulation Model and Rest's Four-Component Model
This study's theory is rooted in two fundamental frameworks that jointly explain the cognitive and moral pathways linking AI literacy to academic misconduct: Brown's (2011, 2021) Self-Regulation Model of Conceptions of Assessment (SCoA Model) and Rest's (1999) Four-Component Model of Moral Functioning. Together, these provide the theoretical architecture for the proposed serial mediation model.

The SCoA Model has been used to explain how students' assessment beliefs shape their self-regulatory responses to evaluative demands across diverse cultural and institutional contexts (Brown, 2011, 2021; Brown & Wang, 2016). In contrast, the Four-Component Model examines the moral-psychological processes through which personal values are—or fail to be—translated into ethical academic behavior (Rest, 1999), and has been regarded as the most important complement to cognitive-motivational models of student conduct (Stephens, 2018). The SCoA Model accounts for the motivational and cognitive factors pertaining to assessment from students' perspectives, whereas the Four-Component Model accounts for students' moral knowledge, motivation, and self-regulatory character required to make and follow through on honest academic decisions. The SCoA Model has been widely validated in higher education research because it explains substantial variance in students' academic self-regulation across more than 22 studies and 11 jurisdictions (Brown, 2021), and the Four-Component Model has consistently produced the most accurate predictions of academic (dis)honest behavior across independent national samples (Perry et al., 2026; Stephens, 2018).

The SCoA Model emphasizes two key pathways: (1) the growth pathway (adaptive Conceptions of Assessment), which in the present study corresponds to the first mediating mechanism that channels AI literacy's influence toward honest academic engagement; and (2) the ego-protective pathway (maladaptive Conceptions of Assessment), which corresponds to the mechanism through which students rationalize dishonest AI use when they view assessment as irrelevant or externally imposed (Brown, 2021). The Four-Component Model emphasizes four components: moral sensitivity and moral judgment—corresponding to AI Literacy's ethics dimension, which equips students to recognize and evaluate the ethical implications of AI use; moral motivation—corresponding to Academic Integrity, the disposition to prioritize honest academic work over self-interested shortcuts; and moral character—corresponding to the behavioral suppression of Academic Misconduct even when temptation is present (Rest, 1999).

This study adopted the SCoA Model as the theoretical basis for the first mediating pathway (AI Literacy → Conceptions of Assessment → Academic Integrity) and the Four-Component Model as the theoretical basis for the second mediating pathway (Academic Integrity → Academic Misconduct). The SCoA Model provides the motivational rationale for why AI literacy should shape how students interpret the meaning and purpose of their assessments, while the Four-Component Model explains why that interpretation subsequently shapes moral motivation and self-regulation in academic conduct decisions. According to these two integrated frameworks, AI Literacy has a significant impact on Academic Misconduct through the sequential mediation of Conceptions of Assessment and Academic Integrity (Brown, 2011, 2021; Rest, 1999; Stephens, 2018).

2.6. The Relationship Between AI Literacy and Academic Misconduct: The Mediating Role of Conceptions of Assessment and Academic Integrity
First, AI literacy is a crucial factor affecting students' academic conduct in AI-enriched environments (Gruenhagen et al., 2024; Lund et al., 2025). Recent studies have highlighted the significant role of AI literacy's ethics dimension in shaping responsible AI use in educational settings (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng et al., 2021b; Schiavo et al., 2024). Students with higher AI literacy possess a more accurate understanding of the capabilities and ethical boundaries of GenAI tools, making them better equipped to distinguish legitimate AI assistance from unauthorized substitution of their own academic work (Lund et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023). Gruenhagen et al. (2024) found that students who did not understand the ethical implications of AI use for assessment were substantially more likely to report using AI tools dishonestly. This reflects the significant influence of AI literacy levels on academic misconduct engagement (Perry et al., 2026; Stephens et al., 2024).

Second, AI literacy significantly shapes students' conceptions of assessment and their academic integrity. Higher levels of AI literacy facilitate a greater understanding of what assessment genuinely measures—authentic knowledge and competence rather than AI-assisted performance—which in turn promotes improvement-oriented rather than irrelevance-oriented conceptions of assessment (Brown, 2021; Ng et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2023). Adaptive conceptions of assessment reduce students' motivation to construct the moral disengagement narratives—such as "this assignment doesn't measure anything real" or "using AI doesn't hurt anyone"—that underpin dishonest AI use (Brown & Wang, 2016; Stephens, 2018). Similarly, AI literacy is expected to promote academic integrity directly: if the AI ethics dimension of AI literacy functions analogously to moral sensitivity in Rest's (1999) model, enabling students to recognize when AI use constitutes dishonesty, then higher AI literacy should strengthen students' moral condemnation of academic dishonesty and their resistance to rationalizing it (Ng et al., 2021b; Perry et al., 2026). Perry et al. (2026) and Stephens et al. (2024) consistently found that stronger moral attitudes were the most robust personal predictors of reduced academic misconduct across independent national samples.

Third, conceptions of assessment directly influence academic integrity, and academic integrity directly reduces academic misconduct. Students who view assessment as a meaningful improvement opportunity have greater intrinsic motivation to engage honestly with academic work and less motivational basis for constructing disengagement rationalizations about AI use (Brown, 2021; Rest, 1999). Brown and Wang (2016) demonstrated that adaptive assessment beliefs consistently predict more constructive self-regulatory academic behavior. Furthermore, academic integrity—operationalized through moral unacceptability judgments and resistance to moral disengagement—is the proximal person-level predictor of reduced misconduct, with Stephens (2018) demonstrating that this moral self-regulation fully accounts for the judgment-to-behavior pathway. Previous research confirms that conceptions of assessment and academic integrity play complementary and sequential mediating roles between individual-difference antecedents and academic misconduct (Brown, 2021; Rest, 1999; Stephens, 2018). Thus, we hypothesize the following:

　　H1. AI Literacy has a significant negative effect on Academic Misconduct.
　　H2. AI Literacy has a significant positive effect on Conceptions of Assessment.
　　H3. AI Literacy has a significant positive effect on Academic Integrity.
　　H4. Conceptions of Assessment has a significant positive effect on Academic Integrity.
　　H5. Academic Integrity has a significant negative effect on Academic Misconduct.
　　H6. Conceptions of Assessment has a significant negative effect on Academic Misconduct.
　　H7. Conceptions of Assessment mediates the effect of AI Literacy on Academic Integrity.
　　H8. Conceptions of Assessment mediates the effect of AI Literacy on Academic Misconduct.
　　H9. Academic Integrity mediates the effect of AI Literacy on Academic Misconduct.

Based on Brown's (2011) Self-Regulation Model of Conceptions of Assessment and Rest's (1999) Four-Component Model of Moral Functioning, this study introduces the independent variable (AI Literacy), mediating variables (Conceptions of Assessment and Academic Integrity), and dependent variable (Academic Misconduct) to propose a serial mediation research model. 3. Methodology

3.1 Sample
In this study, G*Power 3.1 was used to assess the sample size (Kang, 2021). Power analysis "A-priori: Compute required sample size-given alpha, power and effect size" was selected for this research, where the effect size was fixed at 0.15 alpha, error probability 0.05, and power (1-b error probability) was set at 0.95. Four predictors were included in this study. 

3.2. Demographics
A total of X university students from various universities in China willingly participated in this study.

3.3. Measures
To measure the level of teachers’ AI literacy, Zhao et al.’s (2022) scale was AI literacy. The scale included 20 items that sought to collect information on four separate dimensions of teachers’ AI literacy: Knowing and Understanding AI (KUAI), Applying AI (AAI), Evaluating AI Application (EAIA), and AI Ethics (AIE).

To measure Conceptions of Assessment, this study adopted the Chinese Student Conceptions of Assessment (C-SCoA) inventory developed and validated by Brown and Wang (2016) specifically for Chinese university students. The inventory comprises 33 items assessing eight culturally grounded assessment belief factors using a six-point positively packed agreement scale (1 = Strongly Disagree to 6 = Strongly Agree). The improvement-oriented subscales (Class Benefits, Teacher Use, and Accuracy) serve as the primary indicators of adaptive conceptions of assessment in the PLS-SEM analysis.

To measure Academic Integrity, this study adopted the Moral Attitudes scale validated by Perry et al. (2026) and Stephens et al. (2024), comprising two subscales: Moral Unacceptability (5 items rated on a 5-point scale from 0 = Not at all morally/ethically wrong to 4 = Completely morally/ethically wrong) and Moral Disengagement (7 items on a 5-point Likert scale, reverse-scored for the integrity composite). Items were adapted to include AI-specific academic scenarios reflecting current GenAI use contexts. Higher composite scores indicate stronger academic integrity.

To measure Academic Misconduct, this study adopted an adapted version of the McCabe-ICAI Academic Misconduct Instrument (MIAMI; Rettinger et al., 2024), as used in Perry et al. (2026) and Stephens et al. (2024). The 17-item instrument assesses three factors—Collusion, Misuse of Resources, and Fraud/Contract Cheating—on a five-point frequency scale (0 = Never to 4 = 11 or more times), supplemented with three AI-specific items capturing AI-facilitated misconduct (e.g., using a GenAI tool to generate academic work and submitting it as one's own without disclosure). All items were translated into Mandarin Chinese using a forward-backward translation procedure with bilingual researchers.

3.4. Data collection
Data were collected through an anonymous online survey administered via the Wenjuanxing platform. Participants were recruited using convenience and snowball sampling via social media channels. All participants provided informed consent prior to completing the survey. Responses were kept fully anonymous, and participation was voluntary. 

3.5. Data Analysis Procedure
We used a quantitative method to test the study hypotheses and analyzed the data using partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM) with SmartPLS 4.1.0.0. PLS-SEM is suitable for studying complex multiple indicators and constructing hypothesis analyses that can tolerate fewer than 500 study samples. It can also be used to study structural models with causal effects and predictors of multiple variables (Hair et al., 2022).

The PLS-SEM analysis consisted of two phases: (1) assessment of the reflective measurement model and (2) assessment of the structural model. The measurement model assessed the reliability and validity of the scales and study models to ensure convergent and discriminant validity (Hair et al., 2022). For the mediation analysis, the bootstrap method was used to detect indirect effects (Hayes, 2009).We used the Heterotrait-monotrait ratio of correlation (HTMT) to test for discriminant validity (Henseler et al., 2015). In addition, the structural model reported a path analysis, including direct, indirect, and mediating effects, to demonstrate the structural relationship between the hypothesized model variables and the significance of the effects.
