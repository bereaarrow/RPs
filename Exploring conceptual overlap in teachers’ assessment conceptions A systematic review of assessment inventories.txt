1. Introduction
Policies  that  expect  teachers  to  participate  in  assessment  (e.g.,classroom  assessment,  assessment  for learning,  qualifications  assessment,  etc.)  require  an  understanding  of  how  teachers  think  about  the nature  and  purpose  of assessment.  Systematic  survey research  into teachers   assessment  beliefs and  atti- tudes  has proliferated  since Brown s (2004) Teacher Conceptions  of Assessment  inventory.  However, it is possible  that  the  different  inventories  suffer from terminological  overlap, especially because  the  different policy language  around  the  educational  uses  of assessment  seems  to  imply different  constructs.  While there  are  numerous  self-report  inventories  that  measure  what  teachers  believe  and  how  they  act  in assessment,  there  has  been  little work comparing  these  inventories  across  different  research  strands  or contexts.  It remains  unclear  how  the  constructs  used  in  different  inventories  relate  to  each  other,  or whether  they  reflect  similar or distinct  ideas.  The aim  of this  review  is to  map  the  range  of constructs used  in these  inventories  and  examine  the  extent  to which they  overlap  or differ. A systematic  review is therefore  needed  to  summarize  what  has  been  measured  so  far and  to  identify  areas  of similarity and difference  across  inventories.  Thus,  a  systematic  evaluation  of  the  terminology  is  needed  to  identify potential  jingle-jangle  issues in the  field. This paper  contributes  to  the  field by providing  a detailed  sys- tematic  search  and  content  analysis of inventories  developed  since 2000 to  describe  how  teachers  think and  feel about  assessment.

1.1. Teacher conceptions  of assessment
The role  of assessment  in education  has  evolved  significantly  over  the  past  several  decades.  Although traditionally  associated  with  summative  certification  and  external  accountability,  assessment  is  now increasingly  conceptualized  as a formative  process  aimed  at guiding  instruction,  enhancing  learning  and advancing  equity  across  diverse  educational  contexts  (Black & Wiliam, 2018; Klenowski, 2011). This shift reflects broader  pedagogical  and  policy trends  that  emphasize  student  agency,  responsive  teaching  and data-informed  decision-making.  In this context,  growing  attention  has  been  directed  toward  how  teach- ers  conceptualize  assessment,  specifically  their  underlying  beliefs  about  its  purposes,  principles  and expected  standards  of achievement  (Brown, 2004). These  beliefs, often  referred  to  as teachers   concep - tions  of assessment  (TCoA), are  not  superficial preferences;  they  are  rooted  in deeply  held  professional values and  play a central  role in shaping  how  assessment  is interpreted  and  enacted  in practice  (Barnes et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2009; Remesal, 2007).
As part  of teachers   broader  belief systems, conceptions  of assessment  (TCoA) operate  at the  intersec- tion  of individual cognition  and  sociocultural  influence.  They shape  how  teachers  make  sense  of assess- ment  tools,  frame  instructional  decisions  and  interact  with  policy  mandates  (Barnes  et  al., 2015). A substantial  body  of research  has demonstrated  that  TCoA are closely associated  with classroom  practices such  as formative  questioning,  feedback  use,  grading  strategies  and  differentiated  instruction  (Brown & Harris, 2009; DeLuca et al., 2019; Remesal, 2011; Xu & Brown, 2016). Accordingly, understanding  teachers  assessment  conceptions  is increasingly  regarded  as essential  for enhancing  assessment  literacy, empow - ering teacher  agency  and  promoting  pedagogical  improvement.
Over the  past  two  decades,  a number  of inventories  have  been  developed  to  capture  teachers   con- ceptions  of  assessment  (TCoA), in  response  to  the  increasing  diversification  of  assessment  policies (Brown, 2004; Brown et al., 2011; Brown & Remesal, 2012; DeLuca et al., 2016). Among the  most  promin- ent  are  the  Teachers   Conceptions  of  Assessment  Inventory  (TCoA-III; Brown,  2004),  the  Qualitative Model  of  Conceptions  of  Assessment  (QMCoA; Brown  &  Remesal,  2017)  and  the  Approaches  to Classroom  Assessment  Inventory  (ACAI; DeLuca et  al., 2016). Each inventory  offers a distinct  theoretical perspective  on TCoA.
The TCoA-III and  its abridged  and  culturally adapted  versions  (e.g., C-TCoA; Brown et  al., 2011) focus primarily  on  the  nature  and  purposes  of  assessment,  distinguishing  constructs  such  as  improvement, accountability  and  irrelevance.  In  contrast,  the  QMCoA adopts  a  bifactor  model  that  conceptualizes assessment  beliefs along  a continuum  from formative  regulation  to  summative  accreditation,  emphasiz - ing  their  conceptual  orientations  toward  assessment  functions  (e.g.,  teaching,  learning,  certification, accounting).  The ACAI, meanwhile,  integrates  TCoA within  a broader  framework  of assessment  literacy, categorizing  teachers   beliefs and  practices  across  constructs  such  as purpose,  process,  fairness and  the- ory. Thus, while the  TCoA-III and  QMCoA emphasize  what  teachers  believe  assessment  is for, the  ACAI reflects  how  those  beliefs  are  enacted  in  classroom  practice  and  aligned  with  principles  of  effective assessment.  These  conceptual  differences  underline  the  importance  of understanding  not  only  what  is being  measured,  but  also how  divergent  frameworks  may influence  the  interpretation  and  application  of TCoA in research  and  policy.
Despite  their  contributions,  existing  inventories  of  teachers   conceptions  of  assessment  (TCoA) fre- quently  exhibit  conceptual  ambiguities  and  construct  overlap.  Constructs  such  as formative  assessment and  assessment  for learning  are sometimes  used  interchangeably,  even  though  they  reflect distinct  the- oretical  orientations  — one  emphasizing  instructional  responsiveness,  the  other  prioritizing  student agency  and  learning  outcomes  (Newton,  2007). Similarly, dimensions  such  as  accountability  or  grading may conflate  external  policy mandates  with  internal  beliefs, making  it unclear  whether  a construct  cap- tures  normative  orientations,  institutional  pressures,  or  classroom  behaviors.  In some  cases,  belief-ori- ented  constructs  are  merged  with  pedagogical  practices  (e.g., questioning,  feedback)  or  compressed alongside  evaluative  outcomes  (e.g.,  fairness,  student  achievement)  without  adequate  theoretical  or operational  justification (Brown & Remesal, 2017).
These  issues  exemplify  the  jingle-jangle  fallacy  (Kelley, 1927): conceptually  distinct  constructs  are sometimes  given  the  same  term  (i.e., jingle), while similar constructs  are  labeled  differently  (i.e., jangle). For instance,  while  the  TCoA-III model  employs  the  construct  improvement  and  QMCoA uses  teaching and  learning,  both  tap  into  assessment s  formative  purpose.  Likewise, CoA-III separates  student  and school  accountability,  whereas  QMCoA aggregates  these  under  a broader  certification  factor, suggesting potential  overlap  masked  by terminological  variation.
A further  complication  involves conceptual  slippage  between  psychological  beliefs and  structural  con- straints.  Some  inventories  conflate  internal  belief  systems  with  external  systemic  requirements,  such  as standardized  testing  or institutional  mandates.  Terms like formative  assessment  may  be  operationalized as  observable  practices  rather  than  as  attitudinal  constructs,  while  accountability  may  encompass internal  responsibility, professional  norms, or compliance  with top-down  policy pressures.  These ambigu - ities limit construct  interpretability  and  undermine  the  utility of TCoA inventories  for informing  teacher development,  cross-study  comparisons,  or evidence-based  policy design.
While individual  studies  have  provided  valuable  insights  into  how  teachers  think  about  assessment, few have  examined  the  broader  conceptual  terrain  in a systematic  way. Existing reviews have  generally focused  on  the  development  or validation  of a single  inventory  (Brown et  al., 2011) or explored  narrow conceptual  areas  such  as  formative  assessment  (Yan & Pastore,  2022) or  assessment  literacy  (DeLuca et  al., 2016). What remains  missing  is a field-level synthesis  that  maps  how  core  constructs  are  defined, differentiated  and  operationalized  across  major  TCoA inventories.  Such  a synthesis  is clearly needed  to address  theoretical  fragmentation,  clarify conceptual  boundaries  and  inform  the  next  generation  of assessment  belief measures.
1.2. Theoretical framework for cross-inventory conceptual  comparison
This study  uses  construct  validity theory  (Cronbach  & Meehl, 1955) as the  basis  for examining  whether the  constructs  embedded  in different  inventories  refer to  the  same  underlying  attributes.  From this per- spective,  a construct  is defined  not  by its label but  by the  clarity, coherence  and  theoretical  justification of its definition.  In other  words,  if constructs  are  intended  to  represent  different  aspects  of assessment, their  definitions  should  be  clearly  distinguishable;  conversely,  when  definitions  converge,  they  may plausibly  reflect  shared  underlying  features  (Lawson & Robins, 2021). Within this  framework,  examining how  different  inventories  define  their  constructs  becomes  essential  for evaluating  whether  they  refer to shared  or distinct  conceptual  domains.
This issue  becomes  particularly  salient  in light  of the  well-known  jingle—jangle problem:  researchers sometimes  assume  similarity simply  because  two  constructs  share  a  name,  or, conversely,  treat  nearly identical  constructs  as different  merely  because  they  are  labeled  differently  (Fischer et  al., 2023; Kelley, 1927). Both tendencies  blur  conceptual  distinctions  and  complicate  attempts  to  compare  inventories  in a  theoretically  meaningful  way. Accordingly, the  present  study  conducts  a  cross-inventory  conceptual analysis  that  examines  how  each  inventory  articulates,  differentiates  and  organizes  its  constructs. Overlap  in definitions  may  reflect  shared  conceptual  ground,  whereas  inconsistency  or ambiguity  high- lights areas  of misalignment.
1.3. Purpose of the study
To address  these  concerns,  the  present  study  conducts  a systematic  review and  thematic  synthesis  of 26 empirical  studies  published  between  2000  and  2025  that  that  report  on  TCoA inventories.  Specifically, this  study  aims  to: (1) map  the  core  constructs  embedded  in these  inventories  and  examine  how  they are  defined  and  differentiated;  (2) identify  patterns  of conceptual  convergence  and  divergence;  and  (3) evaluate  the  implications  of these  patterns  for construct  validity, cross-cultural  applicability  and  theoret - ical development.
By synthesizing  findings  across  contexts  and  inventories,  this  review  seeks  to  clarify the  conceptual landscape  of teachers   assessment  conceptions.  The results  are  intended  to  inform  the  design  of future inventories,  support  cross-cultural  research  on  assessment  beliefs  and  contribute  to  a  more  integrated and  theoretically  grounded  body  of knowledge  in educational  assessment.
2. Method
This review followed  PRISMA guidelines  (Page et  al., 2021) to  systematically  synthesize  empirical  studies on teachers   conceptions  of assessment  (TCoA).
2.1. Search strategy
A comprehensive  systematic  search  was conducted  to  identify empirical studies  that  investigated  teach- ers   conceptions  of  assessment  through  inventories,  surveys,  or  quantitative  inventories.  The  primary search  was performed  across  three  major  databases  (ERIC, Web of Science  and  Scopus) using  combina - tions of keywords such as  assessment ,  teacher  conception ,  inventory ,  belief , and  attitude .  Synonyms and  related  terms  were  included  to  account  for  terminological  variation  across  disciplines.  Table  1 presents  the  search  strings  used  across databases.
To ensure  comprehensiveness,  a  supplementary  citation  search  was  conducted  in  the  Dimensions open  access  database  (Stahlschmidt  & Stephen,  2020). Brown et  al. (2011) was used  as a starting  point and  both  backward  and  forward  citation  tracing  was  undertaken  to  identify  relevant  studies  beyond those  retrieved  through  database  searches.  The search  was restricted  to peer-reviewed  articles published between  2000  and  2025  to  ensure  alignment  with  contemporary  assessment  practices  and  inventory development.  A total  of 48 sources  were found  this way.
2.2. Eligibility criteria and study selection
Studies  were  included  if they  met  the  following criteria: (1) published  between  2000 and  2025; (2) writ- ten  in English; (3) involved  in-service  primary  or  secondary  school  teachers;  (4) adopted  a  quantitative methodology;  and  (5) employed  a  structured  inventory  to  assess  teachers   conceptions  of assessment. Studies  were  excluded  if they:  (1) were  published  prior  to  2000; (2) were  written  in  languages  other than  English; (3) focused  on  non-school-based  settings  (eg preschools,  higher  education,  or special  edu- cation);  (4) used  qualitative  methods  only  or  (5) addressed  constructs  unrelated  to  teacher  assessment beliefs, such as student  evaluations,  performance  appraisal, or technology-based  assessments.
2.3. Screening
The initial database  search  identified  1,072 records  (ERIC à 261, Web of Science  à 318, Scopus  à 445, Dimensions  à 48). After removing  324 duplicates  through  EndNote s automated  matching  (title, author and  DOI fields) and  subsequent  manual  checking, 748 unique  records  remained.
Title and  abstract  screening  followed  the  pre-established  eligibility criteria, including  publication  year, language,  target  population  (teachers)  and  article  type.  Relevance  to  teachers   conceptions  of  assess- ment  was also assessed  at this stage.  Of the  screened  records, 401 did not  examine  teachers   assessment conceptions  and  were  therefore  excluded.  These studies  focused  on  topics  such  as assessment  technol - ogy  or  digital  tools,  assessment  training  programs,  teacher  or  student  competence,  general  pedagogy, or teacher  beliefs  unrelated  to  assessment.  A further  21 studies  were  excluded  because  they  were  not published  in English and  30 could  not  be  retrieved  in full text.  In total,  296  studies  were  retained  for full-text review. During full-text screening,  239 studies  were  excluded  based  on  the  eligibility criteria. A total  of 131 were  conducted  outside  primary or secondary  education  and  therefore  did not  fit the  focus of this  review. 2.4. Inter-rater reliability
To ensure  the  reliability of study  inclusion, 10% of the  full-text articles were  independently  assessed  by two  trained  reviewers  with  expertise  in  educational  assessment.  Inter-rater  agreement  was  calculated using  Cohen s  kappa,  yielding  a coefficient  of 0.80, indicating  strong  reliability (Cohen, 1960). The lead researcher  also independently  reviewed  the  same  subset.  Agreement  with Reviewer 1 remained  at  0.80, while agreement  with Reviewer 2 was 0.63, reflecting  moderate  to substantial  consistency.  Discrepancies primarily  related  to  the  inclusion  of  preservice  teachers  and  teachers  in  non-public  school  settings. Following consensus  discussions,  preservice  teachers  without  practical  experience  were  excluded.  All in- service primary and  secondary  school teachers  were retained,  regardless  of institutional  type.
2.5. Data extraction and analysis
The final dataset  comprised  26 distinct  inventories  designed  to measure  teachers   conceptions  of assess- ment  across diverse cultural and  policy contexts.  These inventories  formed  the  basis for subsequent  con- struct  mapping,  thematic  analysis  and  comparative  synthesis.  Table  2 summarizes  26  inventories  of teachers   assessment  conceptions  and  practices,  each  reflecting  a  distinct  emphasis  in the  TCoA litera- ture  and  identified  by a numeric  ID.
The inventories  can  be  grouped  into  three  major  emphases.  The first emphasis,  General  Conceptions of Assessment,  is represented  by eight  inventories  (#1, #2, #5, #6, #7, #8, #23). These inventories  examine teachers   overarching  beliefs  about  the  nature,  purposes  and  functions  of  assessment.  They  typically emphasize  broad  philosophical  orientations  such  as  improvement  versus  accountability  or  usefulness versus  irrelevance,  without  reference  to  specific  instructional  practices  or  classroom  enactment.  The second  emphasis,  Conceptions  of  Formative  Assessment  and  Assessment  for  Learning  (AfL), includes four  inventories  (#3, #9,  #16,  #21).  These  inventories  focus  on  teachers   understanding  of  formative assessment  principles  and  their intended  role in supporting  student  learning.  Although  more  pedagogic - ally oriented  than  general  belief inventories,  they  tend  to capture  stated  intentions  or perceptions  rather than  systematically examining  enacted  classroom  practices.
The third  emphasis,  Classroom  Assessment  Alignment:  Beliefs, Attitudes,  and  Practices, is reflected  in 11 inventories  (#4, #10, #11, #13, #15, #17, #18, #19, #20, #22, #24). These inventories  explicitly examine the  alignment  between  teachers   stated  beliefs and  their  reported  assessment  practices.  They commonly incorporate  behavioral  indicators  such as assessment  methods,  classroom  activities, task formats, student involvement,  feedback  provision and  documentation  strategies.  Notably, several more  recent  instruments (#12, #25, #26) move  beyond  the  belief—practice alignment  framework  by  focusing  on  normative  and fairness-related  aspects  of  classroom  assessment.  This trend  suggests  a  gradual  broadening  of  TCoA measurement  beyond  traditional  belief and  practice  constructs.

2.6.1. Phase I: code and checklist development
Initial coding  began  by identifying  all constructs  explicitly named  or defined  across  the  included  inven- tories.  A supervisor  and  the  lead  researcher  then  independently  reviewed  each  construct  and  docu- mented  its  defining  features.  For  example,  the  construct  Improvement,  as  described  by  Brown  et  al. (2011), states  that  assessment  improves  students   learning  and  the  quality  of  teaching    this  view [includes]  a range  of techniques,  including  informal  teacher-based  intuitive  judgment  as well as formal assessment  tools   (p.  317).  Based  on  this  definition,  key  features  such  as  improvement  of  student achievement,  instructional  purposes  and  assessment  methods  were  extracted.  The independently  devel- oped  checklists  were  subsequently  compared  and  synthesized  into  a  unified  set  of  features,  which served  as a draft coding  sheet  for further  analysis.
2.6.2. Phase II: thematic categorization

For instance,  features  related  to  instructional  planning,  pedagogical  decision-making  and teaching  enhancement  were  clustered  under  the  category  Assessment  Supports  Improved  Teaching  and Instruction.

2.6.3. Phase III: validation and refinement
Building on  the  categories  developed  in Phase  II, this  phase  aimed  to  establish  the  reliability and  con- sistency  of construct  classification. Categorization  was  independently  conducted  by the  lead  researcher and  a second  reviewer  with five years of teaching  experience  and  current  doctoral-level  training  in edu- cation. To enhance  analytic rigor, 30% of the  items  were  randomly  selected  for double  coding,  using  the construct  definitions  established  in the  previous  phase  as a reference  point.  Inter-rater  reliability was cal- culated  using  Cohen s kappa,  yielding a coefficient  of 0.86, which indicates  a high  level of agreement  (j 
  0.81; Landis & Koch, 1977).
Most  discrepancies  arose  at  the  boundary  between  the  categories  Teaching  Support  and  Informal Assessment  Activity. For  instance,  one  construct  stated:  The  implementation  of  formative  strategies includes  questioning  techniques,  descriptive  (non-graded)  feedback,  and  instructional  modifications based  on student  needs(Young  & Jackman, 2014, p. 401). The second  reviewer categorized  this construct as Teaching  Support,  emphasizing  its instructional  purpose.  In contrast,  the  lead  researcher  coded  it as Informal Assessment  Activity, focusing  on  the  embedded  nature  of assessment  practices  within the  flow of  classroom  interaction.  Following  discussion,  both  reviewers  reached  consensus  that  the  construct aligned  more  closely with Informal Assessment  Activity, and  the  classification was revised accordingly.
3. Results
3.1. Overview
This review  identified  seven  thematic  categories  that  capture  the  diverse  ways in which  teachers   con- ceptions  of assessment  have  been  operationalized  across  26  inventories.  These  categories  reflect  both the  intended  purposes  of assessment  (e.g., supporting  teaching  and  learning,  enforcing  accountability, or  viewing  assessment  as  irrelevant  or  harmful)  and  its  observed  formats  (e.g.,  formal  vs.  informal activity). Despite  originating  from distinct  educational  and  cultural  contexts,  many  inventories  converge on similar core ideas, though  often  described  using  different  terminologies  or with ambiguous  construct boundaries.  The  following  sections  present  each  theme  in  detail,  with  representative  definitions  and illustrative references.
3.2. Assessment  supports and improves teaching  and instruction
This category  highlights  the  significant  role  assessment  can  play  in enhancing  the  quality  of teaching and  instruction  (Table  4).  Although  labeled  differently,  such  as  improvement,  facilitation,  formative assessment,  or  teaching,  these  constructs  share  a  strong  conceptual  alignment,  consistently  portraying assessment  as  a  central  tool  for pedagogical  refinement.  One  inventory  (Alonzo et  al., 2021) indicates that  assessment  can  help  identify  areas  for  teachers   professional  development,  ultimately  aimed  at improving  instructional  effectiveness.
Assessment  is a crucial process  where  teachers  employ  various tools  to gather  information  about  stu- dents   learning,  which  they  use  to  evaluate  the  quality  and  effectiveness  of  their  instruction  (Brown, 2004; Brown & Gao, 2015; Brown et  al., 2011; Wu, 2017). By integrating  different  assessment  techniques, teachers  can  determine  how  well their  methods  support  student  understanding  and  identify  areas  for improvement  in their teaching  practice.  While assessment  tools can be  helpful, these  constructs  assume - that  teachers  have  the  skills to  correctly  implement,  interpret  and  apply  the  data.  If the  assessment results  are  misunderstood,  it  could  lead  to  adjustments  in  teaching  methods  which  may  not  improve teaching  effectiveness  (Brown et  al., 2011). Assessment  provides  teachers  with  valid  information  that enables  them  to  diagnose  students   needs,  which  in turn  supports  the  continuous  refinement  of their instructional  practices.
Among  the  inventories,  the  term  formative  assessment   is prevalent  with  a  focus  on  the  ongoing gathering  of  information  to  adjust  teaching  strategies  and  enhance  instructional  effectiveness  (Martin et al., 2022; Sach, 2012; Veugen et al., 2021; Zeng & Huang, 2021). Through  this reflective process,  teach- ers  gain  a  deeper  understanding  of  their  instructional  approaches  and  can  make  informed  decisions about  instructional  strategies  and  curriculum  design  (Brown & Remesal,  2017; Min & So, 2024; Zhao et  al., 2018). This process  of  continuous  improvement  helps  refine  teaching  practices  and  ultimately enhances  student  learning  outcomes.  Furthermore,  feedback  acts as a reflection  of the  teacher s  actions, facilitating  further  improvements  in  teaching.  Initially, teachers  gather  student  information  and  upon interpreting  this  data,  they  make  decisions  regarding  the  planning  of teaching  activities. Subsequently, feedback  guides  the  next  steps  in instructional  delivery, allowing for continuous  improvement  and  adap- tation  to students   needs  (Alonzo et al., 2021; Coombs  et al., 2022; Zeng & Huang, 2021).
In summary,  this  category  underscores  the  crucial role  of using  assessment  information  to  enhance teaching  and  instructional  practices.  This is evident  from  the  consistent  use  of terms  such  as improve- ment  of  instruction  and  improvement  of  teaching,  as  extracted  from  the  descriptions  of  various  con- structs  in Table 4. These  terms  reinforce  the  benefits  of assessment  in the  teaching  process.  However, variations  in descriptions  within  this  category  reflect  the  complexity  of teaching,  indicating  that  assess- ment  information  can be used  in different  ways to tailor and  improve  instruction  (Brown, 2008).
3.3. Assessment  supports and improves students   learning
This category  highlights  assessment s  significant  role  in  supporting  and  improving  students   learning and  development  (Table  5). Within  the  constructs,  there  is  strong  conceptual  alignment  in  viewing assessment  not  merely as a means  of evaluating  achievement,  but  as a central  pedagogical  process  that improves  the  quality of students   learning  and  development.
According  to  Brown (2004) and  Brown et  al. (2011), the  construct  of  improvement   emphasizes  the importance  of high-quality  assessment  information  for driving enhanced  learning.  A range  of assessment approaches  and  techniques  are employed  to evaluate  students   progress  and  performance,  allowing stu- dents  to gather  robust  and  valid information  (Brown, 2004). This concept  focuses  on the  value of assess- ment  outcomes,  specifically how  students  use  assessment  information  to  guide  subsequent  learning. 3.4. Assessment  holds students  accountable
Assessment  is perceived  as a way of recognizing  and  holding  students  to account  for their  learning  and achievement  (Table 6). This conception  includes  the  idea  that  assessments  are tools to measure  whether students  meet  required  academic  standards.  When  students  recognize  the  association  between  their efforts  and  their  assessment  results,  they  are  more  likely to  take  their  learning  responsibilities  seriously (Brown, 2004; Brown et  al., 2011). These assessments  predominantly  take  the  form of examinations  and tests,  which  require  students  to  demonstrate  whether  they  met  local and/or  national  curriculum  stand - ards. To achieve  academic  success, students  must  engage  deeply  with the  curriculum  and  prepare  thor- oughly  for  these  evaluations  (Brown & Gao,  2015). This active  engagement  not  only  improves  their understanding  but  also fosters a sense  of responsibility  and  accountability  for their learning.
Moreover, the  assignment  of grades  and  scores provides  teachers  (and others)  with concrete  evidence to  compare  and  rank students,  allowing them  to  distinguish  between  individuals based  on  their  level of understanding  and  proficiency. This comparative  function  reflects a shared  structural  feature  across  con- structs  such  as  norm-referencing  and  certification  of learning,  where  assessment  is used  to  determine relative  performance  within  a cohort  (Alonzo et  al., 2021; Brown & Remesal, 2017). Additionally, grades from  the  various  qualification  examinations  undertaken  by secondary-age  students  are  used  for gradu- ation  and/or  to gain entry  to higher  levels of education  (Brown, 2004; Brown et al., 2011).
In this  context,  examination-oriented  assessment  emphasizes  student  accountability  through  align- ment  with  high-stakes  testing  standards,  ensuring  that  classroom  assessments  are  structured  as prepar - ation  for externally  organized  examinations  (Brown & Gao, 2015; Wu, 2017). While these  constructs  may focus  on  different  outcomes,  such  as  awards,  certification,  or  selection,  they  converge  in  their  use  of assessment  to  formalize  student  responsibility  through  performance-based  decisions.  In a selective  sys- tem,  where  competition  is intense  and  the  criteria for advancement  are strict, such  assessments  become a driving force.
In parallel to  outcome-based  evaluation,  assessment  also serves  as a mechanism  for behavioral  regu- lation,  which  in turn  has  the  potential  to  reinforce  student  accountability. 3.5.1. Performance-based and control-oriented accountability
Assessment  serves as a tool to ensure  the  accountability  of teachers  and  schools  to various stakeholders, including  school  communities,  governments  and  society, by demonstrating  effectiveness  and  adherence to  required  standards  (Table  7). Similar to  student  accountability,  this  form  of accountability  relies  on assessment  data  to evaluate  performance  against  externally defined  expectations.
Assessment  holds  teachers  and  schools accountable  for facilitating student  learning  and  meeting  local and  national  standards.  As articulated  by Brown (2004) and  Brown et  al. (2011), assessment  results  are used  to  publicly demonstrate  whether  teachers  and  schools  meet  societal  goals  and  expectations.  This category  is  typically  operationalized  through  standardized  tests  and  high-stakes  examinations,  with results  reported  to  parents,  policymakers  and  the  public  to  establish  accountability  for teaching  quality and  educational  effectiveness  (Alonzo et  al., 2021; Brown, 2004; Brown  et  al., 2011; Takele & Melese, 2022).
Beyond evaluation,  assessment  also functions  as an institutional  mechanism  of management  and  con- trol. Constructs  such  as management-oriented,  control  and  management  and  inspection  emphasize  the use  of  assessment  to  monitor  and  regulate  the  performance  of  schools  and  teachers  through  formal consequences  linked to assessment  outcomes  (Brown & Gao, 2015; Brown et al., 2011; Brown & Remesal, 2017). As noted  by Brown et  al. (2011), schools  and  teachers  may be  rewarded  or sanctioned  based  on whether  required  standards  are  met  (p. 317). Through  these  performance-based  and  control-oriented mechanisms,  assessment  institutionalizes  teacher  and  school  accountability  and  aligns professional  prac- tice with system-level  goals.
3.5.2. Normative and legitimacy-oriented accountability
Normative  and  legitimacy-oriented  accountability  treats  accountability  as contingent  on  whether  assess- ment  practices  and  decisions  can  be  justified against  shared  normative  principles  and  legitimacy  stand - ards.  In contrast  to  performance-based  control,  it emphasizes  the  normative  justification  of assessment procedures  which are used  in ways that  minimize measurement  error and  are ethically justified and  pro- cedurally fair.

Constructs  within the  first aspect  emphasize  whether  assessment  results  accurately  reflect students   true abilities  or  what  the  assessment  claims  to  measure,  focusing  on  the  avoidance  of  measurement  error and  construct-irrelevant  influences  that  may distort  assessment  outcomes  (e.g., Error, Avoid score  pollu- tion, Balanced). Constructs  within  the  second  aspect  emphasize  the  procedural  justification  and  fair use of assessment,  highlighting  the  importance  of clear, consistent  and  equitable  assessment  practices  that support  the  credibility  and  appropriate  use  of  assessment  outcomes  (e.g.,  Consistent,  Standard, Equitable, Procedural  justice, Unambiguity).
It  should  be  noted  that  these  two  aspects  are  not  independent  but  are  closely  intertwined.  This means  that  how  assessment  is conducted  and  used  directly  affects  the  credibility of assessment  scores and  outcomes.  When  assessment  procedures  are  inappropriate  or  unfairly applied,  the  validity of  the resulting  scores  and  interpretations  is undermined.  Accordingly, procedural  fairness and  consistency  are commonly  treated  as conditions  for reducing  score  distortion  and  error, thereby  supporting  the  credibil- ity  of  assessment  outcomes  (Ahmadi  Safa  & Nasiri, 2025;  Brown  et  al.,  2011;  Coombs  et  al.,  2022; Sonnleitner  & Kovacs, 2020). Empirically, concerns  about  measurement  error  or score  pollution  tend  to co-occur  with  evaluations  of  procedural  clarity, fairness  and  consistency  (Ahmadi  Safa & Nasiri, 2025; Brown et  al., 2011; Liu et  al., 2024). Together,  these  patterns  suggest  that  the  identified  constructs  cap- ture  a  coherent  belief  system  concerning  the  accountable,  trustworthy  and  appropriate  use  of assess- ment  outcomes.
3.6. Assessment  is irrelevant or negative
In this category,  assessment  is perceived  as irrelevant  in relation  to teaching  and  learning  due  to factors such  as  a  lack of ability to  provide  accurate  and  trustworthy  information  about  student  achievement, the  adverse  effect assessment  has on teacher  autonomy  and  on  the  negative  impact  assessment  has on the  nature  of the  curriculum  and  student  well-being  (Table 9). Assessment  is considered  irrelevant  if it does  not  align  with  class learning  outcomes  or goals,  leading  to  a disconnect  between  the  assessment and  its effectiveness  in evaluating  student  progress  and  achievement  (Brown, 2004; Brown et al., 2011).
In practice,  teachers  tend  to  rely on  their  deep  understanding  of the  curriculum  and  their  ongoing interactions  with students  instead  of information  from formal assessment  tasks. 4. Discussion  and conclusion
This systematic  review examined  26 quantitative  inventories  that  aim to  measure  teachers   conceptions of  assessment  (TCoA), revealing  a  complex  and  fragmented  landscape  of  assessment  constructs. Through  inductive  thematic  synthesis,  we  identified  seven  core  categories:  five  purposes  (teaching improvement,  student  development,  student  accountability,  teachers  and  school  accountability,  irrele- vance/negativity)  and  two  reflecting  assessment  formats  (formal, informal). Together,  these  dimensions offer a synthesized  conceptual  framework  for evaluating  how teachers  understand  the  functions  and  val- ues of assessment  in educational  settings.
4.1. Research limitations
This review is limited  to  studies  involving primary  and  secondary  school  teachers  and  may  not  capture distinctive  assessment  conceptions  held  by  educators  in early childhood  or  tertiary  education  settings. Moreover,  while  the  review  synthesizes  construct  definitions  across  26 quantitative  inventories,  it does not  examine  how  these  beliefs  are  enacted  within  specific institutional  contexts  or  shaped  by  broader cultural  and  policy environments.  The analysis is based  on  author-defined  constructs  rather  than  item- level  data,  which  may  limit  the  detection  of  subtle  variations  in  construct  meaning  or  measurement structure.  In addition,  the  findings  are  limited  to  constructs  defined  within  a psychometric  perspective. They  primarily  reflect  how  assessment-related  constructs  are  operationalized  in  quantitative  measure - ment  frameworks  and  may  not  capture  alternative  conceptualizations  identified  with  other  methodo - logical  choices.  For example,  in  this  study,  fairness  is defined  as  procedural  equality,  as  derived  from existing  inventories.  However,  research  adopting  critical  perspectives  conceptualizes  fairness  more broadly  in relation  to  ethical  and  contextual  considerations  encompassing  issues  of bias, representation and  the  equitable  treatment  of diverse  groups  (Tajeddin  et  al., 2022). Such  aspects  of fairness  remain largely absent  from the  assessment  instruments  analyzed  in this study.
Future  research  could  expand  to  other  educational  levels and  further  examine  how  teachers   concep - tions  of assessment  are  shaped  by contextual  factors  such  as teacher—student  accountability  dynamics, school  culture  and  education  policy. It could  also incorporate  qualitative  or mixed-methods  approaches to better  capture  how these  conceptions  are formed,  enacted  and  transformed  in context,  including  eth- ical and  experiential  dimensions  not  represented  in current  inventories.
4.2. Conceptual convergence  across TCoA inventories
The  wide  array  of  existing  TCoA inventories  is commonly  assumed  to  reflect  a  diverse  and  evolving understanding  of  assessment  conceptions.  Yet, our  analysis  reveals  a  surprising  degree  of  conceptual convergence.  Most inventories  emphasize  two dominant  functions  of assessment:  (1) improving  teaching and  learning  and  (2) ensuring  student  or institutional  accountability.  This duality has been  noted  in prior work  on  teachers   assessment  conceptions  as  a  recurring  tension  shaping  how  teachers  think  about assessment  (Bonner, 2016; Brown, 2017). Although  policy frameworks  uniquely  shape  teacher  thinking about  assessment  in a potentially  unique  fashion  within  each  jurisdiction  (Xu & Brown, 2016), this  ana- lysis suggests  that  there  are  two  relatively  universal  pressures  that  shape  teacher  thinking  across  the studied  inventories.
These  purposes  are  not  merely  descriptive;  they  reflect  deeper  ideological  commitments  embedded within  dominant  forms of educational  thinking  and  practice.  The improvement-oriented  view, grounded in  constructivist  and  learner-centred  paradigms  (Black & Wiliam, 1998; Sadler,  1989), positions  assess- ment  as a formative,  dialogic  and  developmental  tool.  In contrast,  the  accountability  perspective  aligns with  managerialist  logics, framing  assessment  as  a  mechanism  of  regulation,  ranking  and  institutional control  (Brown, 2004; Brown & Michaelides,  2011). This dual  orientation  has  been  widely  observed  in empirical  studies.  This finding  is in  parallel  to  Brown s (2004) model,  which  reports  that  teachers  fre- quently  endorse  both  improvement  and  accountability  purposes,  supporting  classroom  learning  while also  complying  with  institutional  expectations.  Similarly, studies  have  been  found  to  navigate  between assessment  for learning  and  assessment  of learning,  reflecting  a dual commitment  to formative  develop - ment  and  systemic accountability  (DeLuca et al., 2016; Remesal, 2011). At the  policy level, efforts to inte- grate  formative  and  summative  functions,  aiming  to  improve  teaching  while  ensuring  public accountability,  have  also been  widely documented  (Looney, 2011).
4.3. Theoretical ambiguity and construct redundancy
4.3.1. Different labels, same meaning
While the  convergence  of core  assessment  purposes  across  TCoA inventories  reflects  a shared  concep - tual  foundation,  it also highlights  a recurring  issue  of construct  redundancy  and  measurement  ambigu - ity. First, many  inventories  suffer  from  terminological  inconsistency,  where  key  constructs  are  labeled ambiguously  or used  interchangeably  without  theoretical  justification. A prominent  source  of confusion lies in  the  inconsistent  use  of  labels  such  as  formative  assessment,  assessment  for  learning  (AfL) and improvement.  These constructs  commonly  appear  across inventories  with overlapping  content  or similar conceptual  intent,  namely  framing  assessment  as  a  tool  to  support  student  learning  and  instructional refinement.  Yet their  meanings  are  seldom  differentiated  in theoretically  meaningful  ways. For example, formative  assessment  is  traditionally  rooted  in  teacher-led  feedback  cycles  (Sadler,  1989),  while  AfL emphasizes  student  agency,  dialogue  and  co-construction  of learning  goals  (Black & Wiliam, 1998). The construct  improvement  frequently  used  in  the  TCoA-III and  its  derivatives  (Brown, 2004; Brown et  al., 2011), is often  loosely  defined,  encompassing  both  student  learning  gains  and  teaching  effectiveness without  specifying underlying  mechanisms.
Redundancy  is further  exacerbated  by  inventories  that  replicate  the  same  construct  across  multiple measurement  levels. For example,  although  Improvement  is explicitly labeled  as  a  factor  in  only  two inventories  (Brown, 2004; Brown et  al., 2011), several  others  implicitly reflect the  same  idea  through  the construct  content  highlighting  assessment s  instructional  or learning  function  (e.g., Brown & Gao, 2015; Brown & Remesal, 2017; Wu, 2017; Zhao  et  al., 2018). These findings  suggest  a shared  belief in the  for- mative  value of assessment,  but  the  lack of terminological  consistency  contributes  to  semantic  ambigu - ity and  structural  redundancy.
A  similar  pattern  emerges  in  the  use  of  control-oriented  constructs  such  as  Examination, Accountability,  Summative  Assessment,  Management,  Control  and  Certification.  Although  all  of  these refer to institutional  and  consequential  purposes  of assessment,  their theoretical  origins, policy semantics and  cognitive  interpretations  vary  considerably.  Across inventories,  these  labels  are  often  used  inter- changeably  without  clarifying  their  intended  referents  or  practical  implications,  resulting  in  semantic conflation  and  conceptual  overlap.  For instance,  Brown and  Remesal (2017) use  Control  to  denote  top- down  enforcement  mechanisms,  while  Alonzo  et  al. (2021) conceptualize  similar regulatory  dynamics under  the  label  Instructional  Accountability,  portraying  it as  teachers   beliefs  about  meeting  mandated standards  and  ensuring  student  performance.  Despite  capturing  analogous  phenomena,  the  constructs are embedded  at different  levels of abstraction  and  framed  inconsistently,  thereby  complicating  theoret -
ical synthesis  and  cross-instrument  comparisons.
4.3.2. Same label, different meaning
Conversely, another  form of conceptual  ambiguity  arises when  the  same  label  is used  to  denote  differ- ent  meanings  across  inventories.  Although  such  terminological  convergence  might  suggest  construct alignment,  closer scrutiny often  reveals divergent  theoretical  assumptions  and  operational  definitions.
This problem  can  be  observed  most  clearly in the  use  of the  labels formative  assessment  and  assess- ment  for learning  (AfL), which  recur  across  many  TCoA-related inventories.  In some  inventories,  forma- tive assessment  is treated  as a broad  belief orientation  reflecting  teachers   views about  learning-centred assessment  (e.g., Sach, 2012; Yan & Pastore,  2022). In others,  however,  the  same  label  is used  to  oper- ationalize  teachers   self-reported  classroom  practices,  shifting  the  construct  away  from  underlying  con- ceptions  toward  enacted  or reported  instructional  behaviors.  This divergence  is evident  in studies  such as Young and  Jackman  (2014), where  formative  assessment  is discussed  in terms  of teachers   perceptions and  attitudes,  yet  the  questionnaire  places  substantial  emphasis  on  the  frequency  of specific practices, including  feedback  provision,  peer  assessment  and  instructional  adjustment.  A similar pattern  appears in Martin et  al. (2022), whose  study  refers  to  teachers   perceptions  of formative  assessment  while  the inventory  primarily captures  reported  use, perceived  benefits  and  implementation  strategies.  As a result, identical  labels  are  used  to  describe  constructs  situated  at  different  levels of abstraction,  ranging  from 
belief-oriented  orientations  to practice-based  enactments.
Another  illustrative  example  is  the  construct  Examination.  In  the  C-TCoA inventory  (Brown  et  al., 2011), Examination  is nested  within  a  broader  Accountability  factor  and  refers  to  external  institutional demands,  such  as  high-stakes  testing  and  systemic  performance  monitoring.  In  contrast,  Wu  (2017) presents  Examination as an independent  construct  more  closely associated  with classroom  management and  instructional  regulation.  Although  the  same  term  is used,  it reflects  distinct  meanings:  one  aligned with  institutional  compliance  and  the  other  with  local pedagogical  control,  highlighting  how  superficial label consistency  may obscure  deeper  theoretical  divergence.
4.4. Implications
This study  examined  inventories  designed  to  operationalize  teachers   conceptions  of  assessment  and found  substantial  convergence  despite  their  distinct  theoretical  origins.  Teachers   beliefs  were  largely organized  around  two dominant  logics (i.e., improvement  vs accountability)  rather  than  clearly differenti- ated  structures.  This pattern  reflects  a  persistent  jingle—jangle problem,  in  which  conceptually  similar constructs  are  labeled  differently  across  inventories,  complicating  cross-instrument  comparison  and interpretation.
These  findings  have  important  implications  for  educational  practice.  Survey  evidence  on  teachers  assessment  conceptions  is  widely  used  to  inform  policy,  instructional  reform  and  professional  learn- ing.  When  similar  assessment  purposes  or  functions  are  operationalized  under  different  labels,  inter- pretations  may  become  inconsistent,  obscuring  teachers   underlying  beliefs  and  weakening  links  to assessment  enactment.  Clarifying construct  boundaries  would  enable  more  consistent  interpretation of  survey  results,  thereby  supporting  more  targeted  professional  learning  initiatives  and  more  coher- ent  assessment  reform  efforts.  At a  broader  level, such  clarification  also  facilitates  shared  assessment language  across  frameworks,  strengthening  the  conceptual  basis  for  instructional  planning  and  feed- back  practices.
These  implications  extend  to  how  teachers   conceptions  are  measured.  Existing inventories  primarily focus  on  how  teachers  conceptualize  assessment  purposes  and  forms; however,  these  orientations  may not  fully capture  how assessment  is enacted  in real classroom  contexts.  Most inventories  rely on abstract and  decontextualized  items,  giving little attention  to  the  specific environments  in which  teachers  work. In practice,  teachers   beliefs about  assessment  are shaped  by a range  of contextual  and  personal  factors, including  policy  and  school  environments  (e.g., Brown & Gao, 2015; Brown & Harris, 2009), emotional experiences  (e.g., Brown et  al., 2018; Estaji et  al., 2026) and  professional  knowledge  (e.g., Xu & Brown, 2016). Therefore,  future  research  could  incorporate  scenario-based  measurements  by  embedding  items within  specific instructional  situations,  prompting  teachers  to  draw  on  their  practical  experience  when making  judgments  (e.g., Banitalebi et  al., 2025; DeLuca et  al., 2016). It is also important  to  consider  the epistemic  and  ethical  foundations  underlying  teachers   conceptions,  as assessment  is not  a purely  tech- nical activity but  is shaped  by  broader  social and  ideological  contexts  (e.g., Tajeddin  et  al., 2022). This helps  avoid oversimplifying what  teachers  actually do in practice  as captured  in assessment  instruments. These  limitations  may  also  point  to  broader  differences  in  how  assessment  is  conceptualized  across 
paradigms.
Building on  this, further  research  is needed  to consolidate  conceptually  overlapping  constructs  across TCoA inventories.  Constructs  labeled  as  improvement,  formative  use,  assessment  for learning,  or  feed- back-mediated  learning  often  reflect a shared  belief in assessment  as instructional  support,  while exami- nation,  accountability,  or  external  control  represent  variations  of  a  common  evaluative  logic.  Future studies  should  empirically  test  whether  these  clusters  can  be  integrated  into  theoretically  coherent higher-order  factors. At the  same  time, inventories  should  move  beyond  purpose-oriented  beliefs to bet- ter  capture  teachers   capacity  to  enact  assessment  in practice.  Drawing  on  assessment  capacity  frame- works  (DeLuca  et  al.,  2024),  integrating  dimensions  such  as  professional  judgment  and  contextual responsiveness  would  broaden  construct  coverage,  clarify belief—practice relations  and  improve  the  prac- tical relevance  of future  instruments.
